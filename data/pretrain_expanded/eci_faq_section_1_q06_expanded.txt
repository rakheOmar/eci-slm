India’s House of the People, the Lok Sabha, is composed of 543 members, each elected from a distinct parliamentary constituency that covers a specific geographic area and contains roughly an equal number of eligible voters. The drawing of these constituency boundaries is not left to political parties or the executive branch; instead, it is the exclusive responsibility of an independent body known as the Delimitation Commission, a constitutionally mandated agency created under Article 82 of the Constitution of India. Article 82 requires that, after every decennial census, Parliament pass a Delimitation Act which authorises the Central Government to set up a Delimitation Commission. The commission is constituted as a high‑level, quasi‑judicial entity and typically includes a retired Supreme Court or High Court judge as its chairperson, the Chief Election Commissioner or a senior Election Commissioner as a member, and the Union Home Secretary or another senior bureaucrat. This composition is deliberately chosen to ensure that the commission operates free from partisan influence, thereby safeguarding the principle of “one person, one vote” and preventing the kind of gerrymandering that can arise when boundary‑drawing is controlled by incumbent politicians.

The Delimitation Commission’s mandate, as laid out in the relevant Delimitation Act, is to examine the latest census data, assess shifts in population density, and redraw constituency maps so that each seat represents, as closely as possible, an equal share of the nation’s populace. In practice this involves a painstaking process of consulting demographic tables, mapping administrative units such as districts, tehsils and municipal wards, and taking into account the constitutional requirement that a certain proportion of seats be reserved for Scheduled Castes (SC) and Scheduled Tribes (ST) in accordance with their share of the total population. For example, after the 2001 census, the Delimitation Commission of 2002 re‑configured the map of the country, merging some small, sparsely populated constituencies in the Himalayan states, splitting rapidly growing urban seats in Delhi and Maharashtra, and adjusting the number of SC‑reserved constituencies in Uttar Pradesh to reflect the state’s high Scheduled‑Caste population. Such changes often have tangible political consequences: a sitting Member of Parliament may find that his or her traditional voter base has been divided between two new constituencies, while a previously under‑represented region may gain a new seat, thereby altering the balance of power among national parties.

The most recent comprehensive delimitation exercise was carried out on the basis of the 2001 census, under the provisions of the Delimitation Act, 2002. That act was itself a product of a broader constitutional amendment, the 84th Amendment, which was adopted in 2001 and introduced a critical “freeze” on the redistribution of seats. The amendment stipulated that, notwithstanding the constitutional requirement to redraw boundaries after each census, the total number of seats allocated to each state would remain unchanged until after the first census conducted following the year 2026. The purpose of this freeze was to encourage states to pursue population‑stabilisation policies without fearing a loss of parliamentary representation, and to provide a stable framework for electoral politics while India’s demographic transition continued. Consequently, although the Delimitation Commission completed its technical work in the early 2000s, the boundaries it established based on the 2001 population figures have remained in force and will continue to do so until the next delimitation is triggered by the post‑2026 census.

The orders issued by the Delimitation Commission carry the force of law and are not subject to amendment by Parliament or the President; the only recourse for any aggrieved party is to file a petition in the Supreme Court, and even then the Court’s jurisdiction is limited to questions of legality, not to revising the substantive content of the commission’s decisions. This finality is intended to cement the commission’s independence and to give electoral stakeholders certainty well in advance of each general election. In addition to delimiting parliamentary constituencies, the commission simultaneously redraws the boundaries of state legislative‑assembly constituencies, thereby ensuring coherence between the two levels of representation.

Historical precedents illustrate the significance of the commission’s work. The first delimitation, undertaken after the 1951 census, created the original map of 489 Lok Sabha seats, reflecting the population distribution of a newly independent nation. Subsequent exercises after the 1961 and 1971 censuses expanded the Lok Sabha to its current size of 543 seats, accommodated the creation of new states such as Haryana and Himachal Pradesh, and responded to massive internal migrations, for instance the influx of people into the industrial belt of Gujarat and the rise of metropolitan areas like Bengaluru. Each round of delimitation has been accompanied by vigorous public debate, with political parties often lobbying for boundary adjustments that might favour their electoral prospects. Nevertheless, the commission’s statutory independence, its reliance on objective census data, and its transparent public‑hearing process have generally insulated the delimitation exercise from overt partisan manipulation.

In summary, the task of demarcating India’s 543 parliamentary constituencies rests with the Delimitation Commission, an autonomous institution constituted under Article 82 of the Constitution following each decennial census. The commission operates under the framework of a Delimitation Act—most recently the Delimitation Act of 2002—and applies the population figures of the 2001 census to define constituency borders, while also incorporating constitutional provisions for SC/ST reservation. Because the 84th Amendment froze the redistribution of seats until after the first census conducted after 2026, the boundaries drawn in the early 2000s continue to govern elections across the country, ensuring that each citizen’s vote carries roughly equal weight until a new delimitation cycle commences in the post‑2026 period. This institutional arrangement exemplifies India’s commitment to equitable representation, legal certainty, and the depoliticisation of the crucial process of electoral map‑making.

The allocation of seats to the different States in the Lok Sabha, the lower house of India’s Parliament, is determined principally by the population of each State as recorded in the national census. Under the Constitution, the principle is that every State should receive a share of the 543 elected seats that is roughly proportional to the number of its inhabitants, thereby ensuring that the weight of each citizen’s vote is as equal as possible across the country. This demographic basis is applied by the Delimitation Commission, an independent body that periodically redraws constituency boundaries and adjusts the number of seats after each decennial census. In practice, larger and more populous States such as Uttar Pradesh, which had a population exceeding 200 million in the 2011 Census, are allotted the greatest number of seats—currently 80—while much smaller States like Sikkim, with a population of about 600,000, receive only one seat. The system seeks to balance the twin goals of fair representation for densely populated regions and a minimum presence for every State, a balance that has been maintained through constitutional freezes on seat re‑allocation (most notably the freeze from 1976 to 2020, extended to 2030) to prevent abrupt political shifts while still reflecting long‑term demographic trends.

The Lok Sabha, the lower house of India’s Parliament, incorporates a system of reserved constituencies designed to ensure that historically disadvantaged groups are represented in the legislative process. Under Article 330 of the Constitution, a specific number of seats are set aside for members of the Scheduled Castes (SC) and Scheduled Tribes (ST); the allocation of these seats is directly linked to the proportion of each community in the national population as recorded in the most recent census. For example, after the 2011 census the Lok Sabha was apportioned 84 seats for SC candidates and 47 seats for ST candidates out of a total of 543, a distribution that mirrors their demographic share. The boundaries of reserved constituencies are periodically redrawn by the Delimitation Commission so that the reservation reflects shifts in population patterns, thereby maintaining equitable political participation for these special categories.

The seats that are set aside for members of the Scheduled Castes (SC) and Scheduled Tribes (ST) in India’s lower house of Parliament, the Lok Sabha, are not assigned arbitrarily; they are determined by a proportional formula that reflects the demographic weight of these communities within each individual state. Specifically, the number of constituencies reserved for SC or ST candidates in a given state is calculated by comparing the percentage of the SC or ST population in that state with the percentage of the same groups in the country as a whole. This method ensures that the representation of historically disadvantaged groups in the national legislature mirrors their actual share of the population, thereby promoting a more equitable political system. The constitutional foundation for this arrangement is found in Article 330 of the Constitution of India, which mandates reservation of seats for SCs and STs in the Lok Sabha. The procedural details for implementing the provision are laid out in Section 3 of the Representation of the People Act, 1950, which translates the constitutional directive into an operable electoral mechanism. For example, if a state’s population comprises 15 % Scheduled Castes, roughly 15 % of its Lok Sabha seats will be earmarked for SC candidates, and a similar calculation applies to Scheduled Tribes, thereby aligning parliamentary representation with demographic realities.

In the lower house of India’s Parliament, known as the Lok Sabha, a total of eighty‑four constituencies are earmarked exclusively for candidates belonging to the Scheduled Castes (SC). This reservation system is rooted in the constitutional commitment to provide political representation to historically disadvantaged groups, and it seeks to ensure that the voices of SC communities are heard in the nation’s legislative deliberations. The legal foundation for this arrangement is found in the First Schedule of the Representation of the People Act, 1950, which was later revised by the Representation of the People (Amendment) Act of 2008. That amendment not only reaffirmed the number of reserved seats but also detailed a state‑wise allocation, matching the proportion of the SC population in each state and Union Territory to the total number of constituencies it contains. For example, states with larger SC populations such as Uttar Pradesh and Bihar have a correspondingly higher count of reserved seats, whereas smaller states have fewer. By distributing the eighty‑four seats across the country in this manner, the legislative framework aims to promote inclusive governance and to reflect the demographic realities of India’s diverse society.

The Lok Sabhā, the lower house of India’s Parliament, includes a system of constituency reservations intended to ensure the political representation of historically disadvantaged communities. Under this system, a total of forty‑seven seats are set aside exclusively for members of the Scheduled Tribes (ST), a classification that encompasses a diverse array of indigenous peoples recognized in the Constitution of India. The allocation of these seats is not arbitrary; it is prescribed in the first schedule of the Representation of the People Act, 1950, which delineates the distribution of ST constituencies on the basis of the proportion of tribal populations in each state and union territory. This schedule was subsequently revised by the Representation of the People (Amendment) Act, 2008, which refined the state‑wise breakdown to reflect demographic changes revealed by the most recent censuses. For example, states such as Madhya Pradesh, Odisha, and Chhattisgarh each contain multiple ST‑reserved constituencies, while smaller tribal‑dominant regions like Mizoram and Nagaland have a larger share of the total forty‑seven seats relative to their population. The reservation mechanism thus seeks to provide tribal communities with a meaningful voice in the legislative process, aligning with the broader constitutional commitment to social justice and inclusive governance.

In the Indian parliamentary system the Lok Sabha, or House of the People, is composed of members elected from constituencies spread across the country, with the number of seats allotted to each state or Union Territory being largely determined by its population. A small number of regions, however, are represented by only a single member, reflecting both their modest population size and the practical need to ensure that even the most sparsely populated areas have a voice in the national legislature. The states and Union Territories that each contribute just one seat to the Lok Sabha are Mizoram, Nagaland, Sikkim, the Andaman and Nicobar Islands, Chandigarh, Dadra and Nagar Haveli, Daman and Diu, Lakshadweep, and Puducherry. For example, the hilly northeastern state of Mizoram, with a population of around 1.2 million, elects a lone representative who speaks for the interests of its diverse tribal communities. Similarly, Nagaland, another northeastern state, sends one MP to Parliament despite its cultural and linguistic richness. The tiny Himalayan kingdom of Sikkim, once an independent monarchy, also enjoys a single seat. The Andaman and Nicobar Islands, a remote archipelago in the Bay of Bengal, are represented by one member, as are the tiny island groups of Lakshadweep in the Arabian Sea. The Union Territories of Chandigarh, a planned city that serves as the joint capital of Punjab and Haryana, and Puducherry, a former French colony on the southeastern coast, each elect one MP. Finally, the former separate Union Territories of Dadra and Nagar Haveli and Daman and Diu, now merged into a single administrative unit, together retain a single Lok Sabha seat, reflecting their combined population of just a few hundred thousand. This arrangement guarantees that even the smallest political entities in India have direct representation in the country's highest legislative body.

India is a federal union composed of twenty‑nine constituent states, each possessing its own elected government, legislature, and administrative apparatus while remaining integral parts of the nation’s single constitutional framework. The roster of these states reflects the country’s remarkable geographic, linguistic, and cultural diversity, ranging from the sun‑kissed coasts of Andhra Pradesh and Kerala in the south to the snow‑capped Himalayas of Himachal Pradesh and the far‑flung reaches of Arunachal Pradesh in the northeast. In the east, the fertile plains of Bihar, Odisha (formerly Orissa), and West Bengal sit alongside the mineral‑rich hills of Jharkhand, whereas central India is dominated by the expansive territories of Madhya Pradesh, Chhattisgarh, and the industrial powerhouse of Maharashtra. The western segment includes the desert state of Rajasthan, the vibrant commercial hub of Gujarat, the coastal enclave of Goa, and the agriculturally important Punjab and Haryana. Further north, the historically contested region of Jammu and Kashmir (now a union territory but often still cited in older enumerations) shares its border with the newly named Uttarakhand (formerly Uttaranchal), while Uttar Pradesh remains the most populous state in the federation. The southern peninsula contributes Karnataka, Tamil Nadu, and the recently formed Telangana, each renowned for technology, cinema, and heritage, respectively. The northeastern corridor adds a mosaic of smaller states—Assam, Manipur, Meghalaya, Mizoram, Nagaland, Sikkim, and Tripura—each preserving distinct tribal languages and customs. Finally, the westernmost state of Gujarat and the coastal state of Goa complete the list, underscoring how the twenty‑nine states together embody the pluralistic tapestry that defines the Indian Union. This enumeration, while reflecting the administrative arrangement at a particular point in time, also illustrates the dynamic evolution of India’s internal boundaries, which have been reshaped over decades through linguistic reorganization, statehood movements, and constitutional amendments.

India is divided into twenty‑eight states and seven Union Territories, the latter being regions that are administered directly by the central government rather than having a full state legislature. These Union Territories are geographically and culturally diverse. The Andaman and Nicobar Islands form an archipelago in the Bay of Bengal and are renowned for their pristine beaches and strategic naval bases. Chandigarh, situated at the foothills of the Himalayas, serves as the shared capital of the neighboring states of Punjab and Haryana while functioning as a standalone Union Territory. In the western part of the country, the former separate entities of Dadra and Nagar Haveli and Daman and Diu were merged in 2020 to create a single Union Territory comprising a mainland enclave and two coastal districts. Delhi, officially the National Capital Territory, houses the nation’s capital and its principal institutions of government. Lakshadweep, a scattered group of atolls off the southwestern coast, is the country’s smallest Union Territory both in area and population, known for its coral reefs and marine life. Finally, Pondicherry (Puducherry) on the southeastern coast retains a distinctive French colonial legacy, reflected in its architecture, cuisine, and bilingual administration. Together, these seven Union Territories illustrate the varied administrative arrangements that complement India’s federal structure.

In the federal structure of India every full‑fledged state is required to have its own Legislative Assembly, the lower house of the state legislature that enacts laws on matters within the state’s jurisdiction. Union Territories, by contrast, are administered directly by the central government and therefore do not automatically possess such a representative body. Of the seven Union Territories that exist today, only two have been granted a locally elected Legislative Assembly: the National Capital Territory of Delhi and the Union Territory of Puducherry (formerly Pondicherry). Delhi’s assembly, established under the Constitution (Sixty‑Ninth Amendment) Act of 1991, exercises powers over subjects such as education, health, and municipal services, while Puducherry’s assembly, created by the Government of Union Territories Act of 1963, similarly handles a range of regional affairs. The remaining Union Territories—Lakshadweep, Chandigarh, Andaman and Nicobar Islands, Dadra and Nagar Haveli and Daman and Diu, and Jammu & Kashmir—are governed without a legislative assembly, relying instead on administrators appointed by the President of India. This arrangement illustrates the nuanced balance between central oversight and local self‑government within the Indian Union.
