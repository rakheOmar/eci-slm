The provision that allows an elector to lodge a formal complaint when the paper slip produced by a Voter‑Verified Paper Audit Trail (VVPAT) printer displays the name or symbol of a candidate other than the one for whom the vote was cast is embodied in Rule 49MA of the Conduct of Elections Rules, 1961, a statutory safeguard designed to reinforce the credibility of electronic voting machines (EVMs) and to provide a transparent remedy in the rare event that a mechanical or software anomaly, or even a deliberate tampering attempt, appears to have altered the recorded choice; under this rule the presiding officer at the polling station must first obtain a written declaration from the aggrieved elector in which the voter expressly states the perceived discrepancy, and the officer is required to caution the voter that making a false declaration constitutes a punishable offence under the Indian Penal Code, thereby deterring frivolous or malicious accusations while underscoring the seriousness of the allegation; once the declaration has been duly recorded, the presiding officer is empowered to permit the elector to cast a “test vote” on the same voting machine, a procedure that must be carried out in the presence of the elector, any candidates or their authorised polling agents who happen to be at the booth, and any other election officials present, so that the entire process is openly observed and any claim of irregularity can be verified in real time; during this test vote the machine prints a fresh VVPAT slip, and the elector, together with the witnesses, examines the printed paper to confirm that the name and symbol correspond to the choice that the elector actually intends to register, thereby providing an immediate visual cross‑check of the machine’s internal record against the external audit trail; if the test slip indeed matches the elector’s intended candidate and the original complaint is therefore substantiated, the presiding officer must promptly inform the returning officer of the fact, cease further use of the implicated EVM to prevent additional potentially compromised votes, and await further instructions from the returning officer, who may order a comprehensive technical inspection of the machine, the replacement of the faulty unit, or even a recount of votes recorded on that device, all measures being aimed at preserving the integrity of the election result; conversely, if the test vote’s VVPAT slip correctly reflects the elector’s selection and thus the original allegation proves to be unfounded, the presiding officer is required to annotate the second entry recorded for that voter in Form 17A (the “Statement of the Poll”) by noting the serial number of the test vote, the name of the candidate for whom the test vote was cast, and a remark that the complaint was found to be baseless, and then to obtain the elector’s signature or thumb impression as a confirmation of the entry, thereby creating an audit trail that documents both the complaint and its resolution; the same information must also be entered in item 5 of Part I of Form 17C (the “Result Sheet”) so that the record of the test vote is preserved in the official tally and can be examined during any post‑election scrutiny, legal challenge, or statistical analysis; this procedural chain not only offers the voter a concrete mechanism to raise a genuine grievance but also ensures that any false claim is formally recorded and can be used, if necessary, in subsequent legal proceedings to deter misuse of the complaint process; the rule thereby balances two essential democratic imperatives—the right of the voter to confidence that his or her vote is accurately captured and the need to protect the electoral system from spurious allegations that could erode public trust—and it exemplifies the layered safeguards embedded in India’s electoral architecture, where electronic voting is complemented by paper‑based verification, statutory penalties for deception, and meticulous documentation through standardized forms; for example, during the 2019 general election several polling stations in the northern states reported isolated incidents in which voters alleged that the VVPAT printout displayed an incorrect symbol, and in each case the prescribed sequence of obtaining a written statement, conducting a supervised test vote, and recording the outcome in Forms 17A and 17C was followed, resulting in either the temporary withdrawal of the affected machine when a genuine fault was detected or the filing of a false‑declaration complaint when the test vote proved the machine to be functioning correctly; such real‑world applications illustrate how Rule 49MA operates not merely as a theoretical provision but as a practical tool that empowers electors, informs election officials, and ultimately reinforces the legitimacy of the democratic process.

The Voter‑Verifiable Paper Audit Trail (VVPAT) system was incorporated into India’s electoral machinery as a direct response to a landmark judgment of the Supreme Court in *Dr. Subramanian Swamy v. Election Commission of India* (2013), in which the Court directed the Election Commission of India (ECI) to furnish voters with a tangible, paper‑based confirmation of each electronic vote cast. Acting on that directive, the ECI issued a series of notifications beginning in 2014 that mandated the deployment of VVPAT units in conjunction with electronic voting machines (EVMs) for selected elections, initially limited to pilot projects in states such as Gujarat, Rajasthan and Uttar Pradesh. The legal framework governing the use of VVPATs is now embedded in several statutory instruments. The Conduct of Election Rules, 1961, were amended in 2015 to prescribe the technical specifications of VVPAT devices, the procedure for printing a slip that shows the symbol and name of the chosen candidate, and the requirement that the slip be visible to the voter for seven seconds before it is automatically cut and stored in a sealed box. Further reinforcement came through the Representation of People (Amendment) Act, 2019, which made VVPATs compulsory for all elections to the Lok Sabha, State Legislative Assemblies and Legislative Councils, thereby extending the earlier pilot‑phase mandate to the entire country. Under these provisions, the paper slips must be retained for a minimum of three days after voting, and they can be produced for verification during recounts, audits or in response to litigations, as clarified by the Supreme Court in a 2020 decision that affirmed VVPAT records as admissible evidence. In practice, the 2019 general election witnessed VVPATs being used at more than 1.1 million polling stations, and several high‑profile disputes—such as the contested result in the Amethi constituency—were resolved by cross‑checking electronic tallies with the corresponding paper trails. Collectively, these legal provisions aim to bolster electoral transparency, enhance voter confidence, and provide a reliable audit mechanism that can be called upon whenever the integrity of electronic voting is called into question.
