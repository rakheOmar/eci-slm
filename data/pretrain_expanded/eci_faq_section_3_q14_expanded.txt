The voter‑verified paper audit trail (VVPAT) that accompanies electronic voting machines in India already gives each elector an immediate visual confirmation of the choice that has been recorded; a printed slip is momentarily displayed behind a transparent window and the voter can see that the symbol and name printed on the paper correspond to the candidate or party selected on the screen. Once the voter is satisfied, the slip automatically drops into a sealed box, and the electronic vote is counted. In addition, the Indian electoral framework incorporates a statutory safeguard—Rule 49 of the Model Act on Electronic Voting—which expressly permits a voter to raise an objection if the printed slip does not match the selection shown on the ballot unit. Under this rule, the poll official can investigate the discrepancy, record it, and, where appropriate, allow the voter to cast a fresh vote. Because this combination of on‑the‑spot visual verification and a formal grievance mechanism already ensures that any mismatch can be reported and corrected, redesigning the VVPAT to require a separate “approval” step before the vote is finally committed would add a layer of procedure that is largely redundant. Moreover, introducing an extra confirmation button or a cancellation option could lengthen voting times, increase the risk of procedural errors, and complicate the already‑tight logistics of polling stations, especially in densely populated constituencies. Consequently, the Election Commission of India has little practical incentive to adopt a more elaborate system when the existing arrangement already satisfies the principle of voter‑verified voting while maintaining efficiency and reliability.

First‑level checking (FLC) of electronic voting machines (EVMs) and their accompanying Voter‑Verified Paper Audit Trails (VVPATs) is a mandatory procedural step that is carried out before any election in which the devices are to be employed. The purpose of this initial inspection is to verify that each machine is in proper working order, that the software has been correctly loaded, and that the VVPAT units are synchronised with their corresponding EVMs, thereby guaranteeing the integrity and reliability of the voting process. In the context of scheduled general elections—whether for a state legislative assembly or for the Lok Sabha (the lower house of Parliament)—the timing of the first‑level check is governed by statutory guidelines that provide ample lead‑time for any remedial action that may be required. For state‑level elections, election authorities typically begin the FLC exercise at least four months before the announced polling date; this interval allows technical teams to conduct thorough functional tests, replace defective units, and train election officials on the handling of the equipment. For national‑level Lok Sabha elections, the schedule is even more expansive, with first‑level checking normally commencing no later than six months prior to the election, reflecting the larger scale of the operation and the greater number of machines that must be inspected across the country. When a vacancy arises between regular electoral cycles, a by‑election is called, and the same procedural safeguards apply, albeit on a compressed timetable. In such cases, the first‑level checking is usually performed within one month of the vacancy being declared, ensuring that the newly required polling stations are equipped with fully vetted EVM‑VVPAT sets before voting takes place. These carefully timed inspections are an essential component of India’s electoral infrastructure, helping to maintain public confidence in the electronic voting system by demonstrating that each device has been examined and certified fit for use well in advance of the ballot.

The first‑level verification of electronic voting machines (EVMs) and their accompanying Voter‑Verifiable Paper Audit Trail (VVPAT) units is a carefully staged procedure that is carried out in full view of representatives from all nationally and state‑recognised political parties, thereby ensuring transparency and confidence in the electoral apparatus before any poll is conducted. The process begins with a thorough cleaning of every machine; technicians dust the exterior housings, remove any residual address tags that were affixed during previous deployments, and extract stray ballot papers or paper‑clips that might have become lodged in the device’s interior. In addition, any inadvertent superscriptions—such as handwritten notes or markings that could be mistaken for official symbols—are wiped away from both the EVM and the VVPAT to guarantee that the visual appearance of the equipment is pristine and that no extraneous information could influence voters or observers.

Once the machines are physically clean, a systematic visual inspection is performed. Inspectors examine the battery unit (BU), the control unit (CU), the VVPAT module, the connecting cable, the cable‑to‑machine connector, the latches that secure the units, and the carrying cases that protect them during transport. The goal of this stage is to detect any external damage—cracks in the plastic casing, broken hinges, loose or missing switches, or any signs of tampering such as scratches that could indicate the removal of components. For example, a cracked latch on a control unit could cause the device to open unintentionally, compromising the integrity of the vote‑recording process, so any such defect would be logged and the affected unit withdrawn from the polling‑station inventory.

The next step delves beneath the surface through an internal inspection of the BU, CU, and VVPAT. In the presence of party observers, authorised election‑engineers open the cabinets of the battery and control units and visually scrutinise the printed circuit boards (PCBs). They look for spurious or unauthorised components, such as extra chips, solder bridges, or any modifications that could alter the machine’s logic. This “open‑box” examination is documented on a prescribed certification form, which the engineer signs to attest that the internal hardware matches the design specifications supplied by the Election Commission. The presence of party representatives during this stage serves a dual purpose: it deters covert tampering and provides the parties with a tangible record that the hardware has not been altered since its last certification.

After the mechanical and structural checks are complete, the functional integrity of the units is verified using a dedicated Pre‑First‑Level‑Check Unit (P‑FLCU). The P‑FLCU is a specialised test rig that can simulate the power‑on sequence, key‑press operations, and data‑transfer pathways of a live election. By connecting the BU, CU, and VVPAT to the P‑FLCU, engineers can confirm that the control unit correctly registers button presses, that the battery supplies stable voltage, and that the VVPAT prints a legible paper record for every simulated vote. Electrical continuity tests are run on the connectors, and software diagnostics confirm that the firmware version on each unit is the current, approved release. Any unit that fails these electronic checks is flagged for repair or replacement before it ever reaches a polling booth.

With the hardware verified, the machines move to the symbol‑loading phase. The VVPATs are placed into a Symbol Loading Unit (SLU), a device that imprints the party symbols on the paper ribbons that will later be printed when a voter casts a ballot. To maintain openness, a large monitor—typically at least 24 inches in diagonal—displays the live loading process so that party delegates can see each symbol being transferred onto the paper strip in real time. Importantly, only dummy symbols are used during this exercise; the symbols are not the actual ones that will appear on ballots, but they are identical in size, shape, and colour, thereby allowing the parties to verify that the loading mechanism works correctly without exposing the actual electoral symbols prematurely. This stage also serves as a rehearsal for the later “mock poll” that will be conducted.

Finally, a mock voting exercise known as the casting of 96 votes is carried out. In this simulated poll, the control unit is programmed to allow exactly ninety‑six vote selections—typically eight candidates for each of twelve constituencies, mirroring the maximum number of options that a voter might encounter in a real election. Each dummy vote is entered via the ballot buttons, and the VVPAT prints a corresponding paper slip, which is then collected and tallied. Observers watch as the printed slips match the selections made on the control unit, confirming that the mechanical linkage between the electronic vote register and the paper audit trail is flawless. The results of this mock poll are recorded, signed by the engineers, and cross‑checked against the electronic counts to ensure perfect concordance. Any discrepancy—however minor—triggers an immediate investigation, and the affected machines are taken out of service until the cause is identified and rectified.

Together, these interconnected steps—cleaning, external visual inspection, internal hardware verification, functional testing with the P‑FLCU, symbol loading via the SLU, and the mock casting of ninety‑six votes—constitute the comprehensive first‑level checking protocol for EVMs and VVPATs. By embedding party representatives throughout the process, the Election Commission not only safeguards the technical soundness of the voting equipment but also builds public trust, demonstrating that the devices used to record the will of the electorate have been rigorously examined and certified as fit for purpose before any ballot is ever cast.

The first‑level checking (FLC) of electronic voting machines (EVMs) is a comprehensive pre‑election quality‑assurance exercise that is carried out by a team of technical engineers, election officials and, where required, representatives of recognised political parties in order to confirm that every component of the voting system is correctly assembled, fully functional and securely sealed before it is dispatched to polling stations.  In the Indian context the EVM is a two‑part device: the Control Unit (CU), which stores the electronic tally, and the Ballot Unit (BU), which the voter interacts with; a third, optional component is the Voter‑Verifiable Paper Audit Trail (VVPAT) printer, which produces a paper slip each time a vote is cast so that a physical record of the vote exists for later verification.  The first step of the FLC procedure begins with the “P‑FLCU” – a portable FLC unit that houses a test version of the CU – being connected to the CU, the BU and the VVPAT after the symbols of the contesting candidates have been loaded onto the machine’s memory.  Once the hardware is linked, a standardised test vote pattern is entered: each of the sixteen candidate buttons on the BU is pressed six times, resulting in a total of ninety‑six test votes (16 × 6 = 96).  This deliberate pattern is chosen because it is simple enough for the technicians to repeat reliably yet sufficiently diverse to exercise every button and every internal circuit of the device.  After the test votes are entered, the electronic total displayed on the CU is compared with the count of the printed VVPAT slips that were automatically generated during the same exercise.  The two tallies must match exactly; any discrepancy would indicate a fault in the electronic counting logic, the paper‑printing mechanism, or the communication link between the BU and the CU, and would trigger an immediate fault‑diagnosis routine.  When the electronic and paper counts are found to be identical, the test data stored in the CU’s volatile memory are deliberately erased – a step described as “clearing the result from the Control Unit” – so that the machine is returned to a pristine, ready‑to‑vote state.  This entire sequence – connection, test‑vote casting, dual verification and data clearing – is repeated for every set of units that are slated for deployment, meaning that each Ballot Unit, each Control Unit and each VVPAT device undergoes identical scrutiny before being certified for use in an actual election.

Having passed these technical checks, each component is then marked with a colour‑coded sticker that serves both as a visual cue and a documented record of its status.  A green “FLC OK” sticker is affixed to the exterior of the CU, the BU and the VVPAT to indicate that the unit has successfully completed the verification protocol; the sticker also bears the signatures of the engineers from Bharat Electronics Limited (BEL) or Electronics Corporation of India Ltd. (ECIL) – the two organisations that design and manufacture the machines – as well as the signatures of the District Election Officer (DEO) or his authorised representative, thereby providing a chain of accountability that can be audited later.  Conversely, if any unit fails to meet the required standards – for example, if the VVPAT printer skips a line, the CU displays an error code, or the BU’s button matrix is sticky – a red rejection sticker is placed on the faulty component, and the sticker explicitly records the reason for rejection (such as “printer jam” or “button malfunction”).  In addition to the stickers, a pink paper seal is applied over the opening of the Control Unit; this seal is signed by the FLC engineer who performed the test and by the FLC supervisor who oversaw the operation.  The seal not only deters tampering after the test is completed but also provides a further point of verification for party observers, because representatives of all recognised national and state political parties are permitted – and indeed encouraged – to sign the seal as witnesses to the integrity of the process.  The dual‑signature system thus creates a robust, multi‑layered record that can be cross‑checked at any later stage, from the time the machines leave the central storage facility to the moment they are finally sealed in the ballot boxes on election day.

The final element of the first‑level checking protocol is the conduct of a “mock poll”, which is a miniature, full‑scale simulation of an actual election and is performed on a statistically representative sample of the EVMs that have just been certified.  The Election Commission’s guidelines prescribe that approximately five percent of the total pool of machines be subjected to this exercise; the sampling is carried out at random so that the mock poll is not biased toward any particular model, batch or geographic region.  In practical terms, if a state has, say, twenty‑four thousand EVMs in total, the mock poll would involve roughly 1 200 machines, and the voting pattern used in the mock poll would be proportioned according to the percentages mandated for each tranche of machines: for the first one percent of machines the mock poll records twelve hundred votes, for the next two percent it records one thousand votes, and for the remaining two percent it records five hundred votes.  Each mock poll is conducted in the presence of authorised observers from the recognised national and state political parties, who are seated in a designated observation area and are given the right to watch the entire process from the loading of symbols to the printing of VVPAT slips.  As the voters (who are actually election officials or trained volunteers acting as dummy voters) press the candidate buttons on the BU, the VVPAT printer produces a paper slip for each vote, and after the predetermined number of votes has been cast, the paper slips are collected, counted manually and then compared, line‑by‑line, with the electronic tallies displayed on the corresponding CUs.  Any mismatch, however small, would be flagged immediately, the affected machine would be withdrawn from the pool, and a detailed fault report would be generated for corrective action.  Once the electronic and paper totals are verified to be identical, the results of the mock poll – including the number of votes cast for each candidate, the serial numbers of the machines involved, and the signatures of the party observers – are entered into a prescribed register that forms part of the official election record.  This register not only serves as a transparent log of the mock poll’s outcome but also provides legal evidence that the machines were tested in an open, supervised environment and that the results were independently corroborated by all major stakeholders.  

Taken together, these three interlocking stages – the technical verification of CU, BU and VVPAT units, the systematic sticker‑labelling and sealing of each component, and the statistically rigorous mock poll – constitute the backbone of the First Level Checking process.  By ensuring that every EVM is functionally sound, properly documented and independently observed before it ever reaches a polling station, the procedure safeguards the integrity of the electoral system, reduces the likelihood of technical glitches on election day, and builds confidence among political parties and the electorate that the voting process is both secure and transparent.  Moreover, the involvement of multiple independent actors – the manufacturing engineers, the election officials, and the party representatives – creates a system of checks and balances that makes it exceedingly difficult for any single party to introduce tampering or to conceal a defect.  In practice, the FLC protocol has been credited with dramatically lowering the incidence of malfunctioning machines in recent Indian elections, and it serves as a model for other jurisdictions that are seeking to blend electronic efficiency with paper‑based auditability in their own voting infrastructures.
