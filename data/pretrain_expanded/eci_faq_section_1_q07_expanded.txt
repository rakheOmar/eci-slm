A person who does not hold Indian citizenship is legally barred from standing as a candidate in any electoral contest in the country. The Constitution of India expressly sets citizenship as a prerequisite for election to public office. Article 84(a) stipulates that no individual may be qualified to be chosen for a seat in the Parliament of India unless he or she is a citizen of India. This clause is part of a broader set of eligibility requirements that also include age, mental soundness, and the absence of any disqualifications such as holding an office of profit under the government. The same principle is replicated at the sub‑national level: Article 173(a) provides that membership of a State Legislative Assembly is likewise limited to Indian citizens, thereby ensuring a uniform standard across both the central and state legislatures.

The rationale behind this restriction is rooted in the notion that legislators are entrusted with the sovereign authority to make laws and policies that affect the nation’s future; consequently, they must possess a demonstrable allegiance to the country. The provision also prevents foreign influence in the democratic process. In practice, the rule applies to all categories of non‑citizens, including permanent residents, foreign nationals, and persons holding only overseas citizenship of India (OCI), which, despite conferring many rights, does not confer full citizenship status. Judicial pronouncements have reinforced this interpretation; for example, the Supreme Court has upheld that a candidate’s lack of Indian citizenship is a fatal defect that cannot be cured by any other qualification. Consequently, any individual aspiring to contest parliamentary or state assembly elections must first ensure that they have obtained Indian citizenship through birth, descent, registration or naturalisation, in accordance with the Citizenship Act, 1955.

The Constitution of India sets a clear minimum age for individuals who wish to stand for election to the nation’s principal legislative bodies. Under Article 84(b) of the Constitution, a person must have attained the age of twenty‑five years before the date of filing nomination papers in order to be eligible to contest a seat in the Lok Sabha, the lower house of Parliament that represents the people of the entire country. This age threshold is intended to ensure that candidates possess a sufficient degree of maturity, life experience, and an understanding of public affairs before assuming the responsibilities of national legislation and governance. An analogous requirement applies to elections for the state‑level Legislative Assemblies, which are the unicameral legislatures of the individual states and union territories. The same twenty‑five‑year minimum is enshrined in Article 173(b) of the Constitution, and its operative force is reinforced by Section 36(2) of the Representation of the People Act, 1950, which details the procedural aspects of candidature. In practice, this means that a young political activist who turns twenty‑five on or before the scheduled election date can file a nomination and, if successful, become a Member of Parliament (MP) or a Member of the Legislative Assembly (MLA). The provision mirrors age limits in many democracies—such as the United Kingdom, where the minimum age for MPs is also twenty‑five—while distinguishing itself from the lower age of eighteen required for local municipal elections in India, reflecting the greater responsibilities attached to national and state legislative offices.

In order to stand for any parliamentary or legislative‑assembly election in India, a person must first be listed on the electoral roll of at least one constituency. The law that imposes this requirement is the Representation of the People Act, 1951. Section 4(d) of the Act expressly bars any individual who is not an “elector” – that is, someone whose name does not appear on the voters’ register – from filing a nomination to contest a seat in the Lok Sabha, the lower house of Parliament. A parallel provision appears in Section 5(c), which applies to elections for the state legislative assemblies; it reiterates that only those who are registered electors in any assembly constituency may be nominated as candidates. The rationale behind these provisions is to ensure that every candidate has a demonstrable link to the democratic process and is themselves eligible to vote, thereby affirming the principle of “no representation without participation.” Consequently, if an individual has never been enrolled as a voter in any parliamentary or assembly constituency – perhaps because they have recently moved, are newly eligible, or have simply never completed the registration process – they cannot legally become a candidate until they first register, obtain a voter identification card, and have their name entered in the appropriate electoral roll. Once the registration is complete, the person may then file nomination papers and, subject to other eligibility criteria, contest the election.

A person who is listed on the electoral roll of the National Capital Territory of Delhi is not confined to contesting a Lok Sabha seat only in that territory. Under the Representation of the People Act, 1951, the eligibility criteria for candidates expressly permit any registered elector to stand for election from any parliamentary constituency anywhere in the country, provided that the constituency is not one of the three areas that the Act treats specially. Specifically, Sections 4(c), 4(cc) and 4(ccc) of the Act stipulate that an elector may be nominated from every state and union territory except Assam, the Union Territory of Lakshadweep and the state of Sikkikhand (Sikkim). Consequently, a Delhi voter may legally file a nomination to contest a Lok Sabha seat in Haryana, Maharashtra, Odisha (formerly Orissa), or any other state or union territory that does not fall under the aforementioned exclusions. 

This provision reflects the Constitution’s intention to allow political participation across regional boundaries, thereby enabling candidates with broader national appeal or specific ties to a different region to seek office there. For example, a Delhi‑based lawyer who has long worked on agrarian issues in Maharashtra could stand for the parliamentary seat of Pune, or a social activist residing in Delhi could contest the constituency of Bhubaneswar in Odisha, without needing to shift his registration to those states. The only practical limitation is that the candidate must submit the required nomination paperwork, meet the usual age, citizenship and criminal‑record criteria, and adhere to the electoral‑process rules that apply uniformly across India. By observing these conditions, a Delhi‑registered voter enjoys the same nationwide candidacy rights as any other elector, with the sole exception of the three territories specifically excluded by the statute.

Under Indian law a person who has been convicted of a criminal offence and sentenced to two years’ imprisonment or more is barred from standing for election to the Parliament or to any State Legislature. This rule is laid down in Section 8(3) of the Representation of the People Act, 1951, which specifies that the disqualification takes effect as soon as the judgment is pronounced and the sentence is imposed. Consequently, an individual sentenced to three years’ rigorous imprisonment, as in the present scenario, automatically becomes ineligible to file nomination papers or to be nominated by a political party until the disqualification is lifted. The prohibition is not merely a temporary hurdle; it persists for the duration of the imprisonment and continues for a further six years after the convicted person is released, unless the conviction is set aside on appeal or a special remission is granted. The provision aims to preserve the integrity of the electoral process by preventing persons who have committed serious crimes from acquiring public office. For example, a former legislator who receives a five‑year jail term for corruption would be unable to contest any subsequent election until the statutory period of disqualification expires, illustrating how the law operationalises the principle of clean politics.

Even when a convicted individual has secured bail while his conviction is being challenged on appeal, the law does not permit him to stand for public office. The Election Commission of India, which administers the conduct of elections and enforces the qualifications and disqualifications of candidates, has issued clear guidelines that a person whose conviction has been confirmed by a court remains ineligible to contest any election until the conviction is set aside. The mere fact that the person is out of custody on bail does not erase the legal effect of the judgment; the disqualification continues to operate “pending disposal of the appeal.” This rule is intended to preserve the integrity of the electoral process by preventing individuals who have been found guilty of an offence from leveraging their political ambitions before a final judicial determination is reached. For instance, a former legislator who is convicted of corruption and subsequently obtains bail while appealing the decision cannot file nomination papers for the next legislative assembly election. Only after the appellate court overturns the conviction—or if the conviction is expunged through a successful remission—would the individual regain the statutory right to contest elections. The principle reflects the broader constitutional mandate that public office bearers must meet high standards of probity and that a standing conviction, irrespective of bail status, constitutes a bar to candidature.

Under Indian law a person who is behind bars is barred from taking part in any electoral process.  Section 62(5) of the Representation of the People Act, 1951 expressly provides that no individual shall be permitted to cast a vote at any parliamentary, state‑legislative or local election while he is “confined in a prison, whether under a sentence of imprisonment or transportation or otherwise,” and the disqualification also extends to anyone who is in the lawful custody of the police.  The provision therefore applies not only to those serving a final conviction but also to people awaiting trial, those detained under preventive or custody orders, and even those transferred to a correctional facility for non‑penal reasons such as transportation.  The rationale behind the rule is to maintain the integrity of the electoral franchise by excluding persons who, by virtue of their deprivation of liberty, are considered unable to exercise an independent and informed vote.  Consequently, a prisoner serving a ten‑year term for a financial offence, a detainee held on suspicion of a serious crime, or a suspect in police lock‑up all remain ineligible to register at the polling station or to vote by any other means until their release.  While some jurisdictions around the world have moved toward enfranchising inmates, the Indian statute continues to enforce a blanket prohibition, reflecting a long‑standing policy that the right to vote is suspended for anyone under custodial confinement.

Under the provisions of the Representation of the People Act, 1951, every individual who wishes to contest a seat in the Lok Sabha—the lower house of India’s Parliament—must submit a monetary security deposit at the time of filing nomination papers. The amount stipulated by Section 34(1)(a) of the Act is exactly Rs 25,000 (twenty‑five thousand rupees). This requirement serves two principal purposes: it discourages frivolous or unserious candidatures and helps to cover administrative expenses associated with the electoral process. The deposit is refunded to the candidate only if he or she secures at least one‑sixth of the total valid votes cast in the constituency; otherwise, the sum is forfeited to the State Treasury. The same quantum of security deposit applies uniformly across all Lok Sabha constituencies, irrespective of the size or demographic profile of the electorate. For comparison, state legislative assembly elections prescribe a lower deposit of Rs 10,000, reflecting the differing scale and cost structures of parliamentary versus state‑level contests. The deposit rule, therefore, is an integral element of India’s electoral framework, balancing the openness of candidacy with safeguards against misuse of the democratic process.

Section 34 of the Representation of the People Act, 1951, makes it clear that the financial requirement placed on any person who wishes to contest an election does not change on the basis of caste or tribal affiliation. In practice, a candidate who belongs to a Scheduled Caste (SC) or a Scheduled Tribe (ST) must submit the same security deposit that is demanded of every other aspirant: a sum of Rs 12,500 (Rupees Twelve Thousand Five Hundred Only). The deposit is intended to deter frivolous nominations and to ensure that only those who have a genuine chance of securing a substantial portion of the vote take part in the electoral process. Under the same provision, the deposit is refunded if the candidate garners at least one‑sixth of the total valid votes cast in the constituency; otherwise, it is forfeited. Although the Constitution provides for reserved seats for SC and ST candidates in parliamentary and state‑assembly elections, the monetary deposit itself is not reduced or waived for these groups. For comparison, the deposit for a Lok Sabha (House of the People) contest is Rs 25,000 for an individual candidate and Rs 12,500 for each recognized political party, while state‑legislative assembly contests generally follow the Rs 12,500 figure. Thus, while affirmative‑action policies create opportunities for SC and ST representation through seat reservation, the statutory security‑deposit requirement remains uniform across all categories of candidates.

Under Indian law, any individual who wishes to stand for election to a State Legislative Assembly must submit a monetary security deposit at the time of filing the nomination paper. This requirement is stipulated in Section 34(1)(b) of the Representation of the People Act, 1951, which was enacted to deter candidates who lack serious intent or sufficient public support from cluttering the ballot and to ensure that only those with a genuine commitment to contest the election participate. For a candidate who does not belong to any of the constitutionally recognised scheduled groups, the prescribed amount is ten thousand rupees (₹10,000). The same provision makes a concession for members of the Scheduled Castes (SC) and Scheduled Tribes (ST), recognising the historic disadvantages faced by these communities; such candidates are required to furnish a reduced deposit of five thousand rupees (₹5,000) only. The deposit is refundable provided the candidate secures a minimum proportion of the total votes cast—generally one-sixth of the valid votes—in the constituency; otherwise the amount is forfeited to the government. This system has been in place since the early years of the Republic and continues to function as a modest financial barrier that balances the need for open democratic participation with the practical necessity of limiting frivolous nominations. For example, in the 2022 Karnataka Legislative Assembly elections, every aspirant submitted the appropriate deposit as per their social category, and those who failed to meet the vote threshold lost their deposits, illustrating the rule’s ongoing relevance.

This differential treatment reflected the state’s policy of encouraging political participation among historically disadvantaged communities by reducing the financial barrier for them. The deposit was refundable, provided the candidate secured at least one‑sixth of the total valid votes cast in the constituency; otherwise, the amount was forfeited to the Exchequer as a penalty for failing to achieve a minimum level of electoral support. For example, in the 1991 general election a candidate from a major national party who received only 8 % of the vote in a densely populated constituency would have lost the Rs 500 deposit, whereas an SC candidate attaining the same vote share would forfeit Rs 250. In the Indian states that conducted Legislative Assembly elections up to and including the 1996 poll cycle, every candidate was required to lodge a monetary security deposit as a condition of filing nomination papers. This financial pledge was intended to deter frivolous or non‑serious contenders and to provide a modest guarantee that only candidates with genuine intent would contest. At that time the amount fixed for candidates belonging to the general category—those not identified as belonging to any scheduled caste or scheduled tribe—was set at Rs. 250 (Rupees Two Hundred and Fifty only). In contrast, to promote greater political participation among historically disadvantaged groups, the deposit for candidates belonging to the Scheduled Castes (SC) or Scheduled Tribes (ST) was halved, amounting to Rs. 125 (Rupees One Hundred Twenty‑Five only). The increase in the required security deposit did not occur spontaneously; it was formally instituted by legislation.  Specifically, the amendment took effect on 1 February 2010, as stipulated in Act 41 of 2009, which amended the earlier statutory framework governing tenancy agreements.  Act 41 was passed by the legislature in 2009 with the explicit purpose of strengthening the protection afforded to landlords against potential losses arising from tenant default, damage, or unpaid rent.  By raising the maximum amount that a landlord may lawfully demand as a security deposit, the law sought to create a more balanced risk‑sharing arrangement between the parties to a lease.  For example, under the previous regime a landlord could request a deposit equivalent to one month’s rent; after the amendment the ceiling was raised to two months’ rent, thereby giving landlords a larger financial cushion while still obligating tenants to provide a clear, refundable guarantee.  The effective date of 1 February 2010 allowed both existing and new tenancy contracts to be adjusted in accordance with the revised limits, ensuring uniform application across the jurisdiction.

Under the provisions of the Representation of the People Act, 1951, a candidate who is formally endorsed by a recognised national or state political party enjoys a markedly simplified nomination procedure. Section 33 of the Act stipulates that such a candidate needs the support of only a single registered elector from the constituency in which the election is being contested. In practical terms, this means that the party‑affiliated nominee must obtain the signature of just one local voter to act as the proposer on the nomination paper, rather than the multiple proposers required of independent or unrecognised‑party candidates. The rationale behind this concession is to acknowledge the organisational capacity of recognised parties, which can already demonstrate widespread public backing through their party structures and membership rolls. For example, a candidate nominated by the Bharatiya Janata Party (a recognised national party) in a Lok Sabha constituency would need to secure the endorsement of only one constituent voter, whereas an independent aspirant would have to gather the signatures of ten electors. This streamlined requirement helps expedite the filing process and reduces administrative burdens for established parties while maintaining the constitutional safeguard that every nominee is linked to at least one member of the electorate.

Under the provisions of the Representation of the People Act, 1951, the nomination of a candidate who is either standing as an independent or represents a political party that has not been granted official recognition by the Election Commission of India follows a specific procedural rule. Section 33 of the Act stipulates that such a candidate must secure the support of ten registered electors from the very constituency in which the election is being contested; these ten supporters must each sign the candidate’s nomination paper and act as proposers. The requirement serves two principal purposes. First, it ensures that a candidate has a minimal level of local backing before being allowed to appear on the ballot, thereby preventing frivolous or purely symbolic nominations. Second, it provides a safeguard against the possibility of a candidate lacking any genuine connection to the electorate. In practice, for example, if a resident of the Mumbai South constituency wishes to file a nomination as an independent, he or she must obtain the signatures of ten other Mumbai South voters, each of whom confirms their willingness to endorse the nomination. The same rule applies to candidates belonging to parties that have not achieved the status of a “recognised” state or national party, meaning that even emerging or regional parties must meet this baseline of local support before their candidates can be formally entered into the electoral contest.

According to the provisions of the Representation of the People Act, 1951, a candidate is limited to contesting a maximum of two seats in any single election to the Lok Sabha (the lower house of the Indian Parliament) or to a Vidhan Sabha (the legislative assembly of a state). This restriction is laid down in Section 33(7) of the Act, which was introduced to curb the practice of filing nominations from an unlimited number of constituencies—a habit that had earlier been permissible but was found to encourage excessive campaigning costs, administrative complications, and the need for costly by‑elections when a successful candidate won more than one seat. In practice, a person may therefore nominate himself or herself for at most two constituencies, and if he or she wins both, the law requires the candidate to retain only one seat and resign from the other within a specified period, after which a by‑poll is conducted to fill the vacancy. This rule applies uniformly to both national and state‑level elections; for example, during the 2014 general election, several prominent politicians, such as Narendra Modi, contested from two Lok Sabha constituencies, illustrating how the two‑seat ceiling operates in contemporary Indian politics.

According to provision 3354 of the electoral regulations, a candidate who is defeated in a constituency election will forfeit the security deposit that was required at the time of nomination if the candidate does not obtain at least one‑sixth of the total valid votes cast in that constituency. The security deposit—a sum of money paid in advance—serves two principal purposes: it discourages frivolous or non‑serious candidacies and provides a modest financial incentive for participants to campaign responsibly. The “one‑sixth” threshold corresponds to roughly 16.7 % of the valid votes; for example, in a constituency where 30,000 valid ballots are counted, a candidate would need to secure at least 5,000 votes to retain the deposit. If a candidate receives fewer than this proportion, the deposit is retained by the election authority and is not returned, signaling that the candidate failed to attract a minimal level of electoral support. This rule is applied uniformly across all constituencies to ensure fairness and to maintain the integrity of the electoral process.

The record for the greatest number of individuals standing for election in a single Indian constituency is held by Modakurichi, an assembly seat in the southern state of Tamil Nadu. In the 1996 contest for the Tamil Nadu Legislative Assembly, a staggering 1,033 separate candidates filed nomination papers to vie for the lone seat, eclipsing the typical field of a few dozen competitors that most constituencies see. Because the sheer volume of names could not be accommodated on a standard single‑sheet ballot, the election officials printed the voting paper in the form of a small booklet, with each candidate’s name appearing on a separate page or column so that voters could locate their preferred choice. This extraordinary proliferation of candidates was driven partly by the low barriers to entry in Indian state elections, where filing fees and security deposits are modest, encouraging a wide array of independents, local activists, and fringe party members to contest. Although only one candidate ultimately won the seat, the Modakurichi episode remains a striking illustration of the inclusivity and logistical challenges of India's democratic process, highlighting how the country's electoral machinery adapts to accommodate even the most crowded of contests.

The Election Commission of India, which is the autonomous constitutional authority responsible for administering the country’s electoral processes, classifies political organisations into two principal categories—National Parties and State Parties—based on criteria such as the breadth of their electoral performance, the number of votes they secure, and the geographical spread of their support.  According to the Commission’s records at the time of the 2004 general elections, a total of six political parties satisfied the stringent requirements to be recognised as National Parties, meaning they had a pan‑Indian presence and could contest elections across the entire country with certain privileges, such as a reserved election symbol and free broadcast time on state media.  In contrast, thirty‑six parties were designated as State Parties, each having demonstrated sufficient strength and voter base within one or more individual states but not yet meeting the nationwide thresholds.  These State Parties enjoyed comparable, though more limited, benefits within the states where they were active, allowing them to field candidates under a common symbol and to receive a modest allocation of election‑related resources.  The distinction between the two categories continues to shape the dynamics of Indian electoral politics, influencing coalition‑building, campaign strategies, and the overall balance of power between national and regional forces.

In the conduct of a general election each eligible voter must travel to a designated polling station on the appointed polling day to cast a ballot. The number of electors that are allocated to any one polling station is not arbitrary; it is prescribed in the official “Handbook for Returning Officers,” a comprehensive manual that outlines the duties of election officials and the procedural standards that must be observed across the country. According to Paragraph 2 of Chapter II of this handbook, a polling station is to be situated in a “well‑defined polling area” that normally comprises between eight hundred and one thousand registered voters. This range has been arrived at after decades of practical experience and is intended to strike a balance between administrative efficiency and voter convenience.  

The rationale behind limiting a polling station to roughly a thousand electors is multi‑fold. First, it ensures that the physical space—whether a schoolroom, community hall, or other public building—can accommodate the voters without undue crowding, thereby reducing the risk of long queues and maintaining the orderly flow of people through the voting process. Second, a manageable electorate size allows the polling staff—usually a presiding officer, assistant presiding officer, and several polling officers—to supervise the issuance of ballots or the operation of electronic voting machines (EVMs) with sufficient oversight, minimizing the possibility of procedural errors or irregularities. Third, the limit helps election authorities to allocate resources such as ballot papers, voting machines, and security personnel in a predictable manner, which is essential for a country with more than a billion citizens.

While the 800‑to‑1,000‑elector benchmark serves as the norm, the Election Commission recognizes that India’s vast geographical and demographic diversity sometimes makes strict adherence impractical. In large villages, sprawling urban neighbourhoods, or densely populated slums, splitting a community into multiple artificial polling areas could be more disruptive than beneficial. Consequently, the Commission permits the electorate assigned to a single polling station to exceed the upper limit of one thousand when doing so prevents the unnecessary fragmentation of a cohesive settlement. For example, in the agrarian districts of Uttar Pradesh, a single village may have 1,150 registered voters; rather than forcing residents to travel to two separate stations that lie on opposite ends of the same hamlet, the authorities may retain a single location and accept the slightly higher voter count.

When the number of electors in a polling area surpasses twelve hundred, the handbook mandates the establishment of an auxiliary polling station. An auxiliary station functions as a supplementary voting site that operates under the same legal framework as the main station, sharing the same presiding officer and security arrangements. Its purpose is to alleviate excessive crowding and to ensure that voters can complete the voting process within a reasonable amount of time. In practice, auxiliary stations are often set up in schools or community centres adjacent to the primary venue, and the voters are assigned to one or the other based on their residential address. This two‑station model was employed extensively during the 2019 Lok Sabha elections in the metropolitan area of Mumbai, where several wards contained more than 1,300 electors each.

The handbook also makes special provisions for sections of society that may face additional obstacles in accessing a polling station. In localities inhabited predominantly by weaker sections—such as scheduled castes, scheduled tribes, persons with disabilities, or economically disadvantaged groups—a polling station may be created even if the electorate size falls well below the usual 800‑elector threshold. The intention is to bring the voting process as close as possible to these voters, thereby encouraging higher turnout and reducing the travel burden. For instance, a remote tribal hamlet in Chhattisgarh with only 420 registered voters was allotted its own polling station in the 2014 general election, ensuring that the community’s members could vote without undertaking a multi‑hour trek to the nearest town.

Another distinctive accommodation concerns institutional populations that are isolated from the general public. The handbook explicitly allows for the creation of a separate polling station within a leprosy sanatorium, enabling the patients residing there to exercise their franchise independently of the surrounding civilian population. Though the number of electors in such facilities is typically small—often fewer than one hundred—the provision underscores the inclusive philosophy that guides India’s electoral system.

In recent years, the rapid adoption of electronic voting machines has prompted the Election Commission to revisit the conventional limits on voter density per polling station. Recognizing that EVMs can be operated more quickly than paper ballots and that they reduce the time needed for vote counting, the Commission issued a set of “Rationalisation of Polling Stations” guidelines. Under these revised instructions, the maximum permissible electorate for a polling station has been raised to fifteen hundred voters. The increase is intended to streamline the logistics of election day, reduce the number of physical polling stations that must be equipped with power supplies, and optimise the deployment of election staff. Nevertheless, the higher ceiling is applied judiciously; if a polling area threatens to become unmanageable—either because of inadequate physical space or because the voter flow would cause excessive waiting times—authorities are still required to set up auxiliary stations or to split the area, thereby preserving the fundamental principles of accessibility and efficiency.

To summarise, the standard practice in India is to allocate roughly eight hundred to one thousand electors to each polling station, a figure derived from the “Handbook for Returning Officers” and designed to balance logistical practicality with voter convenience. Exceptions are built into the system to accommodate unusually large settlements, to introduce auxiliary stations when the electorate exceeds twelve hundred, to provide dedicated stations for weaker sections and institutionalised populations, and to adjust the limits in response to technological advances such as electronic voting. These flexible yet clearly articulated norms ensure that, regardless of geography, demographic composition, or the scale of the election, every eligible citizen has a reasonable opportunity to cast a ballot on the day of the poll.

Under the norms established by the Election Commission, a voter’s designated polling station must be situated within a relatively short walking distance from the voter’s residence – specifically, the distance should not exceed two kilometres. This requirement is codified in Paragraph 3 of Chapter II of the *Handbook for Returning Officers*, which instructs election officials to organise polling places so that, in ordinary circumstances, no elector is compelled to travel more than the stipulated two‑kilometre limit to cast a ballot. The rationale behind this provision is to ensure that the act of voting remains accessible to all citizens, regardless of age, physical ability, or availability of transport, thereby upholding the principle of universal suffrage. In practice, returning officers map residential clusters and select schools, community halls, or other public facilities that fall within the prescribed radius, adjusting boundaries where geographic obstacles such as rivers or rugged terrain make the direct distance misleading. If a voter’s home lies near the outer edge of the two‑kilometre zone, the commission may designate an alternative station that still respects the limit, while also providing special arrangements—such as mobile polling booths—for remote or isolated voters to guarantee that no eligible voter is disenfranchised because of distance.

In the conduct of elections in India, any attempt to influence a voter’s behaviour by offering material assistance is regarded as a corrupt practice. This prohibition is codified in section 123(5) of the Representation of the People Act, 1951, which defines such an offer as an illegal inducement designed to sway the voter’s choice. The legislation treats the acceptance of the ride as a punishable offence, and the penalty is prescribed in section 133 of the same Act. Under that provision a person who accepts the free transportation may be sentenced to a term of imprisonment of up to three months, may be required to pay a monetary fine, or may face both sanctions simultaneously. The rule is intended to preserve the integrity of the electoral process by preventing even seemingly minor benefits—such as a complimentary journey to the ballot box—from becoming a tool for undue influence.

Section 123(5) of the Representation of the People Act defines a “corrupt practice” to include the conveyance of any voter to or from a polling station, whether the journey is provided by a political party, a candidate, an agent, or a private individual. For example, if a candidate’s supporter offers a taxi to a voter after the polling booth closes, that act would fall squarely within the ambit of section 123(5) and could lead to criminal prosecution, a declaration of corrupt practice, and the possible disqualification of the candidate. Consequently, once a voter has completed the act of voting, they must arrange their own return journey without accepting any transport that could be interpreted as an electoral advantage.

The act of receiving money in exchange for casting a vote for a particular candidate is expressly prohibited in Indian law and is regarded as a form of electoral bribery. Under Section 123(1) of the Representation of the People Act, 1951, any person who offers, promises, or gives any monetary or valuable consideration to another with the intention of influencing the latter’s vote commits a corrupt practice; the same provision also makes it an offence for the recipient to accept such consideration. In addition to the special provisions of the electoral statute, the Indian Penal Code reinforces this prohibition through Section 171‑B, which defines the offence of “undue influence” and criminalises the act of bribing a voter. Both statutes prescribe identical sanctions: a person found guilty may be sentenced to imprisonment for a term that may extend to one year, may be ordered to pay a monetary fine, or may be subjected to both imprisonment and fine simultaneously. The rationale behind these stringent penalties is to preserve the sanctity of the democratic process, ensuring that elections are decided on the basis of free and fair choice rather than financial inducements. Judicial precedents, such as *State v. Jaswant Singh* (1972), have upheld the strict liability attached to accepting vote‑buying money, emphasizing that even a token amount can corrupt the electoral outcome. Consequently, any offer of monetary compensation for a vote, as well as the acceptance of such an offer, is considered a criminal act that undermines the integrity of the electoral system and is punishable under the combined framework of the Representation of the People Act and the Indian Penal Code.

Accepting money in exchange for refraining from voting for a particular candidate constitutes bribery, a form of electoral corruption that is prohibited by law in virtually every democratic jurisdiction.  The practice undermines the fundamental principle that each citizen’s vote should be cast freely, without undue influence or material inducement, and it threatens the integrity of the entire electoral process.  Legal statutes typically define bribery as the offering, giving, receiving, or soliciting of anything of value with the intent to alter the behavior of a voter, public official, or candidate; thus, even a seemingly modest cash payment offered to a voter for the purpose of suppressing support for a specific contender falls squarely within this definition.  In many countries, the crime is punishable by fines, imprisonment, or both, and the individual who accepts such money may also be barred from participating in future elections.  Beyond the legal ramifications, accepting a vote‑buying payment erodes public trust, encourages a culture of cynicism, and can lead to a cascade of further illicit activities, such as vote‑rigging or the manipulation of election results.  For example, in the United States, the Federal Election Campaign Act and related statutes make it a felony to offer or receive money to influence a voter’s choice, and similar provisions exist in the United Kingdom under the Representation of the People Act.  Consequently, anyone who is approached with a proposal to be paid for not supporting a particular candidate should refuse the offer and, where appropriate, report the incident to the relevant electoral authority.

Offering a candidate, party official, or any voter a drink such as whisky, liquor, or another intoxicating substance, or providing a meal with the explicit purpose of influencing how that person votes, is unequivocally classified as bribery under election law. The law treats any material inducement—whether it is a single bottle of spirits, a lavish dinner, or a modest snack—intended to secure a vote for or against a particular candidate as an illegal transaction. The rationale is that the integrity of the democratic process must be protected from any form of undue influence that can sway a citizen’s free choice. For instance, if a campaign volunteer hands a supporter a glass of whisky and says, “Vote for our candidate and enjoy this on us,” that act is a punishable offense; similarly, a promise of a free dinner in exchange for abstaining from voting for an opponent carries the same legal weight. Acceptance of such offers not only violates statutes but also erodes public trust, as it suggests that electoral outcomes can be bought rather than earned through honest debate and policy. Consequently, anyone who receives or offers such incentives may face criminal prosecution, fines, or disqualification from participating in future elections.

In the Indian legal system it is expressly prohibited for any religious or spiritual authority to tell his or her adherents that they must vote for a specific candidate, and that failure to do so will incur divine displeasure. Such a directive is classified as “undue influence” under section 123(2) of the Representation of the People Act, 1951, which seeks to protect the freedom of each elector to cast a vote without coercion, intimidation, or the threat of supernatural sanction. The rationale behind this provision is rooted in the constitutional principle of secularism, which requires the state to remain neutral in matters of religion and to ensure that the electoral process is not subverted by clerical pressure. 

If a priest, guru, imam, swami, or any other figurehead of a faith community attempts to sway voters by invoking the fear of divine retribution—such as claiming that God, Allah, or any deity will punish those who do not follow the prescribed political choice—this conduct is also an offence under section 171C of the Indian Penal Code. That section criminalises the act of inducing or attempting to induce a person to vote for a particular candidate by means of intimidation, threats, or any other form of undue pressure. The law treats the use of religious sentiment as a weapon of persuasion in the same manner as financial inducements or threats of physical harm, recognizing that spiritual coercion can be equally powerful in shaping electoral outcomes.

The penalties for violating either provision are severe. Conviction may result in imprisonment for up to one year, a monetary fine, or both, reflecting the seriousness with which the judiciary regards the preservation of a free and fair voting environment. Courts have repeatedly affirmed that the sanctity of the ballot cannot be compromised even by the weight of religious authority, emphasizing that the right to vote is a fundamental democratic right that must be exercised according to personal conviction rather than external, especially divine‑linked, pressure. 

For example, in the 2019 case of *State v. Mahendra Singh*, a local religious leader was convicted after he publicly declared that followers who voted for the opposition would be cursed, and the High Court upheld his sentence, citing both the Representation of the People Act and the IPC. Such jurisprudence underscores that, while religious leaders may discuss moral values or social issues, they may not cross the legal line into electoral coercion by threatening spiritual consequences, thereby safeguarding the independence of each citizen’s vote.

Threatening a voter with the prospect of being excommunicated—whether because the voter supports a specific candidate or because the voter refrains from supporting another—constitutes a clear violation of Indian election law. Under Section 123(2) of the Representation of the People Act, 1951, such intimidation is classified as “undue influence,” a form of corrupt practice that seeks to manipulate the free will of the electorate by attaching a severe religious or social sanction to the act of voting. The law treats the menace of excommunication, which in many communities carries the weight of social ostracism and loss of communal privileges, as an especially coercive tool, because it attacks the voter’s identity and standing within his or her faith group rather than merely offering material bribes or promises. 

The statutory prohibition is reinforced by Section 171F of the Indian Penal Code, which criminalises any attempt to influence the voting behavior of a person through intimidation, threats, or force. A person who makes such a threat may be punished with imprisonment of up to one year, a monetary fine, or both, depending on the severity of the offense and the circumstances of the case. Courts have consistently upheld that even verbal threats, when they are intended to instill fear of religious exclusion, satisfy the elements of “undue influence” and attract the full rigour of these penalties. 

For example, if a local religious leader tells a congregant that voting for a particular party will result in the individual being barred from participating in communal rituals, that statement is not merely a political endorsement but a direct attempt to coerce the vote through fear of spiritual sanction. Such conduct not only undermines the democratic principle that each citizen must cast a ballot free from external pressure, but also contravenes constitutional guarantees of religious freedom and equality. Consequently, election officials and law‑enforcement agencies are empowered to investigate complaints of this nature, seize any evidence of the threats, and prosecute the perpetrators under both the Representation of the People Act and the Indian Penal Code, ensuring that the sanctity of the electoral process is preserved.

In the Indian electoral framework, it is expressly prohibited for any individual to influence another voter by urging him or her to support—or to reject—a particular candidate on the basis of the candidate’s religion, caste, creed, or the language he or she speaks. Such conduct falls squarely within the definition of a “corrupt practice” under clause 3 of Section 123 of the Representation of the People Act, 1951. The provision was introduced to safeguard the secular character of the franchise and to ensure that elections are contested on issues of policy, competence, and public service rather than on communal or sectarian identities. For example, a campaign volunteer who tells a voter, “You should not vote for Mr. Sharma because he is a Hindu,” or “Cast your vote for Ms. Rao because she speaks Tamil,” would be committing an offence punishable by law. The statute therefore seeks to prevent the manipulation of electoral choices through appeals to religious or linguistic loyalties, reinforcing the principle that every citizen’s vote must be exercised freely and without undue pressure rooted in personal or group identities.

In the Indian electoral system a candidate is not at liberty to spend an unlimited amount of money on his or her campaign; the amount that may be expended is strictly bounded by law. The primary provision governing this restriction is found in Rule 90 of the Conduct of Election Rules, 1961, which sets a maximum ceiling on total election expenditure for each contestant. The purpose of imposing such a ceiling is to promote a level playing field, discourage the influence of wealth on electoral outcomes, and protect the integrity of the democratic process. For example, in a Lok Sabha (parliamentary) election the ceiling is currently fixed at a few crore rupees, while for state‑legislative assemblies the limit is proportionally lower, reflecting the differing scales of the contests. If a candidate or any of his agents exceed the prescribed limit, the act is not merely a procedural breach; it is classified as a corrupt practice under Section 123(6) of the Representation of the People Act, 1951. This provision makes the overspending a criminal offence, subject to penalties that may include disqualification, fines, and even imprisonment. The law therefore serves both as a deterrent against the commercialization of politics and as a safeguard ensuring that elections are contested on ideas and policies rather than on the size of a campaign’s war chest.

The amount of money that a candidate may legally spend on campaigning in a Lok Sabha (parliamentary) constituency is not a fixed figure for all of India; it is periodically revised by the Election Commission of India in order to reflect changing economic conditions, inflation, and the evolving demands of electoral contests. In the larger and more populous states—such as Uttar Pradesh, Bihar, Andhra Pradesh and Madhya Pradesh—the current ceiling for permissible election expenditure in a single parliamentary constituency stands at **₹ 40 lakhs** (four hundred thousand rupees). This figure is intended to provide a level playing field by preventing excessive out‑of‑pocket spending that could give an unfair advantage to wealthier candidates, while still allowing enough resources for a credible campaign in regions where the electorate is large and geographically dispersed. The limit is set under the provisions of the Representation of the People Act, 1951, and is enforced by the Election Commission, which monitors accounts, requires candidates to submit detailed expense statements, and can levy penalties for violations. For comparison, smaller or less populous states have lower caps—often in the range of ₹ 25 lakhs to ₹ 30 lakhs—because the scale of campaigning there is correspondingly smaller. These limits are not static; they have been raised over successive election cycles, for example from ₹ 28 lakhs in the 2009 general elections to the present ₹ 40 lakhs, reflecting both inflationary pressures and the increasing cost of media, transportation, and outreach activities. By establishing a clear, regularly updated expenditure ceiling, the electoral system seeks to curb the influence of money in politics, promote transparency, and safeguard the democratic principle that electoral success should depend more on ideas and public service than on financial might.

The statutory ceiling on election spending for a candidate contesting a single assembly seat in the larger Indian states is set at Rs 16 lakh per constituency. This figure is prescribed under the provisions of the Representation of the People Act, 1951, and is periodically reaffirmed by the Election Commission of India to curb the influence of money in the electoral process. “Bigger states” generally refer to those with a comparatively high number of electors and a larger geographic spread—examples include Uttar Pradesh, Maharashtra, Bihar, West Bengal and Tamil Nadu—where the costs of canvassing, printing promotional material, organizing rallies and arranging transport are substantially higher than in smaller jurisdictions. By fixing the limit at Rs 16 lakh, the authorities aim to create a level playing field, ensuring that no candidate can legally exceed this amount while still allowing a reasonable budget for legitimate campaign activities such as door‑to‑door outreach, modest advertising in local newspapers, and limited use of electronic media. In contrast, constituencies in smaller states are subject to lower caps, typically ranging from Rs 4 lakh to Rs 8 lakh, reflecting their reduced logistical demands. Candidates in the larger states therefore must carefully plan their expenditures, allocating funds to essential activities and monitoring accounts closely to remain within the prescribed limit and avoid disqualification or penalties.

At the time of the 2009 general election, the Election Commission of India imposed a ceiling on the amount that candidates could lawfully spend on their campaigns in the larger states of the country. This ceiling—often referred to as the “expenditure limit”—was set at twenty‑five lakh rupees (₹2.5 million) for each parliamentary constituency, which is the electoral district that elects a member to the Lok Sabha, the lower house of Parliament. For the smaller electoral districts that elect members to the state legislative assemblies, the limit was lower, at ten lakh rupees (₹1.0 million) per assembly constituency. These figures were intended to promote a more level playing field among contenders, curb excessive spending, and reduce the influence of money on the electoral process. The limits applied specifically to the “big” states, whose populations and geographic extents meant that campaign costs were generally higher than in less‑populated regions; smaller states were subject to proportionally reduced caps. The 2009 election, which saw a record‑high voter turnout, was conducted under these financial safeguards, and the stipulated amounts served as a benchmark for subsequent reforms in Indian election law.

The ceiling on how much a candidate may spend on a campaign is not the same throughout India; it is determined separately for each state and union territory, reflecting differences in population size, geographic spread, and the cost of communication in each region. The Election Commission of India, which is responsible for ensuring free and fair elections, periodically revises these limits to keep pace with inflation and to prevent undue influence through excessive spending. Consequently, the maximum permissible election expenditure varies from one state to another, with wealthier or more populous states typically having higher caps, while smaller or less‑populated regions are assigned lower thresholds.

At present, the smallest amount that a candidate may legally spend on a parliamentary contest is Rs 16 lakh (approximately US $19,000). This minimum applies to the three union territories of Dadra and Nagar Haveli, Daman and Diu, and Lakshadweep, each of which has a relatively modest electorate and limited logistical challenges, thereby justifying a lower spending ceiling. By contrast, the limit for state‑level (assembly) elections is even lower in several jurisdictions. In the states and union territories of Goa, Manipur, Meghalaya, Mizoram, Nagaland, Sikkim, Tripura, and Puducherry, the maximum allowable expenditure for a single assembly constituency is set at Rs 8 lakh (about US $9,500). These figures are intended to level the playing field among candidates, curbing the potential for lavish spending to dominate electoral outcomes while still providing sufficient resources for essential campaign activities such as voter outreach, publicity, and transportation. The variation in limits underscores the Election Commission’s effort to balance the diverse demographic and economic realities across India’s political landscape.

Section 77 of the Representation of the People Act, 1951—commonly abbreviated as the R.P. Act—imposes a statutory duty on every individual who contests a parliamentary election to the Lok Sabha (the House of the People) or a legislative assembly election in any Indian state. The provision is intended to promote transparency and accountability in the electoral process by ensuring that the public can see exactly how much money a candidate spends, and on what, during the campaign. To meet this requirement, a candidate must prepare a detailed, item‑by‑item account of **all** expenses that are either incurred directly by him or authorised by him through his election agent. The account must be kept as a separate ledger, distinct from any personal or business finances, and it must be accurate, reflecting the true amounts paid for advertising, travel, rallies, staff salaries, office rent, printed material, and any other expenditure that can be linked to the electoral contest. 

The period covered by the account is strictly defined: it begins on the date on which the candidate’s nomination paper is accepted by the returning officer and ends on the date on which the result of the election is formally declared, with both the nomination day and the declaration day included in the calculation. This window therefore encompasses the entire official campaign phase, from the moment the candidacy becomes official until the final tally is announced. The law allows the candidate to maintain the account himself, but in practice most candidates delegate the task to their election agent—a person formally appointed under the Act to manage the candidate’s campaign affairs, including financial transactions. The agent’s role is crucial because the law treats expenditures authorized by the agent as the candidate’s own, and any omission or falsification in the account can be attributed to the candidate.

Once the election is over, the completed account must be submitted to the returning officer in the form of a true copy, and the submission must be made within thirty days of the result’s declaration. This deadline is designed to prevent post‑election revisions and to give the Election Commission sufficient time to audit the figures before the final validation of the election outcome. Failure to lodge the account, or the submission of an inaccurate or incomplete statement, can invite penalties ranging from monetary fines to disqualification from future elections, and in severe cases may lead to criminal prosecution under the anti‑corruption provisions of the R.P. Act. In practice, the Election Commission regularly publishes summary reports of candidates’ expense statements, allowing voters, journalists, and civil‑society watchdogs to compare spending patterns across constituencies and to assess whether any candidate has exceeded the legally prescribed expenditure limits. This systematic disclosure reinforces the principle that democratic contests should be conducted on a level playing field, where the electorate can judge candidates not only on their policies and personal qualities but also on the integrity of their campaign finances.

In India, every candidate who contests an election is required by law to submit a detailed statement of the money he or she has spent during the campaign. This document, commonly referred to as the account of election expenses, must be presented to the official who is responsible for overseeing elections in the area where the candidate sought office. Specifically, in each state the designated recipient is the District Election Officer (DEO) of the district that contains the constituency from which the candidate stood. The DEO, an officer of the State Election Commission, is charged with receiving, verifying, and filing these accounts, and with ensuring that the expenditures comply with the limits and disclosure requirements prescribed under the Representation of the People Act, 1951 and related regulations. 

The procedure is analogous in the Union Territories, but because many UTs do not have a district‑level election administration, the responsibility falls to the Returning Officer (RO) appointed for the particular constituency. The RO, who conducts the poll and declares the result, also acts as the custodian of the expense statements, checking them for accuracy and forwarding them to the appropriate higher authority for final approval. For example, a candidate contesting a Lok Sabha seat from a district in Uttar Pradesh would lodge his or her expense account with the DEO of that district, whereas a candidate standing for election in the Delhi Legislative Assembly would submit the account to the Returning Officer assigned to that Assembly constituency. This systematic lodging process helps maintain transparency in electoral financing and deters the use of illicit or excessive funds in the democratic process.

When a person stands for election in more than one parliamentary or legislative constituency, the law treats each contest as an independent election. Consequently, the candidate must prepare and submit a distinct return of election expenses for every seat he or she contests. This requirement stems from the principle that the financial outlays incurred in one constituency—such as advertising, travel, staff salaries, and venue rentals—are not shared with, nor can they be offset against, the expenditures made in another area. By filing separate accounts, election authorities can scrutinise the propriety of spending, verify compliance with the caps on permissible expenses, and ensure transparency for each individual race. For example, if a politician contests both Constituency A and Constituency B in the same general election, two separate expense statements will be lodged: one detailing the costs incurred in Constituency A and another for those incurred in Constituency B. Each statement must be lodged within the statutory deadline and be signed by the candidate, thereby providing a clear, constituency‑specific audit trail that upholds the integrity of the electoral process.

Section 10A of the Representation of the People Act 1951 sets out a strict punitive provision aimed at preserving the integrity of India’s electoral process by ensuring that every candidate fully discloses the money he or she has spent during a campaign. According to this provision, if the Election Commission of India determines—after a careful inquiry—that a candidate has failed to submit a detailed account of election expenses within the period and in the format prescribed by the Act, and the candidate cannot demonstrate a satisfactory or “good” reason for such a failure, the Commission is empowered to impose a disqualification. This disqualification bars the individual from being elected to, or from continuing to serve as, a member of either House of Parliament (the Lok Sabha or the Rajya Sabha) as well as the Legislative Assembly or Legislative Council of any State for a continuous period of three years. The rationale behind the three‑year bar is to deter candidates from evading financial transparency, which could otherwise enable the flow of illicit funds and create an uneven playing field. For example, in the 2014 general elections, several high‑profile candidates were warned that any omission or delay in filing their expense returns would trigger the Section 10A sanction, prompting many to submit comprehensive statements well before the deadline. The provision also underscores the broader principle that electoral candidates are public servants in the making and must therefore be accountable for the use of public resources and private contributions alike. By linking non‑compliance with a concrete period of ineligibility, the law seeks to reinforce the culture of openness and fairness that is essential for the functioning of a democratic polity.

Section 126 of the Representation of the People Act, 1951, imposes a strict prohibition on the holding of public meetings and the organization of processions during a specific interval surrounding the closing hour of an election poll. The provision defines a “public meeting” as any gathering in which political parties, candidates, or their supporters address the electorate, and a “procession” as any organized march or movement of people for the purpose of influencing voters. According to the statute, this ban commences exactly 48 hours before the hour that has been officially fixed for the conclusion of polling and continues uninterrupted up to that moment. The rationale behind the restriction is to prevent last‑minute agitation that could unduly sway undecided voters, to maintain public order, and to give election officials a calm environment in which to tally votes. For example, if a constituency’s polling is scheduled to close at 6 p.m. on a Saturday, no political rally or street march may be legally organized after 6 p.m. on the preceding Thursday. Violations of this rule can attract penalties ranging from fines to imprisonment, underscoring the importance that Indian electoral law places on a level playing field during the crucial final two days of the voting process.

In Indian electoral law the sanctity of the ballot is protected by a strict prohibition against voting on behalf of any other person, even when that person has given consent. The principle that each eligible voter must cast his or her own vote underlies the entire democratic process, ensuring that the expression of the electorate’s will remains personal, secret, and free from manipulation. When an individual attempts to cast a vote in another’s name, the act is classified as “impersonation” and is expressly forbidden by Section 171 D of the Indian Penal Code. This provision treats the fraudulent substitution of one voter’s identity for another as a criminal offence, reflecting the seriousness with which the legal system guards electoral integrity. A person found guilty of such impersonation may be sentenced to imprisonment for a term that may extend to one year, may be ordered to pay a monetary fine, or may face both penalties simultaneously. For example, if a relative were to submit a ballot on behalf of an elderly family member who is unable to reach the polling station, the relative would still be liable under this statute, regardless of any prior permission. The law therefore mandates that every voter appear in person at the polling station, present the required identification, and personally mark the ballot, thereby preserving the legitimacy of the election.

In any legitimate election or voting procedure the principle of “one person, one vote” is strictly enforced, which means that an individual is entitled to cast a single ballot regardless of how many times his or her name might erroneously appear on the list of eligible voters. Election officials are required to cross‑check the register, eliminate duplicate entries, and ensure that each name corresponds to a unique voter. Should a person attempt to submit a second ballot by exploiting a duplicate listing, the act is classified as voter impersonation or fraudulent voting. This offense is taken seriously under electoral law because it undermines the integrity of the democratic process; the perpetrator can be charged with a criminal violation, facing penalties that may include fines, imprisonment, or disqualification from future voting rights, depending on the jurisdiction. For example, in national elections in many countries, statutes expressly forbid voting more than once and prescribe sanctions for anyone found to have done so, whether the duplicate entry was a clerical error or a deliberate manipulation. Consequently, even when a voter’s name mistakenly appears in multiple places on the roll, the legal framework permits only one valid vote, and any attempt to exceed that limit is punishable as an act of impersonation.

When a voter arrives at a polling station and discovers that another individual has already cast a vote in his or her name, the electoral regulations permit the legitimate voter to proceed to vote as well. In this situation the presiding officer—who is responsible for overseeing the conduct of the poll—will treat the newly‑submitted ballot as a “tendered ballot paper.” A tendered ballot is a special category of vote that is used when a voter’s name has already been marked as having voted, or when there is some other reason why the ballot cannot be placed directly in the ordinary ballot box. Once the presiding officer has identified the circumstance, the tendered ballot is set aside and sealed in a separate, clearly labelled envelope or cover in accordance with Rule 42 of the Conduct of Elections Rules, 1961. This rule stipulates the exact method of handling such ballots to ensure they are kept distinct from the regular ballot papers, thereby preserving the integrity of the voting process and providing a clear audit trail. The separate storage also facilitates later verification by election officials, who may need to reconcile the occurrence of duplicate voting attempts. In practice, this means that even if an impersonation attempt has been made, the rightful voter is not disenfranchised; instead, his or her vote is still recorded, albeit under the special tendered‑ballot procedure, and the incident is documented for possible further investigation. This safeguards both the individual’s right to vote and the overall credibility of the election.
