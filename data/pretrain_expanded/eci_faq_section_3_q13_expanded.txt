The formal commissioning of electronic voting machines (EVMs) in Indian elections is not carried out by the engineers of Bharat Electronics Limited (BEL) or Electronics Corporation of India Limited (ECIL); instead, this critical step is performed by election officials who have been specifically deputed by the District Election Officer (DEO) or the Returning Officer (RO). These officials are responsible for verifying that each machine is correctly configured, sealed and ready for use, and they must conduct the commissioning in the open presence of the candidates contesting the poll or of the candidates’ duly authorized representatives, thereby ensuring transparency and confidence in the electoral process. While the engineers from BEL and ECIL do not commission the machines, they play a supportive role: typically two such engineers are assigned to each assembly constituency or parliamentary segment to assist the election officials with technical tasks such as loading the correct party symbols onto the Voter‑Verified Paper Audit Trail (VVPAT) units that accompany each EVM. Their assistance is limited to these operational details, and they remain on standby to address any technical glitches that might arise during the symbol‑loading phase. For example, in a recent state‑level election, a team of two BEL engineers was stationed at each of the 200 polling stations within a constituency to help verify that the VVPAT slips printed the correct symbols for the four contesting parties, while the DEO’s staff completed the official commissioning checklist and witnessed the process alongside the candidates. This division of responsibilities safeguards the integrity of the voting equipment while also leveraging the technical expertise of the manufacturers in a strictly supervisory capacity.

The Election Commission of India (ECI) does not operate an open‑ended rental scheme for its electronic voting machines (EVMs); instead, it makes these devices available on a loan basis strictly to the State Election Commissions (SECs), the constitutional bodies that are responsible for administering elections to municipal corporations, panchayats and other local‑self‑government institutions. When a state government or a local authority wishes to hold a local‑body poll, the relevant SEC submits a formal request to the ECI, and the central agency evaluates the application against a set of statutory and practical criteria before approving any transfer of equipment.

First, the legal framework governing the particular local‑body election must expressly provide for the use of EVMs. Most Indian states have incorporated electronic voting provisions into their municipal and panchayat election statutes, but a few jurisdictions still rely on paper ballots for certain categories of elections. In those cases the ECI would decline the loan request because the law does not mandate or even permit electronic voting, thereby ensuring that the commission’s actions remain fully compliant with the prevailing legislative authority.

Second, the machines that may be loaned are limited to those models that the ECI has already withdrawn from active use in national or state‑level parliamentary and assembly elections. This restriction serves two purposes. It prevents the deployment of the latest, most sensitive technology outside the tightly controlled environment of general elections, and it allows the commission to make productive use of equipment that has already reached the end of its primary deployment cycle. Nevertheless, even discontinued units may continue to function reliably for up to fifteen years—a period known as their economic life—as long as they have been maintained in accordance with the manufacturer’s specifications and have not suffered any major technical failures.

Third, the availability of such surplus EVMs is a necessary precondition. The ECI maintains an inventory of de‑commissioned machines, and only if sufficient numbers are on hand can it fulfill a loan request without compromising its own operational readiness for upcoming national or state polls. In years when the central election schedule is particularly dense, the pool of loan‑eligible devices may be reduced, and the commission may prioritize requests from states with the most urgent electoral calendars.

Finally, the ECI retains the right to request detailed reports from any SEC that has been allotted loaned EVMs. These reports typically cover the condition of the machines upon receipt, the manner in which they were stored and transported, any maintenance activities performed, and an account of their performance during the polling process. By collecting this information, the ECI can monitor the integrity of the equipment, verify that the loan terms are being observed, and address any technical anomalies that might arise during local elections. In this way, the loan arrangement balances the practical need for resource sharing with the overarching goal of preserving the credibility and security of India’s electoral infrastructure.

The first‑level verification of electronic voting machines (EVMs) and the accompanying Voter‑Verifiable Paper Audit Trail devices (VVPATs) is carried out by a team of specially authorised engineers drawn from the two principal manufacturers of these systems—Bharat Electronics Limited (BEL) and the Electronics Corporation of India Limited (ECIL).  These engineers are deployed to the district headquarters on the day allotted for the pre‑poll checks, where they perform a systematic series of functional and safety tests to confirm that each machine operates in accordance with the technical specifications issued by the Election Commission of India.  The entire procedure is conducted under the direct supervision of the District Election Officer, who is the senior electoral official responsible for overseeing all electoral activities within the district.  To ensure transparency and to allay any doubts about the integrity of the equipment, the inspection is witnessed by representatives of the nationally recognised political parties as well as those of recognised state‑level parties.  Their presence provides a bipartisan verification that the machines have been properly calibrated, that the VVPAT units are correctly linked to the corresponding EVMs, and that any anomalies are promptly reported and rectified before the commencement of polling.  This multilayered oversight mechanism reflects the broader commitment of India’s electoral system to maintain confidence among voters, political stakeholders, and the public at large by subjecting the critical voting infrastructure to rigorous, open, and accountable scrutiny.

Voter‑Verified Paper Audit Trail (VVPAT) devices are an integral component of modern electronic voting systems, providing a tangible record of each ballot cast. Every time a voter completes a vote on the electronic voting machine, the attached VVPAT printer generates a paper slip that displays, in addition to the candidate or party selected, a distinctive identification number that is embedded in the firmware of that particular VVPAT unit. This alphanumeric or numeric code, often printed at the top or bottom of the slip, serves as a fingerprint for the machine that produced it. Because the identifier is unique to each VVPAT terminal, election officials and auditors can trace any individual slip back to its source by consulting the master list of device IDs maintained by the electoral authority. For example, if a slip bears the code “VVPAT‑03‑2024‑00157,” officials can immediately determine that it originated from the third VVPAT unit deployed in the 2024 polling area and locate it in the corresponding log. This capability not only enhances transparency and accountability but also facilitates post‑election recounts, dispute resolution, and forensic analysis, ensuring that every paper record can be reliably linked to the specific voting machine that printed it.

The central unit (CU) that controls electronic voting equipment is powered by a compact rechargeable pack rated at 7.5 volts with a capacity of 2 ampere‑hours (Ah). This modest voltage and energy rating is sufficient to keep the CU operating for the duration of a typical polling session, while the 2 Ah capacity ensures that the unit can tolerate the occasional surge of current that occurs when the display and internal processors are activated. In contrast, the voter‑verified paper audit trail (VVPAT) module, which prints a hard‑copy record of each vote, requires a considerably larger supply; its power pack is specified at 22.5 volts and 4 Ah. The higher voltage provides the necessary drive for the thermal‑print head, and the doubled ampere‑hour rating allows the printer to produce several hundred receipts before the battery must be recharged or replaced. When more than four ballot units (BUs) are deployed in a single voting station, the system is designed to use the same 7.5‑V, 2‑Ah packs that power the CU, thereby simplifying logistics and reducing the number of distinct spare‑battery types that election officials need to stock. For additional clarification on battery interchangeability and maintenance procedures, the General Queries FAQ, question 12, offers a concise summary of the recommended practices.

The paper receipt generated by a Voter‑Verified Paper Audit Trail (VVPAT) system is designed to give the elector a tangible confirmation of the electronic vote that has just been recorded. Each slip carries a concise set of data fields that together identify both the ballot and the chosen candidate. First, a **candidate serial number** appears, which is a unique numeric code assigned to every individual contesting the election and used by election officials to cross‑reference vote counts. Adjacent to this code, the **name of the candidate** is printed in full, ensuring that the voter can verify that the correct individual has been selected. Because many voters recognize political parties more readily by their emblems, the slip also displays the **symbol of the party or candidate**—for example, the “hand” symbol of the Bharatiya Janata Party or the “lotus” of the Indian National Congress—providing an additional visual cue. The **session number** indicates the specific polling session during which the vote was cast, which is useful for audit trails that track voting activity across multiple time slots at a single polling station. Finally, a unique **VVPAT ID** is printed, functioning as a serial identifier for the paper record itself; this code allows auditors to match each slip with the corresponding electronic entry in the voting machine’s memory. All of these elements are visible to the voter through a transparent window before the slip is automatically deposited in a sealed box, thereby giving the voter confidence that the electronic tally accurately reflects their intended choice.

The paper slip produced by a Voter‑Verified Paper Audit Trail (VVPAT) device measures roughly 99 mm in width and 56 mm in height, a dimension that is deliberately chosen to be compact enough to fit comfortably in the small viewing window of the voting machine yet large enough to display the essential information—such as the name of the candidate, the party symbol and the serial number of the vote—clearly to the elector. The slip itself is made of thermal‑sensitive paper, the same type of material commonly used in receipt printers and point‑of‑sale terminals. When the printer’s heating element activates, the paper darkens in the pattern of the printed characters without requiring ink or toner, which makes the device simpler and more reliable under varied field conditions. A notable property of this thermal paper is its ability to retain the printed image for an extended period; under normal storage—dry, cool, and protected from direct sunlight—the imprint remains legible for about five years. This longevity is crucial for post‑election audits, recounts, or legal challenges, as the printed record can be retrieved long after the election day without degradation of the data.

In electronic voting systems the control unit (CU) and the voter‑verified paper‑audit trail (VVPAT) modules each contain a rechargeable power pack that supplies the energy needed for operation during an election. Because these batteries are finite‑life components, the system is designed to track their remaining capacity continuously. The CU interrogates the battery cells at regular intervals and translates the raw voltage and current measurements into a user‑friendly balance‑power indicator that is shown on the unit’s display. This indicator can assume one of five descriptive levels—**High**, **Medium**, **Low**, **Marginal**, and **Change Battery**—and is accompanied by a numeric percentage that quantifies the exact charge remaining. For example, a reading of “Medium – 68 %” tells the operator that the pack still has a comfortable margin of power, whereas “Low – 22 %” warns that the reserve is dwindling and that replacement may soon be required.

When the display reaches the **Change Battery** status, the system has determined that the remaining charge has fallen below a predefined safety threshold (typically around 10 % of the original capacity). At this point the CU also generates an audible alert and logs the event for post‑election auditing. The protocol calls for the operator to retrieve a fresh power pack from the **reserve inventory** that is stored with the sector officers responsible for the polling station. The sector officer’s reserve packs are pre‑tested and labelled to ensure compatibility, and the swap is performed by disconnecting the depleted pack and inserting the new one, after which the CU automatically recalibrates and resumes normal monitoring. This systematic approach guarantees that the CU and VVPAT remain powered throughout the voting period, prevents unexpected shutdowns, and maintains the integrity of the electoral process.

The Election Commission of India (ECI) exercises comprehensive authority over every stage of the electronic voting process, from the manufacture and distribution of electronic voting machines (EVMs) to the conduct of polling and the declaration of results. As a constitutionally mandated independent body, the Commission is responsible for formulating the detailed procedural guidelines that govern the use of EVMs, ensuring that the devices are uniformly calibrated, securely stored, and transported to each polling station under strict protocols. During an election, all officials who manage the voting stations—such as returning officers, presiding officers, and poll workers—are appointed by the Commission and operate exclusively under its direction, supervision, and control. This hierarchical arrangement means that the ECI dictates the timing of the election, the layout of the voting booths, the training of personnel, and the verification of vote counts. For example, in the 2019 general election, the Commission coordinated the deployment of over one million EVMs across the country, with each machine monitored by officials who reported directly to the Commission’s central command. In this way, the ECI maintains full oversight of the entire voting process, guaranteeing that the integrity of the election is upheld from the moment a voter presses a button on an EVM until the final tally is officially announced.

Electronic Voting Machines (EVMs) are sophisticated electronic devices that, like all hardware that relies on circuitry, power supplies, and software, can experience occasional technical faults. Such faults may arise from a variety of sources, including component wear‑out, power surges, software glitches, or environmental factors such as extreme temperature or humidity. When an EVM is found to be non‑functional during routine checks or after a polling day, it is withdrawn from service and dispatched to a dedicated repair facility where trained engineers diagnose the problem, replace defective parts, and verify that the machine’s firmware and security modules are fully restored before it is returned to the inventory of certified voting equipment.

It is important to distinguish ordinary mechanical or electronic failure from the deliberate manipulation of an EVM’s output, a practice commonly referred to as “tampering.” The design architecture of modern EVMs incorporates multiple layers of protection specifically intended to prevent any alteration of vote totals in favor of a particular candidate or party. For instance, the machines are equipped with sealed hardware enclosures, cryptographic hash functions that bind each vote to a tamper‑evident log, and internal watchdog circuits that detect and abort any unauthorized code execution. In addition, the software that runs on the devices is stored in read‑only memory that cannot be rewritten without a secure, factory‑issued key, and any attempt to open the casing triggers an alarm and records the event in an audit trail.

These safeguards are reinforced by procedural controls: before an election, every EVM undergoes a thorough conformity test in the presence of representatives from political parties, election officials, and independent observers; after voting, the machines are sealed and transported under strict chain‑of‑custody protocols; and finally, the vote counts generated by the devices are cross‑checked with paper‑based verification slips or other independent records where such systems are employed. Because of this combination of robust hardware design, cryptographic security, and rigorous operational procedures, the likelihood of an EVM “malfunctioning” in a way that could bias an election outcome is effectively eliminated, even though ordinary technical failures may still occur and are dealt with through standard maintenance and repair channels.

When a voter reaches the electronic voting machine (EVM) in a polling station, the act of confirming that the device has correctly captured the choice is built into the design of the ballot unit (BU) and its accompanying Voter‑Verifiable Paper Audit Trail (VVPAT). After the voter has identified the candidate of preference by locating the printed serial number, name and party symbol on the BU, he or she simply depresses the blue‑coloured button that lies directly beneath that candidate’s listing. This tactile action is the only input required to register the vote electronically. Immediately thereafter a small red LED positioned next to the same candidate’s button illuminates, giving the voter a visual cue that the machine has registered the press. At the same moment the VVPAT mechanism is triggered: a narrow paper slip is fed out of the printer, on which the same serial number, name and symbol appear in clear, legible type. The slip remains exposed through a transparent window for roughly seven seconds, allowing the voter to glance at it and verify that the printed information matches the intended choice. After this brief interval the paper is automatically cut and dropped into a sealed, tamper‑evident box attached to the VVPAT unit, where it will be retained for any future audit or recount. Concurrently, a short, audible beep sounds, reinforcing to the voter that the process has completed successfully. This combination of visual, tactile and auditory feedback—LED illumination, printed slip, and beep—provides a layered assurance that the electronic record and the physical paper record are in agreement, thereby enhancing confidence in the integrity of the voting system.
