The person entrusted with the official counting of ballots and the formal proclamation of an election’s outcome in India is the Returning Officer, a role defined and governed by the Representation of People Act, 1951. Under Section 64 of the Act, the Returning Officer of each parliamentary or assembly constituency is required to supervise, and if necessary to direct, the entire counting process. This responsibility includes arranging the venue, ensuring the presence of authorized staff such as counting officers and polling officers, securing the ballot boxes, and guaranteeing that the counting is conducted in a transparent and orderly manner in accordance with the prescribed procedures. Once the tally of votes for all candidates has been completed and any objections or disputes have been resolved, the Returning Officer proceeds to the next statutory step outlined in Section 66 of the same legislation. At this stage, the Returning Officer publicly announces the result, indicating which candidate has secured the highest number of valid votes and is therefore declared elected. The declaration is made in the presence of witnesses and is typically recorded in an official return that becomes part of the public record. In practice, the Returning Officer is often a senior civil servant—such as a District Magistrate or a senior election official—appointed by the Election Commission of India for the duration of the electoral process in the constituency. Their impartiality and adherence to the procedural safeguards embedded in the Act are crucial for maintaining public confidence in the democratic process, as illustrated by the meticulous counting operations observed in large‑scale elections such as the 2019 Lok Sabha polls, where thousands of polling stations across the nation were overseen by Returning Officers who ensured that every vote was accurately counted and the final results were declared in accordance with the law.

The responsibility for formally constituting the new Lok Sabha after a general election rests with the Election Commission of India, not with the President. This duty is laid down in Section 73 of the Representation of the People Act, 1951, which stipulates that once the counting of votes is completed and the results for every parliamentary constituency have been declared, the Commission must issue an official notification naming each successful candidate. The notification is published in the Gazette of India, the authoritative record of government decisions, and it marks the moment at which the elected representatives are legally recognised as members of the 17th (or whichever) Lok Sabha. 

The Gazette notification serves several practical purposes. First, it provides a definitive, publicly accessible list of members that can be referred to by the Speaker, parliamentary committees, and the courts. Second, it triggers the subsequent constitutional steps: the President, acting on the advice of the Council of Ministers, issues a proclamation summoning the newly constituted Lok Sabha to meet for its first session, usually within a few weeks of the Gazette notification. 

Historically, this process has unfolded in a uniform manner. For example, after the 2019 general election, the Election Commission announced the final results on 23 May and promptly released the Gazette notification the following day, listing the 543 elected members. The President then convened the first meeting of the 17th Lok Sabha on 17 June, when the Speaker was elected and the parliamentary business officially commenced. Thus, while the President plays a ceremonial role in calling the first sitting, it is the Election Commission that legally creates the new Lok Sabha by certifying the election outcome through its Gazette notification.
