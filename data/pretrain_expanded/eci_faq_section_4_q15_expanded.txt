In India the requirement to register with the Election Commission is limited to organisations that intend to function as political parties, not to every association that may exist in civil society. The Election Commission of India (ECI) is the constitutional authority charged with supervising the conduct of elections and, under the Representation of the People Act, 1951, it maintains a register of political parties that wish to contest elections at the national, state or local level. Only those groups that formally describe themselves as a “political party” and that seek the legal advantages conferred by Part IV‑A of the Act—such as the right to a common election symbol, access to party‑wise vote shares, and eligibility for certain forms of state funding—are obligated to apply for registration. By contrast, a wide variety of other collectives—non‑governmental organisations, trade unions, professional societies, cultural clubs, charitable trusts, resident welfare associations, and informal interest groups—may operate freely without any registration with the ECI, even if they engage in public advocacy or mobilise citizens around social or economic issues. For example, an environmental NGO that organises tree‑planting campaigns or a residents’ committee that lobbies for better municipal services does not need to be listed on the Election Commission’s roll, because it does not present itself as a party contesting elections. Should any such body decide later to field candidates, endorse a slate of representatives, or otherwise participate directly in the electoral process, it would then have to undergo the formal registration procedure, submitting details of its constitution, leadership and financial structure to the Commission in order to be recognised as a political party under the law.

Registration of a political party with the Election Commission of India (ECI) confers a series of substantive advantages that are pivotal both for the organisational development of the party and for the practical conduct of its electoral campaigns. One of the most visible benefits concerns the allocation of election symbols, which serve as a crucial visual identifier for voters, many of whom rely on symbols rather than party names, especially in regions with high illiteracy rates. Parties that are formally registered with the Commission are placed on a preferential list when the ECI assigns free symbols for an upcoming poll. In contrast, candidates who contest elections as purely independent individuals must either select from a limited pool of unreserved symbols or apply for a fresh allocation, a process that can be more cumbersome and less certain. The preferential treatment for registered parties therefore enhances their recognisability on ballot papers and electronic voting machines, giving them a strategic edge over independents.

Beyond the immediate benefit of symbol allocation, registration opens the pathway to official recognition as a “State Party” or a “National Party.” The ECI, acting under the provisions of the Election Symbols (Reservation and Allotment) Order, 1968 (as periodically amended), sets out quantitative criteria that a party must satisfy to earn such status. For instance, to be recognised as a State Party, a political organisation must secure either at least six per cent of the total valid votes polled in a state and win a minimum of two legislative assembly seats, or win a specified proportion of seats in that state’s legislative assembly or Lok Sabha representation. National Party status demands a higher threshold: the party must be recognised as a State Party in at least four states, or achieve a certain share of votes and seats across the country. Once these conditions are met, the party is entered into the official register of recognised parties and is allocated a “reserved” symbol that it may use exclusively across all its candidates.

The importance of a reserved symbol cannot be overstated. When a party attains State Party status, the symbol it has secured is reserved for its use in every constituency within the particular state where the recognition applies. This means that, regardless of which individual stands for election—whether for the state legislative assembly, the municipal corporation, or the Lok Sabha—the symbol appears uniformly on all ballot papers, reinforcing brand continuity and voter familiarity. If the party progresses to National Party status, the same exclusive symbol is usable throughout the entire country, thereby amplifying the party’s visual identity on a pan‑Indian scale. Historical examples illustrate the impact of this mechanism: the Indian National Congress’s iconic “hand” symbol, the Bharatiya Janata Party’s “Lotus,” and regional parties such as the Dravida Munnetra Kazhagam’s “Rising Sun” have become instantly recognisable to millions of voters, in part because of their reserved status.

Recognition also simplifies certain procedural requirements that are otherwise burdensome for unrecognised parties or independent candidates. A recognised State or National Party needs to secure only a single proposer when filing a nomination paper for any election, whereas an independent candidate must obtain a minimum of ten proposers. This reduction in the number of required endorsers eases the administrative load on party candidates, especially in remote or sparsely populated constituencies where locating multiple supporters can be logistically challenging.

Further financial and logistical concessions accrue to recognised parties. The ECI supplies each such party with two complete sets of electoral rolls—one for the polling staff and another for the party’s own use—without charge. These rolls contain the names, addresses, and voting eligibility details of all registered electors in a constituency, and having free access to them enables a party to plan targeted canvassing, voter outreach, and get‑out‑the‑vote operations more efficiently. In addition, the Commission accords recognised parties complimentary airtime on the state broadcasting services Akashvani (All India Radio) and Doordarshan (the national television network) during general elections. This broadcast facility includes a prescribed amount of time for party manifestos, speeches by party leaders, and election advertisements, thereby granting a level playing field in the media arena that would otherwise be dominated by parties with deeper financial resources.

The cumulative effect of these benefits is to strengthen the institutional capacity of registered and recognised parties, allowing them to focus more on policy formulation and voter engagement rather than on administrative hurdles. Moreover, the system of symbol reservation, proposer reduction, free electoral rolls, and broadcast access is designed to promote a more organized and transparent electoral process, ensuring that voters can easily identify the candidates representing a given political formation. In practice, the advantages of registration have contributed to the emergence of a vibrant multi‑party system in India, where both national giants such as the Bharatiya Janata Party and the Indian National Congress, as well as regional powerhouses like the All India Trinamool Congress in West Bengal or the Aam Aadmi Party in Delhi, have leveraged their recognised status to build durable electoral brands and to compete effectively across the nation’s diverse political landscape.

The registration of a political party in India is a formal process that must be carried out in strict accordance with the provisions laid down by the Election Commission of India (ECI), the constitutional body entrusted with the conduct of free and fair elections. An aspiring organization that wishes to be recognised as a political party must first obtain the prescribed application form, commonly referred to as the “proforma”. This proforma is not a generic document; it is a specially designed template that captures all the essential details required by the Commission, ranging from the party’s proposed name and symbol to its leadership structure and ideological commitments. The form can be obtained in three convenient ways: a) by sending a written request to the Secretariat of the Election Commission at its headquarters in Nirvachan Sadan, Ashoka Road, New Delhi‑110001, where the proforma is dispatched by post; b) by visiting the ECI office in person and collecting the document from the counter; and c) by downloading it directly from the Commission’s official website. On the website, the form is located under the main heading “Judicial References”, within the sub‑heading “Political Party”, and further nested under the sub‑sub‑heading “Registration of Political Parties”. This online portal not only provides the blank proforma but also offers a complete set of guidelines, explanatory notes, and frequently asked questions that help applicants understand the procedural nuances and avoid common pitfalls.

Once the proforma has been obtained, the applicant must complete it on the official letter‑head of the party, if such a letter‑head exists. Using the party’s own stationery lends an air of officialdom and helps the Commission verify the authenticity of the submission. The completed form must be typed neatly—hand‑written entries are discouraged because they can lead to misinterpretation or illegibility, especially when the document is later scanned for electronic processing. After typing, the form should be signed by the party’s authorized officer, typically the President, General Secretary, or Chairman, and the signature must be accompanied by the party’s seal. The sealed and signed application is then required to reach the Secretary of the Election Commission within thirty days from the date on which the party was formally constituted. The thirty‑day window is a statutory deadline that ensures that only genuinely active and newly formed parties are entered into the official register, thereby preventing the proliferation of dormant or “shell” organizations that could clutter the electoral roll.

The submission can be made in one of two ways: by sending the documents through registered post, which provides a receipt and a tracking number, or by delivering them personally to the Secretary’s office. Registered post is often preferred because it creates a paper trail and protects the applicant against claims of non‑receipt; however, personal delivery allows the applicant to obtain an immediate acknowledgment stamp on the back of the documents, which can be useful for future reference. Along with the completed proforma, the party must attach a set of mandatory documents that collectively demonstrate its legal standing, financial responsibility, and commitment to the constitutional ethos of the Republic of India.

The first mandatory attachment is a demand draft (DD) for a non‑refundable processing fee of Rs. 10,000 (Rupees Ten Thousand Only). The DD must be drawn in favour of the “Under Secretary, Election Commission of India, New Delhi”. This fee covers the administrative costs incurred by the Commission in scrutinising the application, verifying the supplied documents, and maintaining the official register of political parties. Because the fee is non‑refundable, it is advisable for the applicant to ensure that all other requirements are satisfied before dispatching the demand draft, thereby avoiding unnecessary financial loss.

The second attachment is a complete copy of the party’s constitution, memorandum, rules, or regulations. This document must be typed or printed in a clear, legible format and must contain a specific clause that echoes the language of sub‑section (5) of Section 29A of the Representation of the People Act, 1951. The required clause reads: “(Name of the party) shall bear true faith and allegiance to the Constitution of India as by law established, and to the principles of socialism, secularism and democracy and would uphold the sovereignty, unity and integrity of India.” The inclusion of this clause is not merely a formality; it serves as a legal affirmation that the party accepts the foundational values of the Indian polity. By embedding this statement directly into the party’s governing document, the Commission can be assured that the organization is not contravening the nation’s democratic framework. For illustration, a newly formed “People’s Reform Party” would insert a clause such as: “People’s Reform Party shall bear true faith and allegiance to the Constitution of India as by law established, and to the principles of socialism, secularism and democracy and would uphold the sovereignty, unity and integrity of India.” This explicit wording must appear as an article, clause, or rule within the constitution itself, rather than as a separate annex or letter.

The third and final attachment concerns authentication of the constitutional document. Every page of the party’s constitution must bear the signature of the designated signatory—typically the President, General Secretary, or Chairman—along with the official seal of the party. The requirement for page‑wise authentication serves two purposes. First, it prevents the insertion of unauthorised amendments after the initial submission, thereby preserving the integrity of the document as it was presented to the Commission. Second, it facilitates the Commission’s verification process, as the presence of the seal and signature on each page makes it easier for officials to confirm that the document has not been tampered with during transit. In practice, the signatory would sign the bottom of each page, and the seal—often an embossed emblem of the party—would be affixed adjacent to the signature. If the party does not have a formal seal, a simple handwritten “Seal” accompanied by the signatory’s signature is acceptable, provided it is consistent across all pages.

To summarise, the registration procedure involves obtaining the official proforma from the Election Commission (either by post, in‑person collection, or online download), completing it on the party’s letter‑head, and submitting it—together with a Rs. 10,000 demand draft, a constitution that incorporates the mandated allegiance clause, and page‑wise authenticated signatures and seal—within thirty days of the party’s formation. The application can be sent via registered post or delivered personally, and the entire package must be addressed to the Secretary of the Election Commission at Nirvachan Sadan, Ashoka Road, New Delhi‑110001. By adhering meticulously to these steps, a nascent political organization can secure its place on the official register, thereby gaining the legal right to contest elections, receive a permanent election symbol, and enjoy the other privileges accorded to recognised political parties under Indian law.

The registration of a new political party is governed by a series of detailed procedural requirements that are designed to ensure transparency, internal democracy, and the integrity of the electoral system. First, the party’s constitution, its internal rules and regulations, or any official memorandum must contain a clear provision that outlines how organisational elections are to be conducted at every hierarchical level—national, state, district and local—specifying not only the frequency with which these elections must be held but also the length of the terms of office for all elected functionaries. For instance, a party might stipulate that its national executive committee is to be elected every three years, while its state committees are refreshed biennially; such provisions demonstrate a commitment to regular, predictable turnover and prevent the concentration of power in a few individuals. In addition, the same governing document must expressly prescribe the method to be followed in the event of a merger with another party or the voluntary dissolution of the organisation. This might include, for example, a requirement that a two‑thirds majority of the central council approve any merger, or that a formal resolution be passed by a special meeting before the party can be wound up, thereby providing a legal roadmap for extraordinary structural changes.

A further cornerstone of the registration dossier is proof of a genuine membership base. The party must submit certified extracts taken from the most recent electoral rolls that list at least one hundred individuals who are members of the party. Crucially, these one hundred members must include all persons who hold office in the party’s principal decision‑making bodies, such as the Executive Committee or the Executive Council, to demonstrate that the leadership itself is drawn from a verifiable electorate. The purpose of this requirement is to confirm that the party is rooted in the electorate rather than being a purely nominal or “paper” entity. To reinforce this verification, the party’s President or General Secretary must execute a sworn affidavit—signed before a First‑Class Magistrate, an Oath Commissioner, or a Notary Public—affirming that none of the party’s members hold simultaneous membership in any other political party that is also registered with the Election Commission. This single declaration is intended to prevent the duplication of membership and to maintain the exclusivity of party allegiance, a principle that safeguards the clarity of political competition.

In addition to the collective affidavit, each of the hundred identified members must individually provide a personal affidavit, likewise sworn before a First‑Class Magistrate, Oath Commissioner, or Notary Public. Each affidavit must state two facts: first, that the signatory is a registered elector as shown on the electoral roll; second, that the signatory is not a member of any other party registered with the Commission. These individual attestations serve as a double layer of assurance, confirming both the authenticity of the membership list and the singular political affiliation of each member. The requirement that these personal affidavits accompany the certified electoral‑roll extracts creates a robust evidentiary trail that the Commission can readily audit.

Financial disclosure is another essential element of the registration package. The party must furnish details of any bank accounts that it holds, along with the corresponding Permanent Account Number (PAN) if the account is linked to a tax identification. This information enables the Commission to monitor the party’s financial health, trace the source of funds, and ensure compliance with campaign‑finance regulations. For example, a newly formed party that opens a bank account with a national bank would be required to submit a copy of the account opening statement and the PAN certificate, thereby establishing a transparent financial profile from the outset.

All of the foregoing documents must be compiled in accordance with a prescribed checklist that enumerates each required item and provides space for the applicant to indicate that the accompanying material satisfies the stipulated criteria. The checklist functions as both a guide for the applicant and a control tool for the Commission, facilitating a systematic review of the submission. Once the checklist and the accompanying documents are complete, the entire application packet must be delivered to the Secretary of the Election Commission within thirty days of the party’s formal formation. This thirty‑day window is a statutory deadline that underscores the Commission’s interest in timely registration; any application submitted after the expiration of this period is considered time‑barred and will be rejected outright, compelling the party to restart the registration process.

Taken together, these procedural steps—constitutional provisions for internal elections and for merger or dissolution, certified electoral‑roll extracts for a representative sample of members, collective and individual sworn affidavits affirming exclusive party membership, detailed financial disclosures, and a rigorously checked submission within a strict time frame—form a comprehensive framework that balances the need for democratic internal governance with the imperative of maintaining a clean, accountable, and transparent political landscape. By adhering to these requirements, a nascent political organization demonstrates its readiness to participate responsibly in the democratic process and to be held to the same standards of openness and accountability as all other parties operating under the jurisdiction of the Election Commission.

In the Indian constitutional framework a political organisation attains the status of a *recognised* party in any given State only when it satisfies one of two distinct sets of quantitative requirements, and only when it does so without any post‑election defections that would otherwise invalidate the test.  The first route, commonly referred to as **Clause (A)**, demands that the party demonstrate both a sustained period of political engagement and a measurable degree of electoral success.  Specifically, the party must have been continuously active in the political arena for a minimum of five years—a period that is intended to show that the group is more than a fleeting electoral gimmick and has a stable organisational structure, a permanent cadre of workers, and an enduring programme.  In addition to this longevity test, the party must have secured representation in the most recent general election held in that State, either to the Lok Sabha (the House of the People) or to the State’s Legislative Assembly, depending on which body is being considered.  The representation requirement is calibrated to the size of the legislative body: for the Lok Sabha the party must have won at least one seat for every twenty‑five seats allocated to that State (or any fraction thereof), and for a State Assembly the threshold is one seat for every thirty members (or any fraction thereof).  For example, in a State that contributes ten members to the Lok Sabha, a party would need to win at least one Lok Sabha seat to satisfy Clause (A); in a State Assembly consisting of ninety members, the party would need to win at least three seats, because ninety divided by thirty equals three.  

The second pathway, **Clause (B)**, bypasses the seat‑count requirement and instead looks at the party’s share of the popular vote.  Under this test the aggregate number of valid votes cast for all candidates fielded by the party in the last State‑wide general election—whether for the Lok Sabha or for the State Assembly—must amount to at least six per cent of the total valid votes cast for all candidates in that election.  In practical terms, if a State’s Lok Sabha election saw 10 million valid votes cast across all parties, a party would need to have secured at least 600 000 of those votes to be recognised under Clause B, even if it failed to win any seats.  This provision acknowledges the possibility that a party may enjoy substantial public support but, because of the first‑past‑the‑post system, may not translate that support into parliamentary representation.  

Both of these criteria are subject to an additional safeguard designed to prevent parties from gaining recognition through opportunistic party‑switching after an election has been decided.  If a sitting member of the Lok Sabha or of a State Legislative Assembly joins a party **after** being elected, that member’s affiliation does not count toward satisfying either Clause (A) or Clause B for that party.  This rule discourages elected representatives from using their personal mandate to inflate a party’s numbers merely for the purpose of achieving recognised status.  

Finally, it is important to note that the term “State” in these provisions is interpreted broadly.  It not only includes the twenty‑eight states of the Union but also the National Capital Territory of Delhi and the Union Territory of Pondicherry (now Puducherry).  Consequently, a political party operating in Delhi or Puducherry must meet the same quantitative standards as parties in any other State if it wishes to be accorded the privileges of a recognised party—privileges that typically include a reserved election symbol, free broadcast time on state media, and a higher standing in the conduct of elections.  By setting these dual thresholds—one based on seat‑winning and the other on vote‑share—the Constitution seeks to balance the twin objectives of rewarding genuine electoral performance while also recognising parties that command a sizable, though perhaps geographically dispersed, electorate.

In the Indian electoral system a political organization acquires formal status only after it meets the quantitative thresholds laid down by the Election Commission of India, and the nature of that status – whether it is a “National Party” or a “State Party” – is determined by the breadth of its recognised presence across the country’s federal units. A party that is acknowledged as a recognised political party in four or more of the nation’s states and union territories attains the designation of a National Party, a label that carries with it a set of privileges such as a permanent election symbol, free broadcast time on state media during elections, and the right to field candidates in any constituency without the need for additional registration procedures. This national status, however, is not permanent in an absolute sense; it is contingent on the party’s ability to satisfy the same recognition criteria in each subsequent general election, whether the contest is for seats in the Lok Sabha (the House of the People) or for seats in any State Legislative Assembly. In practice, this means that after a party first qualifies as a National Party by winning the requisite share of votes or seats in at least four states in one electoral cycle, the Election Commission will again examine its performance in the next cycle. If the party continues to secure the necessary percentage of votes (typically at least 6 percent of the total votes polled in a state) or a prescribed number of seats (usually three seats) in each of those four or more states, it retains its national standing. Conversely, if it fails to meet the thresholds in any of the qualifying states, the party will be downgraded to a State Party in the affected jurisdictions, losing the national label and the associated advantages.

For political formations that do not manage to secure recognition in the full complement of four states, the Election Commission bestows the more limited status of State Party in each state where the organization meets the minimum performance standards. A State Party enjoys a smaller but still significant set of benefits, including a reserved election symbol within that particular state, the ability to contest elections without having to register anew in every constituency, and access to certain state‑level campaign facilities. As with the National Party, the State Party status is not irrevocable; the party must continue to satisfy the recognition conditions in every subsequent general election for the House of the People or the relevant State Legislative Assembly within the same state or states where it had previously been recognised. In other words, a party that is a State Party in, say, three states must again achieve the prescribed vote share or seat count in each of those three states in the next Lok Sabha or Assembly polls; failure to do so in any one of them will result in the loss of its recognised status in that state, relegating it to the category of an unrecognised or “registered” party there.

The rationale behind these dual thresholds lies in the federal character of India’s polity. By requiring a party to demonstrate a broad, cross‑regional base before granting it national status, the system seeks to ensure that only organisations with genuine pan‑Indian appeal and organisational capacity enjoy the benefits that could otherwise distort competition in favour of a few dominant players. At the same time, the provision for State Parties acknowledges the importance of regional political dynamics, allowing parties that command significant local support to function effectively without being forced into a national arena where they might be outmatched. Historical examples illustrate how the mechanism operates in practice. The Indian National Congress and the Bharatiya Janata Party, for instance, have consistently met the national criteria across more than four states in successive elections, thereby retaining their National Party status and the attendant privileges. On the other hand, parties such as the Dravida Munnetra Kazhagam (DMK) in Tamil Nadu, the All India Trinamool Congress in West Bengal, and the Shiv Sena in Maharashtra are recognised as State Parties because they satisfy the requisite vote‑share or seat‑share thresholds in their home states but have not yet achieved comparable performance in at least three additional states.

It is also worth noting that the criteria for recognition are not limited to a single metric; the Election Commission employs a dual test based on either the percentage of total votes polled in a state (normally six per cent) or the absolute number of seats won (at least three) in the respective legislative body. A party may meet either one of these conditions to be deemed a recognised entity in a particular state. Moreover, the recognition process is applied separately for Lok Sabha elections and for State Assembly elections, meaning that a party could be a National Party on the basis of its performance in parliamentary elections but may lose that status if its subsequent performance in state elections falls short, and vice versa. This dual‑track system adds a layer of flexibility, allowing parties to concentrate their resources strategically, either aiming for a broad national footprint or consolidating power in a limited number of states.

In summary, the classification of a political organisation as a National Party or a State Party in India hinges upon its ability to secure recognised status in at least four states, or in fewer than four states, respectively, and to maintain that status by meeting the Election Commission’s prescribed vote‑share or seat‑share thresholds in every subsequent general election—whether for the Lok Sabha or for any State Legislative Assembly. The design of these criteria reflects a balancing act between encouraging nationwide political competition and respecting the country’s diverse regional political landscapes, while also providing clear incentives for parties to sustain and expand their electoral appeal over time.
