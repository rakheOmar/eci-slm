The Model Code of Conduct (MCC) is a formally articulated set of guidelines that governs the behavior of political parties and individual candidates during the electoral cycle, and it is intended to promote a level playing field, preserve the integrity of the democratic process, and prevent the misuse of state machinery for partisan advantage. Originating from a consensus‑building process among the major political formations in the country, the code reflects the collective agreement of those parties to adhere strictly to the principles it enshrines; by signing onto the MCC, each party voluntarily commits itself to observe both the letter and the spirit of the provisions it contains. The code covers a wide spectrum of electoral conduct, including restrictions on the timing and content of campaign speeches, bans on the use of government resources such as official vehicles, premises, or personnel for campaign purposes, guidelines for the distribution of political material, and rules governing the declaration of assets and liabilities by candidates. It also imposes limits on public gatherings, outlines permissible expenditure thresholds, and mandates that parties refrain from incendiary or communal rhetoric that could inflame social tensions. Enforcement is typically carried out by the Election Commission, which monitors compliance and may issue warnings, impose fines, or even disqualify candidates who flagrantly violate the code. For example, during the 2019 general elections, several parties were cautioned for holding rallies after the prescribed “silence period” that precedes voting day, illustrating how the MCC functions as a real‑time check on electoral excesses. By institutionalising these norms, the Model Code of Conduct seeks to ensure that elections are contested fairly, that voters receive unbiased information, and that the democratic mandate reflects the genuine will of the people rather than the advantage of any single party or candidate.

The Election Commission of India, constituted under Article 324 of the Constitution, occupies a central place in the nation’s democratic architecture by supervising the entire electoral process so that it remains free, fair, peaceful and credible. Its jurisdiction extends over every tier of elected government—national, state and local—requiring it to monitor the conduct of the ruling parties at the Centre, the governments of the various states, and all candidates who contest elections, whether they belong to the incumbent coalition, an opposition outfit or stand as independents. In practice this supervisory role is exercised through a suite of statutory powers and procedural mechanisms that together create a framework within which political actors must operate. First, the Commission issues and enforces the Model Code of Conduct, a set of binding guidelines that come into force the moment an election is announced; the Code curtails the use of government resources for partisan purposes, limits public expenditure by incumbent officials, and stipulates how rallies, advertisements and public meetings may be conducted. By compelling parties and office‑holders to adhere to these rules, the Commission helps prevent the distortion of the electoral playing field that might otherwise arise from the incumbents’ control of administrative machinery.

Equally important is the Commission’s vigilance against the misuse of official resources for electoral advantage. This includes ensuring that government offices, vehicles, staff, and public funds are not employed to mobilise voters, distribute inducements or disseminate campaign material. When the Commission detects any such transgression—say, a state minister using government‑run schools to display party slogans or a central agency allocating extra development projects in a constituency just before polling—it can issue show‑cause notices, impose monetary penalties, or refer the matter to the judiciary for further sanction. In this way, the Commission acts as a guardian of the principle that the state must remain neutral during elections, preserving public confidence that the contest is decided by the electorate rather than by administrative patronage.

Beyond the oversight of official machinery, the Election Commission is tasked with preventing and punishing a wide range of electoral offences that threaten the integrity of the vote. These offences include impersonation (the fraudulent casting of ballots in the name of absent or non‑existent voters), bribery and inducement (offering cash, goods or promises of favours in exchange for votes), and intimidation or threats directed at voters or election officials. To combat impersonation, the Commission has introduced voter‑verified paper audit trails (VVPAT) alongside electronic voting machines, allowing each voter to confirm that his or her vote has been correctly recorded and enabling post‑election audits. Anti‑bribery measures include the prohibition of cash distribution on polling day and the requirement that candidates disclose any gifts or expenditures exceeding prescribed limits; violations trigger disqualification of the candidate, forfeiture of deposits and, in serious cases, criminal prosecution under the Representation of the People Act. Likewise, the Commission empowers law‑enforcement agencies to intervene when candidates or their agents engage in violence, harassment, or threats against voters—examples range from physical assaults on polling agents to the dissemination of false rumors designed to intimidate particular communities. In each instance, the Commission can issue immediate orders to arrest or detain the offenders, suspend the offending candidate’s campaign, or, where the breach is egregious, recommend the cancellation of election results in the affected constituency.

The Commission’s proactive stance does not end with punitive action; it also undertakes extensive preventive work to create an environment conducive to free choice. This includes the widespread deployment of voter education programmes that inform citizens about their rights, the proper use of the secret ballot, and the importance of reporting malpractices. It conducts regular training sessions for polling officials, election officers and political party representatives, thereby standardising procedures and reducing the scope for inadvertent errors or deliberate manipulation. Moreover, the Commission supervises the delimitation of constituencies, the updating of electoral rolls and the issuance of identity cards, all of which are essential to ensure that each eligible citizen has a clear, unambiguous claim to a vote and that no individual is unjustly excluded or duplicated on the roll.

When violations are identified, the Commission follows a graduated response that is calibrated to the seriousness of the breach. Minor infractions, such as the violation of campaign timing restrictions, may result in written warnings or modest fines, whereas more serious transgressions—such as the organized distribution of cash to voters or the use of state resources to influence an election—can lead to the filing of criminal complaints, the disqualification of the offending candidate, and even the annulment of the entire election in the constituency. These corrective measures are designed not only to punish wrongdoing but also to serve as a deterrent, signalling to all political actors that the sanctity of the electoral process is non‑negotiable.

In sum, the Election Commission of India functions as the constitutional sentinel of democracy, ensuring that every election—from the Lok Sabha to the state assemblies—is conducted in a manner that respects the rule of law, upholds the principle of equal opportunity for all participants, and safeguards the electorate from coercion, corruption and the undue influence of power. By overseeing the conduct of ruling parties, regulating the use of official machinery, preventing a spectrum of electoral offences, and imposing appropriate sanctions when violations occur, the Commission preserves the credibility of India’s electoral system and, by extension, the legitimacy of the governments that emerge from it.

This graduated approach seeks to balance the need for uniform fairness in large‑scale elections with the practical realities of local governance in more limited contests, thereby safeguarding the democratic process across India’s varied political landscape.

By freezing the use of such funds from the moment the election timetable is issued, the legal framework seeks to uphold fairness, maintain public confidence in the integrity of the electoral process, and ensure that public resources are managed without partisan bias.

When a work order has already been issued for a particular scheme or programme, the timing of an election‑related announcement does not automatically grant permission to commence the physical work. In practice, any contract that was awarded before the election programme was made public must remain dormant until the election period is over, unless the contractor had already begun tangible activities on the site—such as earth‑moving, foundation laying, or installation of utilities—prior to the announcement. In those cases the work that is already in progress may be allowed to continue, because stopping it would waste resources and could jeopardise safety or quality standards. Conversely, if the contract was merely signed and no field operations had started, the project must be put on hold until after the election period. This rule is intended to prevent the perception that public works are being timed to influence voters, while still recognizing that work already under way cannot be abandoned without causing unnecessary loss. For example, a road‑building contract awarded in February may not see any machinery on the ground before a March election announcement, so the crews would wait; however, a water‑supply pipe‑laying project that began trenching in late February may continue through the election cycle because the work had already been physically initiated.

Funds allocated under the Members of Parliament (MP), Members of Legislative Assembly (MLA), and Members of Legislative Council (MLC) Local Area Development (LAD) schemes are intended to finance small‑scale infrastructure projects—such as the construction of roads, schools, drinking‑water facilities, and community centres—within the elected representative’s constituency. However, the disbursement of any fresh capital from these schemes is expressly prohibited in any constituency where a poll is currently under way. The restriction remains in force until the entire electoral process, including voting, counting, and the official declaration of results, has been completed and the new or returning representative has taken oath of office. This rule is designed to prevent the misuse of development funds for influencing voter behaviour, a practice commonly referred to as “cash for votes” or “developmental patronage.” For example, if a general election is scheduled for a particular district in June, no new LAD project can be approved or funded in that district from the time the election schedule is announced until the Election Commission declares the final outcome. The policy thus safeguards the fairness of the electoral contest by ensuring that public resources are not deployed as a campaign tool.

The central government’s rural development agenda is carried out through a suite of large‑scale programmes—such as the Indira Awas Yojana (IAY), Sampoorna Grameen Rozgar Yojana (SGRY), Swarna Jayanti Gram Swarozgar Yojana (SGSY), the National Food‑for‑Work Programme (NFWP) and the National Rural Employment Guarantee Act (NREGA)—each of which is designed to address a specific dimension of rural poverty, housing, employment and food security, and each of which operates under a set of implementation norms that become especially stringent during election periods in order to safeguard the integrity of public expenditure.  Under the IAY, which seeks to provide pucca houses to the rural poor, the rule is that once a beneficiary has received a sanction for a housing unit and construction has commenced, the work may continue under the usual technical and financial standards; however, no new housing units may be sanctioned or newly initiated until the conclusion of the electoral process, a precaution intended to prevent the politicisation of credit allocation.  In the case of SGRY—an employment‑generation scheme that funds wage‑labour projects in gram panchayats—the policy permits the continuation of works that are already underway, and the release of funds already earmarked for those projects, provided that the necessary administrative clearances have been obtained.  If a particular panchayat has completed all of its ongoing projects and wishes to launch fresh wage‑employment initiatives, it may do so only when the annual action plan for the current financial year includes such works and only after securing prior consent from the District Election Officer, a measure that ensures that new expenditures are not timed to influence the electorate.  When the funds for a panchayat derive directly from the Ministry of Rural Development, the same pre‑approval is required; by contrast, any projects financed from other streams of government financing are barred from initiation during the election window.  SGSY, which focuses on the formation of Self‑Help Groups (SHGs) and the provision of micro‑enterprise subsidies, follows a similarly cautious approach: only those SHGs that have already received a portion of their entitlement—whether in the form of a grant or a subsidy—are eligible to receive the remaining instalments, while the creation of new SHGs or the onboarding of fresh individual beneficiaries is suspended until after the polls have been completed, thereby averting any appearance of vote‑buying through the distribution of new financial assistance.  The NFWP, a scheme that pays participants in exchange for undertaking works such as road construction, water‑conservation structures and afforestation, is allowed to maintain ongoing projects in districts where elections have not yet been announced, and it may even sanction new works in those districts provided that the projects are already in the physical execution phase; nevertheless, the amount of outstanding advance that can be disbursed for any such work is capped at the equivalent of forty‑five days of labour, a limit that curtails the risk of unspent cash being diverted for partisan purposes.  In districts where elections have been announced or are in progress, the programme is restricted to those activities that have already begun, and no fresh initiatives may be launched.  Finally, with respect to NREGA—the flagship employment guarantee law that obliges the state to provide at least one hundred days of wage employment per household—there is a clear directive that the Ministry of Rural Development shall not expand the geographical coverage of the scheme by adding new districts after an election has been called, a safeguard intended to prevent the perception that the government is using the promise of new jobs to sway voters.  Collectively, these guidelines illustrate a consistent pattern across all five rural development interventions: they allow the seamless continuation of projects that are already in motion, they protect the flow of already‑approved financial resources, and they impose a temporary moratorium on the initiation of new beneficiaries, projects or expansions until the electoral process is fully concluded, thereby balancing the twin imperatives of sustaining rural livelihoods and preserving the neutrality of state‑funded development programmes.

In the framework of the central‑government rural‑development programmes—such as the Indira Awas Yojana, Sampoorna Grameen Rozgar Yojana, Swarnajayanti Gram Swarozgar Yojana, the National Food‑for‑Work Programme and the National Rural Employment Guarantee Act (now known as the Mahatma Gandhi National Rural Employment Guarantee Act, MGNREGA)—the issuance of a “job card” to a household serves as a legal guarantee that the holder can be allotted work whenever there is an unfilled demand for labour.  This guarantee does not cease, however, simply because a general election is about to be held; rather, the statutes that govern these schemes contain a specific set of procedural safeguards designed to prevent the disruption of employment generation during the election‑announcement period while also averting the misuse of public works as a tool for electoral advantage.  Consequently, once the Election Commission announces the dates for a forthcoming election, the competent authority—usually the District Programme Officer or the Block Development Officer—must first examine all projects that are already in progress within the district.  If a job‑card holder approaches the authority and requests work, the officer is obliged to place the applicant in one of those ongoing works, provided that there remains an unfilled labour slot.  In practice this means that a mason who has been assigned to a road‑construction contract, for example, can be shifted to a parallel activity such as the construction of a foot‑bridge or the installation of irrigation channels, as long as the original project still requires additional hands.  

When the pool of ongoing projects is exhausted—either because every current worksite has already reached its full staffing capacity or because the nature of the work does not match the skills of the job‑card holder—the competent authority may turn to the “shelf of projects.”  The shelf consists of a pre‑approved inventory of works that have been vetted, budgeted and cleared by the Ministry of Rural Development but have not yet been launched on the ground.  Typical shelf items include the construction of additional rural ponds, the reinforcement of school‑yard fencing, the planting of community horticulture plots, or the execution of minor public‑health infrastructure such as latrine blocks.  By tapping this reserve, the authority can quickly generate new employment opportunities without having to wait for a fresh allocation of funds or a new approval cycle.  Before any new work is initiated from the shelf, the authority must formally inform the District Election Officer (DEO) that the work is being started solely to meet the employment demand of job‑card holders and not to influence the electoral process.  This notification is recorded in writing and a copy is sent to the state‑level Rural Development Department, ensuring transparency and creating an audit trail that can be examined by the Election Commission or by parliamentary committees.  

If, however, the shelf of projects is itself depleted—perhaps because the district has already exhausted all pre‑approved schemes for the fiscal year—or if the nature of the labour demand is such that none of the shelved works can accommodate the skill set of the applicants, the competent authority must seek a higher‑level exemption.  In this situation the officer prepares a detailed reference letter that outlines the specific circumstances: the number of job‑card holders awaiting work, the types of skills they possess, the unavailability of suitable ongoing or shelved projects, and the potential impact on the households’ guaranteed income.  This letter is routed through the DEO to the Rural Development Commission (or the equivalent state‑level body), which may grant a special sanction for the creation of a new project.  The commission’s approval is typically accompanied by a set of conditions—such as a capped budget, a defined timeline, and a requirement that the newly sanctioned work be completed within the election‑free period—to ensure that the intervention remains a genuine employment measure rather than a political exercise.  

Finally, once the new work has been sanctioned, the competent authority must issue a formal certificate to the DEO confirming that the project has received the requisite approval and that it has been launched precisely because no employment could be provided through existing works.  This certificate, often stamped and signed by the District Programme Officer, serves as the concluding piece of documentation that closes the procedural loop.  It reassures the Election Commission that all possible avenues for employing job‑card holders have been exhausted before any new work is undertaken, thereby upholding the twin objectives of the rural‑development programmes: guaranteeing a minimum of 100 days of wage‑earning work per year to rural households, and preserving the integrity of the electoral process by preventing the creation of “dummy” projects aimed at influencing voters.  In real‑world applications, these guidelines have helped states such as Uttar Pradesh and Madhya Pradesh to maintain continuous employment flows even during the intense campaigning periods that accompany national and state elections, illustrating how administrative diligence can align development goals with democratic safeguards.

In the conduct of public administration, elected officials and senior political functionaries are expressly prohibited from making unilateral announcements of monetary assistance, promises of financial grants, or any form of fiscal commitment on behalf of the government. This restriction applies not only to the allocation of funds for specific programmes but also to the public proclamation of intended expenditures, whether the amounts are modest or substantial. The rationale behind this rule is to preserve the integrity of the budgeting process, prevent the politicisation of fiscal policy, and ensure that any commitment of public resources is subject to the rigorous scrutiny of the relevant ministries, parliamentary committees, and statutory approval mechanisms.  

Similarly, ministers and other political leaders are barred from personally laying foundation stones, inaugurating projects, or otherwise ceremonially marking the commencement of schemes that fall within the purview of the civil service. The exception to this prohibition is limited to career civil servants—senior officers who are part of the permanent bureaucracy—who may perform such ceremonial duties when it is necessary to demonstrate governmental support for a project while maintaining administrative neutrality. For instance, when a new water‑treatment plant is being constructed, the chief engineer or the director of the water department may be invited to place the ceremonial stone, whereas a minister would be expected to refrain from doing so in order to avoid the impression that the project’s approval or funding is contingent upon political patronage.  

The ban also extends to any overt promises regarding the construction of roads, the provision of drinking‑water facilities, the erection of schools, or similar infrastructure commitments. A political figure who declares that a particular highway will be built within a specified timeframe without having secured the requisite approvals, budgetary allocations, and environmental clearances is violating the principle that infrastructure development must be planned and executed through established, non‑partisan channels. Such premature promises can create unrealistic public expectations, distort competition among contractors, and undermine the credibility of the planning agencies.  

Finally, the rule prohibits ad‑hoc appointments in government departments, public sector undertakings, and other statutory bodies that are not grounded in the prescribed recruitment processes. These appointments must follow transparent, merit‑based procedures to prevent nepotism and the appearance of cronyism. When a senior government officer, rather than a political leader, lays a foundation stone or inaugurates a project, the action is viewed as a formal, administrative endorsement that does not convey any special political favour. This separation of ceremonial duties between elected officials and career bureaucrats helps to maintain the impartiality of the public service, reinforces the rule of law in the allocation of resources, and safeguards the public’s trust in the fairness and accountability of government actions.

When a government has already allocated funds for a specific programme in the budget or has previously given formal approval to that programme, the timing of any public launch or formal inauguration becomes subject to strict electoral regulations. During the election period—defined by the Model Code of Conduct or comparable statutory provisions that come into force as soon as the election schedule is announced—political parties and incumbent administrations are barred from using newly‑sanctioned or already‑funded schemes as electoral bait. Consequently, even if the scheme is fully funded and ready to roll out, officials may not hold a ribbon‑cutting ceremony, press conference, or any other form of public announcement that could be construed as seeking votes. The prohibition is intended to prevent the incumbent government from gaining an undue advantage by showcasing fresh development initiatives right before voters head to the polls. For example, in several Indian general elections, ministries have been cautioned or penalised for attempting to unveil infrastructure projects or welfare schemes after the election timetable was released, reinforcing the principle that the electoral playing field must remain level and free from government‑sponsored inducements.

When the Model Code of Conduct (MCC) is formally brought into force ahead of a scheduled election, the election‑overseeing body – the Election Commission of India – imposes a blanket prohibition on the initiation of new public‑works schemes or welfare programmes that could be construed as influencing the electorate. Nonetheless, the legal framework recognises that certain projects which have already moved beyond the planning stage should be allowed to proceed so as not to waste the resources already expended, to honour contractual obligations, and to safeguard the interests of beneficiaries who have already been identified. In this respect, the government and its various agencies may continue to implement three broad categories of activities without seeking prior clearance from the Commission. First, any “work‑project” that has physically commenced on the ground after all requisite administrative, technical and financial sanctions have been obtained may be carried forward. For example, if a rural road‑construction contract was awarded, the tender was evaluated, the award letter was issued, and the contractor has already laid the first layer of tarmac before the MCC was announced, the work must be allowed to finish, because halting it would entail a breach of contract and a loss of public money already spent on materials, labour and equipment mobilisation. Second, “beneficiary‑project” schemes in which the individual recipients have been named and notified before the MCC can also be pursued. A typical instance would be a direct‑benefit transfer scheme for a specific set of households that have already been vetted, their bank accounts linked, and the disbursement schedule fixed; stopping these payments after the code is in place would not only punish the identified families but could also raise legal challenges for violating the principle of non‑discrimination. Third, persons already registered under the Mahatma Gandhi National Rural Employment Guarantee Act (MGNREGA) may be covered by ongoing projects. If a Gram Panchayat has already listed a set of MGNREGA works—such as construction of a community water tank or a village‑level road—into its approved project shelf, and the requisite funds for those works have been earmarked in the state’s budget, the implementation of those works can continue, provided that the beneficiaries are among those who are already on the MGNREGA registry. In practice, this means that a farmer who has been assigned a specific work‑order under MGNREGA before the MCC can still receive his/her wages and see the project completed, because the entitlement is tied to an existing registration rather than a newly created scheme. 

In addition to these “existing‑project” categories, the law also permits the commencement of certain “new” works—whether they are oriented toward a specific beneficiary or toward a general public‑interest objective—if they satisfy a stringent set of pre‑conditions that demonstrate the project is already financially and administratively locked in before the MCC takes effect. The first condition is the existence of full funding: the entire budgetary outlay for the work must have been allocated, approved by the relevant finance authority, and transferred to the executing agency. This ensures that no additional fiscal stimulus is being injected into the economy with the express purpose of swaying voters. The second condition requires that all three layers of sanction—administrative (approval by the department head), technical (verification that the design, specifications and feasibility studies are complete), and financial (clearance of the monetary release) — have been obtained and recorded in official documents. Third, a competitive tendering process must have already been conducted: the invitation to bid must have been published, bids received, evaluated on the basis of merit and cost, and a contract awarded to a contractor. Fourth, the contract itself must contain a clear, enforceable clause obligating the contractor to commence the work within a stipulated time‑frame and to complete it by a defined deadline, with a penalty provision for any undue delay or non‑performance. In effect, the project is already a binding legal commitment that would be jeopardised by any interruption. When all these elements are in place before the MCC is triggered, the executing agency may proceed with the work after giving a formal intimation to the Election Commission, thereby maintaining transparency while respecting the spirit of the code. 

If, however, any of the above prerequisites are missing—such as only partial funding being secured, a pending technical sanction, an un‑finalised tender, or a contract lacking a firm commencement date—the project is deemed “new” in the eyes of the MCC and cannot be started without the explicit prior approval of the Election Commission. In such cases, the agency must submit a detailed request outlining the nature of the work, the reasons for urgency, the potential impact on the electorate, and any safeguards that will prevent the project from being used as a political tool. The Commission will then evaluate the proposal against the principles of fairness, neutrality, and non‑influence, granting permission only in exceptional circumstances where the public interest overwhelmingly outweighs the risk of electoral advantage. 

To summarise, the regulatory regime surrounding the Model Code of Conduct distinguishes between projects that are already underway or have fully crystallised contractual and financial commitments and those that are merely in the planning or pre‑award stage. Existing works that have started on the ground, beneficiary‑specific schemes with named recipients, and MGNREGA projects covering already‑registered workers may all be continued uninterrupted, thereby protecting sunk costs, upholding contractual fidelity and preventing hardship for identified beneficiaries. Conversely, any new venture that does not meet the full set of funding, sanction, tendering and contractual conditions must first obtain the Election Commission’s consent before it can be launched, ensuring that the electoral process remains free from the undue influence of freshly created government expenditure. This balanced approach seeks to safeguard both the integrity of the democratic process and the legitimate expectations of citizens who rely on timely delivery of public services.

In the administration of public contracts and similar agreements, the disbursement of funds for work that has already been finished is not obstructed by any procedural prohibition, provided that the parties responsible for overseeing the project give their full and unequivocal approval. In practice, this means that once the contractor has completed the stipulated tasks—whether they involve construction, software development, maintenance services, or any other deliverable—the payment will be released without delay as long as the supervising engineers, inspectors, or other designated officials certify that the work meets all technical specifications, quality standards, and contractual requirements. For example, after a bridge is erected, the civil‑engineer‑in‑charge must sign off on the inspection report; only after this formal “full satisfaction” is recorded can the treasury issue the final invoice payment. This principle safeguards both the integrity of the procurement process and the financial interests of the service provider.

When an unexpected disaster strikes—whether it be a severe drought that deprives farmers of water, a sudden flood that inundates villages, a pestilence that threatens public health, or any other natural calamity that leaves populations in urgent need of assistance—the government is empowered to intervene swiftly to provide relief, rehabilitation, and welfare support. Such interventions may also extend to the provision of regular welfare measures for vulnerable groups, including the elderly, persons with disabilities, and others who require ongoing assistance. However, the exercise of these powers is not without procedural safeguards. Before any relief operation or welfare scheme can be launched, the responsible ministry must first secure the explicit, prior approval of the designated Commission that oversees emergency and welfare programmes. This requirement ensures that the deployment of public resources is coordinated, justified, and consistent with national policy, thereby preventing ad‑hoc or politically motivated actions.

In addition to the formal approval process, the government is instructed to conduct all relief activities with the utmost modesty. “Ostentatious functions” such as lavish ceremonies, public displays of political patronage, or any form of grandiose publicity must be strictly avoided. The underlying rationale is to keep the focus on the needs of the affected citizens rather than on the image of the authorities. Consequently, officials are prohibited from creating, or allowing the creation of, any impression that relief measures are being undertaken for ulterior motives—such as garnering electoral advantage, rewarding loyal supporters, or diverting attention from other governmental shortcomings. 

For example, if a flood‑affected district receives emergency shelter kits, the distribution should be carried out quietly, with clear documentation and transparent accounting, rather than with a televised ribbon‑cutting ceremony that could be interpreted as a political showcase. Similarly, when a pension scheme for senior citizens is expanded during a crisis, the announcement should be factual and devoid of hyperbolic language that might suggest the government is exploiting the situation for popularity gains. By adhering to these principles—securing Commission approval and eschewing any flamboyant or self‑serving presentation—the state can respond effectively to emergencies while maintaining public trust and upholding the ethical standards expected of a democratic administration.

Financial institutions that receive funding from the state—whether they are wholly owned by the government, such as national development banks, or only partially capitalised through public capital injections—are subject to strict regulatory and policy constraints that prohibit them from arbitrarily cancelling or “writing off” debts owed by private borrowers. In practice this means that a government‑backed lender cannot simply erase the principal or accrued interest on a loan that has been extended to an individual, a small‑business firm, a large corporation, or any other type of borrower simply because the repayment becomes difficult or politically expedient. The prohibition is rooted in the principle of fiscal prudence: public resources must be managed in a way that safeguards the overall stability of the financial system and protects taxpayers from bearing the cost of un‑vetted bailouts. Moreover, most jurisdictions embed this restriction in the statutes governing state‑owned banks or in the conditions attached to public capital, thereby ensuring that such institutions operate on a level playing field with private lenders and maintain market discipline.

In addition to the ban on loan write‑offs, these institutions are also required to observe explicit financial limits when they originate or extend credit. Regulatory frameworks—such as capital adequacy standards, loan‑to‑value ratios, and concentration‑risk caps—prevent them from expanding their balance‑sheet indiscriminately. For example, a development bank may be allowed to allocate a certain percentage of its loan portfolio to small‑ and medium‑sized enterprises, but it cannot exceed that quota simply to accommodate political pressure or to favour particular constituencies. Historical episodes illustrate the dangers of ignoring such safeguards: the rapid, unchecked lending that preceded the 1997 Asian financial crisis, or the unsustainable loan growth in some state‑controlled banks during the early 2000s, both resulted in massive write‑downs and required costly government interventions. By adhering to these rules—no arbitrary debt forgiveness and disciplined loan‑making—government‑funded financial institutions help preserve fiscal integrity, promote responsible credit allocation, and reduce the risk of systemic financial distress.

In accordance with the provisions of the Model Code of Conduct that come into force once elections are announced, any commercial activities that involve the issuance of tenders, the holding of auctions, or the award of contracts for items such as liquor licences, the procurement of tendu leaves, or similar commodities must be put on hold until the electoral process in the relevant constituency has been fully completed. The rationale for this restriction is to prevent the perception—or the reality—of undue influence on voters through the strategic allocation of government resources or the granting of favoured business opportunities during a politically sensitive period. Consequently, even routine administrative procedures that would normally be carried out on a continuous basis are suspended, and pending applications are retained in a pending‑status queue. Should an absolutely essential need arise—such as the supply of critical raw material for a government‑run welfare scheme or the continuation of a legally mandated liquor distribution network—the administration is authorised to institute temporary, interim arrangements that are narrowly tailored to address the emergency while still respecting the spirit of the electoral code. These provisional measures are intended to be short‑lived and are subject to strict oversight to ensure that they do not confer any undue advantage to particular parties or individuals during the election cycle.

In the governance of urban local bodies such as Municipal Corporations, Nagar Panchayats and Town Area Committees, it is permissible to convene a meeting expressly for the purpose of examining the latest figures on revenue collection and for preparing a draft of the forthcoming annual budget. However, the scope of such a gathering is limited to issues that pertain to the routine, day‑to‑day administration of the body. This means that while officials may discuss the amounts received from taxes, fees, and other ordinary sources of income, and may outline provisional expenditure plans for services like water supply, waste management, and street lighting, they must refrain from using the same forum to deliberate on broader policy directions, long‑term development programmes, or strategic initiatives that would normally require a full council session or a specially convened policy meeting. For example, a Town Area Committee could meet to compare the current year’s property tax receipts with the previous year’s figures and to draft a tentative budget for maintaining municipal roads, but it could not, in that same session, decide to launch a new urban renewal scheme or to change the zoning regulations. This distinction ensures that routine financial oversight can be carried out efficiently without encroaching on the more deliberative, policy‑setting functions that demand wider participation and formal procedural safeguards.

Sad Bhaavna Diwas, a day dedicated to the celebration of communal harmony and the spirit of national integration, is observed across the length and breadth of India each year. The occasion provides an important platform for the nation’s leaders, civil society groups, educational institutions and ordinary citizens to reaffirm the ideals of unity, brotherly love and respect for diversity that underpin the Indian Constitution. In keeping with the non‑partisan nature of the event, senior political figures—including the Prime Minister, Union Ministers, Chief Ministers, State Ministers and other elected officials—are permitted to take part in the observances, but only under a set of carefully prescribed conditions that safeguard the day’s central theme.

First and foremost, any address delivered by a political functionary on Sad Bhaavna Diwas must be strictly confined to the promotion of harmony among the people. The speaker may speak about the historical achievements of communal amity, cite examples of inter‑religious cooperation, or highlight government programmes that foster inclusive development, but the content must avoid any partisan commentary, electoral promises, or criticism of opposition parties. This restriction is intended to prevent the celebration from being used as a venue for political campaigning or for advancing a particular party’s agenda, thereby preserving the day’s universal appeal.

In addition to oral presentations, written messages or statements that are issued in the name of a Minister or other high‑ranking official must also adhere to the same narrow focus. Such messages should centre exclusively on the theme of national integration, stressing the importance of mutual respect, cultural exchange and the shared destiny of India’s myriad communities. They should not contain any references to policy initiatives that are unrelated to harmony, nor should they be used to promote upcoming elections or legislative proposals that are politically sensitive.

A further procedural detail concerns the visual presentation of these communications. Official messages are required to be issued without the inclusion of a photograph of the Minister or political figure who is the nominal author. By omitting personal images, the organizers aim to keep the emphasis on the collective ideals of Sad Bhaavna Diwas rather than on individual personalities, thereby discouraging any perception that the day is being leveraged for personal publicity.

To illustrate how these guidelines operate in practice, consider a recent celebration in a northern state where the Chief Minister delivered a speech that highlighted the contributions of Sikh, Hindu, Muslim and Buddhist communities to the region’s cultural heritage. The address made no mention of the upcoming state elections, and the accompanying press release was circulated without the Chief Minister’s portrait, containing only the official seal and a brief statement on the importance of unity. Such adherence to the prescribed norms demonstrates how political leaders can respectfully participate in Sad Bhaavna Diwas, reinforcing the day’s core message while steering clear of partisan rhetoric.

State‑level ceremonies commemorating the martyrdom of those who have given their lives for the nation may indeed be chaired or attended by the Chief Minister or other ministers, provided that the protocol governing such events is strictly observed. The primary purpose of these observances is to honour the sacrifice of the martyrs, to reflect on the values for which they fell, and to inspire citizens with examples of courage and self‑lessness. Consequently, any address delivered by a chief minister, cabinet minister or other senior official must be confined to remarks that recount the circumstances of the martyrs’ ultimate sacrifice, extol their personal virtues, and express collective gratitude on behalf of the community or the state. The speaker should avoid any reference to current governmental programmes, policy achievements, or partisan achievements of the ruling party, as such content would detract from the solemn, non‑political nature of the occasion. For instance, a minister might speak of the bravery shown during a historic battle, cite the martyrs’ steadfast commitment to duty, and invoke the timeless principle of service to the nation, while refraining from mentioning recent infrastructure projects or electoral successes. This disciplined approach ensures that the ceremony remains a respectful tribute rather than a platform for political promotion.

Cultural programmes such as Kavi Sammelans, Mushairas and other artistic gatherings may indeed be incorporated into the official celebrations of Independence Day and Republic Day, providing a vibrant showcase of the nation’s literary and musical heritage. Attendance by senior elected representatives – including the Prime Minister, Union Ministers, Chief Ministers, state ministers and other political dignitaries – is expressly permitted, and such participation is often welcomed as a sign of governmental support for the arts and for national unity. Nevertheless, organizers are required to observe a clear line between cultural appreciation and partisan promotion. In practice this means that while politicians may be present on the dais, deliver brief congratulatory remarks or address the audience in a non‑partisan manner, they must refrain from delivering speeches that extol the accomplishments of the ruling party or that turn the event into a political campaign. By maintaining this separation, the celebrations preserve their inclusive, patriotic character and ensure that the focus remains on cultural expression rather than on partisan propaganda.

Political advertisements are not permitted to appear on the reverse side of bus‑ticket inserts issued by government‑owned transit services. This prohibition stems from a combination of electoral‑law safeguards and public‑sector advertising policies that aim to keep state‑run transportation facilities free from partisan influence. In most jurisdictions, the election commission or a comparable regulatory body expressly bans the use of official government property—such as ticket stock, vehicle livery, or station signage—for the promotion of political parties, candidates, or election‑related messages. The rationale is to preserve the neutrality of public services, prevent the inadvertent endorsement of any political group by the state, and avoid the perception that taxpayers’ money is being leveraged for campaign purposes. Similar restrictions apply to other government‑owned media, including pamphlets distributed on official forms, posters in public offices, and audio announcements on municipal radio channels. For instance, a municipal bus authority in a neighboring province faced legal challenges when a political party attempted to place ads on ticket backs, leading to a court ruling that reinforced the ban. Consequently, any political messaging must be confined to privately owned platforms or designated public advertising spaces that do not involve government‑issued ticket material.

The determination of the Minimum Support Price (MSP) for wheat and other agricultural commodities is a policy issue that does not fall under the routine purview of agricultural ministries alone; instead, the appropriate procedural channel for resolving such a matter involves referral to the Election Commission. The MSP, which serves as a price floor intended to protect farmers against market fluctuations, is traditionally set after careful consideration of production costs, market demand, and food‑security objectives. However, when disputes arise concerning the legality, fairness, or political implications of setting or adjusting these prices, the matter is escalated to an institution equipped to ensure procedural integrity and impartiality. In the Indian context, the Election Commission, while primarily tasked with overseeing the conduct of free and fair elections, also functions as an arbiter in certain cases where policy decisions intersect with electoral considerations, such as the timing of MSP announcements relative to voting cycles. By directing inquiries about the feasibility of fixing the MSP for wheat, rice, pulses, and other staples to the Election Commission, authorities ensure that any decision is scrutinized for compliance with democratic norms, transparency requirements, and the broader public interest. This referral mechanism thus provides a structured avenue for resolving complex agricultural pricing issues within the framework of the nation’s governance and electoral oversight systems.

In the Indian constitutional framework the State Government does not have a direct channel for obtaining clarification, clearance or approval of any proposal from the Election Commission of India. Instead, any such request must be routed through the Chief Electoral Officer (CEO) of the respective state. The CEO, who is an officer of the Indian Administrative Service appointed by the Union Government, acts as the intermediary between the state administration and the independent election authority. Upon receiving the state’s proposal—whether it concerns the scheduling of an election, the use of public resources for campaigning, or the implementation of election‑related regulations—the CEO examines the matter and prepares a recommendation for the Commission. This recommendation may endorse the proposal, suggest modifications, or reject it altogether, based on the Commission’s statutes and the need to preserve a level playing field for all political participants. For example, if a state government wishes to seek permission to use government‑run schools as polling stations during an upcoming election, the request cannot be addressed to the Election Commission directly; it must first be submitted to the CEO, who will then forward the matter with his assessment. This procedural safeguard ensures that the Election Commission’s decisions remain insulated from undue political pressure while still allowing state administrations to coordinate effectively with the nation’s chief electoral body.

In any democratic system, the conduct of political parties and individual candidates during an election campaign is governed by a set of normative rules that are intended to preserve social harmony, protect the integrity of public discourse, and keep the focus of the electoral contest on issues of governance rather than on personal or communal animosities. First and foremost, parties and aspirants are prohibited from engaging in any activity that might inflame existing social cleavages or generate fresh hostility among the nation’s diverse groups. This means that speeches, pamphlets, advertisements, rallies, or any other form of campaign communication must not deliberately aggravate tensions between different castes, religious communities, linguistic minorities, or other identifiable social categories. For example, a candidate who were to promise preferential treatment for a particular caste in public employment, or a party that were to vilify a religious minority as a threat to national security, would be violating this principle because such statements could deepen mistrust and provoke communal or caste‑based conflict. The underlying rationale is that elections should not become a battleground for sectarian hate, and that the democratic process must accommodate the pluralistic character of the country without allowing political competition to erode the fabric of mutual respect.

A second, closely related guideline concerns the manner in which parties and candidates may criticize their opponents. While robust debate about policy is a hallmark of a healthy democracy, the law restricts criticism to the sphere of public performance. In practice, this means that any negative commentary must be anchored in an opponent’s policy proposals, election manifesto, legislative record, or concrete actions taken while in office. If a party wishes to point out that the incumbent government failed to deliver on a promised health‑care reform, or to argue that a rival’s economic plan lacks fiscal prudence, such criticism is permissible because it directly addresses public responsibilities. By contrast, attacks that delve into a rival’s private life—such as questioning a candidate’s marital status, personal habits, or family background—are barred unless those aspects have a clear and demonstrable impact on the candidate’s public duties. The intention behind this restriction is to prevent the campaign trail from devolving into a smear campaign that distracts voters from substantive issues and invades personal privacy without justification.

A further safeguard against irresponsible campaigning is the prohibition of unfounded or distorted accusations. Political discourse must be grounded in verifiable facts; therefore, parties and candidates are required to avoid disseminating rumors, unverified allegations, or deliberately misleading information about opponents. For instance, circulating a claim that a rival politician is involved in a financial scandal without any corroborating evidence, or twisting the wording of a past speech to imply extremist sympathies, would constitute a breach of this rule. Election commissions and oversight bodies typically monitor such conduct, and violations can lead to penalties ranging from fines to disqualification, reinforcing the principle that truthfulness and fairness are essential to the legitimacy of the electoral process.

Taken together, these guidelines serve several complementary purposes. They protect vulnerable communities from being used as political pawns, maintain a level playing field where ideas, rather than personal attacks, determine electoral outcomes, and uphold the credibility of the democratic system by ensuring that the information voters receive is accurate and relevant. By insisting that criticism stay within the realm of public policy, by forbidding the exploitation of caste, religious, or linguistic identities for electoral gain, and by demanding that all accusations be substantiated, the electoral framework seeks to foster a campaign environment in which the electorate can make informed choices based on the merits of competing visions for governance, rather than on fear, hatred, or misinformation.

Election law explicitly forbids the use of any place of worship—whether a Hindu temple, a Muslim mosque, a Christian church, a Sikh gurdwara, or any other religious shrine—as a venue for political campaigning or the dissemination of election‑related material. The purpose of this restriction is to preserve the secular character of the electoral process and to prevent candidates from exploiting the spiritual authority, congregational gatherings, or acoustic facilities of such institutions to sway voters. For instance, a candidate may not ask a temple priest to announce his candidacy over the temple’s loud‑speaker system, nor may he distribute pamphlets inside a mosque after the Friday prayer, or hold a rally inside a church hall. In addition to the ban on using religious sites, the law also bars any appeal that stirs caste or communal sentiments in order to win votes. This means that references to a voter’s religious affiliation, caste identity, or communal loyalties cannot be employed as a tactic to secure electoral support, thereby helping to keep elections free from sectarian and identity‑based manipulation.

In the conduct of elections, the filing of a nomination form at the Returning Officer’s office is governed by strict procedural rules that are designed to preserve order, ensure security and prevent any form of undue influence or intimidation.  Accordingly, a candidate is not permitted to approach the office accompanied by a procession or any large public gathering; the presence of a crowd would contravene the regulations that limit movement in the immediate vicinity of the election‑administrative centre.  The statutes further specify that, within a radius of 100 metres from the Returning Officer’s premises, no more than three motor vehicles may be present at any one time.  This restriction helps to control traffic flow and to reduce the risk of congestion or disruption during the often‑busy nomination‑filing period.  Inside the office itself, the number of individuals allowed to enter is capped at five, a figure that explicitly includes the candidate.  In practice, this means that a candidate may be accompanied, for example, by an election agent, a legal adviser and one or two supporters, but no additional persons may be admitted.  These limits are enforced by election officials on the spot, and any breach can result in the denial of entry or other penalties, thereby ensuring that the nomination process proceeds in a calm, orderly, and legally compliant manner.

In the formal stage of examining the papers that a candidate submits for election, the Returning Officer conducts a scrutiny of nominations at a time that has been duly announced. Only a very limited group of individuals is permitted to be present while this verification takes place. The candidate himself may be in the room, together with his duly appointed election agent, who is responsible for managing the campaign’s administrative affairs. In addition, a single proposer – the person who signed the candidate’s nomination form to attest to the candidate’s eligibility – is allowed to attend. The fourth permitted attendant may be any other person, such as a lawyer, provided that the candidate has given that person a written authorization authorising his or her presence. No other friends, relatives, party workers, media representatives, or observers may be admitted. This restriction is prescribed by Section 36(1) of the Representation of the People Act, 1951, and is intended to preserve the confidentiality and orderliness of the scrutiny process while still giving the candidate a modest opportunity to be represented and to raise any immediate objections.

The protocol governing the employment of motor vehicles by elected representatives, ministers, and other political functionaries who are entitled to state‑provided security is deliberately detailed, because the safety of these individuals must be balanced against prudent use of public resources and the operational needs of the protecting agencies. In practice, any person who has been placed under the protective umbrella of a security establishment—whether a Member of Parliament, a state cabinet minister, a candidate in an upcoming election, or a senior bureaucrat appointed to a politically sensitive post—is normally authorised to make use of a single state‑owned, bullet‑proof vehicle that has been expressly allocated to that individual. This allocation is not a matter of personal preference but is contingent upon a formal directive issued by the relevant security agency, which may be the police, a specialised protective service, or the intelligence apparatus, each of which evaluates the threat environment, the travel itinerary, and the risk profile before sanctioning the vehicle’s deployment. 

The rationale for limiting the entitlement to one bullet‑proof automobile lies in the need to minimise the logistical footprint of the protective detail while still ensuring that the principal can be moved quickly and securely between venues such as legislative chambers, constituency offices, hospitals, or campaign rallies. For example, if a state minister is scheduled to travel from the capital to a remote district to inaugurate a new public works project, the security planners will examine the condition of the roads, the presence of any known hostile groups, and the likelihood of traffic disruptions; if the assessment concludes that a single armoured car, driven by a trained pilot and accompanied by a modest escort, is sufficient, then that vehicle will be issued and the security plan will be codified in a written order that both the minister’s office and the security unit must follow. 

Conversely, the guidelines explicitly forbid the routine use of multiple armoured cars “on standby” unless a higher authority—again, the security or intelligence service—has specifically prescribed such an arrangement because of an elevated threat level. In other words, a minister cannot simply request an extra bullet‑proof sedan for personal convenience or to convey staff members, nor can a political candidate justify a fleet of armoured vehicles on the pretext of campaign logistics when the threat assessment does not warrant it. This restriction helps prevent the unnecessary consumption of fuel, the wear and tear on a limited pool of specialised vehicles, and the potential for public criticism that could arise if taxpayers perceive that elected officials are indulging in excessive security luxuries. 

When the security briefing does call for the use of a bullet‑proof vehicle, the cost of operating that vehicle—principally the fuel expense—is borne by the individual to whom the vehicle has been assigned. This means that if the minister’s armoured car consumes, say, 15 litres of diesel per hundred kilometres on a round‑trip journey of 300 kilometres, the minister must either allocate personal funds or have the expense reimbursed from a designated security budget that is distinct from the general departmental allowances. The underlying principle is that the protective equipment itself is a state asset, but the day‑to‑day operational outlay is treated as a personal responsibility, reflecting the view that the protective service is a necessary entitlement, whereas the variable costs of its use are akin to private vehicle operating costs. 

Beyond the single armoured car, a protective convoy typically includes a small number of ancillary vehicles—such as a pilot car that clears the way, an escort vehicle that monitors the immediate surroundings, and occasionally a medical or communications van—each staffed by trained personnel who are part of the official security detail. The quantity and composition of this carcade are strictly governed by the instructions issued by the security authority, and the rules stipulate that no additional vehicles may be added without a formal amendment to the original directive. This ensures that the convoy remains as lean as possible while still providing the layered security that modern threat environments demand. For instance, a high‑profile candidate traveling through a city with known protest activity might be escorted by a pilot car, the main armoured vehicle, and a rear guard vehicle; however, the addition of a second rear guard or a spare armoured car would only be permissible if the intelligence service issues a specific order citing an escalated risk. 

All costs associated with the operation of these ancillary vehicles—whether they are government‑owned assets or privately hired cars contracted for a particular mission—are covered by the state government. This includes fuel, routine maintenance, driver salaries, and any specialized equipment required for communications or medical emergencies. The financial responsibility of the state for these support vehicles reflects the principle that the protective service as a whole, encompassing both the principal’s armoured car and its accompanying fleet, is a public safety function funded by the treasury. By contrast, the individual’s liability for the fuel consumed by the bullet‑proof car itself serves as a modest check against frivolous use while still ensuring that the individual remains adequately protected. 

An important exception to these general provisions concerns the Prime Minister, whose security arrangements are codified in a separate, highly confidential document known as the “Blue Book.” The Blue Book, produced by the central government’s highest security authority, delineates the specific requirements for the Prime Minister’s protection, including the number and type of armoured vehicles, the size of the escort, and the protocols for air and ground movement. Because the Prime Minister’s security profile is unique—given the national and international ramifications of any threat—the standard guidelines described above do not apply, and any deviation from the Blue Book’s stipulations would require direct approval at the highest levels of government. In summary, the vehicle‑use policy for ministers, political functionaries, and candidates with state‑provided security is a carefully calibrated framework that authorises a single state‑owned bullet‑proof car per protected individual, places the fuel cost of that car on the individual, mandates that any additional convoy elements be strictly limited to what the security agencies prescribe, and ensures that all ancillary expenses are funded by the government, while the Prime Minister’s security remains governed by a distinct set of rules encapsulated in the Blue Book.

In the conduct of an electoral campaign a candidate is free to employ as many mechanised or motorised conveyances as he or she deems necessary to reach voters, ranging from automobiles and vans to buses, trucks and even two‑wheelers such as motorcycles and scooters. The law recognises that modern campaigning often relies on a fleet of vehicles to distribute literature, carry supporters, make public appearances and convey the candidate’s message across a wide geographic area. Nonetheless, this freedom is not unfettered; it is subject to a procedural safeguard designed to maintain order on the streets and to prevent the misuse of transport resources for partisan advantage.  

Before any vehicle is taken onto the road for election‑related purposes the candidate must obtain written permission from the Returning Officer, the official appointed to oversee the conduct of the election in the relevant constituency. The application for such a permit is normally made in advance of the campaign period and must specify the make, model and registration number of each vehicle, as well as the name of the candidate for whose campaign the vehicle will be used. Once the Returning Officer is satisfied that the request complies with the statutory guidelines—such as limits on the number of vehicles, their intended routes, and the avoidance of any intimidation or obstruction of traffic—a formal permit is issued.  

The permit is a physical document and must be displayed in its original form, not a photocopied version, in a conspicuous position on the windscreen of the vehicle. This visibility serves two purposes: it informs police, traffic wardens and the general public that the vehicle is authorised for electioneering, and it provides a ready reference for officials to verify that the vehicle is operating within the bounds of its permission. The permit must clearly show the registration number of the vehicle and the full name of the candidate to whom it is issued, thereby linking each vehicle directly to the individual campaign that is responsible for its use.  

Failure to secure prior approval or to exhibit the original permit can result in the vehicle being detained, the issuance of fines, or even more serious penalties under electoral law, such as the disqualification of the candidate’s nomination. For example, if a candidate were to mount a fleet of decorated motorcycles without the requisite authorization, the Returning Officer could order the motorcycles to be taken off the road and impose a monetary sanction, thereby protecting the integrity of the electoral process. In practice, most seasoned campaign teams maintain a register of all approved vehicles, attach the permits firmly to the windshields, and train drivers and volunteers to display the documents prominently at all times. This systematic approach ensures that the use of transportation aids the democratic exercise rather than undermining it.

In Indian election law a motor vehicle that has been formally cleared by the competent authority for use in a campaign is considered to belong, for the duration of that permission, to the candidate who obtained the sanction. The permission is specific to that candidate’s political programme, itinerary and the particular election. Consequently, if a different candidate—whether from the same party, a rival party, or an independent aspirant—attempts to employ the same vehicle for his or her own canvassing, the act is deemed unlawful. Section 171H of the Indian Penal Code expressly penalises “unlawful campaigning”, and the misuse of a vehicle that has been authorised in another candidate’s name falls squarely within that provision. The statute makes it a criminal offence, exposing the offending candidate and any accomplices to prosecution, possible fines, and even imprisonment. For example, if Candidate A obtains a licence to run a rally‑mobile in a constituency and Candidate B subsequently drives the same vehicle to distribute pamphlets, Candidate B could be booked under Section 171H, and the vehicle may be seized as evidence. This rule is intended to prevent the duplication of campaign resources, maintain a clear audit trail of election spending, and protect the integrity of the electoral process by ensuring that each candidate’s authorised assets are used solely for that candidate’s own political activities.

In the conduct of electoral campaigns in India, any automobile that is employed to promote a candidate or a political party must first obtain the appropriate authorization from the District Election Officer or the Returning Officer. This requirement is stipulated to ensure that campaigning activities are regulated, transparent, and do not give undue advantage to any participant through the misuse of public or private resources. If a vehicle is used for electioneering without securing the mandated permit, it is classified as an unauthorized instrument of campaign activity; the law therefore treats such use as a violation. Under Chapter IX A of the Indian Penal Code— which deals with offenses related to unlawful campaigning and the corrupt use of influence— the unauthorized vehicle can give rise to criminal liability, including fines or imprisonment, depending on the severity of the breach. Consequently, the vehicle must be withdrawn from any further campaign-related functions immediately, and the candidate or party involved is prohibited from employing it again for political promotion until proper authorization is obtained.

The Election Commission of India has clarified that, in general, the premises of schools and colleges may be made available for political rallies, meetings and other campaign activities, provided that a series of safeguards are observed so that the educational mission of the institutions is not compromised and the use of public‑or‑private educational property does not become a tool for partisan monopolisation. This policy applies uniformly across the country with the sole exception of the states of Punjab and Haryana, where the Punjab and Haryana High Courts have issued explicit orders prohibiting the use of educational grounds for electoral purposes; in those jurisdictions the commission’s general permission is therefore superseded by the higher judicial directive. The central premise of the commission’s guidelines is that the academic calendar of the school or college must remain untouched; no class, examination, tutorial or any other scheduled instructional activity may be delayed, cancelled or otherwise disrupted because of a political event. To guarantee this, the management of the institution must give an unequivocal, written no‑objection and the campaign organizers must secure prior written permission not only from the school or college authorities but also from the Sub‑Divisional Officer (SDO) of the district, who acts as the administrative overseer of the process. Permissions are allocated on a “first‑come‑first‑served” basis, a procedural rule that prevents any single political party or candidate from repeatedly occupying the same venue and thereby crowding out competitors; the SDO’s ledger of requests is therefore treated as a public register, and each application is evaluated against the calendar of the institution and the order in which it was received. 

In practice, a political party wishing to hold a rally on a college campus would first approach the college’s principal or governing council, present a request that outlines the proposed date, time, expected attendance and the nature of the activity, and obtain a formal endorsement that the event will not interfere with lectures, laboratory sessions, examinations or extracurricular programmes such as sports meets. The same documentation is then forwarded to the Sub‑Divisional Officer, who verifies that the request complies with the commission’s norms, checks that no court order or local by‑law bars the use of the ground, and finally issues an official sanction that is time‑bound and subject to revocation if any condition is breached. The commission has stressed that any violation of these procedures—such as holding a rally without the requisite approvals, scheduling an event that overlaps with the academic timetable, or allowing a single party to dominate the use of a particular campus—will be treated with the utmost seriousness, potentially attracting penal action, de‑registration of the offending party from the electoral roll, or other administrative sanctions. 

Responsibility for monitoring compliance rests primarily with the Sub‑Divisional Officer, who is required to keep a detailed record of all allocations, inspect the premises before and after the event, and ensure that the venue is restored to its original condition. Should the political gathering cause physical damage—whether to the playing fields, auditorium, classroom furniture, audiovisual equipment or any other infrastructure—the organizing party or parties are obliged to bear the cost of repairs or replacement, and must provide a written undertaking to that effect as part of the permission package. In the absence of damage, the ground must still be handed back to the educational institution in the same state of cleanliness and orderliness in which it was received; any litter, temporary structures, sound‑systems or signage must be removed, and the campus must be left ready for the resumption of normal teaching activities. Failure to do so not only invites a demand for compensation but also risks a formal complaint to the election commission, which may result in the denial of future venue requests for the offending political entity. 

The commission’s approach reflects a broader balancing act between the constitutional right to free political expression and the need to safeguard the sanctity of educational environments. By allowing limited, regulated use of school and college grounds, the commission recognises that political participation is a civic duty and that public institutions can serve as neutral platforms for dialogue, provided that the core function of education is not impeded. At the same time, the explicit exclusion of Punjab and Haryana underscores the principle that judicial pronouncements take precedence over administrative guidelines, ensuring that regional legal nuances are respected. In practice, many states have witnessed successful examples of this framework in action: for instance, a university in Karnataka hosted a series of pre‑poll debates during a five‑day window that was carefully coordinated with the academic schedule, and the campus was restored without incident, illustrating how cooperation between electoral authorities, educational administrators and political parties can yield a smooth, disruption‑free process. Conversely, there have also been isolated incidents where candidates attempted to hold rallies on school premises without securing the required SDO clearance, leading to the rapid withdrawal of the event by police and a subsequent notice from the commission warning of possible disqualification. These cases serve as cautionary tales that reinforce the importance of adhering to the established protocol. 

In summary, while the use of government‑aided, private or government‑run schools and colleges for political campaigning is permissible across most of India, it is contingent upon a strict set of conditions: the academic calendar must remain intact; the institution’s management must explicitly consent; prior written approval must be obtained from both the institution and the Sub‑Divisional Officer; allocations are made on a first‑come‑first‑served basis to prevent monopolisation; no standing court order may forbid the use; any breach will be dealt with severely; the Sub‑Divisional Officer is the accountable officer for monitoring compliance; and any damage caused must be compensated by the political party or parties responsible. By observing these provisions, political parties, candidates and campaign workers can responsibly utilise educational venues as civic spaces without jeopardising the educational mission or incurring legal repercussions.

In many democratic societies, political parties and candidates often rely on specially equipped automobiles to reach voters, broadcast messages, and create visual impact during election campaigns. When a campaign vehicle is fitted with external accessories—such as loudspeakers, illuminated signboards, video‑screen “Rath” (chariots), or other decorative elements—the alteration is not merely an aesthetic choice; it triggers a suite of legal requirements rooted in traffic and public‑order legislation. Under the Motor Vehicles Act, 1988, and the accompanying Motor Vehicles Rules of India, any modification that changes a vehicle’s external appearance, adds equipment that emits sound, or alters its dimensions must be evaluated against safety standards, noise‑pollution norms, and road‑worthiness criteria.  

These statutory provisions are reinforced by a patchwork of local regulations enacted by state governments, municipal corporations, and district authorities, which may impose additional restrictions on the height of signage, the decibel level of amplified sound, and the permissible routes for large campaign convoys. For example, the Delhi Pollution Control Committee sets a ceiling of 85 dB for public address systems operating in densely populated areas, while the Maharashtra Motor Vehicle Rules require that any vehicle carrying a video display be equipped with a fire‑extinguishing system and clearly marked emergency exits.  

Consequently, any vehicle intended for use as a “Video Rath,” a mobile stage with video projectors, or even a simple van equipped with a loudspeaker, must first obtain explicit permission from the competent authority—typically the Regional Transport Office (RTO) or an equivalent state transport department—before it can be deployed on public roads. The permission process generally involves submitting detailed schematics of the proposed modifications, proof of compliance with safety and emission standards, and a fee for inspection. An authorized officer will then inspect the vehicle, verify that the loudspeaker’s output does not exceed the prescribed limits, and ensure that the external fittings are securely mounted so as not to pose a hazard to other road users.  

Only after the issuance of a formal clearance, often in the form of a temporary permit stamped on the vehicle’s registration certificate, can the campaign organizers legally operate the modified vehicle. Failure to secure such permission can result in penalties ranging from fines to the seizure of the vehicle, and may also lead to criminal prosecution if the unapproved modifications cause accidents or public disturbances. Thus, while external fitting and modification of campaign vehicles are permissible, they are strictly regulated to balance the right to political expression with road safety, environmental protection, and public order.

In India, the use of government‑owned lodging facilities such as rest houses, dak bungalows, circuit houses and other similar guest accommodations is strictly regulated during an election‑related period so that the democratic process is not compromised by the unfair monopolisation of state resources. The Model Code of Conduct (MCC), which comes into force as soon as the election schedule is announced, expressly forbids any political party, its candidates or their supporters from converting these public guest houses into campaign offices or from using them as venues for public meetings whose sole purpose is to disseminate election propaganda. The rationale behind this restriction is twofold. First, government‑run accommodation is intended for the temporary stay of officials, employees and travellers who are on official duty; allowing a party in power to occupy a rest house for an extended period would give that party an undue advantage by providing it with a free, centrally located, well‑maintained space that could be transformed into a de‑facto campaign headquarters. Second, the principle of a level playing field requires that all parties—whether they are in government, the opposition, or independent candidates—have equal access to state‑owned facilities, and that no single group can claim exclusive use of such premises for political purposes.

According to the MCC, a rest house, dak bungalow or circuit house may be used only for its ordinary purpose of providing boarding and lodging to government functionaries who are travelling for official business. The accommodation may be allotted to a minister, a senior bureaucrat, a member of a statutory body or any other authorised official, but the stay must be limited to the duration strictly required for the transit or official engagement. For example, if a district collector is passing through a town on official business, the district’s circuit house may be booked for a night or two, after which the official must vacate the premises. The rules also stipulate that no political meeting—no matter how informal—may be held inside the guest house, even if the gathering is limited to a handful of party workers or supporters. The presence of a political gathering within the walls of a government‑owned building would be regarded as a breach of the MCC because it would amount to the use of public property for partisan purposes.

The code further limits the number of vehicles that may enter the compound of a government guest house in connection with an accommodation allotment. Only the vehicle that is officially registered for the person staying in the guest house and, at most, two additional vehicles are permitted to enter the premises. This restriction is designed to prevent a situation in which a large convoy of party workers, campaign staff and supporters uses the guest house as a logistical hub, parking numerous cars and vans on the government’s property. By capping vehicle access, the election authorities aim to keep the premises strictly for the personal use of the allotted official and to avoid any perception that the state is providing logistical support to a political campaign.

Another important provision concerns the maximum period that a single individual may occupy a room in a government guest house. The stay must not exceed 48 hours, and the accommodation must be vacated at the end of that period unless a fresh, separate official requirement is documented. This 48‑hour ceiling prevents a party from repeatedly extending the stay of a candidate or a campaign manager under the pretext of “official business,” thereby turning the guest house into a quasi‑permanent campaign base. In practice, if a candidate wishes to remain in a town for a prolonged period during the election, the candidate must make separate arrangements, such as renting a private hotel or hostel, because the government‑owned facilities are not to be used for that purpose.

Finally, the code imposes a blanket freeze on the allocation of any such accommodation within 48 hours of the scheduled close of poll in a particular constituency or polling area. Once the final phase of voting is imminent, the election commission orders that no new allotments of rest houses, dak bungalows or circuit houses be made, and any existing allocations are to be terminated. This “freeze” ensures that no party can gain a last‑minute logistical advantage by securing a government‑run guest house for its supporters or candidates just before the polls close. The freeze remains in effect until the counting of votes is completed, after which the normal process of allocating government accommodation resumes.

Taken together, these provisions form a comprehensive framework that safeguards public assets from partisan exploitation during elections. They reinforce the principle that government‑owned facilities are to serve the public interest and the legitimate administrative needs of officials, not the electoral ambitions of any political entity. By enforcing limits on the purpose, duration, vehicle access and timing of use, the Model Code of Conduct seeks to preserve the fairness and integrity of the electoral process, ensuring that all parties compete on an even playing field and that the state’s resources remain neutral and available to the public at large.

The chartering of government‑owned aircraft and helicopters—whether operated directly by a central ministry, an agency such as the Air Force, or a public‑sector undertaking (PSU) that provides aviation services—is permitted for political parties, individual candidates, and even private entities, but it is subject to a strict set of procedural safeguards designed to preserve the principle of equal treatment, to prevent the appearance of partisan favoritism, and to ensure that public assets are used in a transparent, accountable manner. First and foremost, the rule of non‑discrimination requires that the governing party may not enjoy any advantage over opposition parties or over independent candidates when they request the use of a state‑owned aircraft; the same set of eligibility criteria, pricing schedule, and contractual terms must apply uniformly to every applicant, irrespective of the political affiliation, electoral strength, or geographic base of the requestor. In practice this means that a ruling party that wishes to fly its senior officials from Delhi to a campaign rally in a remote constituency must submit a request through the same administrative channel that an opposition party would use to transport its campaign manager to the same destination, and both parties would be quoted the identical per‑hour or per‑kilometre rate that the government has published for the charter of that particular aircraft type.

Payment for the service is the responsibility of the party or candidate requesting the charter, and the transaction must be recorded in a formal ledger that is maintained by the authorized aviation authority—typically the Directorate General of Civil Aviation (DGCA) or the dedicated charter office within the ministry that owns the fleet. The record‑keeping requirement is not merely bureaucratic; it creates an audit trail that can be examined by parliamentary committees, the Comptroller and Auditor General, or any other oversight body to verify that the charge was settled in full, that no subsidy was inadvertently granted, and that the aircraft was not used for purposes beyond the scope of the charter (such as personal travel unrelated to the campaign). For example, if the Bharatiya Janata Party (BJP) were to charter a C‑130 transport plane for a three‑day rally tour, the party would be required to furnish a bank receipt, a signed charter agreement, and a detailed flight log showing dates, routes, passenger manifests, and fuel consumption, all of which would be entered into the central register and made available for inspection upon request.

Uniformity in rates and conditions is another cornerstone of the policy. The government publishes a schedule of charter rates that takes into account factors such as aircraft type, fuel price fluctuations, crew allowances, and maintenance overheads; these rates are applied without modification to any political user. This eliminates the possibility of “special pricing” that could be used to reward a favoured party or to penalise a rival. Moreover, the terms and conditions attached to the charter—such as the requirement to provide advance notice, the need for a security clearance for passengers, and the obligation to return the aircraft in a clean and serviceable condition—are the same for all applicants. By standardising these elements, the administration reduces the risk of arbitrary decision‑making and makes the entire process more predictable for political actors, who can plan their campaign logistics with confidence that the cost structure will not change midway through an election cycle.

The actual allocation of an aircraft is governed by a “first‑come, first‑served” principle, which further reinforces impartiality. When a party or candidate submits a written application—normally through an online portal or a designated liaison officer—the date and exact time of receipt are recorded by the authorized receiving authority, such as the Air Charter Desk at the Ministry of Civil Aviation. This timestamp becomes the decisive factor in determining who gets the aircraft when multiple requests are received for the same time slot. For instance, if the Indian National Congress submits a request at 09:15 a.m. on March 1 for a helicopter to travel to a rural outreach program, and the Aam Aadmi Party submits a competing request at 09:45 a.m. for the same helicopter on the same day, the Congress request will be honoured first, provided the aircraft is available, because it was lodged earlier. In the rare circumstance where two applications are recorded at precisely the same second—a situation that might arise when a large number of parties submit requests simultaneously during a high‑stakes election—an additional tie‑breaking mechanism is invoked: a draw of lots conducted by a neutral officer. This random selection method ensures that no party can claim preferential treatment based on informal influence, and it preserves the credibility of the allocation system.

Finally, there is a built‑in limitation on the duration for which any single party, firm, or candidate may retain a government aircraft or helicopter: the charter cannot exceed three consecutive days. This three‑day cap serves several purposes. It prevents prolonged monopolisation of a valuable public resource, thereby ensuring that other political actors retain the opportunity to use the aircraft for their own campaign activities. It also limits the exposure of the aircraft to wear and tear, reduces the risk of maintenance backlogs, and curtails any potential perception that a party is “buying” the prestige of flying on a state‑owned jet for an extended period. As an illustrative example, if a regional party in Tamil Nadu wishes to use a government‑operated light transport aircraft for a three‑day series of rallies across three districts, it must complete its itinerary and return the aircraft by the end of the third day; any further use would require a fresh application, which would then be placed in the queue alongside other pending requests. This rule also aligns with broader public‑policy objectives that seek to keep government‑owned assets primarily dedicated to official duties, such as disaster response or inter‑governmental travel, and only secondarily available for political purposes under tightly regulated conditions.

Taken together, these safeguards—non‑discrimination, mandatory payment with documented receipts, uniform pricing and contractual terms, first‑come‑first‑served allocation with precise timestamping, a lot‑draw tie‑breaker for identical submissions, and a strict three‑day usage ceiling—constitute a comprehensive framework that balances the legitimate need of political parties and candidates to access rapid, long‑distance transport with the imperative to protect public assets from misuse, to maintain fairness across the political spectrum, and to uphold the transparency and accountability standards expected of a democratic government.

The display of political material such as posters, placards, banners, flags or any other insignia that represents a political party or a particular candidate on public property is not outright prohibited, but it must be carried out in strict accordance with the statutes and regulations that govern the use of such spaces.  Public property includes streets, sidewalks, government‑owned buildings, parks, public transport terminals and any other area that is owned or maintained by the state or local authorities.  Because these areas are shared by all citizens, the election authorities and municipal administrations routinely issue rules that limit the size, placement and duration of political advertising in order to preserve public order, prevent visual clutter and protect the rights of competing parties.  Consequently, any campaign‑related visual material may be affixed to a public surface only if the relevant municipal by‑law permits it and no prohibitory order—such as a temporary ban issued during a sensitive period of the election cycle—has been issued.  The Election Commission of India has codified these requirements in its procedural guidance, notably in Instruction No. 3/7/2008/JS‑II dated 7 October 2008 and in Instruction No. 437/6/Campaign/ECI/INST/FUNCT/MCC‑2016 dated 4 January 2017.  These documents spell out the permissible dimensions of banners, the need for prior permission from the appropriate local authority, the requirement to remove the material promptly after the election, and the penalties that may be imposed for non‑compliance.  For example, a party wishing to hang a 3‑metre‑by‑2‑metre banner on a municipal wall must first obtain a written clearance from the city’s civic office; if a prohibitory order is in force because a high‑profile polling day is imminent, the same banner must remain uninstalled until the order is lifted.  Observing these rules ensures that political campaigning respects both legal mandates and the collective use of public spaces.

In jurisdictions where local statutes or municipal bye‑laws expressly allow the posting of wall writings, the affixing of posters, and the erection of hoardings or banners on privately owned premises, electoral candidates are still required to observe a separate set of procedural safeguards before they can make use of such spaces for campaign purposes. The fundamental safeguard is the obtainment of the property owner’s written consent. This means that, even though the law may not forbid the physical act of pasting a poster on a shop façade or a residential wall, the candidate must first secure a formal, signed document from the owner that expressly authorises the use of that particular surface for political advertising. 

The written permission must be authentic and unambiguous; a verbal agreement or a casual nod is insufficient. Once the consent has been obtained, the candidate is obligated to provide a photocopy of the permission to the Returning Officer—or to any other election official who has been specifically designated for this purpose—within a strict three‑day window from the date the permission is granted. This submission serves as a verifiable record that the candidate has complied with both local regulatory requirements and the electoral code of conduct, thereby protecting the rights of property owners and ensuring a level playing field among contestants. 

For example, if a candidate wishes to display a banner on the side of a privately owned grocery store, he or she must first ask the store’s proprietor for a written approval, retain the original for his own files, and then forward a photocopy of that approval to the Returning Officer within the stipulated three days. Failure to produce the required documentation in time can result in the removal of the material, a notice of violation, or even disciplinary action under election law. This procedural step, while seemingly administrative, is a crucial component of the broader framework that balances freedom of expression in political campaigning with respect for private property rights and the orderly conduct of elections.

In the conduct of electoral processions, the election authorities have codified a precise set of limits concerning the display of party insignia, candidate symbols, and related promotional material on the various types of vehicles that may be used to carry supporters, campaigners, and candidates themselves. These limits are intended to balance the right of political parties and candidates to advertise their platforms with the need to preserve public order, avoid excessive visual clutter on streets, and ensure that no single vehicle gains an undue advantage through overly large or numerous visual cues. Consequently, the permissible number, dimensions, and placement of flags, banners, and stickers differ according to the class of vehicle, reflecting both practical considerations—such as the surface area available for mounting material—and the principle of visual equity among competing parties.

For two‑wheelers, which include motorcycles and scooters, the regulations are particularly restrictive. A single flag may be affixed, but its dimensions may not exceed one foot in width by a half‑foot in height (approximately 30 cm × 15 cm). This size limitation ensures that the flag remains modest enough to be visible without overwhelming the narrow frame of the vehicle. Banners, which are typically larger rectangular pieces of cloth or vinyl designed to hang from the side or rear of a vehicle, are outright prohibited on two‑wheelers, as their attachment could pose safety hazards, interfere with the rider’s vision, or obstruct the flow of traffic. Nonetheless, the rules allow the placement of one or two stickers of “appropriate size,” a provision that acknowledges the common practice of using small adhesive graphics to convey party logos or candidate photographs. In practice, a campaign rider might therefore attach a compact party flag to the handlebar or rear stand, and complement it with a pair of modest stickers placed on the fuel tank and the side panel, each respecting the size guidelines set by the election commission.

The situation changes somewhat for three‑wheelers, four‑wheelers, and the increasingly popular e‑rickshaws, which are often employed in densely populated urban neighborhoods for their maneuverability and capacity to carry multiple passengers. While banners remain disallowed on these vehicles—again to prevent obstruction of the driver’s field of view and to avoid excessive visual noise—the regulations permit the display of a single flag of a larger size than that allowed on two‑wheelers. Specifically, a flag may measure up to three feet in width by two feet in height (roughly 90 cm × 60 cm). This larger dimension is feasible because the broader bodywork of a three‑ or four‑wheel vehicle provides a more stable and visible platform for the flag, and the increased size can be balanced against the vehicle’s overall visual impact. As with two‑wheelers, one or two stickers of appropriate size may also be affixed, enabling candidates to supplement the flag with additional party branding without breaching the prescribed limits. For example, a campaign van might feature a three‑by‑two‑foot party flag mounted on the rear door, while a pair of stickers—perhaps one displaying the candidate’s portrait and another showing the party’s emblem—could be placed on the side panels.

An additional nuance in the regulation concerns political parties that have entered into pre‑poll alliances or seat‑sharing arrangements. In many electoral systems, especially those with multi‑party coalitions, a single candidate may be jointly supported by two or more parties that have agreed to cooperate. To reflect this collaborative relationship, the rules expressly allow a candidate’s or party’s vehicle to display one flag for each of the allied parties involved in the arrangement. This means that if Party A and Party B have a seat‑sharing agreement, a candidate from Party A may affix both the Party A flag (subject to the size limits above) and a separate Party B flag of comparable dimensions on the same vehicle. The provision ensures that voters can readily identify the coalition nature of the candidature, while still respecting the overall cap on the number of flags and the size restrictions intended to keep the vehicle’s appearance orderly.

These specifications form part of the broader election code of conduct, a legal framework that governs all aspects of campaigning—from the timing of rallies to the permissible volume of public address systems. Enforcement is typically carried out by local police, election officers, and designated monitoring teams, who may inspect vehicles before they join a procession, during the procession, and even after the event concludes. Violations—such as exceeding the allowed number of flags, using banners where they are forbidden, or mounting oversized stickers—can result in warnings, fines, or, in more serious cases, the seizure of the offending vehicle and the issuance of a show‑cause notice to the party or candidate. The rationale behind such strict oversight is to prevent any party from gaining an unfair visual advantage that could influence undecided voters, to reduce the risk of traffic disruptions, and to maintain a dignified, orderly atmosphere during what are often highly charged public gatherings.

In practical terms, campaign organizers must therefore plan their vehicle branding well in advance, ensuring that designers produce flags and stickers that conform precisely to the stipulated dimensions. They must also brief drivers and volunteers on the correct placement of these items, often using templates or marked outlines on the vehicle’s surface to avoid inadvertent oversizing. For instance, a political strategist preparing for a large‑scale rally in a metropolitan area might commission a set of three‑by‑two‑foot flags for the fleet of four‑wheel campaign vans, alongside a pair of 6‑inch‑by‑6‑inch stickers for the side doors, while simultaneously ordering smaller 12‑inch‑by‑6‑inch flags for the accompanying motorbike squad. By adhering to the detailed guidelines, campaigns not only avoid legal repercussions but also project a disciplined and respectful image, reinforcing the democratic principle that while political expression is a fundamental right, it must be exercised within the bounds of fairness and public safety.

In many jurisdictions there is no explicit statutory prohibition against the use of plastic or polythene sheets for printing election‑time posters, banners, and other campaign material, but election officials and environmental authorities alike encourage political parties and individual candidates to forgo such materials whenever possible. The recommendation stems from a growing awareness of the environmental damage caused by single‑use plastics, which can linger in landfills for decades, contribute to urban litter, and pose hazards to wildlife when discarded improperly. During a typical election cycle, thousands of large‑format advertisements are produced, and if each is printed on a non‑degradable polymer sheet, the cumulative waste can be substantial. Consequently, electoral guidelines often advise parties to adopt more sustainable alternatives such as biodegradable paper, cloth banners, or recycled‑cardboard panels, which can be recycled or composted after the campaign ends. Some local governments have even instituted incentive programmes, offering reduced fees for campaign materials that meet eco‑friendly criteria. By voluntarily reducing reliance on plastic sheets, candidates not only comply with the spirit of environmental protection policies but also demonstrate a commitment to responsible stewardship that can resonate positively with environmentally conscious voters.

Section 127A of the Representation of the People Act 1951 imposes a clear restriction on the way electoral material may be produced and disseminated during a campaign. Specifically, any candidate who wishes to circulate a pamphlet, poster, flyer or similar printed item must ensure that the document carries, in a conspicuous manner, the full name and address of both the printer and the publisher responsible for the material. This requirement is intended to promote transparency, allowing voters to trace the source of the information they receive and to hold the parties involved accountable for any false or misleading statements. For example, if a candidate commissions a hand‑out that criticises an opponent’s record on public health, the leaf‑let must display on its front page the details of the printing press and the agency that authored it; omission of these particulars would render the pamphlet unlawful and could subject the candidate to penalties or disqualification. The rule applies uniformly to all forms of printed electoral propaganda, whether distributed at rallies, mailed to households, or posted in public spaces, thereby safeguarding the integrity of the electoral process and ensuring that the electorate is fully informed about the origins of the campaign literature they encounter.

In many democratic jurisdictions, candidates are allowed to wear or distribute modest campaign accessories such as caps, masks, scarves or similar items, provided that the cost of these items is recorded transparently in the candidate’s declared election‑expenditure returns. This accounting requirement ensures that the spending is subject to the same limits and auditing procedures that apply to other campaign outlays, thereby preventing hidden or excessive use of public resources. By contrast, the distribution of full‑size garments—such as sarees, shirts, trousers or other substantial pieces of clothing—by a political party or a candidate is generally prohibited. The rationale behind this restriction is that large‑scale gifting of apparel can be interpreted as an inducement or “bribery” of voters, influencing their choices through material benefit rather than through policy debate or persuasion. For example, while handing out a logo‑printed cap to supporters at a rally is permissible if it is listed in the financial statements, handing out a free shirt to every attendee without such disclosure would likely breach election‑law provisions that seek to maintain a level playing field and protect the integrity of the electoral process.

Section 126 of the Representation of the People Act, 1951 imposes a clear and time‑bound prohibition on the use of visual media for electoral campaigning. Specifically, a candidate or any political party is forbidden from displaying election‑related material through cinematograph, television, or any other comparable apparatus during the final 48‑hour window that ends precisely at the hour fixed for the conclusion of the poll. The purpose of this restriction is to prevent last‑minute propaganda that could unduly influence voters who are about to cast their ballots, thereby safeguarding the fairness and integrity of the electoral process. “Election matter” includes any advertisement, speech, documentary, or visual presentation that promotes a candidate, party, or manifesto. For example, a televised rally broadcast, a campaign film shown in cinemas, or a digital video streamed on a news channel would all be barred if they are aired within the two‑day period leading up to the closing hour of voting. Once the poll‑closing hour has passed, the ban lifts, and candidates may resume normal media activity. Violations of this provision can attract penalties such as fines or disqualification, underscoring the legal emphasis on a level playing field in the moments immediately preceding the election outcome.

The distribution of printed “Stepney Covers” – the small protective sheets used to wrap the spare‑tire compartment of a vehicle – or any comparable printed material that bears the emblem, logo or name of a political party or candidate, is regarded by Indian law as an unlawful act when it takes place during an election period. The prohibition applies even when the material does not explicitly display a party symbol, because the very act of circulating such items can be used as a vehicle for covert electioneering, vote‑buying or the creation of a favorable impression of a particular contender. If the authorities are able to demonstrate that such literature has indeed been handed out, the District Administration is empowered to lodge a formal complaint before the local area magistrate. The complaint invokes Section 171 B of the Indian Penal Code, which criminalises the act of influencing voters through intimidation, undue influence or other illicit means. Under this provision, the magistrate may order the seizure of the offending material, impose fines or even initiate criminal proceedings against the distributors, thereby reinforcing the broader legal framework that seeks to ensure a free and fair electoral process.

Temporary campaign offices that are established by a political party or an individual candidate are subject to a set of statutory and administrative restrictions designed to preserve public order, protect the rights of other institutions, and ensure an equitable electoral environment. First and foremost, such offices may not be erected through any form of encroachment on land that belongs to the government or to private owners; the principle behind this prohibition is to prevent the misuse of public assets and to avoid disputes over property rights. Likewise, the placement of a campaign office within places of worship, on the premises of religious institutions, or on the campuses that are attached to them is expressly forbidden, reflecting a respect for the secular character of the electoral process and for the sanctity of religious spaces. 

Equally important is the requirement that a temporary office must not be situated in immediate proximity to facilities that serve educational or health‑care functions. The regulations therefore bar the establishment of a campaign centre contiguous to any school, college, university, or hospital, recognizing that the presence of partisan activity could disrupt the primary mission of these institutions, interfere with the concentration of students and patients, or create an appearance of undue influence. In addition, a buffer zone of at least two hundred metres must be maintained between any temporary office and an existing polling station; this distance is intended to prevent voter intimidation, reduce the likelihood of confusion on election day, and preserve the neutrality of the polling venue. 

When it comes to visual displays, the law permits each temporary office to exhibit only a single party flag and a banner that incorporates the party’s official symbol or photographs of its candidates. The banner’s dimensions are capped at four feet in height by eight feet in width, a size that balances visibility with the need to avoid visual clutter in public areas. Nonetheless, this ceiling is not absolute; if the municipal or state authorities have enacted more restrictive standards concerning the size of banners, hoardings, or similar signage, the stricter local rule automatically overrides the general limit. These layered provisions collectively ensure that while political parties and candidates can communicate their platforms to the electorate, they must do so within a framework that safeguards the rights of other citizens, respects institutional boundaries, and upholds the integrity of the electoral process.

The legal framework that governs electoral conduct in India stipulates that, once the official campaign period has been closed – a phase that commences 48 hours before the scheduled conclusion of polling – any political functionary who has travelled to a constituency from outside its boundaries and who does not appear on the electoral roll of that constituency must vacate the area without delay. This provision is intended to prevent the lingering presence of non‑resident party workers, strategists, or volunteers from exerting undue influence on voters, disrupting public order, or creating the appearance of an unfair advantage after the electorate has already cast its ballots. The rule therefore obliges such outsiders to leave the constituency immediately after the campaign period ends, ensuring that the environment remains neutral and that the focus shifts from active canvassing to the counting of votes. 

The restriction applies specifically to individuals who are not registered voters in the constituency in question. For example, a campaign manager hired from another state, a cadre of party activists dispatched to assist with last‑minute voter mobilisation, or a senior spokesperson who has been delivering rallies in the area would all be required to depart as soon as the 48‑hour window closes. This measure is part of the broader Model Code of Conduct enforced by the Election Commission of India, which seeks to maintain a level playing field and to curb practices that could intimidate or unduly persuade the electorate after the formal closing of the poll. 

An important exception to this rule is made for the candidate himself (or herself) and the candidate’s election agent. Even if the candidate or the election agent is not a resident voter of the constituency, they are permitted to remain in the area after the campaign period has ended. Their continued presence is considered essential for overseeing the final stages of the electoral process, addressing any last‑minute logistical issues, and preparing for the counting of votes. In practice, this means that while the party’s broader organisational machinery must withdraw, the principal actors directly involved in the contest are allowed to stay, reflecting the balance that the legal system seeks between preventing external interference and recognizing the legitimate duties of the candidates and their immediate representatives.

In many jurisdictions, the organization of a public assembly or the staging of a procession is not a matter of absolute freedom; it is subject to regulatory oversight designed to maintain public order and safety. Anyone who wishes to hold a meeting—whether in a park, a community hall, a private garden that is open to the public, or any other venue accessible to the public—must first obtain written permission from the relevant police authority. The same requirement applies to any planned procession, such as a march, parade, or religious pilgrimage, which will move through streets or public thoroughfares. The written authorization typically specifies the time, location, expected number of participants, and any conditions that must be observed, such as traffic diversions or noise limits. Failure to secure this prior approval can result in the denial of the event, legal penalties, or the dispersal of participants by law‑enforcement officers.

In many jurisdictions, the use of loudspeakers at public gatherings—such as meetings, marches, processions, or for disseminating political or commercial messages—is regulated by law, and individuals or organizations must secure prior written permission from the relevant police or municipal authorities before amplifying sound in a public space. This requirement is intended to balance the right to free expression with the need to maintain public order, prevent excessive noise that could disturb residents or interfere with emergency communications, and ensure that the proposed activity does not pose a safety risk or cause undue congestion. Applicants are typically required to submit a detailed request that specifies the location, date, time, duration, and technical specifications of the loudspeaker system, as well as any planned crowd‑control measures; the police then assess the request in light of traffic flow, nearby institutions such as schools or hospitals, and existing noise ordinances. Failure to obtain the mandated written clearance can result in the seizure of equipment, the issuance of fines, or even criminal charges, especially if the unapproved amplification leads to public disturbance or obstructs law‑enforcement duties. Consequently, organizers of rallies, religious assemblies, cultural festivals, or political campaigns are advised to consult the local police department well in advance, obtain the necessary authorization, and display the permit at the venue to demonstrate compliance with the statutory framework.

In many municipalities and residential areas the operation of public‑address systems, portable megaphones, and other loudspeaker devices is subject to specific temporal restrictions designed to protect the public’s right to quiet during nighttime hours. Typically, the law forbids the use of such amplification equipment from 10 p.m. until 6 a.m., a period commonly defined as “quiet hours.” This prohibition helps to reduce noise pollution that can disturb sleep, impede concentration, and interfere with the normal activities of neighbors. For example, a street vendor who wishes to attract customers with a loudspeaker must cease broadcasting at 10 p.m.; likewise, a community organization planning an outdoor rally must ensure that any amplified announcements are concluded before the night‑time cutoff. Violations of the 10 p.m.–6 a.m. rule can result in fines or other enforcement actions, underscoring the importance of observing the designated time window for loudspeaker use.

In the conduct of elections in India, the law draws a clear line around the times at which political parties and candidates may address the public through rallies, meetings and processions.  Section 126 of the Representation of the People Act, 1951, stipulates two distinct temporal restrictions that are designed both to preserve public order and to give voters a quiet period for reflection before they cast their ballots.  First, any public meeting – defined under the Act as a gathering of voters for the purpose of political discussion, persuasion or propaganda – may not be convened after ten o’clock at night or before six o’clock in the morning.  This night‑time ban, which applies on every day of the election cycle, is intended to reduce noise disturbances, prevent late‑night crowding in public spaces and limit the potential for disorder that can arise when large groups are assembled under the cover of darkness.  Consequently, a rally that begins at nine thirty‑seven in the evening must be wrapped up and the venue cleared by ten o’clock, while a gathering that wishes to start at five thirty in the morning must wait until the six‑o’clock threshold is reached.

The second, and more restrictive, limitation comes into force in the final two days leading up to the conclusion of the poll.  According to the same provision, no candidate or political party is permitted to hold any public meeting or procession during the 48‑hour window that ends exactly at the hour fixed for the poll’s close on the designated election day.  The purpose of this “cooling‑off” period is to give the electorate an uninterrupted interval in which to contemplate the choices before them, free from the immediate influence of fresh campaigning.  For instance, if a constituency’s polling is scheduled for the 15th of July and the polling stations are open from eight in the morning until five in the afternoon, the deadline for the last permissible rally or procession would be five p.m. on the 13th of July.  Any attempt to organize a meeting after that moment would be in breach of the Act and could attract legal penalties, including the issuance of a notice to desist or even the cancellation of the event.

These rules operate alongside other electoral safeguards, such as the requirement for prior permission from the Returning Officer for any public gathering, the imposition of a maximum audience size, and the need to maintain law‑and‑order arrangements.  By combining the nightly curfew with the 48‑hour pre‑poll prohibition, the legislation seeks to balance the democratic right to free speech and assembly with the equally important need to ensure a peaceful, orderly, and fair electoral process.  The practical effect is that political parties must plan their campaign calendars carefully, scheduling the bulk of their mass rallies well before the final two days and wrapping up any evening events before ten at night, thereby providing voters with a clear, undisturbed window in which to make their informed decision at the ballot box.

In many electoral systems, the distribution of unofficial identity slips to voters is governed by clear procedural guidelines that are intended to preserve the neutrality of the voting environment and to prevent the undue influence of political parties or individual candidates. According to the prevailing regulations, an unofficial identity slip must be printed on plain white paper and may include only the essential data that identifies the voter and the polling location. Specifically, the slip should list the voter’s full name, the serial number assigned to the voter in the official electoral roll, the part number (or constituency segment) in which the voter is registered, the sequential number that appears on the roll, the name of the polling station to which the voter is assigned, and the date on which the poll is to be held. No additional markings are permitted; the slip must be free of any candidate‑related information. In practice, this means that the name of any political party or individual candidate, the candidate’s photograph, and the official election symbol associated with a party must all be omitted from the document. The purpose of these restrictions is to ensure that the slip serves solely as a factual reference for the voter’s identity and voting venue, without becoming a vehicle for campaign propaganda or voter intimidation. For example, if a voter named Priya Sharma, serial number 123456, part No. 7, s.no. 45, is assigned to the Riverbank Community Center, her slip would simply read “Priya Sharma – Serial No. 123456 – Part 7 – S.No. 45 – Riverbank Community Center – Poll Date: 15 April 2026,” and would contain no mention of the Bharatiya Janata Party, a photograph of any candidate, or the party’s lotus symbol. By adhering to these standards, electoral authorities help maintain a level playing field and reinforce the principle that the ballot, not ancillary paperwork, is the arena for political expression.

In the conduct of elections, a candidate is prohibited from naming a Minister, Member of Parliament, Member of the Legislative Assembly, Member of the Legislative Council or any other individual who is entitled to personal security as his election agent, polling agent or counting agent. This restriction is rooted in the fundamental need to preserve the integrity of the polling environment and to safeguard the security arrangements that have been put in place for protected persons. Ministers and other high‑profile office‑holders are routinely assigned a cadre of trained security personnel—often drawn from police forces, the Central Reserve Police Force, the Indo‑Tibetan Border Police, or other specialized agencies—who are authorized to accompany their protectees only within a narrowly defined “secure perimeter.” The Election Commission of India, in its Model Code of Conduct and related procedural manuals, delineates a 100‑metre radius around every polling station that is termed the “Polling Station Neighbourhood.” Within this zone, as well as inside the actual voting booths, the precinct of the counting centre and the broader campus of the counting hall, security officers are expressly forbidden from entering, even in the course of escorting a protected individual. The rationale is two‑fold: first, the presence of a VVIP‑level security detail could be perceived as an undue influence on voters or poll officials, thereby compromising the principle of a free and fair election; second, the very act of moving a protected person into a crowded, often chaotic polling place would place the security personnel in a position where they could neither perform their protective duties effectively nor maintain the strict neutrality demanded by the electoral process. Consequently, a candidate cannot rely on a minister or any other person who enjoys such protection to act as his election agent, because the requisite security detail would be required to remain outside the 100‑metre buffer, leaving the protected individual effectively unguarded and exposing both the individual and the election staff to unnecessary risk. Moreover, the law is explicit that an individual who is currently under security cover may not simply “surrender” that cover for the purpose of serving as an agent; the protective assignment is a standing order issued by the relevant security agency and cannot be revoked at the candidate’s discretion. For example, if a senior minister who normally travels with an escort of three police officers were to be appointed as a polling agent, those officers would be compelled to stay outside the polling‑station neighbourhood, thereby stripping the minister of the protection that the government has deemed essential. Conversely, allowing the minister to bring his escort inside would breach the Election Commission’s regulations and could lead to allegations of intimidation or even legal challenges to the validity of the poll. This restriction applies uniformly regardless of the political party to which the candidate belongs, and it also extends to former officials who retain security detail on a temporary basis. By enforcing this rule, the election authorities aim to keep the polling and counting venues free from any semblance of official coercion, to protect the personal safety of all participants, and to ensure that the democratic process proceeds without the complications that arise when individuals under special protection are drawn into the highly sensitive precincts of the ballot‑box and the tallying tables.

The authority responsible for granting road‑transport permits to “Star Campaigners” – that is, the senior leaders of political parties who are entitled to the special benefit provided under Section 77(1) of the Representation of the People Act, 1951 – is the Chief Electoral Officer (CEO) of the State.  The Representation of the People Act, 1951 is the principal piece of legislation governing the conduct of elections in India; Section 77(1) specifically authorises the State Election Commission, through its chief electoral officer, to issue permits that allow designated political figures to use motor vehicles for election‑related travel without being subject to the usual commercial licensing restrictions.  The purpose of this provision is to facilitate the rapid movement of high‑profile candidates and campaign managers across large constituencies, especially during the intense period of pre‑poll and poll‑day campaigning, while still maintaining a measure of regulatory oversight.

When a political party wishes to employ a single vehicle for the entire state – for example, a limousine that will ferry the party president from one rally site to another across districts – the party must submit an application to the CEO’s office.  Upon approval, the CEO issues a centrally authorised permit that is valid for the designated vehicle throughout the State.  The permit must be conspicuously affixed to the windscreen of the vehicle, typically in the form of a laminated card or a metal plate bearing the official seal, the name of the party, the vehicle registration number, and the period of validity.  This visible marking serves two functions: it alerts traffic police and other road‑users that the vehicle is exercising a statutory privilege, and it provides a quick reference for law‑enforcement officials to verify that the vehicle is operating within the scope of the permit.

In many electoral contests, parties prefer to deploy several smaller vehicles – such as SUVs, sedans, or even motorbikes – each assigned to a different leader who will campaign in a distinct geographic zone.  In such cases, the central authority still remains the Chief Electoral Officer, but the permits are issued on an individual‑leader basis rather than for a single vehicle covering the whole state.  The party submits separate applications naming each leader and the specific vehicle that will be used by that leader.  Once approved, each leader receives a distinct permit that must be displayed prominently on the windscreen of his or her own vehicle.  For instance, if the state’s opposition leader is travelling in a white Toyota Corolla while the party’s youth wing chief uses a black Mahindra Scorpio, each vehicle will carry its own CEO‑issued permit, thereby ensuring that every campaign vehicle is identifiable and compliant with the legal framework.

The procedural steps for obtaining these permits are designed to be transparent and time‑sensitive.  A party typically files a written request to the CEO’s office, accompanied by copies of the vehicle registration certificate, a declaration of the vehicle’s intended use for election campaigning, and the identity documents of the leader who will be in charge of the vehicle.  The CEO’s office then verifies that the vehicle is not already engaged in commercial passenger transport, checks that the leader qualifies as a “Star Campaigner” under the statutory definition (usually a candidate for a legislative assembly, parliamentary seat, or a party president), and finally issues the permit with a unique serial number.  The permit’s validity period is usually aligned with the election schedule – often commencing a few weeks before the official poll dates and expiring shortly after the conclusion of voting, thereby preventing misuse of the privilege beyond the electoral context.

These provisions reflect a balance between the logistical needs of political parties and the regulatory imperative to maintain road safety and order.  By centralising the issuance of permits with the Chief Electoral Officer, the system ensures uniformity across the state, reduces the risk of fragmented or contradictory local decisions, and provides a single point of accountability.  Moreover, the requirement that permits be displayed on the windscreen makes enforcement straightforward for traffic police, who can quickly ascertain whether a vehicle is exercising a statutory exemption or is subject to ordinary traffic laws.  In practice, this mechanism has been employed in numerous state and national elections, facilitating the swift movement of campaign leaders while preserving the integrity of the road‑transport regulatory regime.

In the conduct of elections, the dissemination of polling information is subject to strict timing rules that apply to both opinion polls—surveys carried out before voting day to gauge voter intentions—and exit polls—surveys administered to voters as they leave the polling station in order to capture how they actually voted. The law expressly prohibits the publication, publicisation, or any other form of dissemination of the results of such polls through print media, electronic platforms, or any other communication channel during a defined blackout period. In a single‑phase election, this blackout begins 48 hours before the scheduled closing hour of the poll and continues until the poll closes; no poll results may appear in newspapers, television broadcasts, online news sites, social‑media feeds, or even informal messaging groups during that entire window. The purpose of this restriction is to prevent the release of information that could influence voter behaviour, create a perception of momentum for particular candidates, or otherwise distort the fairness of the electoral process.

When an election is conducted in multiple phases—such as national elections that are staggered across different regions or states—the prohibition is extended to cover the whole series of voting days. In that case, the blackout starts 48 hours prior to the closing hour of the very first phase and remains in effect until the final phase is completed everywhere the election is held. For example, if a general election is scheduled in three phases on March 1, March 8, and March 15, the restriction on publishing poll data would commence at 48 hours before the close of the March 1 poll and would not be lifted until voting has formally ended on March 15, regardless of the state or constituency. This rule also applies when several states hold their elections on the same day but in different phases; the blackout stretches from the start of the first phase to the conclusion of the last.

The rationale behind these provisions is rooted in the principle of a level playing field: voters who cast their ballots later in the process should not be swayed by early results that could create a bandwagon effect or discourage participation among supporters of less‑favoured parties. Moreover, media outlets and political analysts are required to respect the embargo, and violations can lead to legal penalties, including fines or the suspension of broadcasting licences. In practice, election commissions monitor compliance closely, issuing reminders to news organisations and even issuing takedown notices when premature poll data appear online. By enforcing a uniform silence on poll outcomes until the electorate has finished voting, the legal framework seeks to preserve the integrity of the democratic choice and ensure that each voter’s decision is made without undue external pressure.

In any democratic system where election‑related activities are carried out in public spaces, the establishment of an election booth is not a matter that can be undertaken arbitrarily; it is subject to explicit legal and administrative controls. The rule that governs this requirement mandates that the organizer must first secure a formal, written authorization from the appropriate government department or the local authority that has jurisdiction over the proposed site. Such permission may be issued by the national election commission, a state election board, a municipal corporation, a district administration office, or any other body that has been delegated the responsibility for overseeing electoral conduct in the area. The written instrument—usually a letter, certificate, or official order—serves as proof that the booth has been vetted for compliance with zoning regulations, security considerations, and public order requirements.

Equally important is the obligation to keep this document physically available at the booth at all times. The personnel who staff the booth are required to present the permission to law‑enforcement officers, election‑monitoring agents, or any other authorized officials who request it. Failure to produce the document on demand can lead to the immediate closure of the booth, fines, or other administrative sanctions, as the authorities need to verify that the activity has been properly sanctioned. For example, in many jurisdictions a political party wishing to set up a temporary voter‑information kiosk in a city park must first obtain a permit from the municipal parks department; the party’s volunteers must then carry that permit and show it to police if asked. This procedural safeguard ensures that election‑related operations are conducted transparently, do not interfere with other public uses, and maintain the integrity and safety of the electoral process.

In India, the conduct of electoral campaigning is subject to specific spatial limitations in order to preserve the integrity and orderliness of the voting process. Under Section 130 of the Representation of the People Act, 1951, any form of canvassing—such as distributing pamphlets, making oral appeals for votes, or displaying partisan signs—is expressly prohibited within a radius of one hundred metres from a polling station on the designated day of the poll. This restriction is intended to prevent undue influence on voters as they approach the ballot box and to ensure that the environment inside and immediately surrounding the polling place remains free from coercion, intimidation, or distraction. Election officials and police are authorized to enforce the rule, and violators may be warned, asked to relocate, or, in more serious cases, face legal penalties such as fines or arrest. For example, a political party that sets up a loudspeaker stand or hands out leaflets at the entrance of a polling booth would be required to cease those activities and move beyond the prescribed buffer zone. By maintaining this clear demarcation, the law seeks to uphold a calm and impartial atmosphere that allows each elector to cast a vote without external pressure. (Reference: Section 130, Representation of the People Act, 1951).

Under Malaysian election law, any person who is carrying a weapon—whether a firearm, a prohibited knife, or any other implement that falls within the definition of “arms” in the Arms Act 1959—is strictly forbidden from entering or even remaining in the vicinity of a polling station on the designated polling day. This prohibition is codified in Section 134B of the Representation of the People Act 1951, which expressly bans the presence of armed individuals within the “neighbourhood” of a voting venue while the poll is in progress. The term “neighbourhood” is interpreted broadly by the authorities to include the immediate surroundings of the polling place, typically extending to the area required for voters to approach, queue, and cast their ballots without fear of intimidation. The rationale behind the rule is to preserve the integrity and peacefulness of the electoral process, preventing any form of coercion, violence, or undue influence that could arise from the display or use of weapons. Violations are treated as criminal offences, subject to arrest without a warrant, and may attract severe penalties ranging from fines to imprisonment, reflecting the state’s commitment to ensuring that elections are conducted in a secure and orderly environment.

On the day of voting, every candidate who is contesting a parliamentary or a state‑legislative election is permitted by the Election Commission of India to make use of a limited number of motor vehicles in order to facilitate the smooth conduct of his or her campaign activities and to ensure that essential personnel can reach the polling stations in a timely manner.  In a Lok Sabha (House of the People) contest, the entitlement consists of three distinct allocations.  First, the candidate may have one vehicle dedicated exclusively to his or her own personal use throughout the entire parliamentary constituency, which typically encompasses a large geographic area and may include several hundred thousand voters.  Second, a second vehicle may be assigned to the candidate’s election agent – the individual who is legally responsible for supervising the campaign, filing nomination papers, and overseeing the expenditure returns – so that the agent can travel freely across the whole constituency without having to compete for ordinary road space.  Third, the rules provide for an additional vehicle for each assembly segment that forms part of the parliamentary constituency; these vehicles are intended for the use of the candidate’s workers or party volunteers, enabling them to move quickly between polling stations, distribute election‑day instructions, and address any last‑minute logistical issues that arise in the field.  For example, a parliamentary seat that is made up of seven legislative‑assembly segments would allow the candidate to command seven separate work‑vehicles, one per segment, in addition to the two personal vehicles.

The entitlement for candidates contesting a State Legislative Assembly election follows a very similar pattern, but the provision for work‑vehicles is streamlined because the geographic scope of an assembly constituency is smaller.  Here, the candidate is allowed a single vehicle for personal use and a second vehicle for the election agent, mirroring the parliamentary arrangement.  In addition, one vehicle may be allocated for the candidate’s workers or party activists, without the need to multiply the allocation by sub‑segments, since each assembly constituency is itself a single, indivisible electoral unit.  Thus, whether the contest is for a seat in the lower house of Parliament or for a state legislature, the election machinery ensures that the candidate, the agent, and the campaign staff each have dedicated transport, thereby reducing the risk of congestion, enhancing coordination on poll day, and helping to maintain the orderly conduct of the election.

In the conduct of elections, each candidate who is entitled to a government‑provided vehicle may use that vehicle only for his or her own official purposes on polling day. The allocation is made in the candidate’s name precisely to ensure that the transport is dedicated to the candidate’s campaign activities, such as moving between the polling station, the party office, and other election‑related venues. Even if the candidate happens to be absent from the constituency on the day of the poll—perhaps because of illness, a personal emergency, or any other reason—the vehicle cannot be handed over to a supporter, a campaign worker, a family member, or any other individual. The rules governing election logistics are clear that the vehicle remains the property of the candidate for the duration of the election, and any unauthorized use is considered a breach of the electoral code. Violations can attract penalties ranging from fines to the revocation of the vehicle’s allocation, and in severe cases may lead to disciplinary action against the candidate’s campaign. For example, in past elections, candidates who permitted friends or party volunteers to drive the allotted car were admonished by the election commission and required to surrender the vehicle, underscoring the strict adherence expected to these regulations.

On the day that voters cast their ballots, the election authorities impose strict limits on the kinds of motorised transport that may be employed by candidates, their election agents and party‐workers. The rule is that only ordinary four‑wheel, three‑wheel or two‑wheel vehicles may be used; in practice this means any type of car—from compact hatchbacks to full‑size sedans and sport‑utility vehicles—as well as licensed taxis, auto‑rickshaws, manually‑pushed rickshaws equipped with a small motor, and two‑wheeled motorbikes or scooters. The purpose of the restriction is to prevent the misuse of large, specially‑equipped or government‑owned vehicles that could give any party an unfair advantage, to reduce congestion around polling stations, and to simplify security arrangements for the Election Commission. In addition to the vehicle‑type limitation, a ceiling is placed on the number of occupants: no more than five people, driver included, may occupy any such vehicle while travelling on poll day. For example, a candidate travelling in a sedan may sit with his driver, a campaign manager, a photographer and one volunteer, but a sixth aide would have to travel separately. Similarly, a party worker using a motorbike can carry only the rider and a single passenger, and a group of volunteers in a taxi must limit themselves to four passengers plus the driver. These provisions apply uniformly across all constituencies and are enforced by polling officials to ensure that the electoral process remains equitable, orderly, and free from any appearance of undue influence through the use of privileged transport.

Under the law governing Indian elections, a political party or a candidate is not permitted to organise any form of transport for voters to reach or leave a polling station, whether the assistance is offered directly by the party itself or through an intermediary. Section 133 of the Representation of the People Act, 1951 expressly characterises such an arrangement as a criminal offence. The prohibition covers the use of any vehicle—buses, taxis, auto‑rickshaws, private cars, or even informal conveyances such as shared motorbikes—and applies whether the transport is provided free of charge, subsidised, or paid for by the party or its supporters. The rationale behind the rule is to prevent undue influence or inducement that could sway a voter’s choice, thereby safeguarding the principle of a free and fair election. For instance, a candidate who hires a coach to shuttle supporters to the polling booth, or a party official who reimburses a voter’s cab fare, would be violating the statute and could face prosecution, including imprisonment and a fine. The Election Commission routinely monitors such activities and may issue notices, suspend election agents, or refer matters to the courts to enforce compliance. Consequently, any attempt—no matter how well‑intentioned—to facilitate voter transport by a political entity is legally barred and punishable under the Act.

In most democratic jurisdictions the leader of a political party is expressly prohibited from employing private fixed‑wing aircraft or helicopters to oversee or monitor the conduct of voting and the tabulation of results on election day. This restriction is rooted in electoral statutes and regulations that aim to preserve the impartiality, transparency and security of the voting process; the use of private air transport by a partisan figure could be perceived as an attempt to intimidate officials, to gain a tactical advantage, or to create an unequal playing field. Consequently, election codes typically forbid any party official—especially a party head—from flying over polling stations, transporting observers, or using aerial platforms to observe ballot counting, and violations can trigger administrative penalties, fines, or even criminal charges. Instead, parties are expected to rely on legally sanctioned methods such as accredited polling‑station observers, stationary monitoring centres, and publicly available reporting mechanisms to monitor the election in accordance with the law.
