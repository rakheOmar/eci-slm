In the conduct of elections in India, the requirement that a candidate take an oath or affirmation of allegiance to the Constitution is a procedural step that must be completed well in advance of the formal scrutiny of nomination papers. The statutory framework, as laid down in the Representation of the People Act, 1951, mandates that the oath or affirmation be made and subscribed before the date fixed by the Election Commission for the scrutiny of nominations. This timing is not a mere formality; it is intended to ensure that the candidate’s eligibility and commitment to the constitutional ethos are verified prior to the point at which the returning officer examines the technical correctness of the nomination documents. The Supreme Court has repeatedly affirmed this interpretation, most notably in the landmark decisions of *Pasupati Nath Singh v. Harihar Prasad Singh* (AIR 1968 SC 1064) and *Khadar Khan Hussain Khan & Others v. Nijalingappa* (1970 (1) SCC 548). 

In *Pasupati Nath Singh*, the Court was called upon to resolve a dispute in which a candidate had attempted to submit the oath on the very day that the returning officer was scheduled to scrutinise the nomination papers. The bench held that the law was clear: the oath cannot be taken on the day of scrutiny because the statutory deadline for taking the oath runs up to, but does not include, that day. The judgment stressed that allowing the oath to be taken on the scrutiny date would undermine the purpose of the pre‑scrutiny requirement, which is to give the election authorities an opportunity to verify that every candidate who is about to be examined for compliance with the nomination criteria has already satisfied the constitutional pledge. 

A similar issue arose in *Khadar Khan Hussain Khan & Others v. Nijalingappa*, where several aspirants argued that the oath could be administered concurrently with the scrutiny process. The Supreme Court reiterated the principle established in the earlier case, holding that the oath must be made after the nomination paper has been delivered to the returning officer but before the official scrutiny date. The Court explained that the delivery of the nomination paper creates a legal relationship between the candidate and the election machinery, and the oath is a prerequisite to that relationship becoming operative. Consequently, any attempt to postpone the oath until the day of scrutiny would be inconsistent with the procedural safeguards embedded in the electoral statutes. 

These rulings have practical implications for candidates, political parties, and election officials alike. For instance, a candidate who files nomination papers on a Monday must ensure that the oath or affirmation is executed and attached to the nomination packet by the Wednesday preceding the Thursday set by the Commission for scrutiny. Failure to do so can lead to the rejection of the nomination, even if the rest of the paperwork is flawless. Election officers, therefore, are required to check not only the completeness of the nomination forms but also the presence of a duly signed oath that complies with the prescribed timing. In practice, many constituency offices provide a checklist that explicitly notes “oath/affirmation to be submitted before scrutiny date” to avoid inadvertent non‑compliance. 

The rationale behind this chronological ordering also reflects a broader constitutional philosophy. By demanding that the oath be taken before scrutiny, the law underscores the primacy of the candidate’s personal commitment to uphold the Constitution, placing it on an equal footing with the technical qualifications that are examined during scrutiny. This pre‑scrutiny oath acts as a filter that safeguards the integrity of the electoral process, ensuring that only those who have formally pledged allegiance are permitted to proceed to the stage where their eligibility is formally assessed. 

In summary, the procedural rule that the oath or affirmation must be made and subscribed before the date fixed for the scrutiny of nomination papers is firmly established in statutory law and reinforced by Supreme Court jurisprudence. The decisions in *Pasupati Nath Singh* and *Khadar Khan Hussain Khan* leave no doubt that a candidate cannot rely on the day of scrutiny to fulfill this obligation; the oath must be completed after the nomination is lodged but well in advance of the scrutiny deadline. This requirement serves both a practical administrative function—allowing election officials to verify compliance efficiently—and a constitutional purpose—affirming the candidate’s allegiance before any further evaluation of their candidacy takes place.

This openness helps to ensure that the pool of candidates reflects the full spectrum of political choice available to the electorate.

Importantly, the statute expressly excludes any other person from presenting the nomination paper, even if that individual possesses a written authorisation from the candidate or from a proposer. The validity of a candidate’s nomination paper in Indian elections hinges on the meticulous completion of the accompanying Affidavit (Form‑26), a requirement that was emphatically clarified by the Supreme Court of India in its landmark judgment dated 13 September 2013 in *Resurgence India Limited v. Election Commission of India* (WP(C) No. 121 of 2008).  The Court observed that the affidavit, which must be filed simultaneously with the nomination paper, is not a mere formality but a statutory instrument designed to furnish the electorate and the election authorities with a comprehensive snapshot of the candidate’s personal, financial, and legal standing.  Accordingly, every column of Form‑26—whether it relates to the candidate’s assets and liabilities, criminal antecedents, educational qualifications, or any other disclosure mandated by the Representation of the People Act—must be completed in full.  The jurisprudence makes it clear that a blank space cannot be left as a placeholder; if a particular item does not apply to the candidate, the prescribed practice is to enter a specific remark such as “NIL,” “None,” or “Not applicable,” thereby signalling that the candidate has consciously considered the requirement and found no relevant information to report.  

In practical terms, the Returning Officer (RO), who is the statutory officer responsible for the entire nomination‑scrutiny process in a constituency, is obliged to verify the completeness of the affidavit at the very moment it is submitted.  The RO’s checklist includes a line‑by‑line inspection of Form‑26, and any omission—however minor—triggers an immediate procedural response.  The officer must issue a formal notice to the candidate, granting a reasonable period—typically a few days—to furnish a corrected affidavit in which every previously empty column is duly populated with the appropriate entry, be it a numerical figure, a textual explanation, or a standard “NIL” notation.  This notice serves both as a safeguard for the integrity of the electoral roll and as a warning that non‑compliance will have tangible consequences.  

The Supreme Court’s decision further underscores that the duty to correct an incomplete affidavit does not cease with the issuance of the notice.  Should the candidate, either through negligence or willful disregard, fail to submit a revised Form‑26 that satisfies the completeness criterion, the RO is empowered—indeed, mandated—to reject the entire nomination at the stage of scrutiny.  This rejection is not a discretionary act but a direct application of the legal principle that the electoral process cannot proceed on the basis of incomplete or misleading disclosures, which could otherwise compromise the fairness of the contest and the informed choice of voters.  

For illustration, imagine a candidate who submits an affidavit that leaves the column concerning “criminal cases pending against the candidate” blank, despite having no such cases.  In compliance with the Court’s guidance, the candidate should have entered “NIL” in that column.  If the RO detects the blank field, the candidate would receive a notice to amend the form.  Should the candidate simply ignore the notice or submit a new affidavit that still leaves the column empty, the RO would be justified in discarding the nomination, thereby preventing an ambiguous or potentially deceptive filing from entering the ballot.  Conversely, a candidate who promptly writes “NIL” in the same column demonstrates adherence to the statutory requirement and avoids any procedural penalty.  

The broader implication of this jurisprudential pronouncement is that the electoral machinery places a premium on transparency and procedural exactness.  By insisting that no column of Form‑26 remain vacant, the Supreme Court has reinforced the principle that candidates must present a complete and truthful portrait of themselves, enabling voters to make an enlightened decision.  It also provides a clear operational protocol for election officials, ensuring uniformity across constituencies and reducing the scope for arbitrary rejection of nominations.  In sum, the Supreme Court’s ruling codifies a strict but essential standard: every field in the candidate’s affidavit must be filled, either with substantive data or with an explicit statement of non‑applicability; failure to observe this rule, even after due notice, renders the nomination paper invalid and subject to rejection during the scrutiny process.

In the Indian electoral system a candidate’s declaration of personal and financial particulars is formalised in a statutory document known as Form 26, which must be filed as an affidavit at the time of nomination. The dual nature of the penalty reflects the legislature’s aim to deter both the act of deception itself (through incarceration) and the potential financial gain that may result from it (through a fine).

The court would then examine the evidence, and if it finds that the affidavit was indeed false, it could impose a six‑month term of imprisonment, a fine, or both, consistent with Section 26. Such judicial scrutiny serves the broader democratic purpose of ensuring that those who seek public office do so with honesty and that the electorate can make informed choices based on accurate disclosures.

In the conduct of elections, the filing of documents that accompany a nomination paper is governed by strict time‑limits designed to ensure that the electoral process proceeds in an orderly and transparent manner. The principal document that a prospective candidate must submit is the affidavit prescribed in Form 26, together with any supplementary affidavit that may be required and the ancillary Forms AA and BB, which relate respectively to the candidate’s criminal record, assets and liabilities, and any pending criminal cases. All of these papers must be lodged with the Returning Officer no later than 3 p.m. on the final day allotted for the receipt of nominations. This deadline is not merely a formality; it is the point at which the election machinery freezes the list of candidates and begins to verify that each nominee satisfies the legal qualifications for contesting the poll.

If, after the initial submission, a candidate discovers that a column in the original Form 26 affidavit has been left blank or contains an inadvertent error, the law provides a remedial window. The Returning Officer may issue a notice requiring the candidate to file a revised affidavit that corrects the omission or mistake, and the candidate is then permitted to submit the corrected document up to the moment when the scrutiny of nominations is scheduled to begin. Scrutiny is the procedural step in which the Returning Officer examines each nomination for compliance with statutory requirements—such as the candidate’s age, citizenship, and the absence of disqualifying criminal convictions. By allowing a revised affidavit to be filed up until the start of scrutiny, the system balances the need for accuracy with the practical reality that paperwork errors can occur at the last minute.

Another essential requirement is the taking of the oath of allegiance, which must be completed after the nomination paper has been filed but before the scrutiny date arrives. The oath serves as a solemn affirmation that the candidate will uphold the Constitution and perform duties faithfully if elected. The timing of the oath is crucial because it ensures that a candidate cannot be sworn in without first having satisfied the documentary prerequisites, and it also guarantees that the oath is recorded before any potential objections are raised during scrutiny.

In addition to the personal disclosures, a candidate must furnish a certified extract of the electoral roll for the constituency in which he or she is contesting. This extract confirms the existence of the candidate’s name on the roll and establishes the correct spelling and details that will appear on the ballot paper. The law permits the certified extract to be submitted at any point up to the commencement of scrutiny, giving candidates the flexibility to obtain the document even if they experience a delay in accessing the electoral office.

To illustrate how these deadlines function in practice, consider a hypothetical scenario in which a candidate submits the nomination forms on the final day but forgets to indicate a minor pending civil case in the affidavit. The Returning Officer, upon reviewing the submission, issues a notice on the evening of the nomination deadline, directing the candidate to file a revised affidavit. Because the notice is served before scrutiny, the candidate may file the corrected affidavit that same night, and the nomination will remain valid. Conversely, if the same oversight is discovered after scrutiny has begun, the nomination would be deemed defective and the candidate would be disqualified from the contest.

Overall, the prescribed time‑limits—3 p.m. on the last nomination day for the original affidavits and related forms, the window up to the start of scrutiny for any revised affidavit, the pre‑scrutiny oath, and the flexible deadline for the certified electoral‑roll extract—are integral components of a procedural framework that safeguards electoral integrity while accommodating reasonable corrections. Adhering to these timelines is essential for any aspirant who wishes to stand for election, and failure to comply can result in the rejection of the nomination and the loss of the opportunity to contest.

The open‑ballot procedure is a special voting arrangement that is employed exclusively in elections to the Council of States, the upper chamber of the Indian Parliament, and it applies only to those legislators who are currently serving as Members of the Legislative Assembly (MLAs) and who belong to an organized political party. Unlike the secret‑ballot system that is used for most other elections, the open ballot is designed to provide party leadership with direct, verifiable evidence of how each of its elected representatives has cast his or her vote. This transparency is intended to reinforce party discipline, especially in matters of great constitutional importance such as the election of the Vice‑President, the election of Rajya Sabha members, or votes on confidence motions, where the government’s stability may hinge on the loyalty of its allies.

Under the open‑ballot rule, every political party that has one or more of its members serving as MLAs may designate a trusted individual—referred to as an authorized agent—to act on its behalf during the voting process. The authorized agent is a party functionary who has been formally appointed and whose name appears on a list submitted to the Returning Officer (R.O.) ahead of the election. On the day of polling, the R.O. arranges a specific set of seats inside the polling station for these agents, ensuring that they are positioned in a location that allows them to observe the voting activity of the party’s MLAs without obstructing the flow of the election machinery. The seating arrangement is standardized so that each party’s agents sit together, which also helps the R.O. keep an accurate record of which agents are present and to prevent any confusion or allegations of bias.

When an MLA enters the booth to vote, the process differs from the standard secret‑ballot routine. After the MLA receives the ballot paper and marks the choice—typically by placing a cross or tick next to the name of the candidate or party they wish to support—the MLA must not immediately fold and deposit the ballot. Instead, the MLA is required to present the marked ballot paper to the authorized agent of his or her own party. This step is carried out in the privacy of the voting compartment, but the presence of the agent means that the party has a first‑hand confirmation of the vote’s content. Only after the agent has inspected the ballot and confirmed that it reflects the party’s desired choice does the MLA proceed to insert the ballot into the ballot box. The agent’s verification is recorded informally, and the act of showing the ballot serves as a legal safeguard against any deviation from the party line.

The rationale behind this system is rooted in the Indian constitutional framework, which seeks to balance the principle of individual conscience with the practical need for cohesive party action in parliamentary governance. By allowing parties to monitor the votes of their legislators in the Council of States, the open ballot helps prevent “cross‑voting” or “defection” that could undermine coalition agreements or destabilize a government. It also provides a mechanism for parties to enforce internal discipline without resorting to punitive measures after the fact, because the act of verification is contemporaneous with the vote itself.

In practice, the open‑ballot system has been invoked in several high‑profile elections. For instance, during the election of the Vice‑President in 2022, the ruling coalition used authorized agents to ensure that all its MLA supporters in the Council of States cast their votes for the coalition’s nominee, thereby minimizing the risk of unexpected outcomes. Similarly, in confidence votes that have historically led to the fall of governments, parties have relied on the open ballot to guarantee that their members in the upper house remain aligned with the party’s strategic decisions.

Critics, however, argue that the open ballot compromises the democratic ideal of a secret vote, potentially pressuring legislators to conform to party directives even when they personally disagree. They contend that this could erode the independence of elected representatives and diminish the quality of legislative deliberation. Proponents counter that the unique constitutional responsibilities of the Council of States—such as representing the interests of the states at the federal level—justify a higher degree of party oversight, particularly when the stability of the central government is at stake.

Overall, the open‑ballot mechanism is a carefully regulated procedure that combines party oversight with procedural safeguards. By mandating the presence of authorized agents, providing designated seating, and requiring the display of marked ballots before insertion into the ballot box, the system seeks to achieve a transparent and accountable voting process for the Council of States while maintaining the procedural integrity of Indian parliamentary democracy.

Similarly, if a voter simply refuses to present the ballot to his own party’s agent, the presumption is that the voter may be seeking to avoid any appearance of undue pressure, and the election officials must intervene to ensure that the ballot cannot be used in a way that would undermine the secrecy of the vote.

During the counting process, the Returning Officer must first identify the suspect ballot—typically by comparing the physical characteristics of the ballots that have been set aside for such breaches—and then separate it from the pool of valid votes. This ensures that the illegal act does not inadvertently affect the final result while also providing a transparent account of how many ballots were invalidated for this specific reason.

These examples demonstrate how the procedural safeguards embedded in Rule 39A operate in real‑world contexts to deter malpractice, uphold the principle of a secret ballot, and ensure that any deviation from the prescribed voting process is systematically documented and neutralised.

When a political party decides to contest these indirect elections, it designates certain individuals as “authorized agents” to represent the party’s interests, to coordinate voting strategies, and to ensure that party members cast their ballots in accordance with the party’s official line. The Election Commission of India, which oversees all electoral processes, does not impose any statutory bar that prevents a sitting Member of the Legislative Assembly (MLA) or a minister from being appointed to this role. Consequently, a legislator who is currently serving as an MLA, or even a minister holding a portfolio in the state cabinet, may be formally nominated by the party as an authorized agent for the Rajya Sabha or Legislative Council polls. This flexibility is intended to allow parties to make pragmatic choices, often leveraging the experience and influence of senior legislators to manage the complex logistics of secret‑ballot voting among a large number of electors. Historical instances illustrate that parties have routinely selected senior MLAs and ministers as agents; for example, during the 2018 Rajya Sabha elections in several states, prominent ministers were entrusted with the responsibilities of authorized agents, coordinating the voting process without any legal impediment from the Commission. The absence of a restriction underscores the Commission’s stance that the eligibility to act as an authorized agent is governed by party discretion rather than by any specific legal disqualification.

Section 152 of the Representation of the People Act, 1951 requires every State to keep a current register of all persons who are eligible to vote in elections to the Council of States (the Rajya Sabha). The register, commonly called the “list of electors”, is normally updated at regular intervals, but the statute does not forbid the addition of a name after the formal notification of a Rajya Sabha election or even after the final date for filing nominations. Consequently, when a candidate wins a vacancy in a State Legislative Assembly through a by‑election and the result is declared later than the Rajya Sabha election notification—perhaps because the by‑election was scheduled after the Rajya Sabha poll was announced, or because the counting of votes was delayed—the newly elected Member of the Legislative Assembly (MLA) must still be entered onto the Section 152 list. Once his or her name has been entered, the MLA acquires the same right to cast a ballot in the Rajya Sabha election as any other sitting legislator, provided that the actual polling takes place after the date on which the MLA’s election to the Assembly became effective. The same principle applies to elections of Legislative Council members that are conducted by the MLAs of the Assembly; any MLA who attains office after the notification but before the poll is entitled to participate. For example, suppose a State announces a Rajya Sabha vacancy on 1 April, sets the last date for nomination on 15 April, and schedules polling for 30 April. If a by‑election for an Assembly seat is held on 20 April and the result is declared on 25 April, the victorious candidate’s name will be added to the electors’ list between 25 April and 30 April, and the candidate will be permitted to vote in the Rajya Sabha poll. This procedural flexibility ensures that the composition of the Assembly at the time of the Rajya Sabha election—rather than at the moment of its notification—determines the electorate, thereby preserving the democratic principle that every sitting legislator, regardless of when they entered office, may influence the election of the upper house.

Section 62(5) of the Representation of the People Act, 1951 expressly disqualifies any individual who is detained in a prison—from serving a sentence of imprisonment, being held for transportation, or any other form of custodial confinement—from exercising the franchise in any election. The same restriction applies to persons who are in the lawful custody of the police, whether they are under investigation, awaiting trial, or otherwise detained under legal authority. The rationale behind this provision is rooted in the principle that the right to vote is a civic privilege linked to the status of a free citizen; incarceration or police detention is viewed as a temporary removal of that status, thereby justifying the suspension of voting rights until the person is released. For example, a person serving a five‑year term for a criminal conviction would be barred from casting a ballot in the general elections held during that period, just as someone detained on remand pending the outcome of a trial would be ineligible to vote. The rule applies uniformly across all levels of elections—parliamentary, state assembly, and local bodies—ensuring consistency in the treatment of incarcerated or police‑custodied individuals. Once the detention ends and the individual regains freedom, the disqualification ceases, and the person may again participate in the electoral process in accordance with the same Act.

The Rajya Sabha, which is the upper house of the Indian Parliament, can contain at most 250 members, a ceiling that is fixed by Article 80 of the Constitution of India. Of these, up to twelve individuals are appointed directly by the President of India; these nominated members are selected for their distinguished contributions in fields such as literature, science, art, and social service, thereby ensuring that the house benefits from expertise that may not emerge through the electoral process. The remaining seats—no more than 238—are filled by representatives elected from the states and union territories. These elections are not conducted by a simple majority vote; instead, they employ a system of proportional representation using the single transferable vote (STV) method. Under this arrangement, elected members of each State Legislative Assembly cast ranked ballots, and seats are allocated in proportion to the strength of the parties in the assembly, allowing smaller parties and regional groups to gain representation in the federal legislature. For example, if a state assembly is dominated by two major parties, the STV system still permits a third party with a modest share of votes to secure at least one Rajya Sabha seat. This blend of nominated experts and proportionally elected state representatives gives the Rajya Sabha its distinctive character as a revising chamber that reflects both the diversity of Indian society and the expertise needed for informed legislative deliberation.

The Rajya Sabha, which serves as the upper chamber of the Indian Parliament, is composed of a total of 245 members. Of these, 233 members attain their seats through indirect elections carried out by the elected legislators of the States and Union Territories, using a single‑transferable‑vote system of proportional representation; this method ensures that the political composition of each state’s legislative assembly is reflected in the composition of the Rajya Sabha. In addition, the President of India appoints 12 members on the basis of their distinguished contributions to fields such as literature, science, art, the cooperative movement and social service, thereby bringing specialized expertise into the legislative process. Each elected member serves a six‑year term, with one‑third of the elected seats vacated every two years, while the nominated members also enjoy the same term length. This mixed structure of elected and nominated representatives gives the Rajya Sabha a unique blend of regional representation and subject‑matter expertise, complementing the directly elected Lok Sabha and contributing to the bicameral nature of India’s legislature.

The Rajya Sabha, commonly known as the Council of States, constitutes the permanent upper chamber of the Indian Parliament and, unlike the Lok Sabha, is not subject to dissolution under Article 83(1) of the Constitution of India. Each member of the Rajya Sabha serves a six‑year term, but the house is designed to retain continuity through a system of staggered retirements: approximately one‑third of its members vacate their seats every two years. This biennial rotation is achieved by holding regular elections or, where applicable, by appointing new members to fill the vacancies, thereby ensuring that the house is never completely dissolved and that experienced legislators remain in place to provide institutional memory and stability. The retiring members are replaced by individuals elected by the legislative assemblies of the States and Union Territories, or, in the case of a limited number of seats, nominated by the President for their expertise in fields such as literature, science, art, or social service. By maintaining a continuous yet partially refreshed composition, the Rajya Sabha can deliberate on legislation, review policies, and represent state interests without the disruption that a full‑scale dissolution would cause, distinguishing its role from that of the lower house, which is dissolved at the end of each general election cycle.

The members of India’s upper house, the Rajya Sabha, are not chosen by a direct popular vote but are elected by the elected representatives of the State Legislative Assemblies. This electorate includes legislators from all the states of the Union as well as those from the Union Territory of Puducherry and the National Capital Territory of Delhi, which are treated as “states” for the purpose of this procedure. The constitutional basis for this system is found in Article 80(4) of the Constitution of India, which stipulates that each Rajya Sabha seat must be filled by an indirect election carried out by the sitting members of the respective state legislatures. The method employed is proportional representation through the single‑transferable‑vote (STV) system, a mechanism that allows legislators to rank candidates in order of preference. As a result, the composition of the Rajya Sabha reflects the political balance of the state assemblies at the time of each election. For example, if a state assembly is dominated by a particular party, that party will secure a proportionate number of Rajya Sabha seats, while smaller parties can also gain representation if they achieve the requisite quota of votes under the STV formula. This indirect, proportional system is intended to give the states a voice in the federal legislative process while ensuring a more nuanced reflection of the political diversity within each state’s elected body.

The authority to nominate members of the Rajya Sabha, the upper house of India’s Parliament, rests exclusively with the President of India. Under Article 80 of the Constitution, the President is empowered to appoint twelve distinguished individuals to the Council of States, thereby enriching the house with expertise that may not emerge through ordinary electoral processes. These nominated members are selected from fields such as literature, science, art, the cooperative movement, and social service, reflecting the framers’ intention to ensure that the legislature benefits from a broad spectrum of knowledge and experience. Although the President acts on the advice of the Council of Ministers, the nominations are formally made by the head of state, and the appointed members enjoy the same rights and responsibilities as elected senators, including participation in debates, voting on legislation, and serving on parliamentary committees. This mechanism helps balance popular representation with specialist input, contributing to more informed and diverse law‑making.

Article 80(3) of the Constitution of India sets out the special qualifications required of the twelve members whom the President may nominate to the Rajya Sabha, the upper house of the Indian Parliament. According to this provision, a nominee must possess “special knowledge or practical experience” in fields that are considered to enrich legislative deliberations, specifically literature, science, art and social service. The rationale behind this clause is to ensure that the upper chamber benefits from expert insight that elected representatives, who may be primarily versed in politics or administration, might lack; for example, a distinguished novelist, a pioneering researcher in biotechnology, a celebrated classical dancer, or a veteran activist working for the under‑privileged can bring a perspective grounded in professional accomplishment rather than partisan considerations. The Constitution further imposes a basic age requirement: Article 84(b) states that any person nominated to the Rajya Sabha must be at least thirty years old, a stipulation intended to guarantee a minimum level of maturity and life experience before assuming the responsibilities of a parliamentary role. In practice, the President’s nominations have historically reflected the diversity of Indian society, with figures such as writer Mahadevi Varma, scientist Dr. Homi Bhabha, artist M.F. Husain and social reformer Mother Teresa having been chosen in different periods. These individuals, while not elected by the public, enjoy the same rights and duties as elected members, participating in debates, serving on committees, and voting on legislation, thereby contributing to a more informed and holistic law‑making process. The dual criteria of expertise and age thus function together to balance the democratic element of the Rajya Sabha with a technocratic infusion of knowledge, reinforcing the Constitution’s aim of integrating both representative and advisory functions within the parliamentary system.

In the Indian federal system, the upper house of Parliament, commonly known as the Council of States (Rajya Sabha), as well as the upper chambers of many state legislatures (the State Legislative Councils), are filled through a carefully regulated electoral procedure that includes a nomination phase. One of the essential requirements of this phase is the submission of a list of “proposers” – elected legislators who endorse a candidate’s candidature. The rules distinguish between candidates who are put forward by political parties that are officially recognised by the Election Commission and those who contest as independents or are supported by unrecognised parties. For a recognised party’s nominee, the nomination paper must bear the signatures of a minimum number of proposers calculated in two ways: it must be signed by at least ten per cent of the total members of the Legislative Assembly of the state in which the election is being held, but the number of signatures cannot exceed ten. In practice, this means that if a state assembly has fewer than one hundred elected members, the required proposers will be ten individuals; if the assembly is larger, the ten‑per‑cent threshold will produce a smaller figure, and the lower of the two numbers is applied. This dual ceiling ensures that the endorsement requirement remains manageable even in states with very large assemblies, while still guaranteeing a baseline level of legislative support for the candidate. By contrast, candidates who are not backed by a recognised party – including independents and those nominated by unrecognised parties – must obtain the signatures of exactly ten elected members of the same Legislative Assembly, irrespective of the house’s size. This uniform stipulation prevents the possibility of a single large assembly diluting the scrutiny of independent candidates, while also simplifying the procedural burden for them. For example, in a state such as Uttar Pradesh, whose Legislative Assembly comprises 403 members, a recognised‑party nominee would need the support of at least 40 legislators (10 % of 403), but because the rule caps the number of proposers at ten, only ten signatures are actually required. Conversely, an independent candidate in the same state would still need to secure ten distinct signatures from sitting MLAs. These provisions are designed to balance the need for broad legislative endorsement with practical considerations of administrative efficiency, thereby upholding the integrity of the indirect election system for the Council of States and State Legislative Councils.

Under the provisions of the Representation of the People Act, 1951, a candidate contesting a Lok Sabha or State Assembly seat is permitted to submit no more than four separate sets of nomination papers for the same constituency. This ceiling is stipulated in sub‑section (6) of section 33 of the Act, and is reinforced by sub‑section (2)(g) of section 39, which together make clear that only four nomination documents may be presented either by the candidate personally or by an authorized representative on the candidate’s behalf. The restriction is designed to prevent frivolous or excessive filing that could clutter the electoral process, while still allowing a modest margin for genuine clerical errors or the need to replace a defective form. For example, if a candidate’s first three nominations are rejected due to minor technical lapses—such as an incorrect declaration or a missing signature—there remains one additional opportunity to correct the mistake without exceeding the statutory limit. Consequently, any attempt to submit a fifth set of papers would be automatically disallowed, ensuring that the electoral administration can manage nominations efficiently and maintain the integrity of the candidacy filing procedure.

In the formal process of standing for elected office, the nomination paper—a document that certifies a candidate’s intent to contest the election—must be delivered to the Returning Officer, the official charged with overseeing the conduct of the poll in a given constituency. The law permits this delivery to be made either by the candidate himself or by any one of the individuals who have agreed to act as a proposer for that candidate. Proposers are typically respected members of the community who attest, by signing the nomination form, that they support the candidate’s eligibility; in many jurisdictions a minimum of two or three such endorsements is required. Thus, if a candidate is unable to appear in person—perhaps because of illness, travel commitments, or other obligations—a trusted proposer may submit the paperwork on the candidate’s behalf, ensuring that the nomination is lodged correctly and on time. This flexibility helps maintain the smooth functioning of the electoral timetable while preserving the integrity of the candidacy verification procedure.

In elections to the Rajya Sabha, the upper house of the Indian Parliament, every aspirant who wishes to stand for a seat must first furnish a monetary security deposit. The statutory requirement, laid down in Section 34 read together with Section 39(2) of the Representation of the People Act, 1951, mandates a deposit of ten thousand rupees (Rs. 10,000) for each candidate. This provision was reinforced and brought into force by the Representation of the People (Amendment) Act, 2009, which took effect on 1 February 2010, thereby standardising the amount across all contests for the Council of States and for the Legislative Councils of the states. The deposit serves a dual purpose: it deters frivolous or non‑serious candidatures and provides a financial guarantee that can be forfeited if the candidate fails to secure a prescribed proportion of votes, typically one-sixth of the total valid votes cast. An important concession is built into the law for candidates belonging to the Scheduled Castes (SC) or Scheduled Tribes (ST); recognising the historic disadvantages faced by these communities, the required security is halved to five thousand rupees (Rs. 5,000). For example, a seasoned politician from a mainstream party would submit the full Rs. 10,000 deposit, whereas an SC‑reserved constituency candidate would be required to deposit only Rs. 5,000, reflecting the legislation’s intent to promote inclusive participation while maintaining electoral integrity.

The Constitution of India provides that the members of a State Legislative Assembly acquire the status of legislators the moment the assembly is formally constituted, a step that is effected by a notification of the Election Commission under Section 73 of the Representation of the People Act, 1951.  This legal moment of “constitution” is distinct from the ceremonial act of taking the oath or affirmation prescribed in Article 188 of the Constitution, which is required only before a member may sit and vote in the Assembly itself.  The practical consequence of the Supreme Court’s interpretation, first articulated in the 1984 decision *Pashupati Nath Sukul v. Nem Chand Jain* (AIR 1984 SC 399), is that once the election results have been declared and the Election Commission has issued its notification, the successful candidates are deemed to be members of the Assembly for all purposes that do not involve the performance of legislative business.  Accordingly, they are entitled to engage in any “non‑legislative” activity that the Constitution or statutory law permits, including participation in the electoral college that chooses members of the Council of States (Rajya Sabha).  In other words, even though a newly elected MLA has not yet taken the prescribed oath, he or she may cast a vote in a Rajya Sabha election, may be counted among the electors, and may also fulfil the ancillary role of proposer or seconder for a candidate’s nomination paper.  

The Court’s reasoning was grounded in the principle that the democratic legitimacy of an elected representative begins with the expression of the electorate’s will, as reflected in the official notification, and not with the later formalities of oath‑taking.  This view was reiterated more than a decade later in *Madhukar Jetly v. Union of India* (1997 (II) SCC III), where a bench of the Supreme Court affirmed that the same rule applied uniformly to all states and Union Territories, thereby giving a clear and consistent answer to any doubt about the eligibility of freshly elected legislators to take part in the Rajya Sabha electoral process.  

The practical upshot of these rulings is evident in the everyday functioning of India’s bicameral parliamentary system.  For example, when a state legislature is newly formed after a general election, the members—although still waiting to be sworn in—may already be called upon to elect the state’s representatives to the Council of States.  Their votes are crucial, because the Rajya Sabha is elected by a proportional representation system using the single transferable vote, and the composition of the Assembly directly determines the balance of power in the upper house.  Moreover, the same members may act as proposers for the nomination of Rajya Sabha candidates, a procedural requirement that demands at least one proposer and one seconder who are themselves electors.  By allowing newly elected legislators to perform these functions before taking their oaths, the Supreme Court ensures that the constitutional machinery does not stall and that the will of the electorate is respected without unnecessary procedural delay.  Thus, the jurisprudence established in *Sukul* and reaffirmed in *Jetly* provides a clear legal framework: an elected MLA becomes an elector for the Council of States and may serve as a proposer for nominations the instant the Election Commission’s notification declares the Assembly constituted, irrespective of whether the oath‑taking ceremony has yet occurred.

The provisions of the Tenth Schedule to the Constitution of India, which are commonly known as the anti‑defection law, do not extend to the situation in which members of a State Legislative Assembly cast their votes by open ballot in elections to the Council of States (the Rajya Sabha).  This principle was articulated by the Supreme Court of India in the landmark judgment rendered on 22 August 2006 in *Kuldip Nayar v. Union of India* (AIR 2006 SC 3127).  The Court observed that the argument that a legislator’s freedom of expression is curtailed by the requirement to vote openly in a Rajya Sabha election is untenable, because the anti‑defection provisions are triggered only when a member voluntarily gives up membership of the party on whose ticket he was elected, or otherwise acts in contravention of the party’s official position in a manner that amounts to “defection”.  In the context of an open ballot, the legislator is merely revealing his personal choice of candidate; the act of voting itself does not, under the Tenth Schedule, create a ground for disqualification from the State Assembly or from the Rajya Sabha.  At most, the party to which the legislator belongs may initiate internal disciplinary measures, such as issuing a warning, withdrawing party support, or, in extreme cases, expelling the member from the party, but none of these actions constitute a legal disqualification under the anti‑defection statute.

The Tenth Schedule, inserted by the 52nd Constitutional Amendment in 1985, was designed to curb political instability caused by frequent party switching and to preserve the integrity of the party system by stipulating that a legislator who votes against the “whip” of his party in a vote on a matter of confidence, a vote of no‑confidence, a money bill, or any other legislation deemed crucial by the party, may be disqualified.  However, the Schedule specifically refers to “defection” in the sense of leaving one party for another or voting contrary to party directives in a manner that undermines the party’s position in the legislature.  The Supreme Court’s interpretation makes clear that the mere fact that the vote is cast openly—i.e., that the ballot is not secret and the choice of each MLA can be seen by party functionaries and the public—does not convert the act into defection.  The open‑ballot system was introduced to promote transparency and reduce the incidence of bribery and coercion in the indirect election of Rajya Sabha members, which is conducted by elected representatives of the State Assemblies rather than by the general electorate.

To illustrate, consider a scenario in which a ruling party holds a slim majority in a State Assembly and must elect three Rajya Sabha members.  If the party issues a whip directing its MLAs to support three specific candidates, an MLA who, for personal or regional reasons, votes for a different candidate in the open ballot will not automatically be disqualified under the anti‑defection law.  The party may, however, view this conduct as a breach of party discipline and could take internal action, such as issuing a notice of reprimand or, if the behavior is repeated, expelling the member from the party.  Nonetheless, the MLA would retain his seat in the State Assembly unless he voluntarily resigns or is otherwise removed through a separate legal process.  Conversely, if the same MLA were to resign from his party and join a rival party, or if he formally declared allegiance to another political group, then the anti‑defection provisions would be triggered, leading to potential disqualification from the Assembly under Sections 2(1)(a) and 2(1)(b) of the Tenth Schedule.

The Court’s decision therefore delineates a clear boundary between the procedural transparency sought by the open‑ballot system and the substantive punitive mechanisms of the anti‑defection law.  While open voting may expose legislators to political pressure and the possibility of party sanctions, it does not, by itself, constitute a statutory ground for removal from office.  This distinction upholds the constitutional balance between the right of a legislator to vote according to his conscience in a transparent manner and the legislative intent of the anti‑defection provisions, which aim to preserve party cohesion without impinging on the internal disciplinary autonomy of political parties.  The ruling continues to guide the conduct of State legislators during Rajya Sabha elections, ensuring that the legal framework respects both the democratic principle of accountability and the structural safeguards against opportunistic party switching.

In the Indian constitutional framework, a person who has been elected to a State Legislative Assembly but whose election has subsequently been annulled by a High Court on the ground of a petition cannot, even while a stay of that decree is in force, take part in the electoral process for the upper houses of Parliament or the State. This principle was expressly reaffirmed by the Supreme Court in its order dated 27 October 1967 in the case of *Satyanarayana Mitra v. Bireswar Ghose* (Appeal No. 1408 (NCE) of 1967). In that decision the Court observed that the conditional stay granted to the legislator – which allowed him merely to sign the attendance register of the Assembly but expressly barred him from engaging in any debate or voting on legislative business – could not be interpreted as conferring any right to participate in subsequent elections for the Rajya Sabha (the Council of States) or for a State Legislative Council. Consequently, the member was disqualified from both proposing a candidate’s name and casting a vote in any such election. The ruling has since become a settled rule of law: no sitting member of a State Assembly whose election has been set aside, even if a temporary stay is in effect, is permitted to influence the composition of the Council of States or a State Legislative Council. The restriction safeguards the integrity of the electoral process by ensuring that only those whose election to the lower house is unequivocally valid may take part in the selection of members to the upper chambers.

Under the Representation of the People Act, 1951, the legal position of a legislator whose election has been invalidated by a High Court, but whose adverse order is subsequently stayed by the Supreme Court, is that the High Court’s decree is treated as though it never came into operation. Section 116B(3) of the Act expressly provides that an order of a High Court setting aside an election “shall, for the purposes of this Act, be deemed never to have taken effect” when a higher court grants an absolute stay of that order. Consequently, the elected representative continues to enjoy the full status, immunities and privileges attached to membership of the legislative assembly, just as if the High Court’s judgment had not been rendered. This uninterrupted status includes the right to take part in any subsequent electoral process that is open to sitting members, such as voting in, or contesting, elections to the Council of States (Rajya Sabha) or to a State Legislative Council where such a body exists.  

The practical effect of the Supreme Court’s stay is therefore to preserve the member’s eligibility to exercise all functions of an MLA, including the ability to cast a ballot for the election of members to the upper houses of Parliament or the state’s bicameral legislature. For instance, if a Member of the Legislative Assembly from Uttar Pradesh had his election annulled by the Allahabad High Court, but the Supreme Court subsequently stayed that order, he would remain entitled to vote in the Rajya Sabha poll for Uttar Pradesh and could also stand as a candidate for the Uttar Pradesh Legislative Council, should he wish. The jurisprudence reflects the principle that a higher court’s stay suspends the operation of the lower court’s decree, thereby safeguarding the elected representative’s constitutional rights until a final decision on the merits is rendered.

In parliamentary practice, the timing and method for filling legislative vacancies are governed by the statutes that created the body as well as by judicial interpretations of those statutes. When a state legislative assembly is not in existence at the moment a biennial election should be held, any seats that become vacant during that interregnum remain empty until the assembly is reconstituted. If, during the prolonged vacancy, additional seats become vacant through ordinary causes such as resignations, deaths, or disqualifications, the law treats each set of vacancies as distinct because they arise under different legal categories: the first group is linked to the failure to convene a biennial election, while the second group stems from routine turnover. The Supreme Court of India, interpreting the provisions of the Representation of the People Act and the relevant state constitution, has held that these two categories cannot be merged into a single electoral process. Each category must be addressed by its own election, even when the authorities adopt a common timetable for the conduct of the polls in order to conserve resources or simplify administration. This principle was expressly affirmed in the 1985 Delhi High Court decision of *Surinder Pal Ratawal v. Shamim Ahmed* (AIR 1985 Delhi 22), where the court rejected the notion that vacancies arising at different moments could be combined into one poll. The same doctrine was reiterated later in *A.K. Walia v. Union of India & Ors.*, Civil Writ No. 132 of 1994, before the Delhi High Court, which emphasized that the statutory framework mandates separate elections for each class of vacancy, notwithstanding any practical convenience of holding the polls simultaneously. The rationale behind this separation is to preserve the integrity of the electoral schedule prescribed for each type of vacancy, to ensure that the representation of the electorate is refreshed in accordance with the specific constitutional timelines, and to prevent the dilution of voter choice that might occur if disparate vacancies were aggregated. Consequently, even when a unified election calendar is drawn up after the assembly’s reconvening, the legal requirement remains that each vacancy be filled by its own distinct electoral exercise, thereby upholding the procedural safeguards embedded in the legislative system.
