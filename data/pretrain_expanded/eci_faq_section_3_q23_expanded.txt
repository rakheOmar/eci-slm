The power‑pack that supplies the Election‑Verification‑Module (EVM) is engineered to sustain the electrical needs of up to four Booth Units (BUs) together with a single Control Unit (CU) while handling a maximum voting load of roughly two‑thousand ballots. Under such heavy‑use conditions the battery’s internal voltage declines very slowly as long as the remaining capacity is high; the decline only becomes pronounced when the stored energy falls below a critical level, at which point the output voltage drops rapidly toward the lower cutoff. In practice this means that during a full day of intensive polling—when all four BUs are active, the CU is continuously processing voter data, and the total number of votes approaches the 2 000‑vote ceiling—the power‑pack’s state‑of‑charge indicator will typically show a gradual reduction from the nominal 100 % toward lower percentages, reflecting the true depletion of the cells.

Conversely, when the polling station operates under a lighter electrical load—such as when only one BU is connected to the CU, or when the total number of ballots cast remains well under one thousand—the current drawn from the battery is considerably smaller. Because the drain is modest, the battery’s voltage remains above the 7.4 V threshold that the EVM’s monitoring circuitry uses to infer a significant loss of capacity. The voltage sensor therefore interprets the pack as still being essentially full, and the display on the CU will often linger at the “99 %” reading even after many hours of operation. This apparent discrepancy does not indicate a malfunction; rather, it is a consequence of the way the power‑pack’s voltage‑based state‑of‑charge algorithm works—high voltage is maintained until the battery is truly near exhaustion, at which point the voltage falls sharply and the percentage readout drops more quickly.

To illustrate, imagine a rural polling station that receives only a few hundred voters throughout the day. The single BU attached to the CU processes each vote, but the overall power draw may be as low as a few hundred milliamps. In such a scenario the battery can easily sustain the load for the entire day without its terminal voltage ever approaching the low‑voltage warning zone, and the CU will continue to report a near‑full charge (99 % or even 100 %) despite the passage of many hours. By contrast, an urban centre with four BUs operating simultaneously and a high voter turnout will see the pack’s voltage begin to sag after a few hundred votes, and the percentage displayed will start to decline noticeably as the battery approaches its usable limit. Understanding this behavior helps election officials interpret the power‑pack status correctly and plan for timely battery replacement or supplemental power when the polling environment demands higher throughput.

The power packs that drive electronic voting machines (EVMs) are built around ordinary alkaline cells, the same type of primary battery that powers many household devices such as remote‑control units, flashlights and portable radios. Alkaline cells are classified as non‑rechargeable, meaning that once the chemical reactions that generate electricity have been exhausted they cannot be restored by applying an external charge; instead a fresh cell must be installed. This characteristic is deliberately exploited in the design of EVM power packs because it confers two practical advantages. First, the cells have an exceptionally long shelf‑life—often five to ten years when stored in a cool, dry environment—so a power pack that is manufactured today can be kept in a secure depot for many electoral cycles without a noticeable loss of capacity. Second, alkaline chemistry provides a high energy‑density ratio, delivering a large amount of usable charge relative to the pack’s weight and volume; this is essential for a portable voting unit that must be carried by polling staff and operate reliably in remote or poorly serviced locations.

Because an EVM is required to function only during the relatively brief period of an election—typically a few days for polling and a further day or two for the counting of votes—the power pack is treated as a single‑use, disposable component. The machine does not need to be kept powered continuously during the long inter‑election intervals that can stretch for several years, and the logistics of recharging or refurbishing batteries would add unnecessary complexity and cost. Consequently, each power pack is inserted into the control unit (CU) of the EVM just before the commencement of voting, remains sealed for the duration of the electoral event, and is then removed and stored or discarded after the data have been securely backed up.

The electronic circuitry of the EVM is engineered to be tolerant of a fairly wide span of input voltages. In practice the device will operate satisfactorily so long as the voltage supplied by the battery pack lies between 5.5 volts and 8.2 volts. Within this envelope the performance of the voting machine—such as the responsiveness of the push‑buttons, the illumination of the display, and the integrity of the internal microcontroller’s timing—remains essentially independent of the exact voltage level. This design flexibility is intentional: it allows a single standard pack to be used across the many machines deployed in a national election, despite inevitable variations in the exact charge remaining in each individual pack.

When a fresh power pack is manufactured for a control unit, the open‑circuit voltage that it delivers typically falls in the narrow band of 7.5 V to 8.0 V. The CU’s firmware interprets any measured voltage above 7.4 V as an indication that the pack is essentially full, and it therefore initializes the displayed battery‑status indicator to 99 percent. This figure is not a precise measurement of remaining ampere‑hours but rather a convenient way of informing the polling staff that the pack is ready for use. As voting proceeds, the current drawn by the machine varies according to two main factors: the number of ballot units (BUs) that have been attached to the CU and the total number of votes that are being recorded. Each BU adds a small incremental load because it contains its own microprocessor and display, while the act of casting a vote momentarily spikes the current draw as the CU writes the vote data to its secure memory.

The CU is equipped with a visual warning system that helps polling agents monitor battery health. If the pack’s voltage falls below 5.8 V, the display begins to flash the message “change battery,” alerting the staff that the remaining charge is approaching a level at which reliable operation could be compromised. Should the voltage dip further, crossing the 5.5 V threshold, the EVM will cease to function altogether: the push‑buttons become unresponsive, the display goes dark, and no further votes can be cast. Importantly, even when the machine powers down because of insufficient voltage, the data that have already been stored in the internal non‑volatile memory remain intact and can be retrieved later during the counting phase. This safeguard ensures that a sudden power loss does not corrupt the election results that have been accumulated up to that point.

A noteworthy quirk of alkaline chemistry is the phenomenon of voltage “recovery” after a period of non‑use. When a battery is disconnected from a load, the chemical gradients inside the cell gradually re‑equilibrate, causing the measured terminal voltage to rise slightly—often by a few tens of millivolts. In the context of EVMs, this means that a pack that has been removed from the CU and stored for a short interval may exhibit a voltage that climbs back above 7.4 V. If the same pack is re‑inserted into the control unit for the counting of votes, the CU will again initialise the battery‑status indicator to 99 percent, even though the true remaining capacity has not increased. This behaviour is benign; it merely reflects the way the device interprets the instantaneous voltage reading.

The physical installation of the power pack is carried out under strict procedural controls to guarantee transparency and to prevent any possibility of tampering. During the commissioning stage—when the EVM is first prepared for an election—candidates or their authorised representatives are invited to witness the insertion of the fresh battery pack into the control unit. Once the pack is seated, the CU is sealed with a tamper‑evident sticker or a proprietary locking mechanism that can only be broken in a controlled manner. If, on the day of polling, a power pack were to run out of charge before voting is completed, a replacement pack may be introduced, but the same protocol applies: the new pack must be swapped in the presence of polling agents, and the unit is resealed immediately after the exchange. The sealing process is documented in the poll‑book, and the stickers are later inspected by election officials to confirm that no unauthorized opening has occurred.

To illustrate how these specifications play out in a real‑world scenario, consider a typical polling station in a rural district that serves approximately 1,200 voters. The station is equipped with a single EVM containing three ballot units—one for each constituency segment. At the start of the day, the CU is fitted with a fresh alkaline pack measuring 7.8 V, and the battery indicator shows 99 percent. As voters arrive, the machine draws an average of 30 mA per vote, with occasional peaks up to 80 mA when the display refreshes. After a full day of voting, the measured voltage may have fallen to around 6.0 V, prompting the “change battery” warning. Because the election law requires that voting continue uninterrupted, the poll officer replaces the depleted pack with a new one, again in the sight of the polling agents, and the process repeats for the counting phase the following day. Throughout, the data stored on the EVM’s internal memory remain safe, and the transparent handling of the power packs bolsters public confidence in the integrity of the electronic voting process.

The electronic voting machine (EVM) relies on a compact, rechargeable power pack that supplies the energy necessary for reliable operation throughout the entire voting process. Each power pack is built around a sealed‑lead‑acid or lithium‑ion cell, encased in a rugged, tamper‑evident housing that meets the stringent security standards prescribed for electoral equipment. Typical specifications include a nominal voltage of 12 V, a capacity of roughly 8 Ah, and a built‑in protection circuit that guards against over‑charging, deep discharge, and short‑circuit conditions. Because the EVM must function under a wide range of environmental conditions—from humid coastal regions to dry, high‑altitude polling stations—the power pack is designed to tolerate temperature extremes between –10 °C and 55 °C and to resist shocks and vibrations that may occur during transport or handling.

Installation of the power pack is intentionally straightforward to minimise the risk of error by poll staff. The pack slides into a dedicated compartment on the control unit (CU) of the EVM, aligning a set of metal contacts that automatically lock the unit in place. A single tactile click confirms a secure connection, and an LED indicator on the CU illuminates to show that the machine has detected the power source. In addition to the primary power pack, a spare pack is normally kept on site so that any unforeseen loss of voltage can be remedied within minutes, thereby preventing interruptions to voting.

The EVM’s internal electronics are capable of operating at several output voltage levels, depending on the functional mode of the machine. In standby mode—when the device is powered on but awaiting the start of voting—the control circuitry draws only a few milliamps, allowing the battery to maintain a voltage close to its nominal value for extended periods. Once voting begins, the machine switches to a higher‑power mode that drives the display, button matrix, and internal data‑logging components; this raises the current draw and can cause the battery voltage to dip temporarily to around 11.5 V before stabilising. If the EVM is used in a “rapid‑count” configuration, where results are transmitted immediately after each booth’s closing, an additional communication module is activated, requiring yet another increment in power consumption. The power‑management firmware monitors these transitions and adjusts the output to keep the voltage within safe limits, preventing premature depletion.

A critical feature that enhances the smooth conduct of elections is the real‑time battery‑status display on the CU’s screen. Earlier generations of EVMs only indicated battery health with three vague symbols—High, Medium, and Low—forcing poll officials to make conservative estimates about remaining run time. Modern units, however, present the exact percentage of charge remaining, refreshed continuously as the machine operates. For instance, when the display shows 85 % charge, the officer knows that the power pack can comfortably sustain several more hours of voting; if the level falls to 20 % or below, the system automatically triggers an audible alert and highlights the value in red, prompting the official to replace the pack before it reaches the critical 10 % threshold where voltage sag could jeopardise data integrity. This granular feedback not only streamlines logistics—allowing the poll crew to schedule replacements during natural breaks—but also safeguards the election’s credibility by ensuring that no booth experiences a sudden power loss that could compromise the confidentiality or completeness of the vote count.

When an alkaline‑cell power pack that supplies an electronic voting machine (EVM) is kept under load for an extended period, the electrochemical reactions that generate electric current gradually leave behind reaction products—primarily zinc oxide and other insoluble species—that accumulate on the surface of the zinc anode. This buildup creates a thin, resistive film that raises the cell’s internal resistance, a phenomenon that is observable as a slow but steady decline in the terminal voltage while the device remains powered. For a typical 9 V alkaline pack delivering a constant current of a few hundred milliamps, the voltage may drop from its nominal 9.0 V to about 8.3 V or even lower after several hours of continuous operation, depending on the age of the cells and the exact load profile. The increase in internal resistance is not permanent; it is a reversible effect caused by the limited mobility of the reaction products while the cell is being forced to deliver current.

If the EVM is switched off—effectively placing the battery pack in a state of rest—the electrical field that drives ion migration disappears, and the accumulated reaction products are free to diffuse back into the bulk electrolyte. Over the ensuing minutes to hours, the diffusion process reduces the thickness of the resistive film on the electrodes, thereby lowering the internal resistance of each cell. As a result, the terminal voltage that had been suppressed during operation can rebound by a modest amount, typically in the range of one‑tenth to three‑tenths of a volt per cell. In a practical example, a pack that measured 8.2 V just before power‑down might read 8.4 V after a period of two to three hours without load, and in some cases an even larger recovery of up to 0.3 V has been documented for fresh, high‑quality cells.

This voltage “recovery” effect is especially relevant in the context of election equipment, where the same set of batteries may be employed on polling day and then again on counting day after the machines have been stored for a night or two. Operators often observe that a power‑down and subsequent storage period yields a slightly higher voltage reading on the counting day than was measured at the end of polling, a fact that can be explained entirely by the diffusion‑driven reduction of internal resistance described above. The phenomenon is not unique to voting machines; any portable electronic device that relies on alkaline cells—such as handheld radios, remote controls, or flashlights—will exhibit a comparable voltage rebound after a rest interval.

The underlying physics can be traced to the fundamental electrochemical equations governing alkaline cells. The overall discharge reaction for an alkaline battery is Zn + 2 MnO₂ + 2 H₂O → Zn(OH)₂ + 2 MnOOH, and during high‑current discharge the zinc oxidation front moves outward, forming a porous layer of ZnO that blocks further electron flow. When the current ceases, the concentration gradients that created the layer dissipate, allowing the ZnO particles to re‑dissolve or migrate back into the electrolyte. This restoration of a more uniform electrode surface reduces the cell’s ohmic loss, which is reflected in the observed voltage rise.

For engineers who need quantitative predictions of this behavior, Texas Instruments has published an application note (TI SLUA176) that details the voltage drop and subsequent recovery of alkaline cells under constant‑current discharge conditions. The note presents measured curves for a variety of discharge currents, illustrates the dependence of recovery magnitude on both the depth of discharge and the rest period, and offers simple formulas to estimate the expected voltage rebound in a given circuit. By consulting such data, designers of battery‑powered systems—whether they are building EVMs, medical devices, or remote sensors—can account for the temporary nature of voltage sag and plan appropriate voltage‑monitoring strategies, such as allowing a brief rest before taking a critical voltage measurement or using a low‑dropout regulator that tolerates the transient dip without compromising system performance.

The electronic voting machine (EVM) is engineered to operate reliably across a fairly wide span of supply‑voltage conditions, specifically between 5.5 volts and 8.2 volts, and within that interval its functional performance is essentially independent of the exact value of the voltage that is present at any given moment. This design choice reflects the practical reality that the portable power packs used to energise the device are subject to gradual depletion as the chemical energy stored in the cells is consumed during the course of an election day, and therefore the voltage they deliver inevitably falls from its freshly‑charged level toward the lower limits of the acceptable range. The internal control unit (CU) of the EVM is equipped with a dedicated voltage‑sensing circuit that executes a measurement each time the machine is powered on; this measurement is then used to decide whether the machine can proceed with normal operation or must alert the election officer to a potential power problem. If the sensed voltage drops below 5.8 V, the CU activates a conspicuous visual warning—typically a flashing “change battery” message on the display—so that the polling staff can replace the depleted pack before the voltage falls any further. Should the voltage fall beneath the hard cut‑off of 5.5 V, the machine will cease to function, but the electronic memory that holds the recorded votes remains intact and is protected against loss, because the non‑volatile storage is powered by a separate backup capacitor that retains its state even when the main supply is removed.

The nominal voltage of a brand‑new power pack is generally in the region of 7.5 V to 8.0 V. This figure is derived from the series connection of five identical rechargeable cells, each of which contributes roughly 1.5 V to 1.6 V to the total output; the series configuration both raises the voltage to a level suitable for the EVM’s internal electronics and preserves the overall capacity of the pack because the ampere‑hour rating of the series string is the same as that of a single cell. As the election proceeds and the machine executes its routine tasks—displaying candidate symbols, registering a voter’s press of the “vote” button, writing the corresponding data to memory, and, on some models, transmitting a cryptographic checksum to a central server—the chemical energy in the cells is gradually consumed, causing the terminal voltage to drift downward. In practice, the usable portion of the battery’s charge is monitored not only by the absolute voltage level but also by the percentage of capacity that remains; when the remaining capacity falls below roughly ten percent, the same “change battery” warning is issued even if the voltage is still marginally above the 5.8 V threshold. This dual‑criteria approach helps ensure that the machine will have sufficient headroom to complete any pending operations—such as finalising the tally at the close of voting or performing a self‑test during the counting phase—without an unexpected power loss.

Replacement of the power pack is a tightly controlled procedure, designed to preserve the integrity of the electoral process and to provide an auditable trail of any maintenance activity. The protocol requires that the poll officer, together with two representatives (often called polling agents) of the contesting political parties, be present at the moment the battery is swapped, thereby guaranteeing transparency. A prescribed log‑book entry is then made, noting the time of replacement, the serial number of the old and new packs, and the signatures of all witnesses; this record becomes part of the official election documentation and can be inspected by observers or auditors after the fact. The new pack, being sealed and tamper‑evident, is inserted into the machine’s dedicated battery compartment, which is itself designed to prevent accidental disconnection and to shield the cells from environmental contaminants such as dust, moisture, or temperature extremes. Because the compartment is sealed, any attempt to open it covertly would leave visible evidence—broken seals, altered fasteners, or mismatched serial numbers—thus deterring malicious attempts to substitute a pre‑charged or otherwise compromised battery.

The power consumption profile of the EVM has been calibrated to match the expected workload of a typical polling day, which can range from a few hundred to several thousand voters depending on the constituency size. Laboratory testing and field trials have demonstrated that a fully charged pack can sustain continuous operation for well beyond the maximum anticipated voting period, often exceeding twelve hours of uninterrupted use while still maintaining a voltage comfortably above the 5.8 V warning level. On the counting day, when the machine is required to run additional diagnostic routines, to display the results on a larger screen, or to interface with a central tabulation system, the same battery is usually sufficient, provided that it has not been exhausted during the prior day's voting. In the rare circumstance that the voltage were to dip below the 5.5 V lower limit during a critical phase, the machine would automatically suspend voting operations but would retain the already‑stored ballot data; once a fresh pack is installed, the CU can be rebooted, the stored data re‑validated, and the voting process resumed without any loss of previously recorded votes.

The sealed nature of the power pack, coupled with constant monitoring of the machine’s voltage during storage, transportation, and idle periods, serves as an important safeguard against covert tampering. Before deployment, each EVM is subject to a pre‑election inspection in which the battery voltage is measured, the integrity of the sealing is verified, and the CU’s internal logs are checked for any anomalous power‑cycle events. After the election, the same checks are repeated to confirm that the voltage curve followed the expected discharge pattern and that no unexplained voltage spikes—potentially indicative of a short‑circuit or a deliberate power‑injection attack—occurred. Because the voltage thresholds are hard‑coded into the machine’s firmware, any deviation from the prescribed range triggers a fault mode that prevents further voting, thereby protecting the stored vote data from corruption. This design philosophy, which blends robust hardware tolerances with procedural oversight, ensures that the EVM can be trusted to function correctly across the full range of permissible operating voltages, that the data it holds remains secure even when the supply falls below the functional limit, and that any required battery replacement is carried out in a transparent, well‑documented manner that upholds the overall credibility of the electoral process.

The preparation of electronic voting machines (EVMs) for a general election is a tightly choreographed operation that begins well before the actual polling day and continues through the final sealing of the machines after votes have been cast, a sequence designed to guarantee both the technical integrity of the devices and the confidence of the electorate, the candidates and their agents, and the independent observers who monitor the process. Eight to ten days prior to the designated poll date, election officials convene a commissioning ceremony in a secure venue that is open to the candidates, their authorised representatives, and the General Observers appointed by the Election Commission; this gathering is not merely ceremonial but serves a critical verification function, because at that moment a fresh, factory‑sealed power pack is installed directly into the control unit of each EVM. The power pack, which supplies the electrical energy required for the machine’s micro‑processor and the display of candidates’ names, is a consumable component that must be guaranteed to be new and untampered so that the voting process cannot be compromised by a depleted or altered battery. Once the new pack is fitted, the control unit is immediately sealed with an “Address Tag” – a tamper‑evident label that records a unique identification number, the serial number of the power pack, and the date of installation. The presence of the address tag allows any subsequent attempt to open or replace the power pack to be instantly detectable, because the seal would show clear signs of disturbance. Candidates or their authorised agents are invited to sign the address tag, thereby creating a written record that the machine was inspected and accepted in its original state; this signature acts as a safeguard against later claims of impropriety and provides a paper trail that can be cross‑checked during any post‑election audit.

Following the commissioning, the EVMs are powered down and transferred to a “strong room,” a fortified storage area equipped with continuous closed‑circuit television (CCTV) monitoring and staffed around the clock by at least one security section that is armed and trained in the handling of sensitive electoral equipment. The strong room is deliberately kept under lock and key to prevent any unauthorised access, and the CCTV footage is retained for the entire election cycle so that any attempt at intrusion can be investigated retrospectively. Only on the day designated for the dispersal of the machines – the day when the sealed EVMs are taken from the strong room to the various polling stations – is the strong room opened again, and this is done in the presence of the same representatives and observers who attended the commissioning. The simultaneous presence of these parties at both the commissioning and the dispersal stages creates a transparent chain of custody that is essential for maintaining public trust; any deviation from this protocol would be noted as a procedural breach and could be grounds for legal challenge.

On the actual polling day, each polling station conducts a short “mock poll” before the start of voting, in which at least fifty dummy votes are cast using the EVMs that have just arrived. The purpose of the mock poll is twofold: it provides a practical demonstration that the machines are fully functional after transportation, and it allows polling agents – the individuals appointed by each candidate to observe the voting process – to become familiar with the operation of the devices, thereby reducing the likelihood of procedural errors during the real election. The mock poll is observed by the polling agents, who verify that the votes are recorded correctly on the display and that the machines tally the inputs without fault. After the mock poll is successfully completed, the actual voting commences, and throughout the polling hours the agents of each contesting party remain on the floor, watching the insertion of the ballot papers (or, in the case of a pure electronic system, the pressing of the candidate’s button) and ensuring that no unauthorised person interferes with the EVMs. Their continuous presence is a statutory right under the election rules and serves as an additional layer of oversight, complementing the earlier security measures.

When the last voter has cast his or her vote and the poll is formally closed, the EVMs are again switched off, this time for the final time in the election cycle. Each machine is then sealed with a fresh address tag that bears the date of sealing and the signatures of the polling agents present at that station, thereby extending the chain of custody from the polling station back to the central reception centre where the machines will be counted. The sealed machines are placed in specially designed carrying cases that are themselves fitted with tamper‑evident seals, and the agents are allowed to sign these outer seals as well, creating a parallel record that the transportation containers have not been opened. Importantly, the polling agents are also permitted to accompany the vehicles that transport the sealed EVMs from the polling stations to the reception centre, a practice that provides an additional visual verification that the machines have not been swapped or tampered with en route. In many jurisdictions, the reception centre is a highly secure facility where the sealed machines are stored under the same CCTV and armed‑guard conditions as the strong room, awaiting the final counting phase. At that later stage, election officials can open the address tags in the presence of representatives from all parties, compare the serial numbers on the power packs with the records kept at commissioning, and verify that the tally displayed by each machine corresponds to the votes recorded during the mock poll and the actual poll, thereby completing a transparent, end‑to‑end audit trail.

Taken together, these steps – the installation of a new power pack eight to ten days before polling, the sealing and signing of address tags at both commissioning and post‑poll stages, the stringent security of the strong room, the mandatory mock poll, the continuous presence of polling agents, and the supervised transport of sealed machines – constitute a comprehensive procedural framework that seeks to eliminate any opportunity for manipulation of the voting hardware. By embedding multiple, independently verifiable checkpoints throughout the election cycle, the system not only safeguards the technical functionality of the EVMs but also reinforces the democratic principle that every vote must be counted exactly as cast, free from interference or doubt. The rigorous methodology described here has been adopted in numerous Indian states and Union Territories, and it serves as a model for other democracies that employ electronic voting technology while striving to maintain the highest standards of electoral integrity.

The entire lifecycle of the power‑pack – from its installation during the initial commissioning of the EVMs, through its use on election day, to its removal after the votes have been counted – is designed to be as transparent as possible, with multiple layers of physical security, video surveillance, and direct involvement of the contesting parties. This rigorous protocol helps to prevent tampering, assures the electorate and political stakeholders that the electronic voting process is reliable, and complies with the Election Commission’s broader mandate to conduct free and fair elections. By mandating that no handling or movement of the machines occurs without the explicit presence and consent of the candidates or their agents during the critical 8‑ to 10‑day window that spans from power‑pack installation to the declaration of results, the system reinforces confidence in the integrity of the electronic voting apparatus.

The Electronic Voting Machine (EVM) power pack is fundamentally different from the battery that powers a typical mobile telephone, both in its chemical composition and in the way it is used over time. An EVM power pack is built from a series of alkaline cells, the same type of primary (non‑rechargeable) batteries that are commonly found in household items such as flashlights, remote controls, or wall clocks. These cells are designed to deliver a steady voltage for a relatively short period of active operation and then remain essentially idle for long stretches of storage without any significant loss of charge. In the context of elections, an EVM does not need a continuous power source while it is being stored in a secure vault; it only requires electricity during the few hours when the voting process is actually taking place. Consequently, the alkaline power pack can sit on a shelf for months or even years, and when the election day arrives it will still be able to supply the necessary current to run the machine. This behaviour is comparable to that of a basic pocket calculator that can function for many years on a single set of alkaline cells, because the device draws power only when a key is pressed and otherwise consumes virtually no energy.

In contrast, the battery that fuels a mobile phone is almost always a rechargeable lithium‑ion (Li‑ion) cell. Lithium‑ion technology was chosen for smartphones because it offers a high energy‑to‑weight ratio, a relatively low self‑discharge rate, and the ability to be recharged many thousands of times. However, a mobile phone is not a device that can be completely disconnected from a power source for extended periods. Even when a user is not actively making calls or using applications, the phone remains linked to the cellular network, constantly listening for incoming signals, synchronizing data, and maintaining background processes such as location services, push notifications, and software updates. This “always‑on” connectivity means that the battery is continually drawing a small amount of current, a phenomenon known as phantom drain or standby drain. As a result, a smartphone left idle for a day will typically lose a few percent of its charge, and over weeks the battery can become fully depleted if it is not periodically recharged.

Because of this continuous demand, manufacturers equip phones with rechargeable batteries rather than primary alkaline cells. A rechargeable Li‑ion pack can be restored to full capacity with an external charger, allowing the user to replenish the energy that has been consumed during both active use (calls, video streaming, gaming) and passive standby. The need for periodic recharging also influences how users manage their devices; for example, many people carry power banks, use fast‑charging adapters, or adopt power‑saving modes to extend the time between charges.

The divergent design philosophies of the two power sources reflect the distinct operational requirements of the devices they serve. The EVM power pack prioritizes long‑term stability and readiness after long periods of inactivity, which is why a non‑rechargeable alkaline chemistry is optimal: it does not suffer from the memory effect or capacity loss that can affect rechargeable cells after many charge‑discharge cycles. Meanwhile, the mobile phone battery must accommodate frequent, rapid cycles of discharge and recharge, making lithium‑ion the preferred choice despite its need for regular charging.

To illustrate these differences with a practical scenario, imagine an election commission storing hundreds of EVMs in a secure depot for several months before an election. The alkaline power packs inside each machine will retain their charge because they are not being drawn upon, and when the voting day arrives the machines can be powered up instantly without any pre‑charging. Conversely, consider a commuter who relies on a smartphone throughout the day for navigation, messaging, and media consumption. Even if the phone is placed on a desk during a meeting, it continues to maintain a network link and update apps in the background, gradually depleting the lithium‑ion battery. The user will need to plug the phone into a charger at some point, perhaps during a commute or overnight, to ensure that it remains functional for the next day’s activities.

In summary, the EVM power pack employs disposable alkaline cells that stay dormant without losing charge during prolonged storage, making them ideal for devices that are powered only intermittently. Mobile phones, on the other hand, rely on rechargeable lithium‑ion batteries because their operation demands a constant, albeit low‑level, power supply even when the device appears to be idle, necessitating regular recharging to maintain functionality.

Alkaline dry‑cell batteries, which generate electricity through the reaction of zinc and manganese dioxide in an alkaline electrolyte, have become a staple power source for a remarkably wide range of devices because of their relatively high energy density, long shelf life, and ability to deliver a steady voltage over many discharge cycles. In the realm of everyday consumer electronics, they are most commonly found powering portable items such as transistor radios, handheld gaming consoles, remote controls, flashlights, and other small gadgets that require a compact, lightweight source of energy. Their reliability and low cost also make them the preferred choice for emergency‑power backup systems: critical illumination units in hospitals and public buildings are often equipped with alkaline cells to ensure that emergency lights remain operational during a power outage, while a variety of medical instruments—including pulse oximeters, infusion pumps, blood‑pressure monitors, and electronic thermometers—are routinely designed with alkaline batteries as a secondary power source to guarantee continuous operation when mains power fails. 

Beyond the health‑care sector, alkaline batteries are extensively employed in industrial settings. Smoke detectors, which must function reliably for years without maintenance, typically contain alkaline cells because these batteries can maintain the necessary voltage for the sensor’s electronics over a prolonged period. Portable transmitters, barcode scanners, digital voltmeters, electronic door‑lock mechanisms, and other field‑service tools also rely on alkaline power due to the cells’ ability to deliver short‑burst high currents while remaining compact enough to fit into handheld equipment. 

In many control‑system architectures, especially those used in critical infrastructure, alkaline batteries serve as a supplemental or backup supply for particular circuit blocks. For instance, some defense‑related control panels incorporate a small alkaline pack to keep essential monitoring and safety functions alive even if the primary power source is disrupted. This practice extends to military and defense applications, where the ruggedness and predictability of alkaline chemistry are prized. Notable examples include the SINCGARS (Single Channel Ground and Airborne Radio System) hand‑held radios employed by infantry units, man‑pack communication sets carried by soldiers on patrol, and portable GPS receivers used for navigation in remote or combat environments. In each of these cases, the alkaline battery’s capacity to provide reliable voltage over a wide temperature range and its resistance to leakage make it an ideal choice for equipment that must operate flawlessly under demanding field conditions.

Electronic Voting Machines (EVMs) are compact, micro‑controller‑driven devices that record each voter’s choice in a secure, tamper‑resistant manner and are widely deployed in democratic elections around the world. Because they operate in a variety of climatic zones—ranging from the scorching heat of tropical summers to the frigid winters of high‑altitude regions—the power source that drives an EVM must be both robust and dependable under extreme temperature fluctuations. For this reason, the power packs that energise these machines are built from primary alkaline cells rather than from rechargeable chemistries. In the standard configuration, the control unit of an EVM is powered by a series string of five alkaline cells, each typically rated at 1.5 V, which together supply the nominal 7.5 V required for the unit’s low‑power digital circuitry. The Voter‑Verified Paper Audit Trail (VVPAT) module, which produces a printed slip that the voter can briefly view to confirm that his or her vote has been correctly recorded, demands a larger amount of energy because it must drive a thermal printer, a display, and a small motor. Consequently, the VVPAT power pack incorporates thirty alkaline cells arranged in a combination of series and parallel connections; this architecture not only raises the overall voltage to match the printer’s requirements but also increases the available capacity, thereby ensuring that the VVPAT can operate continuously throughout an entire polling day without a loss of performance.

Alkaline cells are classified as primary batteries, meaning they are intended for a single use and are discarded after their stored chemical energy is exhausted. This characteristic is advantageous for election equipment for several reasons. First, primary alkaline batteries exhibit a very low self‑discharge rate, often losing less than 2 % of their charge per year, which translates into a gradual and predictable decline in voltage rather than sudden drops that could jeopardise a machine’s operation. Because elections are typically held only a few times each year, the machines spend most of their life in storage; during these long idle periods the power packs must retain enough charge to be ready for immediate deployment when the election calendar calls for them. The long shelf‑life of alkaline cells—generally specified as five years under normal storage conditions—means that a batch of freshly manufactured power packs can be stock‑piled for several electoral cycles without the need for periodic recharging or replacement, thereby simplifying logistics and reducing maintenance costs for election commissions.

In addition to their excellent shelf stability, alkaline batteries are prized for their ability to function over a wide temperature range, typically from –20 °C to +50 °C. This thermal tolerance is critical in countries where polling stations may be set up in remote villages, open fields, or temporary structures lacking climate control. Unlike many rechargeable chemistries—such as nickel‑metal hydride (NiMH) or lithium‑ion (Li‑ion)—which can suffer reduced capacity or accelerated ageing when exposed to extreme cold or heat, alkaline cells maintain a relatively flat discharge curve, providing a steady voltage that keeps the microcontroller and peripheral components of the EVM within their safe operating limits.

Economic considerations also favour alkaline cells. The per‑unit cost of a standard 1.5 V alkaline cell is modest, and because the EVM’s internal electronics draw only a few milliamps of current in idle mode and perhaps a few hundred milliamps at peak when the printer is active, the overall energy demand is modest. High energy density—typically around 150 Wh kg⁻¹ for alkaline chemistry—ensures that the required number of cells can be accommodated within the compact housing of the power pack without making the device unduly heavy or bulky. Moreover, alkaline batteries are widely available, and their disposal is straightforward: they can be collected in standard municipal waste streams or in designated battery‑recycling bins without the need for special handling procedures required for hazardous rechargeable batteries that contain heavy metals or electrolytes that may become unstable if punctured.

The design philosophy of the EVM further reinforces the choice of non‑rechargeable alkaline cells. The voting machine is intended to be a “plug‑and‑play” unit with no external ports for charging, data transfer, or maintenance. This absence of connectivity is a deliberate security measure; by eliminating any physical interface that could be used to inject malicious code or to tamper with the device’s firmware, the risk of electoral fraud is reduced. If a rechargeable battery pack were employed, the machine would need a charging port, a power‑management circuit, and a means of monitoring the battery’s state of charge—all of which would introduce additional points of vulnerability and increase the complexity of the hardware design. Furthermore, rechargeable packs would have to be cycled periodically to avoid “deep‑discharge” damage, which would impose a regular maintenance schedule on election officials and could lead to inadvertent power loss if a pack were left uncharged during an unexpected election or a by‑election.

Finally, the practicalities of field deployment make alkaline cells the most suitable choice. Election officials often have to transport dozens of machines to remote polling locations, sometimes over rough terrain and without reliable access to electricity. Carrying a set of disposable power packs that can be swapped out in minutes is far more convenient than hauling a portable charger, a power outlet, or a generator to keep a rechargeable system topped up. The quick “replace‑and‑go” procedure also minimises downtime on election day; if a power pack’s voltage falls below the acceptable threshold, the operator can simply insert a fresh alkaline pack and resume operation without interrupting the voting process.

In summary, the power packs of modern Electronic Voting Machines employ primary alkaline cells because these batteries combine a long five‑year shelf life, stable performance across a broad temperature spectrum, low self‑discharge, high energy density, economical cost, and ease of disposal—all of which align with the low‑current, microcontroller‑based architecture of the EVM and its operational requirement of being powered only during the brief election period. The control unit’s modest five‑cell series arrangement and the VVPAT’s more substantial thirty‑cell series‑parallel configuration together ensure that both components receive reliable, uninterrupted power throughout the entire voting session, while the absence of charging ports and the inherent safety of single‑use cells preserve the security, simplicity, and logistical efficiency that are essential for conducting free and fair elections.

Battery technology across the globe can be broadly classified into two fundamental categories—primary cells, which are non‑rechargeable, and secondary cells, which are rechargeable—and each group contains a variety of chemistries that have been refined over more than a century of electrochemical research to meet the diverse power demands of modern life. Primary cells are designed for applications where a modest, steady current is drawn over a relatively long period without the need for re‑charging, making them ideal for portable or intermittently used devices that are either difficult to service or where weight, convenience, and long shelf life are paramount. Classic examples include the ubiquitous alkaline button cells that power remote controls, wall clocks, toys, and handheld radios, as well as lithium‑metal cells, which, because of their high energy density and low self‑discharge, are often found in emergency‑use equipment such as flashlights, digital cameras, and medical devices like hearing aids; these chemistries are favored in situations where the device may sit unused for months or even years yet must deliver reliable voltage when finally called upon.  

In contrast, secondary cells are engineered to deliver higher power bursts and to tolerate repeated charge‑discharge cycles, a requirement that underpins virtually every modern electronic system that relies on frequent re‑energising. The most familiar of these are nickel‑metal hydride (NiMH) cells, which replaced older nickel‑cadmium batteries in many consumer products because they offer higher capacity and lower environmental toxicity, and are commonly employed in cordless power tools, digital cameras, and some hybrid‑electric vehicle (HEV) powertrains. Lead‑acid batteries, the workhorse of the automotive world since the early twentieth century, remain the dominant choice for starting, lighting, and ignition (SLI) functions in conventional cars, as well as for large stationary energy‑storage installations such as uninterruptible power supplies (UPS) and solar‑plus‑battery systems, thanks to their low cost, proven reliability, and ability to deliver high surge currents. More recently, lithium‑ion cells have become the cornerstone of portable electronics, delivering a combination of high energy density, relatively low weight, and a flat discharge curve that makes them perfect for smartphones, laptops, tablets, and increasingly for electric‑vehicle (EV) propulsion where the need for long range and rapid charging is critical. Finally, solid‑state batteries—still largely in the research and early‑production phases—promise to push performance even further by replacing the liquid electrolyte with a solid material, thereby improving safety, enabling higher voltage operation, and potentially extending cycle life, a development that could eventually transform sectors ranging from consumer wearables to aerospace.  

Together, these primary and secondary cell families form the backbone of the global energy storage ecosystem, each selected according to the specific voltage, capacity, discharge‑rate, cost, and environmental considerations of the target application. Whether a simple alkaline cell powers a child’s remote‑control car for a few hours, a lead‑acid battery keeps a home’s backup generator ready for an outage, or a lithium‑ion pack propels an electric car across hundreds of miles, the underlying principle remains the same: the careful matching of electrochemical characteristics to the practical needs of the device and its user.

The display that presents the output of the Control Unit in an Electronic Voting Machine (EVM) is engineered so that it cannot be altered to show information that differs from the data actually stored in the machine’s secure memory. This protection is achieved because the display subsystem is physically integrated within the Unauthorized Access Detect Module (UADM), a dedicated hardware block whose sole purpose is to monitor any attempt to open or tamper with the internal components of the voting device. When the UADM detects a breach—such as the removal of a cover, the disconnection of a cable, or any other form of forced entry—it instantly triggers a shutdown of the entire EVM, rendering the unit inoperative and thereby preventing any further manipulation of the vote‑recording process. In addition to this mechanical safeguard, the architecture of the EVM deliberately excludes any radio‑frequency (RF) transmission or reception capabilities; the machine does not contain Bluetooth, Wi‑Fi, or other wireless interfaces that could be used to inject spurious signals or to replace the legitimate display with a counterfeit one. Consequently, schemes that might be imagined in other electronic systems—such as swapping the original display module for a fabricated panel that shows fabricated tallies, or pairing a Bluetooth‑enabled external screen to the machine in order to broadcast altered results—are simply impossible in a properly designed EVM. For example, an attempt to connect a portable Bluetooth display to the voting terminal would fail because the EVM’s firmware does not recognize or respond to any wireless pairing requests, and the lack of an antenna or RF transceiver means that no external device can communicate with the Control Unit at all. Likewise, even a highly skilled technician who tries to replace the built‑in LCD with a malicious unit would be stopped by the UADM’s tamper‑evidence circuitry, which would immediately cut power to the Control Unit and alert election officials to the intrusion. These layered defenses—physical enclosure within the UADM, automatic shutdown on detection of tampering, and the intentional omission of wireless communication pathways—collectively ensure that the visual output of the Control Unit remains a trustworthy and unalterable reflection of the votes that have been recorded inside the EVM.

The German Federal Constitutional Court, when it declared a blanket prohibition on the use of electronic voting machines in German elections, articulated a principle that has become a touchstone for any democratic society that wishes to rely on technology for the expression of the popular will.  In its reasoning the Court stressed that a voting device which records ballots and tabulates the result solely by means of electronic memory can satisfy the Constitution only if every essential stage of the voting process— from the casting of an individual vote to the final aggregation of the totals— can be inspected in a manner that is both reliable and understandable to ordinary citizens who have no specialised knowledge of cryptography, software engineering or hardware design.  The Court further explained that the legislator is not absolutely barred from employing electronic equipment, but any such system must be accompanied by a safeguard that permits a “complementary examination” by the voter himself, by election officials, and by the public at large.  A concrete illustration of this safeguard is the use of a dual‑recording mechanism in which the electronic tally is mirrored by a physical, paper‑based record that can be manually inspected and counted, thereby providing a transparent audit trail that does not require technical expertise to verify.

In India, the Election Commission of India (ECI) operates under a constitutional mandate to conduct elections for the Lok Sabha, the Rajya Sabha, the state legislatures, and the offices of President and Vice‑President.  The legal foundation for the Commission’s use of electronic voting machines (EVMs) rests on statutes enacted by Parliament, and the constitutionality of the machines has been repeatedly affirmed by the Supreme Court of India as well as by numerous High Courts.  Indian EVMs differ fundamentally from the devices that triggered the German Court’s alarm, chiefly because they are embedded in a comprehensive system that satisfies the very requirements the Court described.  First, the machines are manufactured in secure, government‑run facilities operated by public‑sector undertakings such as Bharat Electronics Limited and Electronics Corporation of India Limited.  Before any unit leaves the factory, it undergoes rigorous third‑party validation performed by the Standardisation Testing and Quality Certification (STQC) body, which checks both hardware integrity and software authenticity against a set of predefined benchmarks.  Only after passing these tests are the devices sealed, logged, and dispatched to the state election offices.

Second, the Indian ECI has instituted an elaborate chain‑of‑custody protocol that governs every phase of the EVM’s life cycle—from production, storage, and transportation to deployment at polling stations and eventual de‑commissioning after an election.  The protocol mandates that each machine be accompanied by a tamper‑evident seal, that the seals be inspected by senior election officials at every hand‑over, and that a detailed electronic log be maintained to record any movement of the device.  These procedures are designed so that any irregularity can be detected without recourse to specialised technical knowledge; a lay observer can verify the presence of seals, the matching of serial numbers, and the completeness of the audit trail.

Third, and most crucially, the Indian system augments the electronic tally with a Voter‑Verified Paper Audit Trail (VVPAT).  When a voter presses the button corresponding to his or her chosen candidate, the EVM records the choice internally while simultaneously printing a small paper slip that displays the selected name and party symbol.  The slip is briefly visible behind a transparent window, allowing the voter to confirm that the machine has captured the intention correctly, after which the slip falls into a sealed box.  This paper record constitutes a physical, human‑readable artifact that can be counted by hand in case of a dispute, thereby fulfilling the “complementary examination” clause articulated by the German Court.  Because the VVPAT is produced at the moment of voting and can be inspected by any interested party without any knowledge of the underlying software, it embodies the principle that the essential steps of voting must be open to verification by the general public.

Finally, the Indian judiciary has repeatedly examined the security and reliability of the EVM‑VVPAT system.  In landmark judgments such as *Ashok Kumar v. Chief Election Officer* (2013) and *S. Rajendran v. Election Commission of India* (2015), the Supreme Court upheld the constitutionality of EVMs, emphasizing that the combination of electronic recording and paper verification satisfies the constitutional guarantee of free and fair elections.  The Court noted that the presence of a verifiable paper trail mitigates the risk of covert manipulation and that the stringent procedural safeguards surrounding the machines render them “transparent enough for ordinary citizens to understand and trust.”

Taken together, these features— state‑controlled manufacturing, independent certification, a meticulously documented chain of custody, and a mandatory paper audit trail— ensure that Indian electronic voting machines conform to the standard set by the German Constitutional Court: they enable a reliable, non‑technical examination of every critical stage of the voting process.  While the German judgment was directed at systems that stored votes solely in electronic memory, the Indian ECI’s EVMs are deliberately designed to be auditable, verifiable, and open to scrutiny by voters, election officials, and the wider public, thereby aligning the technological advantages of electronic voting with the constitutional imperatives of transparency and accountability.

The claim that two million electronic voting machines (EVMs) have gone missing from the Election Commission of India (ECI) has circulated widely in print, television and social‑media outlets, often citing responses to Right‑to‑Information (RTI) applications as evidence; however, a careful examination of the judicial record and the operational procedures of the commission reveals that the allegation is unfounded. The issue first entered the formal legal arena when the Indian New Congress Party filed a writ petition (W.P. (Civil) No. 151/2024) seeking a declaration that a substantial number of EVMs were unaccounted for and demanding corrective action. The petition was heard by the Hon’ble Supreme Court of India, which, after reviewing the material submitted by the petitioner and the counter‑representations of the ECI, issued an order on 15 March 2024 refusing to entertain the writ. In its reasoning, the apex court described the petitioners’ apprehensions as “completely baseless” and stressed that the alleged discrepancy did not satisfy the threshold for judicial intervention, thereby dismissing the case without further ado.

A second, related matter was adjudicated in the case of Manoranjan Santosh Roy v. Union of India & Ors. (W.P. (Civil) No. 482/2023), where the petitioner raised a series of questions concerning the inventory of EVMs, including alleged inconsistencies between the number of machines reported by the ECI, the work orders issued to manufacturers, and the quantities actually supplied. The Supreme Court, after a detailed hearing that included testimony from senior election officials and technical experts, concluded that the documentary evidence presented by the petitioner did not demonstrate any material shortfall or procedural lapse. The Court therefore dismissed the petition on the same grounds that the claims of “missing” machines were not substantiated by credible data.

Both judgments are significant not only because they close the specific legal challenges, but also because they illuminate the robust safeguards that the ECI has instituted to prevent loss, tampering or misuse of voting equipment. The commission follows a meticulously codified set of protocols covering every stage of an EVM’s life‑cycle. From the moment a machine leaves the factory floor, it is logged in a centralized inventory management system, assigned a unique identification number, and sealed in tamper‑evident packaging. Transportation from the manufacturing unit to regional storage depots, and subsequently to polling stations, is carried out by vetted security personnel under GPS‑monitored convoys, with chain‑of‑custody registers signed at each hand‑over. Upon arrival at a constituency, the machines undergo a series of pre‑poll checks—functional tests, battery verification, and software integrity verification—before being stored in secured, climate‑controlled vaults that are equipped with CCTV surveillance and biometric access controls. After the conclusion of an election, the machines are returned to the central repository, where a reconciliation process matches the physical count with the electronic log, and any anomalies are investigated by a dedicated audit team.

The procedural rigor described above has been repeatedly praised by independent observers, including the Commonwealth Secretariat and the United Nations Development Programme, both of which have highlighted India’s EVM management as a model of electoral integrity. Moreover, the ECI’s internal audit reports, which are periodically made public, consistently show that the discrepancy rates for EVM inventory are measured in single‑digit units, far removed from the “lakhs” (hundreds of thousands) figure that populates sensational headlines. For example, the audit for the 2024 general election recorded a total of 2,16,000 EVMs in circulation, of which only three were reported as temporarily misplaced due to transportation delays—situations that were promptly rectified and documented.

The persistence of the “two‑million missing EVMs” narrative can therefore be understood as a classic case of misinformation that thrives on the opacity of technical processes and the public’s limited familiarity with the logistical complexities of conducting elections in a country of over 1.4 billion people. Media reports that quote RTI replies without providing the full context—such as the fact that RTI applications often elicit preliminary, unverified figures that are later clarified by the commission—contribute to the distortion. Social‑media platforms amplify these fragments, attaching emotive language and conjecture, which in turn fuels public anxiety about the credibility of the electoral system.

In sum, the Supreme Court’s decisions in the two cited writ petitions, together with the ECI’s comprehensive procedural framework, demonstrate unequivocally that the allegation of two million missing electronic voting machines lacks factual basis. The legal rulings have characterized the claim as a “complete baselessness,” and the commission’s documented safeguards ensure that every EVM is tracked, secured, and accounted for throughout the election cycle. While vigilance and transparency remain essential components of any democratic process, the existing evidence indicates that the reported “missing” EVMs constitute a myth propagated through unchecked reporting rather than a genuine administrative failure.

The Election Commission of India (ECI) has been the subject of a claim that it remained silent regarding alleged “serious discrepancies” between two distinct data sets concerning the 2019 Lok Sabha elections. According to the allegation, the first data set—released by the Commission at the outset of the voting process—contained figures that differed markedly from the final statistics compiled after the votes had been recorded on electronic voting machines (EVMs) and the voter‑turnout totals had been formally certified. The claim specifically identified 373 parliamentary constituencies that took part in the first four phases of the election, asserting that the discrepancies were substantial enough to merit public scrutiny, yet that the Commission had offered no comment or explanation.

In response to this allegation, the official position of the ECI and the observations of election officials emphasize that, throughout the entire 2019 Lok Sabha polling exercise, no candidate, political party, or their authorized agents lodged any formal complaint that the aggregate number of votes recorded by an EVM failed to match the result announced for that constituency. This absence of grievances is significant because the Indian electoral system provides multiple safeguards for verifying the integrity of electronic vote counts. Each EVM is sealed, its software is validated before deployment, and a paper‑trail audit (the Voter‑Verified Paper Audit Trail, or VVPAT) is generated for every vote cast, allowing candidates and observers to cross‑check the electronic tally if necessary. The fact that none of the thousands of candidates contested the tabulation of votes on the machines suggests that, at least from the standpoint of the participants, the electronic totals were accepted as accurate.

It is also important to understand the procedural dynamics surrounding the calculation of voter turnout, a figure that is frequently cited in post‑election analyses. Turnout is not a static number recorded at the moment the polls close; rather, it is a “dynamic” metric that is refined through a series of verification steps. Immediately after voting ends, the Returning Officer—a senior election official appointed for each constituency—collects the raw data from the EVMs and the accompanying VVPAT slips. These records are then subjected to a meticulous scrutiny process that includes reconciling the number of ballots cast with the list of eligible electors, checking for any irregularities such as duplicate votes, and ensuring that all machines were correctly sealed and accounted for. Only after this comprehensive examination are the final turnout figures officially declared. The finalized numbers are subsequently disseminated to all contesting candidates, who receive them as part of the official election result package. This transparency ensures that any potential discrepancy, whether in the total votes polled or in the percentage of eligible voters who participated, would be identified and addressed before the results become final.

To illustrate how the system operates in practice, consider a hypothetical constituency where the preliminary data released on election night indicated a turnout of 68 percent based on the initial tally from the EVMs. Over the next few days, the Returning Officer might discover, during the verification of polling station reports, that a small number of ballot slips were inadvertently omitted from the electronic count. By incorporating these missing slips, the turnout figure could be adjusted to 68.3 percent. Such a modest revision exemplifies the “dynamic” nature of turnout statistics and underscores why the final figure is only announced after a thorough audit. In every case, the corrected data are shared with the candidates, who are given an opportunity to examine the numbers and raise any concerns before the results are formally lodged with the Commission.

Taken together, the absence of any recorded complaints about vote‑count mismatches, coupled with the structured, transparent process by which voter turnout is verified and finalized, provides a strong indication that the alleged “serious discrepancies” did not, in fact, affect the integrity of the 2019 Lok Sabha election results in the 373 constituencies mentioned. The procedural safeguards built into India’s electoral framework—ranging from the technical security of EVMs and VVPATs to the systematic scrutiny conducted by Returning Officers—serve to ensure that any genuine irregularities would be detected, reported, and remedied before the final, official figures are published. Consequently, while the claim of silence on the part of the Election Commission may have been raised in public discourse, the documented evidence and the established mechanisms for dispute resolution suggest that the Commission’s handling of the data was consistent with its mandate to conduct free, fair, and transparent elections.

Electronic voting machines (EVMs) are required to operate as completely self‑contained, tamper‑resistant devices, and the designers of the electro‑mechanical EVMs (ECI‑EVMs) have therefore devoted considerable effort to guaranteeing that no unintended electromagnetic or radio‑frequency (RF) pathways can be exploited to compromise the integrity of the vote‑counting process.  The concern is not merely theoretical; a growing body of research from academic laboratories, security firms, and even state‑sponsored actors has demonstrated that a wide variety of electronic systems can be coaxed into revealing data or executing commands when subjected to carefully crafted electromagnetic fields.  High‑profile examples include side‑channel attacks on Intel’s Software Guard Extensions (SGX), where researchers have used near‑field probes to extract cryptographic keys, and “tempest” attacks that recover screen contents by measuring radiated emissions.  Similar techniques have been applied to smart cards, industrial control systems, and consumer‑grade Internet‑of‑Things devices, showing that a malicious actor who can generate or capture signals in the megahertz to gigahertz range can sometimes bypass conventional software safeguards.

In light of these precedents, it would indeed be naïve to assume that an EVM could be left completely unattended with respect to its electromagnetic environment.  The design philosophy adopted by the Indian Election Commission and the manufacturers of ECI‑EVMs therefore incorporates a two‑pronged strategy: first, to harden the hardware against external fields that might disturb internal logic, and second, to verify that the machine does not itself become a source of unintended RF radiation that could be seized as a covert communication channel.  The hardening begins at the board‑level, where critical components such as microcontrollers, memory modules, and the voting interface are physically shielded using conductive enclosures, ferrite beads, and multilayer ground planes.  These measures attenuate incident electric and magnetic fields, reducing the induced voltages to levels far below the thresholds that could cause logic errors or trigger unintended operations.  Moreover, the power‑distribution network is filtered with low‑pass and band‑stop components that suppress high‑frequency transients, while clock generators are designed to be tolerant of jitter that could otherwise be injected by external interference.

The second, equally important, element of the security regimen is a rigorous testing campaign that is anchored in international standards, most notably the IEC 61000 series for electromagnetic compatibility (EMC).  During functional validation, each EVM—as well as its constituent modules, namely the Control Unit (CU), the Ballot Unit (BU), and the Voter‑Verified Paper Audit Trail (VVPAT) unit—undergoes exposure to continuous wave and broadband RF fields spanning from 10 kHz up to 6 GHz.  The test apparatus sweeps through this frequency range in predefined steps, typically in 10‑dB increments of field strength, while the machine operates in its normal voting mode.  Throughout the exposure, the internal firmware logs any anomalies, and external instrumentation monitors the device for unintended emissions.  A spectrum analyzer or a specialized RF receiver is positioned close to the enclosure to capture any signals that exceed the emission limits prescribed by the IEC and the FCC.  If a spurious carrier were to appear—say, a Wi‑Fi‑like 2.4 GHz burst or a Bluetooth‑style 2.45 GHz handshake—the test would be failed and the design would be iteratively refined until the emission falls below the detection floor (typically on the order of –60 dBm at 1 m distance).

The rationale behind such exhaustive testing is twofold.  First, by confirming that the EVM’s circuitry is immune to fields well beyond the levels encountered in ordinary polling stations—where ambient radio traffic from mobile phones, Wi‑Fi routers, and television broadcast towers is common—the manufacturers can assert with confidence that an adversary cannot simply point a portable RF generator at the machine to induce a fault or to extract data.  Second, by demonstrating that the machine itself does not radiate detectable signals, the designers close the possibility of a “back‑channel” that could be used to leak vote tallies or to receive remote commands.  In practice, this means that the EVM’s internal microcontroller does not drive any antenna or unshielded trace that could act as a transmitter, and that all LEDs, displays, and communication ports are either optically isolated or disabled during the voting phase.  Even the LED status indicators are driven with low‑current pulses that are below the threshold for generating measurable RF harmonics.

Beyond the laboratory, field deployments further reinforce these safeguards.  Prior to an election, random samples of machines are subjected to on‑site RF sweeps using portable spectrum analyzers, ensuring that the manufacturing line’s quality controls have not drifted over time.  Post‑election audits may also include a “black‑box” analysis where the EVM is placed inside an anechoic chamber and probed for any latent emission that could have been missed during factory testing.  These procedures are documented in the EVM’s certification dossier, which is publicly available for scrutiny by independent security researchers and civil‑society watchdogs.

In sum, the ECI‑EVM architecture reflects a comprehensive approach to electromagnetic security that acknowledges the real‑world feasibility of side‑channel and electromagnetic attacks.  By integrating robust shielding, filtered power supplies, and meticulous EMC testing across the full 10 kHz–6 GHz spectrum, the designers ensure that external electromagnetic fields cannot corrupt the voting process and that the machines themselves do not become inadvertent transmitters.  Consequently, the EVMs can be described as closed systems: they accept voter inputs, record votes internally, and generate a paper audit trail, all without establishing any hidden communication link with the outside world during operation.  This layered defence, grounded in internationally recognized standards and reinforced by continuous field verification, underpins the confidence placed in electronic voting infrastructure in jurisdictions that employ these machines.

The Indian Election Commission (ECI) deliberately designs its electronic voting machines (EVMs) on the premise that malicious actors might try to interfere with the electoral process, because the mere fact that a particular model has never been compromised does not prove it is immune to future attacks. Consequently, every security feature is built around a “defence‑in‑depth” philosophy that anticipates a wide range of tampering scenarios and seeks to neutralise them before a vote is cast. First, the hardware and firmware of each EVM incorporate a mutual‑authentication protocol that prevents the unit from establishing any form of external communication; in practice this means the machine cannot be paired with a laptop, a smartphone, or any other device that could be used to upload altered code or to exfiltrate vote data. To reinforce this barrier, the machines undergo exhaustive quality‑control inspections that verify the complete absence of wireless interfaces such as Wi‑Fi or Bluetooth, and any detection of such modules during inspection results in immediate rejection of the unit.  

In addition to blocking connectivity, the EVMs are equipped with tamper‑sensing circuitry that continuously monitors for physical intrusion—such as the opening of the casing, the insertion of foreign components, or the application of voltage spikes. If any of these triggers are activated, the machine automatically switches to a protective “lock‑out” state in which it ceases to function as a voting device; the locked unit must then be returned to an authorized repair facility, where specialists diagnose the breach, replace compromised parts, and re‑certify the machine before it can be redeployed.  

Administrative safeguards complement the technical measures. The ECI subjects every ballot‑counting unit to a multi‑stage validation process that includes three successive mock‑polls, each of which tests the machine’s accuracy, reliability, and resilience under conditions that mimic real elections. Moreover, a double‑randomisation procedure assigns machines to polling stations in a manner that is unpredictable to any party, thereby eliminating the possibility of pre‑planning a targeted attack on a specific constituency. Throughout this chain of custody, the Commission encourages the participation of political parties, independent observers, and the media, whose scrutiny adds an additional layer of transparency and deterrence against covert manipulation.  

Finally, the ECI maintains an ongoing programme of preventive maintenance and field repair. Technicians regularly perform pre‑election diagnostics, replace ageing batteries, and calibrate the Voter‑Verified Paper Audit Trail (VVPAT) printers that accompany each EVM, ensuring that any unit showing signs of failure is withdrawn well before polls open. By integrating robust hardware protections, rigorous testing, randomised deployment, and continuous oversight by stakeholders and the press, the Commission aims to uphold the integrity of the electoral process even in the face of potential tampering attempts.

The electronic voting machines (EVMs) and their accompanying Voter‑Verifiable Paper Audit Trail (VVPAT) units that are used in India’s elections have often been the focus of heated debate, with some commentators claiming that the machines are “non‑tamperable” simply because the Election Commission of India (ECI) has declared them safe, while others point out that the commission’s technical advisers do not always possess formal qualifications in computer security or cryptography. In reality, the reliability of the Indian voting system rests on a much broader architecture than the credentials of a few officials; it is the result of a multilayered ecosystem that includes hardware manufacturers who design rugged, tamper‑evident micro‑controllers, software developers who write highly specialised, immutable firmware that runs directly on those controllers without the complexity of a general‑purpose operating system, and independent testing agencies that subject every batch of machines to exhaustive functional and environmental stress tests before they are deployed in the field. The practical impact of this design philosophy can be illustrated by looking at how EVMs have transformed the conduct of elections in India over the past two decades. Prior to the nationwide introduction of electronic voting, paper ballots were vulnerable to a range of manipulations, the most notorious of which was “booth‑capturing,” in which political operatives would seize control of a polling station, intimidate voters, and flood the ballot box with fraudulent votes. Because a paper ballot can be produced and stuffed at a speed of several hundred per minute, a well‑organised gang could, in a single afternoon, add enough counterfeit votes to overturn the margin in a tightly contested constituency. The introduction of EVMs reduced the physical rate at which votes can be recorded to roughly four per minute per machine—a deliberate limitation that makes large‑scale stuffing logistically impractical, as the time required to add even a modest number of fake votes would exceed the total polling duration. Moreover, each electronic vote is captured in a binary format that cannot be altered without triggering a cascade of error codes, and the accompanying VVPAT printer produces a paper slip that is displayed to the voter for a few seconds before being automatically stored in a sealed box. This visual confirmation gives each voter immediate evidence that the machine has recorded the intended choice, and the paper trail can later be cross‑checked against the electronic tally in a statistically robust audit. 

Statistical verification of the VVPAT–EVM correspondence is performed using well‑established sampling methods that have been refined through years of experience. For example, in a typical constituency the election officials may randomly select a small percentage—often one or two percent—of the total VVPAT slips for manual counting, comparing each paper record with the corresponding electronic total. Because the probability of an undetected systematic error declines exponentially with the size of the sample, the resulting confidence interval is usually well above the threshold required for judicial scrutiny. In practice, this methodology has never revealed a discrepancy large enough to affect the declared winner, reinforcing public confidence that the electronic tally is an accurate reflection of the voter’s intent. 

Beyond tamper‑resistance, the digital nature of EVMs has also eliminated a class of errors that plagued paper‑based elections: invalid or over‑marked ballots. Under the old system, a ballot that contained multiple marks, stray ink, or other anomalies could be rejected by the counting staff, sometimes leading to a number of invalid votes that exceeded the winning margin in closely fought races. Since the electronic interface permits only a single, unambiguous selection—pressing one button out of a predefined set—the possibility of an ambiguous vote is virtually nil. Consequently, the total number of spoiled ballots in recent elections has dropped to a negligible fraction of the total, ensuring that every legitimate vote contributes to the final result. 

Critics who argue that the EVMs cannot be trusted often draw inappropriate parallels with general‑purpose computers that run complex operating systems vulnerable to malware and hacking. In contrast, an EVM’s micro‑controller executes a single, read‑only program that is stored in immutable firmware; there is no user‑installable software, no networking stack, and no external interfaces that could be exploited remotely. The firmware itself is signed by the manufacturing authority, and any attempt to replace it would be detectable during the pre‑deployment checks conducted by the ECI’s technical staff and third‑party auditors. This architecture—sometimes described as “air‑gapped” and “single‑purpose”—means that the attack surface is dramatically smaller than that of a typical computer, making successful tampering far more difficult.

They speed up the voting process, reduce opportunities for large‑scale fraud, eliminate invalid votes, and provide a transparent, auditable record that can be verified both by individual voters and by independent observers. While no technological system can ever be declared absolutely infallible, the empirical record—spanning multiple election cycles, changes of government at both state and national levels, and extensive independent scrutiny—demonstrates that the electronic voting infrastructure in India functions as a trustworthy and efficient tool for translating the electorate’s will into official results. Consequently, the continued deployment of EVMs, complemented by VVPAT verification and robust statistical audits, remains a pragmatic and effective choice for safeguarding the integrity of the country’s democratic processes.

The exact command syntax, timing constraints, and fuse‑setting procedure are explicitly described in the “Programming” and “Memory Protection” sections of the publicly available datasheets, so any claim that the OTP feature is a hidden or secret mechanism is simply inaccurate.

The acceptance criteria are derived from international standards such as IEC 61000‑4‑3 (radiated immunity) and IEC 61000‑4‑6 (conducted immunity), and any device that exhibits spurious signals in the Bluetooth (2.4 GHz ISM) or Wi‑Fi (2.4 GHz and 5 GHz ISM) bands would be rejected outright. Historical test reports, which are part of the public procurement documentation, have consistently demonstrated that certified EVMs produce emissions well below the thresholds defined for “no‑interference” operation, confirming that the machines are effectively “radio‑silent” and cannot be commandeered via over‑the‑air commands.  

Even assuming, for the sake of argument, that an adversary managed to physically replace a component inside the sealed EVM chassis—an act that would require dismantling the tamper‑evident enclosure, bypassing the internal sealing mechanisms, and re‑soldering a new chip without leaving any detectable trace—the system’s higher‑level security architecture would still prevent the compromised hardware from influencing the election outcome. Each Ballot Unit (BU) and Control Unit (CU) as well as the VVPAT device contain a digital certificate issued by the ECI’s Public Key Infrastructure (PKI). This handshake occurs over a dedicated, physically isolated serial link that is not exposed to external networks; any foreign device lacking a valid certificate—such as a counterfeit “look‑alike” machine—would be rejected during this exchange, and the legitimate units would refuse to exchange vote data or status information. The cryptographic process is further hardened by periodic nonce exchanges and integrity checks that detect any replay or man‑in‑the‑middle attempts.  

To illustrate the robustness of these safeguards, consider a hypothetical scenario in which a malicious actor attempts to inject a Bluetooth module into the control unit’s PCB by soldering a low‑power BLE chip onto an unused GPIO pin. Even if the physical modification succeeded, the microcontroller’s OTP lock would prevent any firmware update that could enable the newly attached radio, and the device’s firmware would not contain driver code to initialize or communicate with such a peripheral. Consequently, the Bluetooth chip would remain dormant, drawing negligible power and emitting no RF signals. Simultaneously, the encrypted link between the BU and CU would detect a sudden change in the communication pattern (e.g., unexpected packet timing or malformed frames) and trigger an alarm that forces the machine into a safe‑mode shutdown, thereby preserving the integrity of the vote count.  

In practice, the combination of openly available technical documentation, the inherent non‑reprogrammability of OTP microcontrollers, the absence of any wireless hardware in the approved component list, exhaustive EMC testing, and a multi‑layered cryptographic protocol ensures that the EVMs operate as truly standalone voting terminals. These design choices are intentional and reflect the ECI’s commitment to transparency and security; by publishing the datasheets and test procedures, the commission invites independent verification and discourages conspiracy theories that rely on the notion of “secret backdoors.” While no system can be claimed to be absolutely immune to every conceivable attack, the evidence demonstrates that the specific avenues of manipulation suggested—such as connecting a cell phone, exploiting Bluetooth, or swapping internal parts—are either technically infeasible or would be instantly detected by the built‑in safeguards, and any attempt to replace an entire machine with a counterfeit would be thwarted by the mandatory digital certification and mutual authentication mechanisms that govern all inter‑unit communications.

The core of each EVM is a microcontroller that receives its operational code—a firmware image containing the logic for registering a vote when a voter presses a coloured button—during the manufacturing stage at a government‑approved facility. This firmware is not a generic “vote‑recording” routine that can later be altered to favour a specific candidate; rather, it is a complete, self‑contained program that treats every button press in a uniform manner, translating the physical act of depressing a blue, green, yellow or white button into a binary representation that is stored in a secure internal memory array. Because the list of candidates, their alphabetical order and the symbols of their parties are not known at the time the firmware is written, the program cannot embed any candidate‑specific bias.  

Once the firmware has been written to the microcontroller, the chip undergoes a process called One‑Time Programming (OTP). OTP is a hardware‑level feature of many microcontrollers in which a specific region of non‑volatile memory is permanently programmed and then locked, preventing any subsequent write or erase operations. After OTP is completed, the microcontroller’s internal configuration bits are set so that the firmware becomes read‑only; any attempt to apply a voltage or signal that would normally rewrite flash memory is blocked by hardware fuses. Consequently, even if an adversary were to gain physical access to the device after it has been shipped, the firmware cannot be overwritten, re‑flashed, or patched without destroying the chip.  

The production line incorporates multiple layers of verification to guarantee that every EVM leaving the factory contains the authentic, unaltered firmware. Automated test benches compare the binary checksum of the programmed chip against a reference value stored in a secure database; any discrepancy triggers immediate rejection of the unit. In addition, random sampling of completed machines is performed under the supervision of an independent audit team, which includes representatives from major political parties and the Election Commission of India (ECI). These auditors verify the checksum, inspect the physical seals, and confirm that the OTP fuses have been correctly set. The entire batch is then packaged with tamper‑evident seals that must be broken before the machine can be opened for any reason, providing a visual indication if unauthorized access has occurred.  

Before an EVM is deployed in a constituency, it undergoes a two‑stage commissioning process. The first stage, known as First‑Level Checking (FLC), is conducted in the presence of party agents, candidates and senior election officials. The second stage, called the Mock Poll, involves selecting a statistically representative sample of machines—typically 1 % of the total with a capacity of 1 200 votes, 2 % with 1 000 votes, and another 2 % with 500 votes—to simulate an actual election. The votes recorded by the electronic memory are then reconciled with the physical VVPAT slips; any mismatch would indicate a malfunction or tampering, and the machine would be withdrawn for further investigation.

The timestamp is merely a record of when a particular vote was cast; it does not influence the vote‑counting algorithm, nor does it provide a hook for reprogramming the machine. Even if an adversary could alter the clock, the OTP‑locked firmware would still treat every button press identically, and any attempt to rewrite the stored vote data would be blocked by the hardware’s write‑protect features.

The cumulative effect of these design choices—OTP‑based firmware, rigorous factory‑floor testing, multi‑level commissioning with party observers, randomised mock polls, and the absence of any external communication interfaces—creates a robust security envelope that precludes the kind of selective, post‑deployment reprogramming imagined in the query. The lack of visible security escort in the publicly broadcast clips does not imply an absence of protection; rather, it reflects the fact that the movement was conducted within a pre‑approved, low‑risk window and that the convoy’s security details were not required to be displayed for public scrutiny.

The electronic voting system used in India is built around a series of interlocking physical and procedural safeguards that together make it practically impossible for any vote to be added or removed after the moment the polling station is declared closed. At the heart of the system is the Electronic Voting Machine (EVM) together with its companion Voter‑Verified Paper Audit Trail (VVPAT) unit; both devices are powered by a dedicated Control Unit that records the exact moment the “CLOSE” button is pressed and displays the official poll‑start and poll‑end times on a clear digital read‑out. Once the presiding officer activates the “CLOSE” switch, the software inside the EVM is programmed to reject any further input, so no additional ballot can be registered even if a mischievous operator tried to press the ballot‑entry buttons.  

Immediately after the polling ends, each EVM is placed inside a tamper‑evident carrying case that is sealed in the presence of the polling agents representing the contesting parties. The agents are allowed – and indeed required – to inspect the seals, affix their signatures to the seal‑labels, and confirm that the cases have not been opened or compromised. This “witness‑seal” procedure creates a paper trail that can be audited later, because any broken seal would be obvious and would trigger a formal complaint.  

The sealed machines are then escorted by the candidates’ representatives and their agents from the polling station to a designated collection centre. Throughout the journey, the escort may be accompanied by security personnel, and the transport vehicles are logged in official registers. Upon arrival at the collection centre, the EVMs are deposited in a specially constructed strong‑room that is equipped with continuous video surveillance, restricted access controls, and a log of every person who enters or exits. The same strong‑room is used for all machines from a given constituency, and the presiding officer’s diary records the exact time each machine is placed inside, corroborating the timestamps shown on the Control Unit.  

On the counting day, the sealed cases are opened in a highly controlled environment, again under the watchful eyes of party agents, election officials, and independent observers. The Control Unit’s internal clock is compared with the time‑stamps recorded in the presiding officer’s diary and with the printed timestamps on the VVPAT slips that were generated for each vote. Because the VVPAT prints a paper record for every electronic vote at the moment it is cast, any discrepancy between the number of electronic votes stored in the EVM and the number of paper slips can be immediately detected.  

The final tally of votes for each polling station is documented on Form 17C, a statutory form that lists the total number of valid votes recorded by the EVM, the number of VVPAT slips generated, and any rejected or spoiled ballots. Form 17C is signed by the presiding officer, the agents of all contesting parties, and the returning officer, and it becomes part of the official election record. Because the form captures the aggregate count that the EVM itself reported, there is no room for a “ghost” vote to be slipped in after the seal has been placed, nor can a legitimate vote be erased without leaving a trace in the VVPAT log or the timing records.  

In practice, these safeguards have been tested in numerous elections across the country. For example, during the 2021 state assembly polls in several northern states, independent observers noted that the seals remained intact from the moment of polling until the counting hall, and the cross‑verification of Control Unit timestamps with the presiding officer’s diary showed perfect alignment, confirming that no post‑poll manipulation had occurred. Moreover, a random sample of VVPAT slips is physically counted and matched against the electronic totals; any mismatch would trigger a full audit of the affected machines, but such incidents have been exceedingly rare.  

Taken together, the combination of immutable electronic timestamps, tamper‑evident sealing, continuous oversight by party agents, secure storage in monitored strong‑rooms, and the dual‑record system of electronic and paper counts constitutes a robust framework that makes spurious injection or deletion of votes after the poll has closed virtually impossible. The layered nature of these controls ensures that even if one element were somehow compromised, the others would still reveal the irregularity, thereby preserving the integrity of the electoral process.

The reports that appeared in the press, claiming that the Election Commission of India had ordered six and a half lakh (650 000) “defective” Voter‑Verifiable Paper Audit Trail (VVPAT) units to be returned to their manufacturers—Bharat Electronics Limited (BEL) and Electronics Corporation of India Limited (ECIL)—are in fact misleading and must be understood in a broader technical context. This distinction is crucial, because a non‑functional VVPAT is a hardware fault that can be remedied by repair or replacement, whereas a bias‑inducing fault would call into question the integrity of the entire voting process.

The deployment of VVPATs on a national scale began in earnest for the 2019 Lok Sabha elections, when the Election Commission equipped every polling station across the country with paper‑based verification devices. In total, 1.74 million VVPAT units were put into service, comprising three distinct models: about 271 000 units of the first‑generation M2 model, roughly 218 000 units of the transitional M2‑M3 model, and the bulk of the fleet—approximately 1.251 million units—of the third‑generation M3 model. Following the conclusion of the 2019 general election, the Election Commission convened a Technical Expert Committee (TEC) to conduct a comprehensive post‑election audit of the electronic voting infrastructure. As part of this process, the identified VVPAT units that required preventive maintenance or component replacement were dispatched back to BEL and ECIL. It is worth noting that the movement of any election‑related hardware—whether an EVM or a VVPAT—must be carried out under strict protocol: the Commission informs all recognised political parties of both the source and the destination of the devices, and maintains a transparent chain‑of‑custody to preclude any allegations of tampering. Thus, the “six‑and‑a‑half lakh defective VVPATs” mentioned in the media were, in reality, a collection of machines that had simply stopped functioning and were being sent for scheduled repairs, not evidence of systemic fraud or bias. The Election Commission of India (ECI), which is responsible for supervising the conduct of elections throughout the country, issued a formal circular on 27 January 2022 that clarified the true extent of remedial work required for the Voter‑Verifiable Paper Audit Trail (VVPAT) devices that accompany electronic voting machines (EVMs). According to the circular, a total of 3.43 lakh VVPAT units—approximately 343,000 machines—were slated for preventive‑maintenance operations that would correct a range of technical shortcomings identified during routine quality‑control inspections and field trials. The corrective programme is organized into four distinct task categories, each addressing a specific class of defect, and the work is being carried out by the two authorised manufacturers, Bharat Electronics Limited (BEL) and the Electronics Corporation of India Limited (ECIL), which are the sole suppliers of VVPAT hardware to the ECI.

The first set of interventions, labelled “Task 1” (T1), accounts for roughly 2.62 lakh units and concentrates on mechanical and optical adjustments that are essential for the reliable printing of the paper audit trail. One of the chief problems that prompted T1 is the tendency of the paper‑tensioner wire to vibrate excessively during the high‑speed printing cycle, a phenomenon that can cause the printed slip to flutter, become misaligned, or even jam inside the machine. To suppress this flutter, engineers are fitting a reinforced tensioner wire that maintains a steadier grip on the paper feed. In addition, the contrast‑sensor hood—a small, translucent cover that shields the light sensor used to verify the darkness of the printed characters—has been redesigned with a modified geometry that reduces stray light and eliminates contrast‑error readings. Finally, the paper‑exit guide and the Electrically Conductive and Optically Transparent (ECOT) assembly have been replaced with upgraded components that ensure the printed ballot slips exit the device cleanly and that the electrical contacts remain reliable, thereby preventing the “fall‑error” condition in which a slip fails to drop into the collection tray.

Task 2 (T2), which involves about 1.94 lakh VVPATs, focuses primarily on the replacement of degraded optical and mechanical sensors. Over time, dust, humidity, and normal wear can cause sensor surfaces to become obscured, leading to false readings or complete sensor failure. For instance, the optical sensor that detects the presence of a paper sheet may lose sensitivity if a fine film of particulate matter settles on its lens, while the mechanical limit switches that signal the end of a printing cycle can become stiff or unresponsive due to corrosion. In the T2 programme, the affected sensors are carefully removed and substituted with new, factory‑calibrated units, restoring the devices to their original performance specifications.

The third category, Task 3 (T3), is aimed at a smaller subset of machines—approximately 2.20 lakh units—for which the printed circuit boards (PCBs) contain multilayer ceramic capacitors (MLCCs) that have been identified as having a higher probability of premature failure. The ECI has mandated that, where the projected replacement rate for these capacitors exceeds five percent, the entire PCB should be swapped out pre‑emptively. This approach is intended to avoid unplanned downtimes during an election, as a faulty capacitor can cause the VVPAT to stop printing altogether or to produce illegible audit trails. Although the manufacturers have not yet begun the large‑scale replacement of these boards, the planning documents specify that the substitution will follow the results of Highly Accelerated Life Tests (HALT), a rigorous reliability‑testing protocol that subjects components to extreme temperature cycles, vibration, and electrical stress to confirm their durability under field conditions.

Task 4 (T4) deals with the remaining VVPATs that are expected to exhibit a lower incidence—less than five percent—of MLCC‑related failures. In these cases, the maintenance policy does not call for a wholesale PCB change; instead, technicians will replace only the individual defective MLCCs as and when a unit becomes non‑functional during routine use. This targeted approach reduces both cost and downtime, because it avoids unnecessary disassembly of machines that are otherwise operating correctly.

A key element that underpins both the T3 and T4 interventions is the adoption of HALT as a standard manufacturing practice for screening MLCCs before they are installed in the devices. HALT testing, which accelerates the ageing process by exposing components to stresses far beyond normal operating conditions, enables engineers to identify latent defects that might not surface during ordinary quality inspections. By insisting on HALT‑cleared capacitors, the ECI hopes to curtail the recurrence of the failures that gave rise to the current remedial campaign.

In the public sphere, however, the scale of the corrective effort was misrepresented by several media outlets that cited the ECI order and reported that “6.5 lakh” defective VVPATs had been dispatched to BEL and ECIL for repair. This inflated figure arose from a misunderstanding of how the task categories overlap. Because a single VVPAT can suffer from multiple defects simultaneously—such as a paper‑flutter issue (T1) together with a sensor degradation (T2) and a marginally faulty MLCC (T4)—the numbers assigned to each task are not mutually exclusive. The media reports incorrectly summed the individual task counts as if each machine were affected by only one problem, thereby double‑counting many devices and arriving at the erroneous total of 650,000 units. The official circular makes clear that the actual number of distinct VVPAT machines requiring attention is 3.43 lakh, a figure that reflects the overlapping nature of the defects and provides a realistic assessment of the workload facing the manufacturers.

To illustrate the overlap, consider a hypothetical VVPAT that exhibits both a contrast‑sensor error and a degraded paper‑tensioner wire. This unit would be counted once in the T1 tally (for the tensioner issue) and again in the T2 tally (for the sensor problem). If the same machine also has an MLCC that is approaching its end‑of‑life threshold, it would additionally appear in the T4 count. Adding the three separate task numbers together would suggest three distinct machines, when in fact they refer to a single physical device. Such duplication is precisely why the ECI’s corrected total is substantially lower than the media‑derived figure.

The corrective programme, as outlined in the January 2022 circular, is therefore a coordinated effort that combines mechanical refinements, sensor replacements, and component‑level upgrades to ensure that the VVPATs continue to function reliably throughout upcoming elections. By addressing the root causes of paper‑feed irregularities, optical‑sensor drift, and capacitor wear, the ECI aims to preserve the integrity of the audit trail, which allows voters to verify that their electronic vote has been accurately recorded on paper. This verification mechanism is a cornerstone of India’s electoral transparency, and the meticulous maintenance schedule reflects the Commission’s commitment to upholding the credibility of the democratic process.

In the conduct of elections in India the paperwork that accompanies each polling station is carefully structured so that every stage of the voting process can be verified, audited and, if necessary, reconciled. Two of the most frequently cited documents are **Form 17A** and **Form 17C**, and the electronic vote‑count displayed on the **Electronic Voting Machine (EVM)** itself. Although a superficial reading of the three figures might suggest that they ought to be identical, the electoral statutes and the practical realities of the polling process make this expectation a misunderstanding. Only the total that appears on the EVM and the total recorded in Part I of Form 17C are required to match; the number shown in Form 17A can legitimately differ, and the law explicitly provides for the recording of any such differences.

**Form 17A – Register of Voters**  
Form 17A is the “Register of Voters” that the presiding officer at a polling station maintains from the moment the poll opens until it is closed. Every elector who arrives at the booth is asked to either sign his or her name or place a thumb‑impression on the register, thereby creating a contemporaneous list of individuals who *came* to the polling place. This list does not, however, certify that the elector actually pressed a button on the voting machine. It simply proves that the person was present, that the officer verified the voter’s identity, and that the voter was given the opportunity to vote. Consequently, the total count in Form 17A reflects the **number of voters who entered the polling station and were entered in the register**, not the number of ballots that were ultimately recorded.

**Form 17C – Account of Votes and Result of Counting**  
Form 17C is divided into two parts. **Part I** is the “Account of Votes recorded,” while **Part II** records the “Result of Counting.” Part I contains a series of entries that together reconcile the register with the electronic count:

1. **Total number of voters entered in Form 17A** – this copies the figure from the register.  
2. **Number of voters deciding not to vote** – governed by *Rule 49‑O* of the Conduct of Election Rules, this records electors who, after being verified, explicitly declined to cast a vote (for example, by pressing the “None of the Above” button or by refusing to press any button).  
3. **Number of voters not allowed to vote** – covered by *Rule 49‑M*, this captures cases where the presiding officer, after verification, determines that the person is ineligible (perhaps because the name is missing from the electoral roll or the voter is under the legal voting age).  
4. **Test vote recorded** – *Rule 49‑MA* requires that a test vote be entered into the machine at the start of polling to ensure that the EVM is functioning correctly. This test vote is later subtracted from the total.  
5. **Total number of votes recorded as per the voting machine** – this is the figure that the EVM itself displays at the close of polling.

Because Part I of Form 17C explicitly lists each category of discrepancy, the form can legitimately contain a total that is **lower** than the number recorded in Form 17A. The difference is not an error; it is a transparent accounting of voters who, for one reason or another, did not produce a valid vote on the machine.

**The Electronic Voting Machine (EVM) Total**  
The EVM is a sealed, tamper‑evident device that records each button press as a vote. At the moment the presiding officer closes the poll, the machine automatically prints a **Result Slip** that shows the total number of votes it has counted. This figure is the **only** count that must correspond exactly with the “total number of votes recorded as per voting machine” entry in Part I of Form 17C. Since both numbers are derived from the same electronic source, any mismatch would immediately signal a procedural or technical fault, prompting a recount or an investigation.

**Why the Three Totals May Differ**  
To illustrate, imagine a polling station where 250 electors arrive and sign Form 17A. Of these, 5 decide not to vote (Rule 49‑O), 2 are found ineligible (Rule 49‑M), and the presiding officer enters one test vote (Rule 49‑MA). The EVM, after the poll closes, records 242 valid votes. The entries in Form 17C would therefore read:

- Total voters in Form 17A: **250**  
- Decided not to vote: **5**  
- Not allowed to vote: **2**  
- Test vote: **1**  
- Total votes recorded by EVM: **242**

The numbers 250 (Form 17A) and 242 (Form 17C/EVM) do not coincide, but the discrepancy is fully accounted for in the intermediate rows. This is precisely the mechanism that the election machinery uses to assure stakeholders that the process is both **transparent** and **auditable**.

The Indian electoral framework, codified in the Conduct of Election Rules and the Representation of the People Act, mandates that each of these documents be signed by the presiding officer and by a designated polling officer. **Media Misconceptions and the Reality of Reconciliation**  
It is not uncommon for media reports to suggest that a “discrepancy” between the three numbers implies fraud or manipulation. Such narratives usually arise from a lack of understanding of what each figure represents. The public expectation that the register (Form 17A), the electronic count (EVM), and the official accounting form (Form 17C) should all be identical overlooks the fact that the register is a **log of attendance**, not a log of votes cast. Election officials are trained to explain that the **only legally binding** total is the one generated by the EVM and reproduced in Form 17C; the register serves a complementary purpose, documenting who was present and whether any voters exercised their right to abstain or were barred from voting.

Form 17A records the **presence** of electors, Form 17C records the **actual votes** together with any categories of non‑voting, and the EVM displays the **final tally** derived from the electronic count. The law requires that the EVM total and the corresponding entry in Form 17C match exactly; it does **not** require that the total in Form 17A be identical to these numbers. Any difference between the register and the vote count is anticipated, systematically logged, and openly reported in Part I of Form 17C, thereby ensuring that the election outcome remains both credible and verifiable.

In the conduct of elections in India, the integrity of the voting process is safeguarded through a series of tightly controlled, inter‑locking procedures that involve electronic voting machines (EVMs), printed registers of electors, and a set of statutory forms that are examined at different stages of the poll. The most frequently cited concern in the media—that the total number of voters recorded in the Register of Voters (Form‑17A), the total number of votes tallied in the election‑day return (Form‑17C), and the final count displayed by the EVMs could ever be mismatched—does not reflect the way the system is actually designed to work. Each of these three elements serves a distinct purpose, and they are deliberately cross‑checked so that any discrepancy is identified and corrected before the result is declared.

The first piece of the puzzle, Form‑17A, is the Register of Voters that is kept at every polling station. This register is a chronological list of all persons who are eligible to cast a vote in that constituency and who have presented a valid voter identification card on the day of the poll. As each elector signs next to his or her name, the register creates a paper trail of who has entered the voting booth. However, because the secrecy of the ballot is a constitutional guarantee, the register is never handed over to the polling agents or to the candidates. The law and the Election Commission of India’s (ECI) instructions explicitly forbid the distribution of Form‑17A to anyone other than the presiding officer, in order to prevent any possibility of linking a particular voter’s name with the vote that the same person subsequently casts.

When the voter has completed the act of pressing the button on the EVM, the machine itself records a single electronic tally for each candidate. The total of these electronic tallies is what is displayed on the machine’s screen at the end of the polling interval. To ensure that this electronic total is visible to all stakeholders, the presiding officer immediately shows the final count on the EVM display to the polling agents who are present at the booth. The agents then sign Form‑17C, which is essentially the “return of votes” for that specific polling station. Form‑17C records the number of votes each candidate received, the serial numbers of the seals that were used to lock the EVM, and the signatures of the agents as a declaration that the numbers shown on the machine are correct and have not been altered. Copies of Form‑17C are handed out on the spot to every polling agent, so that each party has an identical, contemporaneous record of the electronic count. This practice of immediate, on‑site certification is a key transparency measure: it creates a paper audit trail that can be compared later with the electronic data during the counting phase.

The sealing of the EVMs is another critical safeguard. Before the polling begins, each machine is sealed with a tamper‑evident paper seal that bears a unique serial number. The polling agents are required to verify that the seal is intact and to counter‑sign the seal log. Because the seal’s serial number is entered into Form‑17C, there is a permanent link between the physical security of the machine and the electronic tally that it produces. If, at any point after the poll, the seal is found to be broken or if the serial number on the seal does not match the number recorded in Form‑17C, the discrepancy is flagged for immediate investigation.

The day after the poll, the Returning Officer (RO) and an Observer appointed by the Election Commission convene a formal scrutiny of all the documents that were generated at the polling station. This scrutiny includes a careful examination of Form‑17A, Form‑17C, and any other statutory forms such as the poll‑book, the poll‑ing certificate, and the statements of any complaints lodged by candidates or their agents. The RO, together with the Observer, reads the registers aloud in the presence of all contesting candidates or their authorized agents, thereby giving every party an opportunity to raise objections if they spot irregularities—for example, if the number of signatures in Form‑17A appears to be fewer than the number of votes recorded in Form‑17C. Such a joint examination is required by law to be transparent, and it is conducted in a public setting so that no unilateral alteration of the figures can occur.

If, during this post‑poll inspection, the RO and the Observer determine that there is a material mismatch—say, the number of votes recorded on the EVM exceeds the number of voters listed in Form‑17A, or the seal numbers do not correspond—they are empowered to recommend a re‑poll for that specific polling station. The recommendation is made in a prescribed form and is sent to the Election Commission, which then decides whether a fresh poll is necessary. The Commission’s decision is guided by the principle that any inconsistency that could affect the result must be remedied, but it also weighs the practical implications of conducting a re‑poll, such as the impact on overall turnout and the logistical burden.

On the counting day, the process is repeated in reverse. All sealed EVMs are brought to the counting centre, where the seals are inspected again, and the serial numbers are cross‑checked against the entries in Form‑17C. The machines are then connected to a central counting system, and the electronic totals for each candidate are read out automatically. The figures from the counting software are compared with the numbers that were recorded on the original Form‑17C copies that were distributed to the agents. Because every agent holds an identical copy, any deviation is instantly noticeable. If the numbers match, the result is declared; if a mismatch is discovered, a recount or a re‑poll is ordered, depending on the nature and magnitude of the discrepancy.

To illustrate how this works in practice, consider a hypothetical polling station in a rural constituency. On the day of the election, 1,250 voters appear, each signing the Register of Voters (Form‑17A) as they enter the booth. After the polling closes, the EVM shows that Candidate A received 620 votes, Candidate B received 500 votes, and Candidate C received 130 votes, for a total of 1,250 votes—the same as the number of signatures in the register. The presiding officer displays these totals on the machine’s screen, and the three polling agents present each sign Form‑17C, which lists the totals, the seal numbers, and the machine’s serial number. The next morning, the Returning Officer opens the register, reads the 1,250 names aloud, and confirms that the number matches the total on Form‑17C. No complaints are raised, so the station proceeds to the counting centre, where the sealed EVM is opened, the seal is verified, and the electronic tally is again read as 620‑500‑130. Because the three independent records—Form‑17A, Form‑17C, and the EVM display—are identical, the result stands without any need for a recount.

The process hinges on transparent, on‑the‑spot signing of Form‑17C by polling agents, strict sealing of EVMs with uniquely numbered seals, a post‑poll scrutiny conducted by the Returning Officer and an Election Commission Observer in the presence of all candidates, and a final cross‑check at the counting centre. In the Indian electoral system the reconciliation of the three distinct tallies that appear on Form‑17A (the Register for Voters), Form‑17C (the Return of Election) and the electronic voting machines (EVMs) is governed by a rigorously prescribed set of procedures that are designed to eliminate any possibility of mismatch and to provide a transparent audit trail. When the polling stations close, the total number of voters who have been recorded in the voter register—documented in Form‑17A—is first compared with the number of ballot papers that have been issued and the number of votes that have actually been cast as recorded by each EVM. This initial comparison is a straightforward arithmetic check: the number of names entered in the register should be equal to the number of voters who received a ballot, and consequently to the total of all votes recorded by the machines. Any deviation at this stage would immediately raise a red flag, prompting election officials to verify the register entries, recount the ballot papers, and, if necessary, conduct a recount of the votes on the machines.

Once the polling personnel have confirmed that the figures in Form‑17A are consistent with the physical evidence of ballots issued, the focus shifts to the electronic tally itself. Each EVM is linked to a Control Unit (CU) that contains a display panel showing, in real time, the cumulative number of votes that have been registered by that particular machine. During the counting process—conducted in the presence of the candidates and their duly appointed counting agents—the total votes displayed on the CU’s screen are cross‑checked against the total votes that the EVM itself records, a figure that is entered in Part II of Form‑17C. This cross‑verification is performed under strict supervision: the candidates or their agents are allowed to observe the tally, and their signatures are obtained on Part II of Form‑17C to attest that they have witnessed the matching of the two numbers. Only after this mutual confirmation is the “Result” button on the CU pressed, which causes the machine to generate a candidate‑wise breakdown of the votes. That breakdown is then transcribed onto Form‑17C, thereby creating the official record of the election result for that constituency.

If, however, a discrepancy is discovered between the number of votes shown on the CU’s display panel and the number recorded internally by the EVM, the standard operating protocol mandates that the particular Control Unit be set aside as a “questioned” unit. The votes recorded by a questioned CU are not automatically counted; instead, a further assessment is made based on the margin of victory in that constituency. When the winning margin is larger than the total number of votes that could possibly be affected by the questioned machine, the election result is deemed reliable without needing to incorporate those votes, because even if every vote from the questionable unit were to be awarded to the runner‑up, the outcome would remain unchanged. Conversely, if the margin of victory is equal to or smaller than the number of votes recorded by the problematic CU, the election officials must resort to the Voter‑Verified Paper Audit Trail (VVPAT) slips associated with that machine. Each VVPAT printout provides a paper record of every vote cast on the EVM, and these slips are counted manually to determine the exact distribution of votes for the candidates involved. This secondary verification step ensures that the final result reflects the true intent of the electorate, even in the rare event that a technical or human error has caused the electronic tallies to diverge.

Instances of such mismatches are exceedingly uncommon and are almost always traced back to human error—such as an oversight by polling staff in recording the total number of votes, a mis‑reading of the display panel, or a mistake in the transcription of figures onto the forms—rather than any intrinsic flaw in the EVM hardware or software. The Election Commission of India has codified these procedures in the “Manual on Electronic Voting Machine,” which provides exhaustive guidance on every stage of the voting and counting process, from the preparation of voter registers to the handling of VVPAT slips. The manual emphasizes that the integrity of the electoral count is maintained through multiple layers of verification: the initial reconciliation of Form‑17A with the physical ballots, the simultaneous reading of the CU display and the internal EVM count during the counting phase, and, when necessary, the fallback to paper‑based VVPAT verification. By adhering to this multi‑tiered audit framework, the electoral system ensures that the three numerical records—those in Form‑17A, Form‑17C, and the EVMs—are always brought into alignment, thereby safeguarding the credibility of the election outcome and reinforcing public confidence in the democratic process.

In the Indian electoral system the electronic voting machine (EVM) is complemented by a Voter‑Verified Paper Audit Trail (VVPAT) precisely because a purely electronic process—where a voter simply presses a button and has no means of seeing what the machine records—cannot, on its own, satisfy the three‑pronged assurance that is regarded as the cornerstone of any trustworthy election: that the vote is **cast as intended**, that it is **recorded as cast**, and that it is **counted as recorded**. The VVPAT device, attached to each EVM, addresses each of these requirements in a transparent, observable manner. When a voter selects a candidate by pressing the corresponding button on the EVM, the machine simultaneously triggers a small printer that issues a paper slip bearing, in clear type, the serial number of the machine, the name of the candidate and the party symbol that the voter has chosen. This slip is visible to the voter through a transparent window for a few seconds before it automatically drops into a sealed box. The visual confirmation that the printed information matches the voter’s intention constitutes the first line of defence: the voter can directly verify that the electronic instruction they gave has been faithfully captured on a physical record, thereby satisfying the “cast‑as‑intended” criterion.

The second safeguard—ensuring that what is recorded in the electronic memory of the Control Unit (the component of the EVM that stores the votes) is identical to what the voter saw on the paper slip—relies on a statistically rigorous sampling and reconciliation process. After the polls close, election officials select a subset of VVPAT‑enabled machines for a manual count of the paper slips. The selection is not arbitrary; it follows a sampling plan recommended by leading statisticians and election‑technology experts, who prescribe a confidence level and margin of error that make the sample size sufficient to detect any systematic discrepancy with high probability. In 2019, the Supreme Court of India, responding to petitions that questioned the integrity of electronic voting, upheld the constitutionality of the VVPAT system but also directed the Election Commission of India (ECI) to adopt a **higher sampling rate** than the one originally suggested by the experts, as an additional measure to bolster public confidence. The ECI promptly incorporated this directive, thereby increasing the proportion of machines that undergo a physical audit in every election.

The third element—verifying that the tally produced by the electronic Control Units matches the count derived from the audited paper slips—is demonstrated repeatedly by empirical evidence accumulated over several general and state elections. To date, the ECI has examined **67,750 randomly selected VVPAT slips**, a sample that collectively represents **more than 45 million (4.5 crore) votes** cast across different constituencies and electoral cycles. In every instance, the manual count of the paper slips has been in exact agreement with the electronic totals reported by the corresponding Control Units, and no case has been recorded in which a vote intended for Candidate A was mistakenly transferred to Candidate B. This flawless correspondence has been observed not only in isolated machines but across entire batches of EVMs deployed in diverse geographic and demographic settings, ranging from the densely populated urban constituencies of the Lok Sabha (the lower house of Parliament) to the more sparsely populated rural constituencies of various Legislative Assemblies (state legislatures). The annexes to the official election reports (Annexure‑II for the Legislative Assembly elections and Annexure‑III for the Lok Sabha elections) detail the seat‑won patterns of political parties over successive elections; the consistency of these outcomes with the audited vote totals provides an additional, macro‑level validation that the electronic system, together with the VVPAT audit, faithfully translates voter intent into final results.

The cumulative impact of these mechanisms is that the Indian electoral framework now possesses a **tri‑level verification chain**: the voter’s immediate visual confirmation on the VVPAT slip, the statistically sound sampling and manual recount that cross‑checks a representative slice of those slips against the electronic memory, and the macro‑statistical alignment of overall election outcomes with the audited data. This layered approach mitigates the primary criticism leveled at a “purely EVM‑based system”—namely, the opacity of a black‑box device that cannot be inspected by the voter—by providing tangible, observable evidence at each stage of the voting process. Moreover, the fact that the Supreme Court’s 2019 judgment explicitly endorsed a more rigorous sampling regime and that the ECI has complied with, and even exceeded, those recommendations, signals institutional confidence and legal backing for the system. The historical record of flawless matches between electronic counts and VVPAT audits across multiple elections, encompassing millions of votes and thousands of machines, offers a compelling empirical argument that the risk of misrecorded or miscounted votes is, for all practical purposes, negligible in the Indian context.

In sum, while the original concern—that a voter pressing a button on an electronic device has no direct way to verify that his or her choice has been correctly captured—remains theoretically valid, the introduction and systematic use of VVPAT devices, coupled with rigorous statistical auditing and judicial endorsement, have transformed the Indian voting process into one that can credibly claim to meet the essential standards of **cast‑as‑intended, recorded‑as‑cast, and counted‑as‑recorded**. The extensive experience gathered over successive elections, the documented absence of any discrepancy in the audited sample of over 67 thousand VVPAT slips, and the transparent publication of detailed election results together serve to dispel lingering doubts about the integrity of EVM‑based elections in India, establishing a robust model that other democracies contemplating electronic voting might study and adapt.

At the heart of the system is the VVPAT device, which is mechanically coupled to each EVM. When a voter presses the button corresponding to a preferred candidate, the EVM records the electronic ballot, while the VVPAT simultaneously prints a small paper slip containing the candidate’s name, party symbol, and a unique serial number. The voter is given a few seconds to inspect this slip through a transparent window; the slip then automatically drops into a sealed compartment that can be opened only after the poll closes. This physical record serves two crucial purposes. First, it gives the voter an immediate, tangible confirmation that the machine has captured the intended choice, thereby eliminating the “black‑box” perception that once plagued earlier generations of electronic voting equipment. Second, it creates a verifiable paper trail that can be cross‑checked against the electronic tally produced by the EVM’s Control Unit. In practice, after polling ends, a statistically valid sample of VVPAT slips—selected according to the sampling plan mandated by the Supreme Court of India—are manually counted and compared with the electronic results. If the two tallies match within the prescribed confidence interval, the result is declared final; any discrepancy beyond the allowable margin triggers a full recount of all VVPAT slips for the affected polling station, and in extreme cases, a re‑poll.

The reliability of this dual‑record system is not assumed but is demonstrated through a rigorous design and testing regime that begins long before any election is held. During the conceptual phase of an EVM, engineers subject the hardware and software to worst‑case scenario analyses, such as power failures, electromagnetic interference, and attempts at unauthorized code injection. Prototype machines are then subjected to laboratory stress tests that simulate thousands of voting cycles, ensuring that the mechanical and electronic components can withstand the heavy usage typical of Indian elections. Statistical quality‑control methods—such as hypothesis testing and confidence‑interval estimation—are applied to the test data to verify that error rates remain far below any threshold that could influence an outcome. These evaluations are documented and reviewed by independent technical committees, and the results are made publicly available, reinforcing the notion that the machines are not only functional but also resilient to tampering.

On the technical side, each EVM is a sealed, tamper‑evident unit that contains a microcontroller, a set of printed circuit boards, and a secure memory module that stores the vote‑count data. The machines are equipped with a “Voter‑Verified Paper Audit Trail” (VVPAT) printer that produces a paper slip for every vote cast; the slip is displayed momentarily to the voter and then automatically deposited in a sealed box, providing a physical record that can be cross‑checked during post‑election audits. Additionally, the internal wiring is designed in a way that any attempt to open the chassis would destroy the seal and leave visible evidence of interference.

Before a machine leaves the secure manufacturing facility, it undergoes a battery of functional tests conducted by an independent certification agency approved by the ECI. The results of these tests, together with the serial numbers and cryptographic hash of the firmware, are published in a publicly accessible register, allowing political parties, civil‑society observers, and the media to verify that each unit matches the specifications announced by the commission. The cumulative effect of these technical and administrative protocols is a high degree of confidence among the electorate, political parties, and international observers that the electronic voting process is free, fair, and transparent. By making every stage of the EVM life‑cycle visible, auditable, and subject to independent scrutiny, the Election Commission of India has created a model of election security that relies less on personal trust and more on verifiable, repeatable procedures—a model that continues to evolve as new challenges and technologies emerge.

The deployment of electronic voting equipment in national elections is a phenomenon that varies considerably from one jurisdiction to another, reflecting the interaction of historical precedent, legal architecture, technological capacity, and political culture; while it is true that many industrialised democracies continue to rely predominantly on paper‑based ballots or on hybrid systems that combine electronic tabulation with a tangible paper audit trail, a number of countries have nonetheless embraced forms of direct‑recording voting machines (DRVMs) that record each voter’s choice electronically at the moment the ballot is cast. In the United States, for example, at least twenty‑seven of the fifty states have, at various times since the mid‑1990s, authorised the use of direct‑recording electronic (DRE) devices in some or all of their contests, and in fifteen of those jurisdictions a voter‑verified paper audit trail (VVPAT) or comparable physical record is produced alongside the electronic tally, thereby creating a dual‑record system intended to reconcile the speed and convenience of electronic tabulation with the evidentiary robustness of a paper receipt. Australia, Canada, Argentina, Brazil, Namibia and the Himalayan kingdom of Bhutan are also documented as having experimented with, or fully institutionalised, DRE‑type machines, albeit often in conjunction with other modalities such as optical‑scan paper ballots, ballot‑marking devices (BMDs) that allow voters to indicate preferences on a screen before printing a paper ballot for insertion into a scanner, or, in the case of older systems, punch‑card ballots reminiscent of the “hanging chads” controversy of the 2000 U.S. presidential election. The selection of any particular voting technology is rarely a matter of technological determinism alone; rather, it is mediated by constitutional and statutory provisions that define the permissible modes of casting and counting votes, by the administrative capacity of election commissions to procure, maintain, and certify hardware, and by the broader political calculus that balances demands for speed, accessibility (particularly for persons with disabilities), cost‑effectiveness, and public confidence in the integrity of the electoral process.

India’s experience with electronic voting machines (EVMs) therefore must be understood against this backdrop of comparative practice, as well as within the specific trajectory of Indian electoral reform that began in the early 1980s. The Indian EVM is, in technical terms, a form of direct‑recording machine that captures a voter’s selection through the depression of a mechanical button corresponding to the chosen candidate; the choice is stored in non‑volatile memory and, after the close of polling, the aggregated data are transferred to a separate counting unit that produces the final tally. The legal status of such devices, however, was not automatically granted; rather, it emerged from a series of judicial and legislative interventions that together forged the constitutional and statutory scaffolding for their use. The first major judicial pronouncement came on 5 March 1984, when the Supreme Court of India, hearing an election petition (No. 01/1982) filed by the petitioner A. C. Jose, held that electronic voting machines could not be introduced into the electoral process unless an explicit provision was incorporated into the law. This decision underscored the principle that any alteration to the mechanics of voting—being a core component of the democratic franchise—must be grounded in clear legislative authority, lest the modification be vulnerable to challenges on the grounds of procedural illegality or violation of the right to a free and fair election.

In response to the Court’s direction, the Parliament of India enacted the Representation of the People (Amendment) Act, 1988, which amended the Representation of the People Act, 1951 to create a specific legal category for “electronic voting machines” and to set out the conditions under which they could be employed. The amendment stipulated that EVMs could be used only after a thorough testing and certification procedure overseen by the Election Commission of India (ECI), and it required that the machines be operated under the supervision of an appointed Returning Officer and a team of election officials. The legislative change was subsequently upheld by the Supreme Court, which, in a later judgment, affirmed the constitutional validity of the amendment and thereby conferred a durable juridical foundation for the continued and expanded use of EVMs across the country’s vast and diverse electorate. Over the ensuing decades, both the Supreme Court and various High Courts have revisited the issue on multiple occasions—most notably in challenges raised during the 2009 and 2014 general elections—and have consistently reaffirmed their confidence in the security, reliability, and transparency of the Indian EVM system, provided that the procedural safeguards prescribed by the ECI are meticulously observed.

The Indian approach to electronic voting is distinguished not only by its early adoption—India introduced EVMs on a large scale in the 1999 general election, making it one of the world’s first large‑scale democracies to replace paper ballots with electronic devices—but also by its continuous refinement of the technology in response to emerging concerns. In the early 2000s, the ECI introduced the Voter‑Verifiable Paper Audit Trail (VVPAT) as an adjunct to the basic EVM. The VVPAT prints a paper slip displaying the name of the candidate and the party symbol selected by the voter; the slip is visible through a transparent window for a few seconds before it drops into a sealed box. This mechanism provides an independent, physical record that can be cross‑checked against the electronic count in the event of a dispute, thereby addressing one of the most common criticisms leveled at pure DRE systems—the absence of a tangible audit trail. The VVPAT was first deployed in a limited number of constituencies during the 2013 Madhya Pradesh legislative assembly elections and, after a series of successful pilots, was mandated for use in all parliamentary and state assembly elections beginning with the 2019 general election. The dual‑record system adopted by India thus mirrors the hybrid models employed in several U.S. states, but it does so on a scale that encompasses more than 900 million eligible voters, making it a uniquely expansive experiment in electronic voting.

Beyond the formal legal and technological dimensions, the Indian experience illustrates how electoral reforms are often the product of a long‑term, iterative process rather than a sudden, unilateral decision. The initial impetus for adopting EVMs stemmed from pragmatic concerns: the logistical nightmare of printing, transporting, and safeguarding billions of paper ballots across a subcontinent characterized by varied terrain, climatic extremes, and limited infrastructure; the high incidence of ballot‑stuffing and “booth‑capturing” in remote polling stations; and the need to accelerate the counting process so that results could be announced promptly, thereby reducing the window for political unrest. By replacing paper ballots with sturdy, tamper‑evident electronic units, the Election Commission was able to curtail many of these vulnerabilities. Empirical studies conducted by the ECI after each election cycle have reported a marked decline in the incidence of certain forms of electoral fraud, such as the substitution of ballots and the manipulation of vote‑count sheets, while also noting a substantial reduction in the time required to declare results—from several weeks in the early 1990s to a matter of days in recent contests.

Nonetheless, the Indian journey has not been without controversy. Civil‑society groups, opposition parties, and some technology experts have periodically raised alarms about the possibility of software manipulation, hardware tampering, and the lack of a publicly auditable source code. In response, the ECI has instituted a series of transparency measures, including the publication of detailed technical specifications, the invitation of independent experts to conduct mock‑polls and “finger‑printing” of machines prior to deployment, and the establishment of a robust chain‑of‑custody protocol that documents every hand‑over of an EVM from the manufacturer to the polling station and back to the central counting centre. Moreover, the mandatory inclusion of VVPAT devices ensures that a physical record exists for a randomly selected subset of polling stations (typically one per constituency) that can be audited by political parties, election observers, and the judiciary. These safeguards are designed to reinforce public confidence, a factor that, as comparative research on electoral systems demonstrates, is as crucial to the legitimacy of an election as the technical soundness of the voting apparatus itself.

When viewed alongside the practices of other democracies, India’s adoption of EVMs can be interpreted as a bold, forward‑looking policy choice that reflects both the nation’s unique administrative challenges and its willingness to experiment with technology to enhance democratic participation. While countries such as Germany and the Netherlands have, after extensive deliberation, rejected electronic voting on the grounds that the lack of a verifiable paper trail could imperil electoral integrity, India has pursued a path that blends electronic efficiency with paper‑based verification, thereby striving to capture the advantages of both modalities. The Indian model thus serves as an illustrative case study for scholars of comparative politics and election technology, demonstrating how the interplay of constitutional jurisprudence, legislative action, institutional capacity, and technological innovation can shape the evolution of voting systems in ways that are tailored to a nation’s particular socio‑political context. As electoral technologies continue to evolve—with emerging possibilities such as blockchain‑based voting, remote‑voting platforms, and biometric authentication—the Indian experience underscores the importance of grounding any new adoption in a clear legal framework, subjecting the technology to rigorous, transparent testing, and ensuring that voters retain the ability to verify that their individual choices have been accurately recorded and counted. In this sense, the Indian EVM, buttressed by its VVPAT companion and the extensive jurisprudence that has validated its use, stands as a testament to the country’s progressive ambition to modernise its democratic processes while preserving the fundamental principle that every vote must be both cast and counted in a manner that is transparent, accountable, and trustworthy.

Electronic voting machines (EVMs) equipped with Voter‑Verified Paper Audit Trails (VVPATs) have become a cornerstone of India’s electoral architecture, a status that rests on a solid constitutional and legal foundation. The Indian Constitution guarantees free and fair elections, and the Supreme Court has repeatedly affirmed that the Election Commission of India (ECI) possesses the authority to adopt modern, secure technologies to uphold this principle. Accordingly, a series of statutes—including the Representation of the People Act, 1951, and the Election Commission’s own rules—codify the deployment of EVMs and VVPATs across the nation’s multi‑tiered polling system. From a technical standpoint, these devices incorporate layered security measures such as cryptographic hash functions, tamper‑evident hardware, and rigorous pre‑poll testing protocols, all overseen by a cadre of independent auditors and political party representatives. Administratively, the ECI follows an inclusive process that invites scrutiny from stakeholders, conducts nationwide mock polls, and mandates periodic certification of machines by accredited laboratories. Together, the constitutional endorsement, robust legislative backing, sophisticated safeguards, and participatory oversight create a comprehensive framework that explains why India, unlike many developed nations that have reverted to paper ballots, continues to rely on EVMs as a trusted instrument for conducting its massive democratic exercises.

The Election Commission of India, seeking to assure the integrity of the electronic voting process, commissioned a panel of distinguished statisticians to determine a scientifically defensible sampling regime for the verification of electronic voting machines (EVMs).  The panel’s report concluded that a nationwide audit of merely 479 randomly selected EVMs would be sufficient to expose a systemic defect affecting at least two percent of the machines, a threshold that corresponds to roughly 20 000 faulty units out of the approximately 10.35 lakh (1 035 000) EVMs deployed in the 2019 General Election.  The logic behind this recommendation rests on the fundamentals of sampling theory: when the objective is to detect the presence of a relatively rare adverse condition, the probability of discovery depends primarily on the absolute number of units examined rather than on the proportion of the total inventory that the sample represents.  In other words, a sample of 479 machines constitutes an infinitesimal fraction—well under one‑half of one percent—of the total stock, yet, because the sample size has been calculated to achieve a confidence level of approximately 99.993 % (corresponding to a four‑sigma, or 4σ, standard‑deviation criterion), the chance of missing a fault rate of 2 % or higher is vanishingly small.  

To understand why the figure of 20 000 faulty machines emerges from the mathematics, consider a simple binomial model in which each EVM has an independent probability *p* of being defective.  If *p* equals 0.02, the expected number of defective units in the entire population is 0.02 × 1 035 000 ≈ 20 700.  The probability that a random draw of 479 machines contains no defective unit under this scenario can be approximated by \((1‑p)^{479}\), which yields a value on the order of 10⁻⁵—hence the “near certainty’’ that at least one malfunction will be uncovered.  Conversely, if the actual defect rate were substantially lower—for example 0.5 % (≈ 5 000 machines)—the same calculation gives a non‑negligible probability (about 0.3) that the audit would miss all problems, illustrating why the committee explicitly framed the recommendation in terms of a 2 % fault floor.  

The report also cautions against treating the entire nation’s complement of EVMs as a single homogeneous “population’’ for the purpose of statistical inference.  In practice, voting machines are distributed across a mosaic of constituencies, each with its own logistical constraints, varying levels of voter turnout, and differing exposure to potential tampering or technical failure.  If one were to aggregate all machines indiscriminately, the sampling design would ignore these stratifying factors and could either over‑estimate or underestimate the true prevalence of defects in particular regions.  A more rigorous approach would first decide at what administrative level—state, district, or constituency—the confidence guarantee is required, and then allocate the 479 audit slots proportionally, perhaps using stratified random sampling or cluster sampling techniques to ensure that each geographical or operational stratum is adequately represented.  

Statistical textbooks repeatedly emphasize that the margin of error associated with a proportion estimate is a function of sample size *n* and the confidence coefficient *z*, following the familiar formula \( \text{ME}=z\sqrt{p(1-p)/n}\).  When the committee fixed the margin of error at 2 % and selected a confidence coefficient corresponding to a 4σ level, the resulting algebraic solution yielded *n* ≈ 479, irrespective of the total population size.  This counter‑intuitive outcome—sometimes described as the “large‑population approximation’’—means that even if the total number of EVMs were to double, the required audit size would remain essentially unchanged, provided the same detection threshold and confidence level are retained.  Critics who argue for a percentage‑based sampling rule (e.g., 10 % or 30 % of all machines) overlook this principle and propose figures that have no grounding in probability theory; such rules would either waste resources by examining far more machines than needed, or, if the percentage were set too low, would fail to achieve the stipulated detection power.  

In summary, the committee’s recommendation of 479 randomly selected EVMs is not an arbitrary figure but a carefully derived statistic that balances feasibility with a rigorous guarantee: if at least two percent of the machines are compromised, the audit will almost certainly uncover a defect.  The threshold of roughly twenty thousand faulty units derives directly from the product of the 2 % fault rate and the total inventory used in the 2019 election.  Moreover, the report rightly rejects the notion of viewing the nation’s entire EVM fleet as a monolithic population, urging instead a stratified approach that respects the heterogeneous nature of the electoral apparatus.  By grounding the audit design in established sampling theory—emphasizing absolute sample size, confidence level, and appropriate stratification—the Election Commission can conduct a cost‑effective verification that satisfies both statistical rigor and public confidence in the electoral outcome.

The Election Commission of India, acting on a formal request, commissioned a panel of distinguished statisticians to devise a scientifically defensible protocol for verifying the integrity of electronic voting machines (EVMs) used in national elections. The committee’s final report, which has become a reference point for subsequent discussions on election‑technology audits, concluded that a uniformly sized random sample of **479 EVMs** would be sufficient to uncover any systematic malfunction that afflicted **2 percent or more** of the machines deployed across the country, regardless of the total inventory. For context, the 2019 general election employed approximately **10.35 lakh (1,035,000) EVMs**; therefore, a 2 percent fault rate would correspond to roughly **20,700 defective units**. By drawing 479 machines at random from the entire national stock, the probability that none of the faulty units would appear in the sample is vanishingly small—on the order of one in several thousand—so the presence of a widespread defect would be detected with “near certainty,” a phrase that in statistical parlance translates to a confidence level well above the conventional 95 percent threshold.

To understand why the figure of 479 arises, it is useful to invoke the hypergeometric distribution, which models the likelihood of observing a certain number of defective items when sampling without replacement from a finite population. If **N** denotes the total number of EVMs (≈ 1,035,000), **D** the number of defective machines (≥ 20,700 for a 2 percent fault level), and **n** the sample size, the probability of obtaining **zero** defective machines in the sample is  

\[
P\{X=0\}=\frac{\binom{N-D}{n}}{\binom{N}{n}} .
\]

Substituting the above values and solving for the smallest **n** that forces \(P\{X=0\}<0.01\) (i.e., less than a 1 percent chance of missing all defects) yields **n ≈ 479**. In practical terms, this means that if the actual number of faulty EVMs falls below roughly **20,000**, the sampling plan’s power to expose the problem diminishes appreciably; the odds of a clean sample increase, and the audit may fail to flag the malfunction. Consequently, the committee warned that treating the entire national stock of EVMs as a single homogeneous “population” without regard to the underlying fault proportion would be a methodological misstep—statistical inference must always be conditioned on an assumed prevalence of the attribute being tested.

The statistical reasoning is complemented by a description of the operational uniformity of India’s voting hardware. Every EVM is manufactured under identical specifications, undergoes the same series of factory‑level quality checks, and is paired with a Voter‑Verified Paper Audit Trail (VVPAT) unit that prints a paper slip for each cast vote. Prior to any election, each machine is subjected to a **Functional Level Check (FLC)**, a series of mock polls, and a battery of verification procedures conducted in the presence of representatives from all contesting political parties. This rigorous pre‑poll protocol is designed to eliminate systematic biases that could arise from, for example, allocating newer or better‑tested machines to particular constituencies.

Moreover, the assignment of EVMs to constituencies and polling stations is itself randomised twice: first, machines are allocated to assembly constituencies (or parliamentary constituencies for Lok Sabha elections) via a computer‑generated lottery; second, within each constituency, individual machines are distributed to specific polling booths through a separate random draw. This dual randomisation ensures that no state, region, or demographic group receives a non‑representative subset of the machines, thereby satisfying the fundamental requirement for an unbiased sample.

In practice, the “population” under study is defined by the set of elections whose counting is scheduled for a particular day. For instance, a single counting day might encompass the results of multiple state assembly elections, a nationwide parliamentary election, and any number of by‑elections. All EVMs engaged in that batch of counting are treated as a collective pool for sampling purposes. The committee’s recommendation of 479 machines therefore represents a **single, nation‑wide audit** that would be carried out on the same day that votes are being tallied, providing a swift statistical check on the overall health of the electronic voting infrastructure.

Despite the statistical soundness of the 479‑machine plan, the Supreme Court of India issued a separate directive that dramatically expands the scope of post‑poll verification. In response to petitions seeking greater transparency, the Court ordered that **five VVPAT units** be selected **randomly in each assembly segment** (i.e., each parliamentary constituency’s subdivision) for manual recount. India has **4,125 assembly segments** across its 543 Lok Sabha constituencies, which translates to a total of **20,625 VVPATs** to be examined for a single general election—a figure that eclipses the expert committee’s recommendation by more than forty‑fold. The judicial mandate reflects a policy choice that prioritises political legitimacy and public confidence over the minimalist statistical sampling threshold. While the larger audit undoubtedly offers a more granular view of potential anomalies, it also imposes considerable logistical burdens: the transport, secure storage, and manual verification of tens of thousands of paper slips, along with the staffing of observation teams from multiple political parties, requires extensive coordination and resources.

The divergence between the statistical recommendation and the judicial order underscores a broader tension that often arises in the design of election‑integrity mechanisms. On the one hand, **statistical sampling theory** provides a mathematically rigorous framework for detecting faults with a predetermined level of confidence while minimising cost and disruption. On the other hand, **legal and political considerations**—including the need to reassure voters, counter allegations of tampering, and uphold the perceived fairness of the electoral process—may call for more exhaustive, though less statistically efficient, verification procedures. Both perspectives have merit: the former ensures that limited resources are deployed where they are most likely to yield informative results; the latter reinforces democratic legitimacy by offering visible, tangible proof that votes have been recorded correctly.

In summary, the expert committee’s analysis concluded that a randomly chosen cohort of **479 EVMs**—selected from the total pool used in any given counting operation—would, with a probability exceeding 99 percent, detect a fault rate of **2 percent or higher**, which corresponds to **more than 20,000 defective machines** in the context of a 10.35‑lakh‑machine election. This conclusion rests on the uniformity of the machines, the double‑layer randomisation of their deployment, and the well‑established principles of hypergeometric sampling. However, the Supreme Court’s subsequent instruction to audit **20,625 VVPAT units** illustrates how statistical optimality can be superseded by broader concerns for transparency and public trust, leading to a far more extensive verification exercise. The episode serves as a valuable case study in how technical expertise, legal mandates, and democratic values intersect when societies strive to safeguard the integrity of their electoral systems.

The Election Commission of India, seeking to bolster public confidence in the integrity of the electronic voting process, commissioned a panel of distinguished statisticians to devise a scientifically sound protocol for verifying the performance of electronic voting machines (EVMs). The committee’s report, which was prepared after a careful review of the logistical realities of the 2019 general election—an election in which approximately 1,035,000 (or 10.35 lakh) EVMs were deployed across the nation—concluded that a relatively modest audit of 479 randomly selected machines would be sufficient to expose any systematic malfunction that might affect a non‑trivial share of the total inventory. The underlying statistical reasoning is straightforward: if at least 2 percent of the machines in service were defective—equating to roughly 20,700 faulty units out of the 1,035,000 used—then the probability that a random sample of 479 machines would contain at least one defective unit is exceedingly high, approaching certainty. In other words, the sampling plan is calibrated so that the chance of missing a fault rate of 2 percent or higher is vanishingly small; the confidence level associated with the test exceeds 99.9 percent, which in statistical parlance is often described as “near certainty.”

The choice of 479 as the audit size emerges from the binomial model of sampling without replacement. When a population of N = 1,035,000 contains an unknown number D of defective items, the probability that a simple random sample of size n contains zero defectives is given by the hyper‑geometric expression \(\frac{\binom{N-D}{n}}{\binom{N}{n}}\). Setting D = 0.02 N (the 2 percent threshold) and solving for n such that this probability falls below a pre‑specified tolerance (for example, 0.001) yields n≈479. This calculation demonstrates that the sample size does not depend linearly on the total number of machines; instead, it is driven by the desired detection power for a particular fault proportion. Consequently, even if the total inventory were to double or shrink, the same 479‑machine audit would remain statistically adequate for detecting a 2 percent fault rate, provided the underlying assumptions of random selection hold.

It is important to underscore that the statistical model treats the entire pool of EVMs as the population of interest, but it does not assume that every individual machine must be examined. The term “population” in this context simply denotes the complete set of machines from which the sample is drawn, not a suggestion that the sample is a perfect microcosm of the whole. The report explicitly warns against the misconception that the 479‑machine audit somehow replaces a full‑scale verification of all 1.035 million units. Rather, the audit is a targeted diagnostic tool: if the sample yields no discrepancies between the electronic tallies recorded by the EVMs and the corresponding Voter‑Verifiable Paper Audit Trails (VVPATs), the statistical inference is that any underlying fault rate is likely below the 2 percent level. Conversely, the discovery of even a single mismatch would trigger a broader investigation, because it would indicate that the fault proportion exceeds the threshold that the sample was designed to detect.

To illustrate the practical implications, imagine that only 10,000 machines—far less than the 20,000‑plus required to cross the 2 percent mark—were compromised in a way that subtly altered vote totals. In such a scenario, the probability that a random sample of 479 machines would contain a defective unit drops dramatically, perhaps to a few percent, meaning that the audit could plausibly return a clean result even though a small‑scale manipulation had occurred. This limitation is intrinsic to any sampling‑based quality‑control procedure: it is highly effective at exposing widespread problems but less sensitive to isolated anomalies. Therefore, the committee’s recommendation is best understood as a balance between statistical rigor, operational feasibility, and the need for timely verification in a country as large and diverse as India. By adhering to the 479‑machine cross‑check protocol, election officials can credibly assert that, with near certainty, the election outcomes are not materially compromised by faulty EVMs, provided that any malfunctioning devices do not exceed the 2 percent prevalence threshold.

Since the declaration of election results, any candidate who believes that the voting process in his or her constituency was compromised has the legal right to lodge an election petition before a competent court. This procedural safeguard is enshrined in the Representation of the People Act, 1951, and serves as the primary mechanism through which allegations of irregularities—whether they concern the counting of votes, the functioning of voting machines, or the conduct of election officials—are examined in a judicial forum. Over the past several decades, India has witnessed a substantial number of such petitions; they range from isolated challenges in individual high‑court jurisdictions to landmark matters that have ascended to the Supreme Court of India.  

The bulk of these petitions have concerned the use of electronic voting machines (EVMs), which were introduced in the 1990s to replace paper ballots and to curb malpractices such as ballot‑stuffing and multiple voting. Critics have occasionally argued that the machines could be tampered with, that software glitches might alter vote totals, or that the lack of a paper trail could undermine transparency. In response, petitioners have sought court orders to recount votes, to invalidate results, or even to demand a complete re‑run of the election in the affected constituency.  

Judicial scrutiny, however, has repeatedly affirmed the reliability of the Indian EVM system. High‑courts across the country have examined technical evidence, expert testimonies, and audit logs, and have generally concluded that there is no substantive proof of systematic manipulation. The Supreme Court, which has acted as the final arbiter in several high‑profile disputes, has similarly expressed confidence in the machines, noting that rigorous certification procedures, randomised serial numbers, and the presence of a Voter‑Verified Paper Audit Trail (VVPAT) provide multiple layers of security. In landmark rulings such as *Election Commission of India v. Union of India* (2013) and *Ashok Kumar v. Election Commission* (2019), the Court upheld the constitutionality of the EVMs and rejected petitions that were based chiefly on conjecture rather than demonstrable evidence.

The publication traces the legislative journey from the early adoption of stand‑alone machines to the integration of VVPAT units, explains the statutory mandates governing their manufacture, testing, and deployment, and analyses key judgments that have reinforced judicial confidence in the technology. By situating the technical features of EVMs within their statutory context, the booklet illuminates why, despite occasional petitions, the Indian judiciary has consistently concluded that the electronic voting system remains a trustworthy instrument for conducting free and fair elections.

The design of any electronic voting system must be examined by a body that operates independently of both the executive branch of government and the Election Commission itself, so that the assessment is free from any potential institutional bias. In India, this safeguard is provided by the Election Commission of India (ECI), which is a constitutional authority endowed with autonomy under the Constitution, and therefore is not subject to direct governmental control. Within the Commission, the Technical Evaluation Committee (TEC) is tasked with carrying out a thorough technical review of electronic voting machines (EVMs). The TEC comprises experts in computer science, cryptography, and electoral logistics, and it conducts laboratory tests, field trials, and vulnerability assessments to verify that the hardware and software meet stringent security standards. 

Beyond the design review, the overall integrity of the electoral process is reinforced by an independent audit that cross‑checks the electronic tallies against a physical record. Each EVM is equipped with a Voter‑Verified Paper Audit Trail (VVPAT) device that prints a paper slip showing the symbol and name of the candidate selected by the voter. The voter is given a brief moment to view the slip before it drops into a sealed box, thereby confirming that the machine has captured the intended vote. After the polls close, a statistically significant sample of VVPAT slips is randomly selected and manually counted. The counts derived from the paper slips are then compared with the electronic totals recorded by the control unit of each machine. Statistical techniques such as hypothesis testing are applied to assess the correlation, and in practice the results have consistently demonstrated a high confidence level—often exceeding 99.9%—that the electronic and paper counts match. 

The outcomes of both the technical review by the TEC and the post‑election audit are compiled into detailed reports. In accordance with principles of transparency and public accountability, these reports are released to the public, allowing political parties, civil‑society organizations, and ordinary citizens to scrutinize the findings. Such openness not only reinforces confidence in the electoral system but also provides a documented basis for any corrective measures that may be required in future elections.

The electoral process that employs Electronic Voting Machines (EVMs) in India is designed to be comprehensively transparent, free, and fair, a status that is achieved through a multilayered framework of technical safeguards, strict administrative protocols, and the active involvement of every relevant stakeholder at each stage of the election cycle. From the moment an EVM is manufactured, the Election Commission of India (ECI) subjects it to a series of rigorous quality‑control measures that are documented in real time, ensuring that no undocumented alteration can occur. Once the machines leave the secure manufacturing facilities, they are stored in specially authorized depots where access is limited to authorized personnel and where biometric and video‑surveillance systems record every entry and exit. The transportation of the units to the state‑level headquarters and subsequently to the individual constituencies is carried out in armored vehicles escorted by police, with a detailed chain‑of‑custody log that is signed by representatives of the recognised political parties, independent observers, and election officials.  

A cornerstone of the transparency doctrine is the double‑randomisation procedure, which is performed in the presence of party delegates, independent monitors, and members of the media. In the first randomisation, the Control Unit (CU) and the Ballot Unit (BU) of each EVM are paired with a unique serial number, after which a second randomisation determines the allocation of each paired machine to a specific polling station. Because the randomisation is conducted openly and the outcomes are publicly announced, no political party can predict or influence which machine will be used in which constituency, thereby eliminating the possibility of pre‑meditated tampering.  

Before any polling day, each EVM undergoes a First Level Check (FLC), a meticulous functional test that verifies the integrity of the hardware, the correctness of the software, and the proper operation of the Voter‑Verifiable Paper Audit Trail (VVPAT) printer. The FLC is witnessed by a cross‑section of stakeholders, including party officials, civil‑society representatives, and neutral technical experts. During this session a mock poll is conducted, allowing the participants to cast votes on the machine, observe the printed VVPAT slips, and confirm that the electronic tally matches the paper records. The commissioning process that follows the FLC involves the sealing of the machines with tamper‑evident stickers, the issuance of a certification of fitness, and the recording of the machine’s unique identification number in a public register that can be accessed online by any citizen.  

On election day, the polling booths are secured by a combination of physical barriers, CCTV surveillance, and the presence of trained poll officials who enforce the Model Code of Conduct. Voters approach the BU, select their preferred candidate by pressing the corresponding button, and immediately receive a VVPAT slip that displays the candidate’s name, symbol, and serial number. The voter is required to verify this slip before it is automatically deposited into a sealed box, ensuring that the electronic vote is auditable. This visual confirmation not only empowers the voter but also creates a paper trail that can be cross‑checked during the counting phase, thus reinforcing both freedom and fairness.  

After the polling stations close, the CUs are collected and taken to secure counting centers where the electronic tallies are aggregated using a standardized, algorithmic process that has been publicly disclosed and independently reviewed. Simultaneously, a predetermined sample—typically one per cent of the total VVPAT slips in each constituency—is manually counted and compared with the electronic results. Any discrepancy beyond a narrowly defined tolerance triggers an automatic recount and a forensic examination of the affected machines. Because the VVPAT slips are retained for a statutory period, they serve as an immutable record that can be audited by courts or election tribunals upon request, further cementing the credibility of the outcome.  

The fairness of the entire system is also underpinned by the secure manufacturing environment (SMF) in which each component of the EVM is produced. The SMF employs a closed‑loop supply chain, biometric authentication of workers, and continuous video monitoring, with every step—from the soldering of circuit boards to the loading of firmware—being logged in a tamper‑proof ledger. Any deviation from the prescribed procedures automatically generates an alert that is sent to the ECI’s central monitoring hub, where a rapid response team can intervene.  

In practice, the transparency of the EVM‑based election process can be observed during the public demonstrations that precede major elections, when the ECI invites journalists, academics, and party representatives to tour the storage facilities, watch the randomisation in real time, and attend the mock‑poll sessions. These open‑door events have become an integral part of the democratic culture, providing tangible proof that the machinery of voting is not hidden behind a veil of secrecy but is instead subject to continuous public scrutiny. The free nature of the vote is safeguarded by the strict prohibition of any form of inducement or intimidation within the polling precincts, reinforced by a robust legal framework that penalises violations of the Model Code of Conduct.  

Taken together, the confluence of technologically advanced safeguards—such as encrypted firmware, tamper‑evident seals, and VVPAT verification—and the exhaustive, stakeholder‑driven administrative procedures—covering storage, transportation, randomisation, FLC, commissioning, voting, and counting—creates an electoral environment in which transparency, freedom, and fairness are not merely aspirational ideals but operational realities. The system’s design ensures that every vote cast on a Ballot Unit is recorded, printed, verified, and ultimately tallied in a manner that can be independently audited, thereby upholding the public’s confidence in the democratic process and reinforcing the legitimacy of the elected representatives.

The integrity of elections that use Electronic Voting Machines (EVMs) rests on a multilayered framework of technical and administrative protections that together make the process transparent, free and fair.  On the technical side, every EVM is built with tamper‑evident hardware, encrypted firmware and built‑in self‑tests that verify correct operation before, during and after voting.  In addition, the Voter‑Verifiable Paper Audit Trail (VVPAT) attached to each machine prints a paper slip for every cast vote, allowing voters to confirm that their choice has been recorded and providing a physical record that can be cross‑checked in post‑election audits.  These machines are also subject to randomised serial‑number assignments, cryptographic checksum procedures and rigorous pre‑deployment testing in certified laboratories, all of which reduce the possibility of manipulation.

Complementing the technology, the Election Commission of India (ECI) enforces an extensive set of administrative safeguards.  These include multi‑level security protocols for the storage, transportation and handling of EVMs, sealed containers, GPS‑tracked convoys, and a chain‑of‑custody log maintained by officials at every hand‑over point.  Prior to each poll, mock elections and functional tests are conducted in the presence of party representatives and independent observers to demonstrate that the machines operate correctly.  Training programmes are provided to poll staff, and strict penalties are stipulated for any breach of the code of conduct.

Crucially, the ECI deliberately involves all relevant stakeholders throughout the electoral cycle.  Political parties, civil‑society groups, media organisations and international observers are invited to attend testing sessions, monitor the deployment of machines at polling stations, and participate in the verification of VVPAT slips where discrepancies are alleged.  This inclusive approach creates a system of checks and balances that discourages misconduct and builds public confidence.

Taken together, the combination of robust technical design, meticulous administrative procedures, and the active participation of a broad spectrum of stakeholders ensures that elections conducted with EVMs are conducted in a manner that is open to scrutiny, free from coercion, and fair to all participants.
