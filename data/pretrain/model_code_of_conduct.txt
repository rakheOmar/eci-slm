The Model Code of Conduct (MCC) represents the normative and administrative cornerstone of the Indian electoral process, serving as a complex instrument of self-regulation and external oversight designed to safeguard the sanctity of the democratic franchise. At its core, the MCC is a set of guidelines issued by the Election Commission of India (ECI) to govern the conduct of political parties and candidates during the election cycle, ensuring that the principles of free and fair elections remain paramount.1 The primary philosophical objective of the MCC is to maintain a "level playing field," a concept that demands the neutralization of the inherent advantages of incumbency and the prevention of the misuse of state machinery for partisan gain.3
The legal and constitutional legitimacy of the MCC is anchored in Article 324 of the Constitution of India, which vests the "superintendence, direction, and control" of the preparation of electoral rolls and the conduct of all elections to Parliament and State Legislatures in the Election Commission.6 Jurisprudential analysis of Article 324 reveals it as a "reservoir of power," a term immortalized by the Supreme Court in the landmark case of Mohinder Singh Gill v. Chief Election Commissioner (1978).8 The Court held that where the enacted law is silent or insufficient to deal with a particular situation in the conduct of elections, the ECI possesses the plenary power to act in the interest of a free and fair poll.6 This constitutional mandate allows the MCC to operate as a potent regulatory framework despite its lack of direct statutory backing, effectively bridging the gaps between the Representation of the People Act, 1951 (RPA) and the dynamic realities of political campaigning.3
The evolution of the MCC is a testament to the maturation of India’s democratic infrastructure. It is not merely a legalistic document but a moral consensus reached among political stakeholders.1 This consensus-driven origin is critical, as it imbues the code with a unique form of social and political legitimacy that complements its administrative enforcement.1 By regulating speech, financial promises, and the use of public resources, the MCC seeks to prevent the electoral process from being vitiated by "corrupt practices," as defined under Section 123 of the RPA 1951, which includes bribery, intimidation, and appeals to communal or linguistic sentiments.
Historical Evolution: From State-Level Experiment to National Institutionalization
The historical trajectory of the MCC is marked by a transition from a voluntary state-level agreement to a robust, institutionally enforced national protocol. The genesis of the code can be traced to the 1960 State Assembly elections in Kerala, where the state administration, in consultation with political parties, drafted a localized 'Code of Conduct' to manage the behavioral expectations of political actors.1 The success of this experiment led the Election Commission of India to circulate a revised version of the code to all recognized political parties and state governments during the 1962 Lok Sabha elections.1 In these early decades, the MCC functioned primarily as a voluntary set of norms, relying on the "noblesse oblige" of the political class to maintain the decorum of the campaign.1
The 1970s witnessed a shift toward formalization. In 1974, the ECI directed Chief Electoral Officers to constitute district-level standing committees, headed by District Collectors, to monitor violations and serve as local grievance redressal forums.15 However, the most significant structural expansion occurred in 1979, when the Commission introduced a dedicated section titled "Party in Power".5 This was an institutional response to the growing concern that the ruling party at the Center and in the States could leverage their control over state resources—including aircraft, government vehicles, and the official press—to create an insuperable incumbency advantage.1
The transformative phase of the MCC coincided with the tenure of Chief Election Commissioner T.N. Seshan in 1991.1 Recognizing that the "moral authority" of the code was frequently flouted by entrenched political interests, Seshan utilized the Commission’s plenary powers under Article 324 to institutionalize strict enforcement mechanisms.3 This era redefined the relationship between the ECI and the political executive, as the Commission began cancelling elections, censuring senior ministers, and deploying a massive administrative apparatus to ensure compliance.3 The code was no longer a "gentleman’s agreement" but a formidable administrative tool for electoral purity.3
In the 21st century, the MCC has continued to evolve in response to judicial directives and technological shifts. In 2013, following the Supreme Court’s judgment in S. Subramaniam Balaji v. State of Tamil Nadu, the ECI incorporated guidelines regarding election manifestos into the MCC.3 The Court noted that while manifesto promises do not technically constitute "corrupt practices" under the RPA, they undeniably influence the level playing field, necessitating rules that require parties to provide a rationale for their promises and detail the financial means to fulfill them.17 Most recently, the 2019 and 2024 cycles have seen the MCC expand into the digital realm with the introduction of the "Voluntary Code of Ethics" for social media platforms and strict advisories against the use of AI-generated deepfakes.10
Temporal and Geographic Extent of Application
The operational life of the MCC is tied to the official election calendar. It comes into effect immediately upon the announcement of the election schedule by the Election Commission and remains in force until the formal declaration of results.15 For a General Election to the Lok Sabha, the MCC typically spans a duration of approximately 45 to 60 days.2 This period is critical, as it marks the transition of the government from a proactive policy-making body into a "caretaker" entity restricted from initiating new projects or making financial commitments that could influence the electorate.4
The geographic scope of the MCC is determined by the type of election being conducted:
General Election to Lok Sabha: The code is applicable throughout the entire country, affecting both the Union Government and all State/Union Territory governments.2
General Election to State Assembly: The MCC applies to the entire territory of the concerned state.2
Bye-Elections: The application is more nuanced. For bye-elections to the Lok Sabha or State Assembly, the MCC is generally enforced in the entire district covering the constituency.2 However, if the constituency is located within a state capital or a metropolitan city, the code applies only to the area of the constituency itself to prevent the disruption of broader urban administration.2
The code binds a diverse array of actors, including all recognized national and state political parties, candidates, and independent contestants.2 Crucially, its reach extends to Central and State government ministries, departments, and all organizations funded wholly or partially by the government, such as Public Sector Undertakings (PSUs), municipal corporations, and various commissions.4 Even non-political organizations found campaigning for a particular party are subject to the conduct guidelines of the MCC.3
Structural Analysis: The Seven Pillars of the MCC
The MCC is structured into seven primary sections, each targeting a specific domain of electoral activity to ensure comprehensive governance.1 These sections are designed to regulate behavior from the start of the campaign until the last vote is cast and counted.
General Conduct
The first section serves as the ethical foundation of the MCC. It prohibits any activity that aggravates existing communal, caste, or religious differences or creates mutual hatred.1 Political parties are expected to limit their criticism to the policies, programs, and past records of their rivals, expressly forbidding personal attacks on the private lives of candidates that have no bearing on their public duties.1 Furthermore, this section places a strict ban on the use of places of worship—such as temples, mosques, churches, or gurudwaras—as forums for election propaganda.1 It also reinforces the individual’s right to a peaceful and undisturbed home life, prohibiting protests or picketing in front of private residences.5
Meetings and Processions
The logistical sections (Sections II and III) focus on public order. Parties are required to inform the local police of the venue and timing of any proposed meeting well in advance to allow for traffic management and security.1 Similarly, for processions, organizers must decide on the starting and terminating points and communicate the exact route to the authorities.1 This ensures that rival processions do not clash, a measure critical for preventing political violence in densely populated areas.1 The use of loudspeakers is strictly regulated, often requiring specific licenses and adhering to local time restrictions (typically banned between 10 p.m. and 6 a.m.).27
Polling Day and the Polling Booth
Sections IV and V manage the conduct during the climax of the electoral cycle. On polling day, parties and candidates are mandated to cooperate with election officials to ensure a peaceful voting process.1 No party symbols or propaganda materials are allowed within the polling booth or within a 100-meter radius.1 Candidates are prohibited from serving or distributing liquor 48 hours before the poll closes.1 To facilitate grievance handling, any complaints related to the polling booth must be taken up with the official Observers appointed by the ECI, rather than being settled through direct confrontation.1
The Party in Power: Mitigating Incumbency
Section VII is the most critical and frequently cited part of the code, designed to prevent the ruling party from using its official position to further its election campaign.1 It enforces several prohibitions:
Official Visits and Machinery: Ministers are forbidden from combining official visits with electioneering work and cannot use government personnel or machinery for campaigning.4
Government Transport: Official aircraft, vehicles, and other machinery cannot be used to serve the interests of the party in power.1
Public Resources: Public places like maidans and helipads must not be monopolized by the ruling party; opposition candidates must be granted access on equal terms.4
Publicity and Advertisements: The code bans the issuance of advertisements at the cost of the public exchequer that highlight government achievements to further the prospects of the party in power.4
Financial and Administrative Commitments: Once elections are announced, ministers cannot sanction grants from discretionary funds, announce new projects/schemes, or make ad-hoc appointments in the government or PSUs that could influence voters.3
Election Manifestos
The final section, added in 2013, governs the content and timing of manifestos. It directs that manifestos must not contain anything repugnant to the Constitution and should be consistent with the Directive Principles of State Policy.17 Crucially, manifestos are expected to reflect the rationale for promises and broadly indicate the financial requirements and funding sources.17 This is intended to curb the culture of "irrational freebies" and ensure that political promises are fiscally grounded.17
The Enforcement Ecosystem: Observers and Field Units
The enforcement of the MCC is not merely a central administrative task but a massive field operation involving thousands of senior government officials. These officials act as the "eyes and ears" of the Election Commission, providing real-time data and intervention to ensure the integrity of the poll.13
The Hierarchy of Election Observers
The ECI deploys Central Observers under the powers conferred by Section 20B of the RPA 1951 and Article 324 of the Constitution.13 These observers are senior civil servants drawn from the IAS, IPS, and revenue services (IRS/IRAS) who are assigned to states other than their home cadre to maintain total impartiality.13
General Observers: They are IAS officers responsible for the overall supervision of the election process in a constituency. They monitor the neutrality of the district administration, the scrutiny of nomination papers, and the enforcement of MCC guidelines.13
Expenditure Observers: They are IRS officers tasked with monitoring the campaign spending of candidates. They maintain a "Shadow Observation Register" and collaborate with Flying Squads to track illicit cash, liquor, or gifts used to induce voters.13
Police Observers: IPS officers who oversee the deployment of security forces and the overall law and order situation. They identify "vulnerable" polling stations where voter intimidation is likely and ensure that the deployment of central paramilitary forces is neutral.13
Micro-Observers: These are usually central government employees deployed at sensitive polling stations to observe the actual voting process and report directly to the General Observer.14
These observers submit periodic reports to the ECI through the "Observer Portal," a web-based application that allows for instant communication and the correction of field-level irregularities.31
Field Enforcement Teams
Below the observers are the District Election Officer (DEO) and the Returning Officer (RO), who manage the day-to-day enforcement.28 They are supported by:
Flying Squad Teams (FSTs): Mobile units that respond to MCC violations reported by citizens or observers.28
Static Surveillance Teams (SSTs): Checkpoints set up at major roads and borders to intercept the movement of illegal cash, liquor, or arms during the election period.3
Video Surveillance Teams (VSTs): Units that record all major political rallies and meetings to create evidentiary proof for expenditure monitoring and conduct violations.29
Technological Transformation: cVIGIL and Suvidha Portals
In the last decade, the ECI has shifted from a reactive enforcement model to a proactive, technology-driven one. This shift has democratized election oversight, allowing the common citizen to play a direct role in upholding the MCC.
cVIGIL: The 100-Minute Redressal Standard
The cVIGIL mobile application, launched in 2018, is perhaps the most significant technological innovation in MCC history. It empowers citizens to report violations by capturing live photos or videos of incidents such as bribery, free gifts, or the use of loudspeakers beyond permitted hours.28 The app’s unique features include:
GIS Tagging: It automatically records the location and timestamp of the incident, providing evidentiary proof that allows Flying Squads to navigate directly to the spot.29
100-Minute Timeline: The ECI has committed to a strict timeline where every cVIGIL case is acted upon and replied to within 100 minutes.28
Workflow: DCC (District Control Centre) addresses the complaint  assigns nearest FST  FST investigates and uploads report  RO takes final decision (Drop, Dispose, or Escalate).28
During the 2024 elections, over 4.22 lakh complaints were filed via cVIGIL, with a resolution rate of 99.9%, demonstrating the app's massive impact on reducing illicit campaign activities.35
Suvidha: Ensuring FIFO Transparency in Permissions
The Suvidha Portal is a single-window system for candidates and political parties to apply for various election-related permissions, such as organizing rallies, opening temporary offices, or obtaining vehicle permits.38 It operates on the "First In, First Out" (FIFO) principle, which is critical for ensuring a level playing field.35 By digitizing the permission process, the ECI has removed administrative discretion, preventing the ruling party from using its local influence to block or delay opposition campaign activities.35 In just the first 20 days of the 2024 campaign, Suvidha processed over 73,000 permission requests.39
National Grievance Services Portal (NGSP)
Beyond the cVIGIL app, the NGSP (Samadhan) provides a comprehensive web-based solution for handling both election-related and non-election-related grievances.41 Citizens can register complaints about voter registration, polling booth locations, or electoral malpractice and track their status online.41 This system integrates with the 1950 helpline, providing a multi-channel interface for voter support.23
The 2024 Lok Sabha Elections: Statistics and Emerging Paradigms
The 2024 General Elections were the largest democratic exercise in human history, characterized by an unprecedented scale of mobilization and monitoring. The statistical report released by the ECI provides a detailed overview of the enforcement landscape.33
Star Campaigner Accountability: A New Course
A major enforcement shift in 2024 was the Commission's decision to hold political party presidents directly accountable for the statements of their "Star Campaigners".35 In April and May 2024, the ECI issued formal notices to the National Presidents of the BJP (J.P. Nadda) and the INC (Mallikarjun Kharge) regarding divisive speeches made by senior leaders like Narendra Modi and Rahul Gandhi.46 The ECI argued that political parties have the "prime responsibility" to rein in their star campaigners and maintain the "delicately balanced social fabric".35 This move represents a legal "course correction," aiming to prevent individual leaders from escaping MCC strictures through the shield of their party’s platform.45
Managing Synthetic Media and Deepfakes
The 2024 cycle was the first to face the acute threat of AI-generated misinformation. On May 6, 2024, the ECI issued a comprehensive advisory to political parties regarding the ethical use of social media and the specific threat of deepfakes.20 Key directives included:
Removal within 3 Hours: Parties were directed to remove misinformation or deepfake content within three hours of it being brought to their notice.20
Mandatory Labeling: Any AI-altered audio or video used for campaigning must bear a clear label (e.g., "AI-Generated" or "Digitally Enhanced") covering at least 10% of the display area.21
Voluntary Code of Ethics: In coordination with the IAMAI and major platforms like Meta, Google, and X, the ECI enforced a voluntary code where platforms agreed to process ECI’s legal requests expeditiously during the 48-hour silence period.10
Legal Challenges and the "Freebies" Paradox
While the MCC is widely respected, it faces a profound legal and economic challenge: the rise of "freebie" politics. The tension lies between the Directive Principles of State Policy (which mandate welfare measures) and the ECI’s mandate to maintain a level playing field.17
The Judicial Intervention: Ashwini Upadhyay v. Union of India
Between 2022 and 2026, the Supreme Court has been hearing a PIL seeking a ban on "irrational freebies" offered by political parties.54 The petitioner argues that such promises are analogous to bribery under Sections 171B and 171C of the IPC and unduly influence the voter, shaking the roots of free and fair elections.54
The Court’s observations have been stark:
Distinction from Welfare: Chief Justice Surya Kant observed that "distribution of state largesse to individuals at a large scale is different from investing state largesse in public welfare schemes".52
Fiscal Prudence: The court expressed anxiety over debt-ridden states (e.g., Tamil Nadu, Punjab, Haryana) splurging on "irrational largesse" while failing to provide basic infrastructure like roads or schools.52
Electoral Purity: The Court is considering whether making such promises should be deemed a "corrupt practice" under Section 123 of the RPA 1951, which would make them a ground for challenging an election result.53
The ECI has taken a cautious stance, stating in its April 2022 affidavit that it cannot regulate the practice of promising freebies as it is a policy decision and that it is the "voters' duty" to assess financial viability.55 However, the 2024 MCC updates now require parties to disclose the "financial requirements" and "ways of financing" their manifesto promises, using transparency as a deterrent against fiscal recklessness.17
Enforcement Measures to Strengthen the MCC
To ensure the MCC remains more than just a moral code, the ECI has institutionalized several administrative measures that carry the force of law.
Transfer and Posting Protocols
Once the election is announced, there is a total ban on the transfer of all officers and staff connected with the conduct of elections.4 No officer likely to be connected with elections can be transferred without the prior approval of the Commission.4 Furthermore, the ECI often orders the "suo motu" transfer of DMs and SPs if they have served in their home districts or have spent more than three years in a single district, ensuring that local biases do not infect the electoral machinery.33
The "Silence Period" (Section 126)
The 48-hour period before the hour fixed for the conclusion of the poll is known as the "Silence Period." Under Section 126 of the RPA, no person can hold public meetings or display "election matter" by means of television or other similar apparatus.3 In the digital age, this restriction has expanded to include a ban on the publication of opinion polls or exit polls during this period.5 The ECI has also urged star campaigners to desist from giving press conferences or interviews that could indirectly influence voters in constituencies where the silence period is in effect.12
Restrictions on Government Advertising
The ECI maintains a strict ban on "Publicity at Public Cost." Advertisements highlighting the government's achievements in the print or electronic media are prohibited during the election period.4 If advertisements were released before the announcement, they must be stopped immediately.23 For metropolitan cities, the MCC restricts the display of such hoardings only in the areas where the election is taking place, but the general principle remains that public money should not be used for partisan propaganda.24
Expenditure Monitoring Framework
The ceiling for election expenses by candidates is strictly enforced.61 In 2024, the ECI reported record seizures of over ₹1,000 crore in states like Maharashtra during bypolls, illustrating the intensity of expenditure monitoring.62 Candidates are required to maintain a daily account of their expenses in a proforma prescribed by the ECI, which is then scrutinized by the Expenditure Observer.24
Landmark Case Law and the Plenary Character of ECI
The evolution of the MCC is inextricably linked to judicial interpretations that have defined the boundaries of electoral ethics and administrative power.
Mohinder Singh Gill v. CEC (1978)
In this seminal case, the Supreme Court dealt with the ECI's decision to order a re-poll in the Ferozepore constituency following mob violence and the destruction of ballot boxes.9 The Court upheld the ECI’s decision, ruling that Article 324 is a "reservoir of plenary power" that empowers the Commission to take all necessary steps—even those not explicitly mentioned in the RPA—to ensure a fair poll.8 This judgment is the "Bible of election law," providing the primary constitutional justification for the enforcement of the MCC.9
S. Subramaniam Balaji v. Govt. of Tamil Nadu (2013)
This case challenged the distribution of freebies (like mixers, grinders, and laptops) promised in manifestos.17 While the Court ruled that these promises do not constitute "corrupt practices" because they are made by parties and not individual candidates, it directed the ECI to frame guidelines on manifestos to be included in the MCC.17 This led to the 2013-14 amendments that introduced Section VIII into the code.17
A.C. Jose v. Sivan Pillai (1984)
The Supreme Court held that the ECI cannot act against the provisions of a law passed by the legislature.6 However, in areas where the law is silent, the Commission has the absolute power to issue directions.6 This established the hierarchy of legal instruments in elections, placing the MCC as the primary regulator in areas not explicitly covered by the RPA.3
Criticisms and Reform Suggestions: The "Toothless Tiger" Debate
The MCC, despite its successes, remains a subject of intense debate regarding its legal efficacy and the scope of the ECI's powers.
The Problem of Statutory Backing
Critics often argue that the MCC is a "moral code" rather than a legal one, leading to descriptions of the ECI as a "tiger without teeth".3 The argument for giving the MCC statutory backing—as proposed by the Dinesh Goswami Committee (1990) and the Standing Committee on Law and Justice (2013)—is that it would allow the ECI to impose legal penalties directly.2
However, the ECI has consistently opposed this move for several reasons:
Impracticality of Judicial Delay: Once the MCC becomes a law, every action of the ECI (like a warning or a transfer) can be challenged in a trial court.2 In the Indian judicial system, this could lead to stay orders that would paralyze the electoral process during the critical 45-day window.2
Need for Flexibility: The current non-statutory status allows the ECI to respond instantly to emerging threats, like digital deepfakes, without waiting for an act of Parliament.3
Digital Disruption and Section 126
The Umesh Sinha Committee (2019) highlighted that Section 126 of the RPA is outdated as it does not explicitly cover the internet and social media platforms.10 The Committee suggested that the 48-hour silence period must be expanded to include social media, OTT platforms, and print media (which is currently exempt).3 The 2024 experience showed that while the "Voluntary Code of Ethics" helped, inflammatory content often spreads through encrypted messaging (WhatsApp) that remains outside the ECI's surveillance.3
Suggested Reforms for a New Era
Fast-Track Election Tribunals: To solve the problem of post-election redressal taking years, the Law Commission has suggested establishing special tribunals to dispose of election petitions within six months.3
Capping Party Expenditure: While individual candidate spending is capped, political party spending remains limitless.3 Reforms suggest a statutory cap on total party expenditure to ensure a level playing field.3
Deregistration Powers: Currently, the ECI can register a party but lacks the explicit power to deregister it.62 Granting the ECI the power to deregister or suspend a party for severe and repeated MCC violations would significantly strengthen its authority.3
Mandatory AI Watermarking: Future codes could mandate cryptographic watermarks for all digital campaign materials to ensure the traceability of fake news back to specific party IT cells.3
Conclusion: The Resilience of the MCC in the World’s Largest Democracy
The Model Code of Conduct, over its sixty-year history, has transformed from a state-level consensus into the primary guardian of India’s electoral integrity. Its resilience stems not from statutory penalties but from its moral authority and the sophisticated administrative infrastructure built by the Election Commission of India. By integrating senior civil servants as observers and leveraging cutting-edge technology like cVIGIL and Suvidha, the ECI has created a multi-layered oversight system that remains remarkably effective given the scale of the Indian electorate.
The 2024 General Elections have underscored both the strengths and the emerging vulnerabilities of the MCC. While the "course correction" to hold party presidents accountable and the proactive regulation of AI deepfakes demonstrate an institution that is actively learning, the persistent challenges of "irrational freebies" and digital misinformation suggest that the code must continue to evolve. Whether through judicial declarations in the Ashwini Upadhyay case or through future legislative reforms aimed at providing the ECI with stronger punitive powers, the Model Code of Conduct remains the essential "moral conscience" of the Indian Republic, ensuring that the democratic will is expressed in an environment of peace, order, and relative fairness.
