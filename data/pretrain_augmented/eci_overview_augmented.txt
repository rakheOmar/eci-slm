India is defined in its Constitution as a socialist, secular, democratic republic, a description that captures the nation’s commitment to social welfare, freedom of religion, and popular sovereignty. It is also the world’s largest democracy, a status that stems from its immense population and the regular exercise of voting rights by its citizens. The modern Indian nation‑state was formally inaugurated on the 15th of August 1947, when the British colonial administration transferred authority to an independent government. Since that historic moment, India has conducted free and fair elections at regular intervals, adhering to the principles and procedures enshrined in its Constitution, as well as in the various electoral laws and the overall electoral system that have been refined over the decades. These elections are the cornerstone of Indian democracy, ensuring that the people’s will determines the composition of the Parliament, the state legislatures, and the highest constitutional offices such as the President and the Vice‑President.

The Constitution of India vests in the Election Commission of India the supreme authority—described in constitutional language as “superintendence, direction and control”—over the entire electoral process. This mandate covers elections to the Lok Sabha (the lower house of Parliament), the Rajya Sabha (the upper house), the legislative assemblies and councils of every state, and the indirect elections to the offices of President and Vice‑President. The Election Commission is a permanent constitutional body, meaning that its existence is guaranteed by the Constitution itself and it cannot be abolished or altered without a constitutional amendment. The Commission was formally established on the 25th of January 1950, shortly after the Constitution came into force, and it celebrated its golden jubilee—the fiftieth anniversary— in the year 2001, marking five decades of continuous service to the nation’s democratic processes.

When the Commission was first created, it consisted of a single officer known as the Chief Election Commissioner. Over time, the need for a broader decision‑making body became apparent, leading to the addition of more commissioners. On 16 October 1989, two additional Election Commissioners were appointed, but their tenure was brief, ending on 1 January 1990. A more permanent expansion occurred on 1 October 1993, when two more Election Commissioners joined the Chief Election Commissioner, establishing the multi‑member structure that continues to function today. Under this arrangement, decisions are taken by majority vote, ensuring that no single individual can dominate the Commission’s policy direction. This collective approach reflects the democratic ethos of the institution itself, as it mirrors the broader principle of collective decision‑making that underlies parliamentary democracy.

The appointment of the Chief Election Commissioner and the other Election Commissioners is made by the President of India, who acts on the advice of the Union Council of Ministers. Once appointed, each commissioner serves a term of six years, or until reaching the age of sixty‑five, whichever comes first. This dual condition on tenure is designed to balance experience with renewal, preventing overly long incumbencies while also allowing sufficient time for commissioners to acquire deep institutional knowledge. In terms of status, remuneration, and privileges, the commissioners are placed on par with judges of the Supreme Court of India, the highest judicial authority in the country. This parity underscores the independence and dignity of the Election Commission, signalling that its members should not be subject to undue influence. The removal procedure for the Chief Election Commissioner is equally stringent: he or she can be removed only in the same manner and on the same grounds as a Supreme Court judge, which requires a special impeachment process involving both houses of Parliament and a majority on the basis of proven misbehavior or incapacity.

The business of the Commission is conducted through regular meetings of its members, where policy decisions, procedural guidelines, and administrative matters are debated and resolved. In addition to face‑to‑face meetings, the Commission also circulates memoranda and written papers among its members, allowing for detailed scrutiny of proposals and for record‑keeping. All Election Commissioners—whether the Chief or the other two—possess equal voting rights, ensuring that each voice carries the same weight in deliberations. To manage the vast array of functions required for conducting elections across a country as large and diverse as India, the Commission delegates many executive responsibilities to its permanent secretariat, a bureaucratic apparatus that handles day‑to‑day operations.

The secretariat of the Election Commission is headquartered in New Delhi, the national capital, and comprises roughly 550 officials organized in a hierarchical structure. At the apex of this administrative chain are five or six Deputy Election Commissioners and Director Generals, who serve as senior officers assisting the three Commissioners. These senior officials are generally drawn from the All India Services—particularly the Indian Administrative Service (IAS)—which is the elite national civil service cadre that supplies officers to the central and state governments. Their appointment is made directly by the Election Commission, and they serve for a fixed tenure that promotes continuity and expertise. Below the Deputy Election Commissioners and Director Generals are directors, principal secretaries, secretaries, under secretaries and deputy secretaries, each of whom provides support and supervision to the senior officers. The workload within the Commission is divided both functionally and territorially. Functionally, the work is organized into divisions, branches and sections; each section is headed by a Section Officer who is responsible for a specific operational area. The principal functional divisions include Administration, which handles overall management and human resources; Electoral Roll, which maintains and updates the list of eligible voters; Election Expenditure Management, which monitors the spending of candidates and political parties; Information Systems, which develops and maintains the technological infrastructure for election management; International Cooperation, which engages with electoral bodies of other nations; Judicial, which deals with legal issues and election disputes; Media, which coordinates communication with the press and the public; Planning, which devises the logistical framework for elections; Political Parties, which registers and monitors party activities; Secretariat Coordination, which ensures smooth internal communication; and Systematic Voters’ Education and Electoral Participation (SVEEP), a program aimed at educating citizens about the voting process and encouraging voter turnout.

Territorially, the country’s 36 states and union territories are grouped into several zones for administrative convenience, and each zone is overseen by a dedicated team within the Commission. At the state level, the election machinery is supervised by a Chief Electoral Officer (CEO), who acts under the overall superintendence, direction and control of the Election Commission. The CEO is appointed by the Commission from among senior civil servants, selected from a panel of candidates proposed by the respective state government. In most states the CEO is a full‑time officer, supported by a modest staff, and is responsible for coordinating all election‑related activities within the state, including the preparation of electoral rolls, the training of election officials, and the deployment of security forces.

Below the state level, the administrative hierarchy extends to districts and individual constituencies. District Election Officers (DEOs), Electoral Registration Officers (EROs) and Returning Officers (ROs) are the key functionaries who carry out the day‑to‑day tasks of conducting elections. They are assisted by a large number of subordinate staff, such as booth‑level officers, police personnel, and temporary workers hired for specific election phases. Although these officers have regular duties unrelated to elections, during the election period they devote the majority of their working time to the electoral process, effectively operating as a full‑time workforce for the Commission.

The scale of the operation required to conduct a general election across the entire nation is staggering. More than twelve million individuals serve as polling personnel, including presiding officers at each polling station, auxiliary staff, and security personnel drawn from the civil police forces of the various states. These individuals are placed on deputation to the Election Commission, meaning that they are temporarily transferred from their regular posts to serve the electoral mission. During the election window, which typically spans one and a half to two months, this massive force is under the direct control, supervision and disciplinary authority of the Election Commission. The Commission’s responsibility during this period includes ensuring that polling stations are set up correctly, that voting machines and ballot papers are distributed and secured, that voter identification is verified, and that the entire process proceeds smoothly, transparently and without intimidation. The logistical feat of moving equipment, personnel and security resources across a country that stretches from the Himalayas in the north to the coastal plains in the south, and from the deserts of the west to the tropical forests of the east, exemplifies the extraordinary capacity of the Election Commission of India to uphold the democratic aspirations of its people.

The Secretariat of the Election Commission of India operates with a financial plan that is set apart from the ordinary government appropriations and enjoys a degree of autonomy that is essential for the impartial conduct of the nation’s electoral processes. The budget for the Secretariat is not drawn up in the usual fashion of a departmental request that must pass through a series of administrative filters; instead, it is concluded through a direct dialogue between the Commission itself and the Ministry of Finance, which is the department of the Union Government responsible for the overall fiscal management of the country. In this consultation, the Commission submits its detailed estimates of the resources required to carry out its constitutional duties, including personnel costs, information‑technology upgrades, logistical arrangements for elections, and the development of electoral rolls. The Finance Ministry, which has the authority to allocate funds from the central treasury, usually accepts the figures proposed by the Commission, reflecting a tacit acknowledgment of the Commission’s expertise and the need to keep its financial resources insulated from political bargaining. This acceptance also underscores the principle that the independence of the electoral body should be protected by granting it a predictable and reliable source of funding.

While the Secretariat’s own budget covers the administrative backbone of the Commission, the bulk of the actual expenditure needed for the conduct of elections flows through other government entities. The most visible and costly elements of an election—such as the deployment of security personnel, the procurement of voting machines, the establishment of polling stations, and the transportation of ballot boxes—are financed by the Ministry of Law and Justice on behalf of the Union Government for elections that are held across the country, and by the respective State or Union Territory administrations for elections that are confined to a single state or territory. When the nation votes only for members of Parliament, that is, for the Lok Sabha (the lower house) and the Rajya Sabha (the upper house), the entire financial burden falls on the Union Government, because these bodies are part of the central legislative system. Conversely, when the election is limited to a State Legislative Assembly or a State Legislative Council, the cost is shouldered fully by the State Government, as the election pertains solely to that state’s own law‑making bodies.

In the relatively rare circumstance that a general election for Parliament and a state election are scheduled to occur on the same day—a situation sometimes referred to as “simultaneous elections”—the Constitution and the relevant statutory provisions dictate a cost‑sharing arrangement. In such cases, the Union Government and the State Government each contribute half of the total expenditure required for the joint operation. This arrangement is intended to distribute the financial load fairly, recognizing that both levels of government benefit from the efficiency gains that arise when elections are held together, such as the shared use of polling stations and security forces.

A further dimension of the budgetary framework involves capital expenditures that are not directly tied to a single election cycle but are essential for the long‑term integrity of the electoral system. These include the procurement of durable equipment such as electronic voting machines, the development and maintenance of the electoral roll (the official list of eligible voters), and the issuance of Electors’ Identity Cards, commonly known as voter ID cards. Because these items support the functioning of the election machinery over many years and across all elections, the costs associated with them are split equally between the Union Government and the State Governments. This shared financing reflects the collaborative responsibility for maintaining a robust, up‑to‑date, and secure voter database and for ensuring that the physical tools used to capture voter choices are reliable and uniformly available throughout the country.

The independence of the Election Commission is further reinforced by a constitutional safeguard that bars any interference from the executive branch of government in the performance of the Commission’s duties. The term “executive interference” refers to attempts by the government, whether at the central or state level, to influence the timing, conduct, or outcome of elections. The Constitution explicitly places the Commission in a position of autonomy, granting it the exclusive authority to decide the schedule of elections, which includes the dates on which voting will take place, the periods allotted for the filing of nominations, and the timelines for the counting of votes. This authority extends to both general elections, which determine the composition of the national Parliament, and by‑elections, which fill vacancies that arise in the middle of a legislative term.

Beyond the overarching timetable, the Commission also exercises full control over the logistical details that are critical for a free and fair vote. It determines where each polling station will be situated, ensuring that voting places are accessible to the electorate, taking into account factors such as population density, geographic spread, and the need for special arrangements for persons with disabilities. The Commission assigns voters to specific polling stations based on their residential addresses, a process that is essential to avoid duplication and to maintain the principle of “one person, one vote.” Moreover, the Commission designates the venues where votes will be counted, known as counting centres, and it establishes the security and procedural arrangements that must be in place both at the polling stations and the counting centres. These arrangements cover the deployment of security personnel, the provision of voting machines or ballot papers, the training of poll officials, and the protocols for handling any complaints or irregularities that might arise on election day.

Political parties, which are the primary actors in the democratic contest, must be formally registered with the Election Commission under the provisions of the Representation of the People Act. Registration is a prerequisite for a party to contest elections, and it confers a legal identity on the organization, allowing it to field candidates, receive election symbols, and access certain benefits such as free broadcast time on state-owned media during election campaigns. The Commission also monitors the internal democratic functioning of these parties. To promote transparency and accountability, it requires parties to hold regular internal elections for their leadership positions, such as the party president, general secretary, and other office bearers. These internal elections must be conducted at intervals prescribed by the Commission, ensuring that party leadership remains accountable to its members and that the party’s decision‑making processes are not monopolized by a single individual or a small group of individuals.

The recognition of a political party at either the state level or the national level is based on its performance in previous elections. The Commission applies a set of quantitative criteria, such as the percentage of votes secured or the number of seats won in the legislative bodies, to determine whether a party qualifies for recognition. Recognized parties enjoy certain privileges, including the allocation of a reserved election symbol that appears on ballot papers, free or subsidized airtime on state media, and the right to a certain number of party workers in polling stations. When a party experiences internal splits or factions—known as “splinter groups”—the Commission exercises its quasi‑judicial authority to adjudicate disputes over the rightful ownership of the party’s name, symbol, and other legal attributes. This function is essential to prevent confusion among voters and to maintain the integrity of the party system.

A cornerstone of the Commission’s role in ensuring a fair electoral contest is the enforcement of the Model Code of Conduct (MCC). The MCC is a set of guidelines that political parties and candidates must follow once the election schedule is announced. It covers a broad spectrum of conduct, ranging from the prohibition of hate speech and the use of government resources for campaign purposes, to the regulation of rallies, the dissemination of election‑related advertisements, and the treatment of voters. The MCC is not a law passed by the legislature but a self‑imposed code that derives its authority from the consensus of political parties and the Commission’s power to enforce it. Violations of the MCC can result in warnings, fines, or, in severe cases, the cancellation of a candidate’s nomination. The Commission maintains a constant dialogue with political parties, consulting them on the interpretation of the MCC, seeking their feedback on proposed amendments, and discussing new measures that may be introduced to address emerging challenges, such as the influence of social media on voter perceptions.

In addition to its executive functions, the Election Commission possesses a distinctive advisory jurisdiction that becomes operative in specific post‑election scenarios. One of the most significant of these is the disqualification of sitting members of Parliament or State Legislatures. The Constitution empowers the Commission to advise the President of India, or the Governor of a state, on whether an elected representative should be removed from office on grounds of violating electoral laws. Such grounds may include the commission of corrupt practices—such as bribery, intimidation, or the use of false statements—to influence voters, or the failure to submit a required account of election expenses within the timeframe mandated by law. When a case involving alleged corrupt practices reaches the Supreme Court or a High Court, the judiciary may refer the matter to the Commission for its expert opinion on the appropriate period of disqualification. The Commission’s opinion, once furnished, is binding on the President or Governor, meaning that the executive head of state must act in accordance with the Commission’s recommendation.

The Commission’s authority to impose disqualification also extends to candidates who neglect to lodge the detailed statement of their election expenses, a requirement intended to promote transparency and prevent the misuse of financial resources. If a candidate does not comply with the prescribed filing deadline or fails to provide a complete account in the manner stipulated by law, the Commission may declare the candidate disqualified. Conversely, the Commission retains the power to reduce or remove a period of disqualification if it finds mitigating circumstances, such as an inadvertent error or a genuine inability to comply due to factors beyond the candidate’s control. This flexibility ensures that the punitive measures are proportionate and that the rights of candidates are protected while upholding the integrity of the electoral process.

The final safeguard against electoral malpractice lies in the system of judicial review, which allows aggrieved parties to challenge the validity of an election after the voting has concluded and the results have been announced. Under Article 329 of the Constitution, the judiciary is barred from intervening in the conduct of elections once the polling process has begun, a principle designed to prevent endless litigation that could stall the democratic process. However, after the polls are closed and the results declared, any person who believes that an election was conducted in violation of legal provisions may file an election petition. For elections to the Parliament and to State Legislatures, such petitions are filed before the High Court of the respective state. The High Court then examines the evidence, which may include allegations of bribery, intimidation, improper counting, or any procedural irregularities, and can issue orders ranging from a declaration of the election’s nullity to the ordering of a fresh election. In the case of elections to the offices of President and Vice‑President, which are conducted by an electoral college comprising members of Parliament, the jurisdiction to entertain election petitions lies exclusively with the Supreme Court of India. The Supreme Court’s decisions are final and binding, and they serve as the ultimate arbiter of electoral disputes, ensuring that the highest offices in the land are occupied by individuals whose election has withstood rigorous legal scrutiny.

The Election Commission also recognizes the pivotal role that the media plays in informing the electorate and in providing transparency during the electoral process. To this end, it has formulated a comprehensive media policy that outlines the rights and responsibilities of journalists and media houses during elections. The Commission conducts regular briefings for both print and electronic media outlets, providing updates on the election schedule, procedural changes, and any emerging issues that may affect the conduct of voting. These briefings are scheduled at close intervals as the election period progresses, ensuring that journalists receive timely and accurate information. On occasions that demand special attention—such as the issuance of new guidelines, the occurrence of an unexpected incident at a polling station, or the introduction of a novel voting technology—the Commission convenes additional sessions to address media queries.

Members of the press, both from national and international organizations, are granted specific facilities to enable them to report on the ground. The Commission issues authority letters—official credentials that permit accredited journalists to enter polling stations and counting centres. This access is carefully regulated to balance the need for transparency with the imperative to maintain the secrecy of the ballot and the safety of voters and election officials. Journalists are permitted to observe the voting process, note the conduct of poll staff, and report on the environment within the counting centres where votes are tallied. The Commission’s policy emphasizes that while media personnel may be present, they must not interfere with the voting or counting procedures, and any breach of these conditions can result in revocation of accreditation.

To further promote openness, the Election Commission publishes a wide array of statistical reports, research papers, and operational guidelines. These documents encompass voter turnout figures, the performance of electronic voting machines, the status of voter registration, and the outcomes of various pilot projects aimed at improving the electoral system. All such publications are made available in the public domain, often through the Commission’s official website, libraries, and designated information centers. By disseminating this data, the Commission enables scholars, civil society organizations, and ordinary citizens to scrutinize the electoral process, fostering a culture of accountability and informed public participation.

In sum, the budgetary autonomy, the insulation from executive interference, the regulatory oversight of political parties, the advisory and quasi‑judicial powers, the avenue for judicial review, and the transparent media engagement together constitute a robust institutional framework that underpins the functioning of the Election Commission of India. This framework is designed to safeguard the democratic ethos of the nation, ensuring that elections are conducted in a manner that is free, fair, and reflective of the will of the people, while also providing mechanisms to address grievances, enforce accountability, and adapt to evolving challenges in the electoral landscape.

Voter education in India is pursued through a comprehensive and multifaceted scheme known as the Systematic Voters’ Education and Electoral Participation programme, commonly abbreviated as SVEEP. This initiative is not a single‑track activity but rather a series of coordinated interventions that together aim to reach the broadest possible segment of the citizenry with information about how elections are conducted, why participation matters, and what responsibilities voters have. The central idea behind systematic voter education is to move beyond a one‑off campaign and to embed a continuous process of awareness‑raising that begins long before a particular election is called and extends well after the ballot boxes have been closed. By informing people about the mechanics of registration—such as where and how to enroll, the documents required, and the deadlines involved—SVEEP seeks to boost the number of citizens who are formally entered onto the electoral rolls. In addition, the programme places a strong emphasis on encouraging actual turnout at polling stations, recognizing that registration alone does not guarantee that a democratic choice will be expressed on election day. To that end, SVEEP organizes outreach events, distributes informational material in regional languages, and employs multimedia campaigns that highlight the civic importance of casting a vote.

Beyond merely increasing the quantity of participants, SVEEP also aspires to raise the quality of electoral engagement. The term “qualitative participation” in this context refers to the notion that voters should not only be present at the polling station but also be equipped with the knowledge to make an informed, ethical decision. Accordingly, the programme includes components that explain the substance of policy proposals, the ideological positions of political parties, and the ways in which government functions after the election. It also stresses the principles of free and fair voting, such as the secrecy of the ballot, the prohibition of coercion, and the importance of respecting the outcomes regardless of personal preference. By providing continuous electoral and democracy education, SVEEP attempts to create a citizenry that sees voting as a lifelong duty rather than a sporadic act, thereby reinforcing the foundations of a vibrant democratic culture.

India occupies a singular place in the world as the largest democracy by population, a status that has been reinforced by more than seventy‑five years of experience in conducting elections that are widely regarded as free, transparent, and credible. This long‑standing record has given the Election Commission of India, the constitutional body charged with overseeing elections at the national, state, and local levels, a prominent voice in international forums that seek to promote participatory democracy and effective election administration across the globe. The Commission was a founding member and has served as chair of the Association of World Election Bodies, an organization that now brings together 119 election management bodies from every continent, allowing them to exchange best practices and coordinate on common challenges. It also helped establish and subsequently chaired the Association of the Asian Election Authorities, a regional consortium that focuses on the specific needs and experiences of Asian nations. In South Asia, the Commission previously led the Forum of Election Management Bodies of South Asia, a platform for cooperation among neighboring states. Moreover, the Election Commission holds membership in the International Institute for Democracy and Electoral Assistance, a United Nations‑affiliated think‑tank that conducts research on electoral systems, as well as in the Commonwealth Electoral Network, which links election officials from countries that share the historic legacy of the British Commonwealth. Through these memberships, the Commission has consistently advocated for international cooperation among democracies, arguing that sharing experiences, technical expertise, and legislative reforms can strengthen the overall health of democratic institutions worldwide.

To give substance to its collaborative ethos, the Election Commission of India has entered into formal agreements known as Memoranda of Understanding with a broad array of partners. In total, twenty‑eight election management bodies from different nations have signed such agreements, along with three major international organisations: the International Foundation for Electoral Systems, the International Institute for Democracy and Electoral Assistance, and the United Nations. A Memorandum of Understanding, or MoU, serves as a legally recognised framework that outlines the terms under which the parties will engage in joint activities. These activities include the sharing of experiences through conferences and seminars, capacity‑building programmes that enhance the skills of election officials, technical cooperation on the design and deployment of voting technology, and the organisation of exchange visits and study missions that allow participants to observe elections in diverse settings. By institutionalising these exchanges, the Commission ensures that knowledge transfer is systematic rather than incidental.

A concrete manifestation of the Commission’s commitment to regional cooperation emerged after a decisive meeting of the Extraordinary Executive Board of the Association of World Election Bodies held on the second of September, 2019. Following that meeting, the Commission established a dedicated regional centre in New Delhi that is formally known as the India A‑WEB Centre. The centre’s mandate is to serve as a hub for documentation, research, and training on behalf of all A‑WEB members. In practice, this means that the centre collects detailed case studies of election management from participating countries, analyses them to extract lessons, and then disseminates the findings through workshops, webinars, and published manuals. By providing a centralised repository of knowledge and a venue for capacity‑building initiatives, the centre helps member states adopt proven strategies for improving the integrity and efficiency of their own electoral processes.

Since its inception in 2011, the Election Commission’s premier training institution, the Indian Institute of Democratic Elections and Management (IIIDEM), has played a pivotal role in turning the Commission’s international outreach into tangible skill development. Over the course of more than a decade, IIIDEM has delivered training programmes to sixty‑nine thousand three hundred and sixty‑two election officials drawn from one hundred and nine different countries. The curriculum offered to these participants encompasses the legal framework governing elections, the logistics of ballot handling, the use of electronic voting machines, and the application of information and communication technology to enhance transparency and voter participation. In addition to classroom instruction, the Commission has dispatched Indian election experts and observers to serve on election missions in a variety of settings, ranging from the Pacific island nation of Fiji to Southeast Asian states such as Cambodia, Thailand, and Nepal, and extending to Central Asian republics like Uzbekistan and Kazakhstan, as well as African nations including Zimbabwe and Sri Lanka. A notable example of technical assistance is the provision of Indian‑manufactured electronic voting machines to Nepal, Bhutan, and Namibia, accompanied by on‑site training and maintenance support to ensure that these countries could operate the machines effectively within their own legal and logistical contexts.

One of the flagship initiatives that encapsulates the spirit of international collaboration is the International Election Visitors’ Program. This programme is designed to invite delegations of election officials, scholars, and representatives of international organisations to India for an in‑depth exposure to the country’s electoral architecture. Participants are given a comprehensive overview of the scale and complexity of India’s elections, which involve the coordination of hundreds of millions of voters across a vast and diverse geographical landscape. The itinerary typically includes visits to polling stations, meetings with senior election officials, demonstrations of cutting‑edge technologies such as biometric verification and real‑time results transmission, and briefings on recent reforms aimed at improving voter facilitation, enhancing transparency, and making the electoral system more accessible to persons with disabilities. Since the programme’s launch in 2014, the Commission has hosted numerous delegations, and when the COVID‑19 pandemic rendered travel difficult, the three most recent editions of the International Election Visitors’ Program in 2020, 2021, and 2022 were conducted entirely in a virtual format. This adaptation ensured that the flow of knowledge and best practices continued uninterrupted, allowing participants to attend live webinars, engage in virtual tours of election infrastructure, and interact with Indian officials through online discussion forums, thereby demonstrating the Commission’s flexibility and commitment to sustaining global electoral cooperation even in the face of unprecedented challenges.

The Election Commission of India has embarked on a series of fresh initiatives in recent years with the aim of modernising the electoral process, enhancing transparency, and ensuring that the conduct of elections is fair, clean and accessible to all sections of the population. Among the most visible steps taken by the Commission are the introduction of a scheme that allows political parties to use state‑owned electronic media for broadcasting or telecasting their messages, the systematic effort to curb the criminalisation of politics by making information about candidates’ legal histories publicly available, the computerisation of the electoral rolls so that the list of eligible voters can be maintained in a digital format, the issuance of identity cards to every elector as a proof of citizenship, the simplification of the accounting procedures that candidates must follow when filing their expenses, and a host of other measures designed to enforce strict compliance with the Model Code of Conduct – a set of guidelines that seeks to provide a level playing field for all contestants during an election campaign. Each of these reforms is intended to reduce the scope for manipulation, to improve the efficiency of the election administration and to make the democratic process more understandable for the ordinary citizen.

In line with the Commission’s broader agenda of reforming the electoral process, several additional steps have been taken that address specific procedural gaps and respond to recent judicial pronouncements. One of the most significant of these measures concerns the requirement that political parties disclose, in a clear and uniform manner, the details of any criminal cases pending against their candidates. This initiative stems directly from an order of the Supreme Court of India dated 13 February 2020, in which the apex court instructed the Election Commission to ensure that voters are fully informed about the criminal backgrounds of those who seek public office. In compliance with that directive, the Commission has issued comprehensive guidelines that obligate all political parties to publish, on a uniform format, the names of candidates who have either pending criminal charges or past convictions. The guidelines require that the information be accurate, up‑to‑date and made available to the electorate well before the election so that voters can make an informed choice. The Commission has stressed that any party that fails to adhere to these instructions may be subject to legal scrutiny and possible sanctions under the Representation of the People Act.

Another notable development is the introduction of an optional postal ballot facility for what the Commission calls “absentee voters”. The Conduct of Elections Rules, which were originally framed in 1961, were amended by two notifications dated 22 October 2019 and 19 June 2020, thereby inserting a new Rule 27A that recognises the right of certain categories of voters to cast their votes by mail. Under the amended rule, an absentee voter is defined as a person who is engaged in essential services – a classification that includes doctors, police officers, railway staff and other workers whose duties are critical to the functioning of the nation – as well as senior citizens, persons with disabilities, and individuals who are suspected of having contracted COVID‑19 or who have been affected by the pandemic. The list of essential services is itself determined by the Election Commission in consultation with the Central Government, pursuant to Section 60(c) of the Representation of the People Act, 1951. By allowing these voters to request a postal ballot, the Commission seeks to remove the barrier that distance or occupational constraints might otherwise create, thereby ensuring that every eligible citizen has the opportunity to participate in the democratic process.

In keeping with the Government’s “Digital India” vision, the Commission has also introduced an optional facility that permits candidates to enter their personal details online when filling out the nomination form and the accompanying affidavit, formally known as Form‑26. This online entry system, which is hosted on the Election Commission’s Suvidha portal, is intended to minimise the likelihood of errors that can occur when information is handwritten or typed manually. The portal guides candidates through a step‑by‑step process, automatically validates the data entered, and generates a printable version of the completed form that can be submitted to the Returning Officer. By moving this part of the nomination process into a secure digital environment, the Commission hopes to accelerate the overall timeline of elections and reduce the administrative burden on both the candidates and election officials.

A further refinement of the criminal‑background disclosure requirement has been made after the Supreme Court’s judgment in the same matter. The Commission has now issued detailed instructions that require both political parties and individual candidates to publish the criminal antecedents of every candidate contesting an election. To facilitate uniformity, the Commission has designed specific formats in which the information must be presented. These formats include fields for the nature of the offence, the status of the case (whether pending, under trial, or concluded), and any conviction that may have resulted in a sentence of imprisonment of two years or more. The intention behind the standardised format is to make the data easily comparable across parties and to avoid any ambiguity that could mislead voters.

Environmental considerations have also entered the election administration agenda through the “eco‑friendly elections” initiative. The Commission has repeatedly urged political parties to refrain from using single‑use plastic items such as disposable posters, banners, and other campaign materials that contribute to pollution and pose a threat to human health. Instead, parties are encouraged to adopt reusable or biodegradable alternatives. This policy reflects a growing awareness of the need to align democratic practices with broader sustainability goals, recognizing that the massive scale of election campaigning can have a substantial environmental footprint if left unchecked.

A practical reform that directly benefits young citizens is the amendment of the law governing enrolment in the electoral roll to introduce four qualifying dates for registration. Previously, a citizen could only be added to the roll on a single annual cut‑off date. Under the new rule, any person who turns eighteen on the first day of January, April, July or October is eligible to file an enrolment claim in the quarter that follows his or her birthday. This change spreads the influx of new voters more evenly throughout the year, reduces bottlenecks at the time of a general election, and ensures that a larger number of newly eligible voters can exercise their franchise without delay.

The Commission has also revisited the historic scheme that provides free time on the state‑run broadcasting services—Doordarshan (DD) for television and All India Radio (AIR) for radio—to political parties for the purpose of communicating their policies to the electorate. First introduced on 16 January 1998 and grounded in Section 39A of the Representation of the People Act, 1951, the scheme guarantees that each party receives a specified amount of airtime free of charge, thereby helping to level the playing field between parties with disparate financial resources. In a recent amendment, the Commission has modernised the mechanism by introducing digital time vouchers that can be issued through an information‑technology platform. This innovation eliminates the need for party representatives to travel to the offices of the Election Commissioner or the Chief Electoral Officer to collect physical vouchers, streamlining the process and reducing administrative delays, especially during the intense period leading up to an election.

Collectively, these initiatives represent a concerted effort by the Election Commission to bring the Indian electoral system into the twenty‑first century, to make it more transparent, more inclusive, more environmentally responsible, and more aligned with the digital infrastructure that now underpins much of public life. By responding to Supreme Court directions, adapting to the challenges posed by a global pandemic, and embracing technology and sustainability, the Commission seeks to safeguard the integrity of the democratic process while ensuring that every eligible citizen, regardless of age, disability, occupation or location, can participate fully in the election of their representatives.
