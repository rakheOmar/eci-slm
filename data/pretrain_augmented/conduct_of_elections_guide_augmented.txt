The legal instrument known as the Conduct of Elections Rules, 1961, is a set of procedural regulations that governs the manner in which elections are to be conducted in the Republic of India. These rules constitute a subsidiary legislation, meaning that they derive their authority from a parent statute, namely the Representation of the People Act, 1951, which is often abbreviated as “the Act.” The short title of the rules, as provided in the introductory clause, allows them to be referred to succinctly as the Conduct of Elections Rules, 1961, and the date on which they become operative is expressly fixed as the twenty‑fifth day of April in the year 1961. By specifying a commencement date the legislature intended to give election officials, political parties, candidates and the electorate a clear point in time after which the new procedural requirements would be applicable to all electoral processes. However, a transitional safeguard was inserted to prevent the rules from disrupting any election that had already been called but not yet completed by that date. In such circumstances the earlier set of regulations, the Representation of the People (Conduct of Elections and Election Petitions) Rules, 1956, would continue to govern the unfinished election as though the 1961 Rules had never been enacted. This preservation of the status quo for pending elections reflects a common legislative practice of avoiding retroactive application of new procedural mandates that could otherwise unfairly affect the conduct or outcome of an ongoing electoral contest.

The second clause of the preliminary part is dedicated to the interpretation of terms used throughout the Rules, and it functions much like a glossary that defines technical expressions in plain language so that every official, officer, and participant can understand exactly what is meant when the Rules are applied. The definitions are introduced with a proviso that they apply unless the surrounding context clearly indicates a different meaning, thereby allowing for flexibility where the ordinary meaning of a word would be more appropriate. The first definition assigns the abbreviation “Act” to the Representation of the People Act, 1951, which is a comprehensive statute that lays down the fundamental legal framework for the conduct of elections to the Parliament of India, the State Legislatures, and local bodies. By referencing the Act, the Rules make it clear that they are not an independent source of law but a detailed set of procedures meant to implement the broader principles established by the Act.

The term “ballot box” is broadened beyond the literal wooden box traditionally associated with secret voting. In the context of these Rules, a ballot box includes any container—such as a box, a bag, or any other receptacle—that is used for the insertion of a ballot paper by an elector. This inclusive definition anticipates the variety of materials that might be employed in different parts of the country, especially in remote or difficult terrains where conventional ballot boxes might be impractical. The definition of “counterfoil” refers to a small attached slip of paper that is printed on the reverse side of a ballot paper, according to the specifications set out in the Rules. The counterfoil serves several administrative purposes, including facilitating the tracking of ballot papers and ensuring that each ballot can be accounted for in the tallying process, while preserving the secrecy of the vote.

The Rules also describe a special category of elections known as “election by assembly members.” This phrase refers to indirect elections where the electorate is not the general public but rather the elected members of a legislative body. For example, the Council of States, commonly known as the Rajya Sabha, is filled partly by members of the Legislative Assemblies of the States and by members of the electoral college of Union territories. Similarly, a State Legislative Council, where one exists, is elected by the members of that State’s Legislative Assembly. In these elections the voters are the legislators themselves, and the Rules provide specific terminology to address this mode of selection. The term “elector,” when used in reference to such an election, denotes any person who is entitled to cast a vote in the election by assembly members; in practical terms, these are the duly elected members of the respective legislative bodies. The “electoral roll” in this context is the list of eligible voters—i.e., the legislators—maintained by the returning officer, who is the official responsible for overseeing the conduct of the election. Section 152 of the Act mandates the preparation and upkeep of this roll, and the Rules clarify that the same provision applies to elections by assembly members.

Each person listed on an electoral roll is identified by an “electoral roll number.” This number consists of three components: first, the serial number of the entry, which is the unique position of the individual’s name in the roll; second, the serial number of the part of the roll in which that entry appears, since rolls are often divided into parts for administrative convenience; and third, the name of the constituency to which the roll pertains. By breaking down the identification into these three elements, the Rules ensure that each entry can be precisely located, verified, and cross‑referenced during the election process.

The Rules make reference to “Form,” a term that denotes any standardized document attached to the Rules for use in elections. Forms might include nomination papers, declaration statements, or any other official paper that candidates, voters, or officials must complete. When an election takes place in a State that uses languages other than Hindi or English for official purposes, the Rules require that the relevant Form be provided in a translation that is acceptable for use in that State. This requirement reflects India’s linguistic diversity and the constitutional guarantee that official business may be conducted in the languages prescribed for each State.

An additional term that appears in the Rules, albeit with a notation indicating it has been inserted and later omitted by subsequent notifications, is “marked copy of the electoral roll.” This phrase refers to a specific copy of the electoral roll that is set aside for the purpose of marking the names of those electors to whom ballot papers have been issued during an election. By marking the copy, officials can keep a clear record of which voters have received ballots, which aids in the verification of voter turnout and the detection of any irregularities. Though the term has been subject to amendment, its inclusion in the original text illustrates the detailed procedural safeguards envisioned by the drafters.

The definition of “polling station” is also tailored to the context of elections by assembly members. In such elections the polling station is the location that has been fixed under Section 29 of the Act for taking the poll. Section 29 deals with the designation of a place where voting is to be conducted, and in the case of indirect elections the same procedural framework applies, ensuring that the voting of legislators takes place in a formally designated venue.

The concept of “presiding officer” is broadened to encompass any polling officer who performs any of the functions of a presiding officer as laid down in sub‑section (2) or sub‑section (3) of Section 26 of the Act, as well as any returning officer who is presiding over an election under sub‑section (2) of Section 29. In practice, this means that the individual overseeing the actual act of voting—whether a dedicated polling officer or the returning officer who has overall responsibility for the election—may be considered the presiding officer for purposes of the Rules. This inclusive definition ensures that the authority to manage the polling process is not unduly constrained and that responsibility can be delegated as needed.

Similarly, the definition of “returning officer” extends to any assistant returning officer who performs any function authorized under sub‑section (2) of Section 22 of the Act. The returning officer is the chief official responsible for the conduct of an election in a particular constituency, and the assistant returning officer may act on his behalf in various capacities, such as supervising the counting of votes or managing the election staff. By recognizing the assistant as a “returning officer” for the purposes of the Rules, the legislation provides flexibility in the delegation of duties without compromising legal authority.

The term “section” is defined simply as a section of the Act, which is a standard legislative reference indicating a specific numbered provision within the Representation of the People Act. This definition eliminates any ambiguity when the Rules cite a particular “section” and ensures that readers understand the reference is to the parent statute.

Paragraph (2) of the preliminary part addresses a practical concern: how to deal with individuals who are unable to write their own names. In an electoral system that emphasizes the right of every eligible citizen to vote, it is essential to accommodate those who are illiterate or otherwise physically unable to sign documents. The Rules provide that unless a specific provision states otherwise, a person who cannot write his name shall be deemed to have signed any instrument, declaration, or other paper if he places a mark—such as a cross, a stamp, or any other symbol—on the document in the presence of an authorized official. This official may be the returning officer, the presiding officer, or any other officer designated by the Election Commission of India for this purpose. The officer must be satisfied that the mark indeed belongs to the person in question, and must then attest the mark, effectively certifying that the person’s intention to sign has been fulfilled. This mechanism safeguards the participation of illiterate voters while maintaining the integrity of the documentation process.

Paragraph (3) deals with the publication of official notices, orders, declarations, and lists that are required under the Rules or the Act. It states that any requirement for a document to be published in the Official Gazette—an official government publication that disseminates legal and administrative notices—shall be interpreted as a requirement that the notice be published in the Gazette of India when the matter concerns an election to, or membership of, either House of Parliament (the Lok Sabha or the Rajya Sabha) or an electoral college. Conversely, if the matter pertains to an election to, or membership of, the State Legislature—either the Legislative Assembly or the Legislative Council—then the appropriate publication venue is the Official Gazette of the respective State. This bifurcated approach ensures that relevant information reaches the appropriate jurisdictional audience, whether it be at the national or state level, thereby promoting transparency and public awareness.

Finally, paragraph (4) invokes the General Clauses Act, 1897, which is a piece of legislation that provides general rules for the interpretation of statutes in India. By stating that the General Clauses Act shall apply for the interpretation of these Rules in the same way it applies to an Act of Parliament, the drafters guarantee that the established principles of statutory construction—such as the presumption against retrospective operation, the interpretation of words in their ordinary sense unless a special meaning is given, and the treatment of definitions—are applicable to the Conduct of Elections Rules. This incorporation of a well‑established interpretative framework helps to avoid ambiguity and ensures consistency with other Indian statutes.

The Preliminary part of the Conduct of Elections Rules, 1961, therefore serves as both a foundation and a roadmap for the detailed procedural requirements that follow in the subsequent parts of the Rules. By assigning a clear title and commencement date, providing transitional provisions for elections already in progress, defining the terminology used throughout the document, establishing safeguards for illiterate voters, specifying the proper channels for official publication, and anchoring the Rules within the broader interpretative scheme of the General Clauses Act, this introductory segment creates the legal scaffolding needed for the orderly and fair conduct of elections across the nation. The detailed definitions and procedural safeguards embedded in this section reflect the Indian legislature’s commitment to ensuring that the democratic process is accessible, transparent, and uniformly administered, whether the election involves the general populace voting in a constituency or an indirect election in which elected legislators choose among themselves representatives for higher offices.

The law that governs the conduct of elections in the Republic of India contains a series of detailed procedural rules that must be followed by the officials who organize each poll, as well as by the persons who seek to become candidates. The purpose of these rules is to ensure that the entire electoral process is transparent, uniform and free from ambiguity, thereby giving confidence to the electorate that every contest is conducted on a level playing field. The provisions that follow elaborate on the various forms that must be used, the manner in which public notices are issued, the requirements for nomination papers, the handling of symbols that identify political parties or independent candidates, and the steps that are taken to prepare and publish the official lists of candidates.

When an election is about to be held, the returning officer – the public officer who is appointed for a particular constituency to supervise the conduct of the election, receive nominations, and declare results – is required to give advance warning to the electorate that a poll is imminent. This warning is known as the public notice of an intended election. The statute specifies that such a notice must be printed on a standardized document called Form 1. Form 1 is a pre‑designed paper that contains fields for the name of the constituency, the date on which the election is scheduled to take place, and other essential particulars such as the name of the returning officer and the reference to the relevant statutory provision. The law further provides that, although Form 1 is the prescribed format, the returning officer has a degree of discretion to determine how the notice will be disseminated to the public. This discretion is exercised in accordance with any directions that may be issued by the Election Commission of India, the constitutional body that oversees the entire electoral machinery of the country. The returning officer may therefore decide, for example, whether the notice will appear in local newspapers, be posted on public notice boards, broadcast on radio or television, or be made available through electronic means, so long as the method chosen fulfills the requirement that the electorate be properly informed in a timely manner.

The next stage of the electoral process concerns the submission of nomination papers. A nomination paper is the formal document through which an individual declares his or her intention to contest a particular seat. Section 33 of the Act sets out the basic requirements for such a paper, and sub‑section 1 of that section stipulates that the paper must be completed in one of a series of prescribed formats, namely Forms 2A, 2B, 2C, 2D or 2E. Each of these forms is tailored to a specific category of candidate. For instance, Form 2A is used by a candidate who wishes to contest as a representative of a recognized political party, while Form 2B is employed by a candidate who intends to stand as an independent but wishes to be associated with a symbol that has been allotted to him. Forms 2C, 2D and 2E are used in other particular circumstances, such as when a candidate is nominated by a coalition of parties or when a candidate wishes to contest multiple seats. The law provides an important safeguard: if a candidate fails to fill in, or makes a mistake in, the part of the nomination paper that declares the electoral symbol – the emblem that will appear next to the candidate’s name on the ballot paper – and the form in question is either Form 2A or Form 2B, that omission or error shall not be treated as a “defect of a substantial character”. In other words, the defect will be considered minor, and the nomination will not be rejected on that basis alone. This protective clause is contained in sub‑section 4 of section 36, which deals with the validity of nominations, and it ensures that a technical lapse in the symbol declaration does not automatically disqualify a candidate.

In addition to the nomination paper itself, the law requires the candidate, or the person who is acting as his or her proposer, to submit a sworn statement at the same time. This sworn statement is called an affidavit and it must be executed before a judicial officer of sufficient authority – either a Magistrate of the first class, who is a judicial officer with jurisdiction over serious criminal matters, or a Notary public, who is authorized to administer oaths and certify documents. The affidavit must be prepared in the format known as Form 26. Its purpose is to attest that all the information supplied in the nomination paper is true and accurate, and that the candidate meets all the eligibility criteria laid down by law, such as age, citizenship, and the absence of disqualifying criminal convictions. By requiring the affidavit to be sworn before an official, the statute adds an extra layer of verification, thereby reducing the risk of false statements or fraudulent nominations.

A distinctive feature of Indian elections is the use of symbols to help voters identify the candidates on the ballot paper. This practice is especially important in a country where a substantial portion of the electorate may be illiterate or may not be familiar with the names of all the political parties. The Election Commission therefore maintains a list of symbols that may be allotted to candidates contesting seats in both the Lok Sabha (the lower house of Parliament) and the State Legislative Assemblies. According to subsection 1 of the relevant provision, the Commission publishes this list by issuing an official notification in the Gazette of India, which is the central government’s official journal of record, and also in the Official Gazette of each State, which serves the same function at the state level. The notification not only identifies which symbols are available but also sets out any restrictions that apply to their use. For example, a symbol that has been reserved for a recognized national party cannot be assigned to an independent candidate, and certain symbols may be withdrawn from circulation if they have become associated with a particular party or cause.

In certain circumstances more than one nomination paper may be lodged on behalf of the same candidate. This can happen when a candidate’s supporters each submit a separate copy of the paper, perhaps to ensure that at least one of them reaches the returning officer in time. The law anticipates this situation and provides guidance on how to treat the declarations of symbols in such duplicate submissions. Specifically, the declaration of the symbol that appears on the first nomination paper that is delivered to the returning officer is the one that will be taken into account, even if that paper later turns out to be defective and is rejected. This rule is applied under rule 10 of the election regulations, unless the Election Commission issues a general or special direction under sub‑rule 4 or sub‑rule 5 of that same rule, which would modify the default procedure. The intention behind this rule is to avoid confusion and to prevent a candidate from attempting to change his or her symbol after the initial declaration has been made.

Certificates issued by the Election Commission also have to meet strict standards of authentication. For example, a certificate that confirms a candidate’s eligibility under subsection 2 of section 9, or a certificate that confirms that a nomination paper satisfies the requirements of subsection 3 of section 33, must bear the signature of the Secretary to the Election Commission, who is the senior official responsible for the administration of the Commission’s day‑to‑day affairs. In addition, the certificate must display the official seal of the Commission, which is a distinctive embossment that signifies the document’s authenticity and legal force. The presence of both the signature and the seal assures the returning officer and the public that the document has been duly verified by the highest electoral authority.

The law also prescribes the exact form that must be used to publish the notice of nominations, which is the formal announcement that the names of all the individuals who have successfully submitted their nomination papers will be made public. This notice must be printed on one of the three forms identified as Forms 3A, 3B or 3C, depending on the particular circumstances of the election. Each form contains fields for the name of the constituency, the names of the nominated candidates, their party affiliations or symbols, and other essential details such as the date of filing. By standardising the format, the statute ensures that the information is presented consistently across the entire country, which facilitates the work of election officials, political analysts and the media.

Once the nominations have been accepted, the returning officer prepares a list of all candidates who have been validly nominated. The legal requirement for this list is set out in subsection 8 of section 36. The list must be compiled on Form 4, which is a pre‑designed tabular document that records each candidate’s name exactly as it appears in the original nomination paper. The statute recognises, however, that a candidate may discover after the fact that his or her name has been misspelled, truncated or otherwise presented in a way that does not reflect the name by which he or she is commonly known. If the candidate believes that such an error exists, he or she may, at any time before the final list of contesting candidates is prepared, submit a written request to the returning officer specifying the correct spelling and format of the name. The returning officer, upon being satisfied that the request is genuine – for example, by verifying identity documents or obtaining corroboration from the candidate’s proposers – is obliged to amend the entry in Form 4 accordingly. This correction ensures that the ballot paper will bear the correct name, thereby reducing the possibility of confusion among voters.

The withdrawal of candidature is another procedural step that must be documented in a prescribed manner. If a candidate decides, for whatever reason, to withdraw from the contest after having filed his or her nomination, the law requires that the candidate submit a formal notice of withdrawal. This notice must be in Form 5 and must contain all the particulars specified by the statute, such as the name of the candidate, the constituency, the date of withdrawal and the signature of the candidate. Upon receipt of this notice, the returning officer is required to note the exact date and time at which the notice was delivered, thereby creating a clear record of when the withdrawal took place. In cases where the withdrawal is made under a different subsection of the law – specifically sub‑section 3 of section 37 – the notice must be prepared on Form 6, which is tailored to that particular scenario. The dual system of forms helps the returning officer distinguish between different types of withdrawal, for example, those that occur before the close of nominations and those that happen after the list of candidates has already been published.

The preparation of the final list of contesting candidates – the list that will appear on the ballot paper and that will be made public for the benefit of voters – is governed by section 38. The list must be prepared on either Form 7A or Form 7B, whichever is appropriate for the type of election in question. For instance, Form 7A may be used for parliamentary elections, while Form 7B could be designated for state assembly elections, or the opposite could be true depending on the specific instructions issued by the Election Commission. The list must contain all the particulars required by law, such as the candidate’s name, the party or symbol, and any other data that the Commission has prescribed. Moreover, the language in which the list is prepared must be one of the languages directed by the Election Commission. India is a multilingual nation, and the Commission may require that the list be printed in Hindi, English, or any other official language of the state in which the election is taking place. If the list is prepared in more than one language – for example, both Hindi and English – the names of the candidates must be arranged in alphabetical order according to the script of a particular language that the Commission designates. This ensures that the ordering of names is consistent across different language versions, which helps avoid disputes about the placement of a candidate’s name on the ballot.

When a poll becomes necessary in a parliamentary or assembly constituency – that is, when the election proceeds to the stage where voters are called upon to cast their ballots – the returning officer must also consider the symbols that the contesting candidates have indicated they wish to use. The statutes grant the returning officer the authority to allot a distinct symbol to each candidate, seeking to honor the candidate’s preference as far as practicable. This allocation is subject to any general or special directions that the Election Commission may issue on the matter, which could, for example, limit the use of certain symbols to recognized parties or impose caps on the number of candidates who may share a symbol. If more than one candidate expresses a desire for the same symbol – a situation that can arise when several independent candidates claim the same emblem – the law instructs the returning officer to resolve the conflict by drawing lots, a random selection method that ensures fairness. The symbol that is ultimately allotted to each candidate will appear next to his or her name on the ballot paper, thereby enabling voters to identify their preferred candidate quickly, even if they are unable to read the candidate’s name.

Taken together, these provisions constitute a comprehensive procedural framework that governs every step of the electoral process, from the first public announcement of an upcoming election to the final determination of which symbol each candidate will bear on the ballot. By mandating the use of specific forms, requiring sworn affidavits, stipulating the method of publishing notices, and defining the manner in which symbols are allocated, the law seeks to promote uniformity, reduce the scope for error or manipulation, and protect the rights of both candidates and voters. The involvement of the Election Commission at each stage – whether through issuing gazette notifications, giving directions to returning officers, or authenticating certificates – provides an overarching supervisory authority that helps maintain the integrity of the democratic process across the diverse and populous nation of India.

The returning officer, who is the public official charged with the administration of an election in a particular constituency, is vested with the authority to assign to each candidate a distinctive emblem or symbol that will appear on the ballot paper beside the candidate’s name. This assignment of a symbol is intended to help voters, many of whom may be illiterate or may have limited familiarity with the full names of the candidates, to recognise the candidate they wish to support. Once the returning officer has selected a particular emblem for a candidate, that choice is regarded as final and binding, except in the limited circumstance where the symbol that has been allocated conflicts with a specific directive or instruction that has been issued by the Election Commission. The Election Commission, which is the independent constitutional body responsible for supervising the conduct of elections throughout the nation, may at any time issue directions that prescribe particular rules regarding the use of symbols, for example to avoid duplication, to prevent the use of symbols that are likely to cause confusion, or to enforce other policy considerations. If a symbol allotted by the returning officer is found to be inconsistent with such a direction, the Commission is empowered to modify the allocation. In exercising this power, the Commission may revise the assignment in any manner it deems appropriate, taking into account the need to preserve the integrity of the electoral process, the fairness to the candidates, and the clarity for the electorate. The Commission’s decision in this regard is conclusive and overrides the original allocation made by the returning officer.

Immediately after a symbol has been allotted, the returning officer is required to inform the candidate, or the candidate’s authorized election agent, without delay. The candidate must receive a written notice that identifies the specific emblem that has been assigned, and together with that notice the returning officer must also provide a specimen of the symbol. A specimen is a clear reproduction of the emblem in the form that will appear on the ballot paper, enabling the candidate and his or her agent to verify that the correct image has been reproduced and to ensure that there are no errors that could mislead voters. This prompt communication serves two purposes: it confirms to the candidate that the administrative step of symbol allocation has been completed, and it gives the candidate an opportunity to raise any objections or to seek clarification before the printing of the ballot papers proceeds.

The publication of the list of contesting candidates and the declaration of the result in cases where the election is uncontested are governed by detailed procedural rules. As soon as the returning officer has compiled the list of all persons who have filed valid nomination papers and whose nominations have not been withdrawn or rejected, the officer must cause a copy of that list to be displayed in a conspicuous place within his or her office. The purpose of this public posting is to make the information readily accessible to any interested party, including members of the public, the press, and representatives of the candidates. If the number of candidates who have been duly nominated is equal to, or fewer than, the number of seats that are to be filled in the constituency, the election is deemed to be uncontested because there is no competition for any of the available seats. In such a scenario, the returning officer is required, immediately after the list has been posted, to declare the result of the election in accordance with the relevant provisions of section 53, sub‑section (2) or sub‑section (3), whichever applies. The declaration must be made using one of the prescribed forms, which are numbered from Form 21 to Form 21B, each of which is tailored to a particular type of election outcome. The officer must sign the appropriate form, complete all required particulars, and then forward signed copies of the declaration to the appropriate authority, which may be the district election office, as well as to the Election Commission and to the chief electoral officer of the state. This ensures that all supervisory bodies receive official notice of the election result and can record it in their registers.

In the event that a poll is required because the number of candidates exceeds the number of seats, the returning officer must provide each candidate, or the candidate’s election agent, with a copy of the list of contesting candidates. This distribution enables each candidate to verify that the list accurately reflects the individuals who are standing for election, and to prepare any necessary legal challenges or objections if they believe that a rival’s nomination is invalid. After furnishing the copies to the candidates, the returning officer must also publish the list in the Official Gazette, which is the government’s official journal of record. Publication in the Gazette gives the information a formal legal status, making it part of the public record and thereby ensuring that it is accessible to all voters and interested parties. This dual process of private distribution and public notice underpins the transparency and fairness of the electoral process.

The appointment of an election agent is regulated by a specific procedural form known as Form 8. An election agent is a person who is authorized by a candidate to act on the candidate’s behalf in the conduct of the election, to manage the campaign, to receive communications from the returning officer, and to represent the candidate in any procedural matters. To formalize this appointment, the candidate must complete Form 8, which records the name, address, and particulars of the agent, as well as the candidate’s declaration that the agent is being appointed in accordance with the law. The completed form must be submitted in duplicate to the returning officer. Upon receipt, the returning officer affixes his official seal and signature to one copy of the form, thereby indicating his approval of the appointment. He then returns the sealed copy to the election agent. This exchange creates a paper trail that confirms the legitimacy of the agent’s authority and allows election officials to verify the identity and credentials of the person acting on the candidate’s behalf throughout the election period.

If for any reason the candidate wishes to terminate the appointment of an election agent, the revocation must be carried out using another prescribed form, known as Form 9. This form records the details of the agent whose appointment is being withdrawn, the date of revocation, and the signature of the candidate or the candidate’s authorized representative. By using the standardized form, the authorities ensure that the revocation is documented in a clear and consistent manner, reducing the risk of misunderstandings or disputes about the status of the agent.

The rules governing the appointment of polling agents are set out in section 46 of the election legislation. A polling agent is a person designated by a candidate to attend the polling station on election day, to observe the conduct of the poll, to verify that the ballot papers are correctly handled, and to raise any objections to irregularities. The law permits each candidate to appoint one primary polling agent and two relief agents who can serve as substitutes if the primary agent is unavailable. All appointments of polling agents must be made using Form 10, a standardized document that records the identity of the agent, his or her address, and the signature of the candidate or the candidate’s election agent confirming the appointment. The completed Form 10 must be handed over to the polling agent, who is then required to present it at the polling station or at the designated place where the poll is being held. The instrument of appointment serves as proof of the agent’s authority to enter the polling station and to perform the duties assigned to him or her.

No polling agent may be admitted to the polling station unless he or she has first delivered the appointment instrument to the presiding officer, who is the official in charge of the polling station on election day. Upon receiving the form, the polling agent must complete a declaration that is printed on the same form, and must sign it in the presence of the presiding officer. This declaration typically includes statements that the agent will observe the polling process impartially, will not interfere with the voting of electors, and will report any irregularities promptly. The signing of the declaration before the presiding officer provides an additional safeguard that the agent understands and accepts his or her responsibilities, and it creates a record that can be referred to if any allegations of misconduct arise.

If a candidate decides to withdraw a polling agent after the appointment has been made, the revocation must be effected using Form 11. This form records the name of the agent whose appointment is being cancelled, the date of revocation, and the signature of the candidate or his or her election agent. The completed Form 11 must be lodged with the presiding officer at the polling station. Once the presiding officer receives the revocation, the former polling agent loses the right to be present at the polling station. Nevertheless, the law provides that the candidate or his election agent retains the ability to make a fresh appointment of a new polling agent at any time before the poll is closed. The new appointment must follow the same procedure set out in the earlier rule, namely the completion of Form 10, delivery of the instrument to the presiding officer, and signing of the declaration. This flexibility ensures that a candidate can always have a representative present to monitor the voting process, even if circumstances change during the course of the election day.

The hours during which polling is to take place are established under section 56 of the election statutes. These polling hours must be formally announced to the public by means of a notification published in the Official Gazette. The Gazette is the authoritative source for all legal notices, and publication therein gives the polling schedule legal effect, making it enforceable and binding on all parties. By publishing the polling hours in the Gazette, the election authorities guarantee that every voter, candidate, election official, and interested observer has access to the same official timetable, thereby reducing confusion and preventing disputes about the timing of the poll.

With very few exceptions, the law requires that every elector cast his or her vote in person at the polling station that has been assigned to the elector under section 25, or, where a different location has been designated under section 29, at that alternative place. This principle of personal voting is grounded in the democratic ideal that each citizen should have a direct and private opportunity to express a choice on the ballot. The requirement for personal presence also helps to preserve the secrecy of the ballot and to minimise the risk of coercion, fraud, or undue influence that might arise if votes were cast by proxy. Exceptions to this rule are provided for in subsequent provisions, most notably for certain categories of voters who, because of their service obligations, physical circumstances, or other special conditions, are permitted to vote by postal ballot rather than in person.

The third part of the electoral framework deals specifically with the postal ballot, a mechanism that enables certain voters to cast their vote remotely when they cannot attend the polling station in person. The first provision in this part offers precise definitions of the categories of voters who are eligible to make use of the postal voting system. The term “service voter” is defined to include any person who falls within the categories enumerated in clause (a) or clause (b) of section 60 of the Representation of the People Act. However, the definition expressly excludes “classified service voters,” a separate class that is defined elsewhere, in rule 27M, and which is subject to its own set of regulations. The “special voter” category comprises any individual who holds an office that is specifically listed in subsection (4) of section 20 of the Representation of the People Act, 1950. This includes certain government officials, members of the armed forces, or other persons whose official duties are recognised as necessitating a special provision for voting. The definition also extends to the spouse of such a person, provided that the spouse has been entered onto the electoral roll on the basis of a declaration made under subsection (5) of the same section. Finally, the term “voter on election duty” refers to any person who, because of his or her role as a polling agent, polling officer, presiding officer, or another public servant engaged in election‑related duties, is physically unable to vote at the polling station where the individual is registered. This situation arises because the individual is required to be present at a different location to perform official responsibilities, and therefore the law allows such a person to vote by post.

The next clause enumerates the classes of individuals who, provided they satisfy the procedural requirements laid down in the subsequent sections, are entitled to cast their ballot by post in a parliamentary or assembly constituency election. The provision begins by stating that, in elections for a seat in either the national parliament or a state legislative assembly, a specific set of persons may be permitted to use the postal ballot system. Although the original excerpt stops before listing those persons in detail, the broader statutory context makes clear that the categories typically include the service voters, special voters, and voters on election duty described earlier, as well as other persons who may be unable to appear in person because of physical disability, residence abroad, or other compelling reasons. Each of these categories is subject to a set of eligibility criteria, such as the need to submit an application for a postal ballot within a prescribed time‑frame, to provide satisfactory proof of identity and residence, and to comply with any additional procedural safeguards designed to protect the integrity of the vote. The law also stipulates the manner in which the postal ballot must be issued, the deadlines for returning it, and the safeguards that ensure that the ballot is cast in secret and counted in the same manner as ballots cast in the polling station. By establishing these detailed rules, the legislation seeks to balance the fundamental right of every eligible voter to participate in the democratic process with the need to maintain a secure and trustworthy electoral system.

The provisions set out in this section identify four distinct categories of persons who are entitled to receive a postal ballot in an election, each defined by the particular circumstances of their status or duties. The first category is that of special voters, a term which refers to individuals who, because of their official position, residence, or other qualifying condition, are not able to cast their vote in person at the ordinary polling place and therefore may request a ballot to be sent to them by mail. The second category comprises service voters, meaning members of the armed forces or other uniformed services who are stationed away from their normal place of residence, often in remote or overseas locations, and consequently cannot attend the regular polling station. The third category includes voters who are engaged in election duty; these are persons such as polling officers, presiding officers, or other election officials who are required to be present at a polling station on the day of the poll in order to administer the election, and who therefore cannot vote for themselves at the same time. The fourth category consists of electors who are being held under preventive detention, that is, individuals who have been detained by the authorities for reasons of public safety or security before any trial has taken place, and who nevertheless retain the right to participate in the democratic process. In addition to these four groups, the law also makes provision for persons who have been specifically notified under clause (c) of section 60 of the Act, a provision that includes absentee voters—people who are temporarily absent from their constituency for reasons such as employment, study, or health, and who therefore require a separate mechanism to ensure that their vote can be cast even though they are not physically present.

When the election in question is for a seat in a council constituency, the categories of persons entitled to a postal ballot are slightly different. In that context, the law provides that any voter who is on election duty, any elector who is subject to preventive detention, and any elector who resides in the whole constituency or in any part of it that has been specifically identified by the Election Commission may request a postal ballot. The Election Commission has the authority, under clause (b) of rule 68, to direct that all electors in a particular area of the constituency receive a postal ballot, a measure that can be employed when circumstances make it impractical for a large number of voters to reach the polling station, for example in cases of natural disaster, severe weather, or widespread disruption of transport.

In the case of an election for members of a legislative assembly, the law narrows the categories again. Here, only electors who are under preventive detention are automatically eligible for a postal ballot, but the Election Commission may also issue a directive, under clause (a) of rule 68, that all electors in the constituency be permitted to vote by post if the circumstances so require. This flexibility allows the Commission to respond to emergencies or other extraordinary situations that could otherwise disenfranchise a large segment of the electorate.

A special provision, identified as clause 18A, deals specifically with voters who are on election duty and who need to cast their vote at a facilitation centre. The rule states that notwithstanding any other requirement in the same part of the law, a voter who is on election duty must first receive his or her postal ballot, then mark the vote on that ballot in accordance with the procedures laid down in the part, and finally return the completed ballot to the designated facilitation centre. The location of the facilitation centre is determined by the returning officer, who must specify it in writing. This arrangement ensures that the voter on election duty can fulfil both his or her official responsibilities at the polling station and the civic duty of voting, without having to travel to a separate polling place.

The process by which a special voter may request a postal ballot is set out in rule 19. A special voter who wishes to exercise the right to vote by post must send an intimation, that is, a formal notice, using the prescribed Form 12, to the returning officer. The notice must be received by the returning officer no later than ten days before the scheduled date of the poll. Once the returning officer receives this intimation, he is obligated to issue a postal ballot paper to the applicant. This ten‑day period provides sufficient time for the ballot to be prepared, dispatched, and returned in time to be counted with the other votes.

Rule 20 governs the procedure for voters who are on election duty and who also wish to vote by post. The first part of the rule, sub‑paragraph (1), requires such a voter to submit an application in Form 12 to the returning officer, ensuring that the application reaches the officer at least seven days before the poll, although the returning officer has the discretion to permit a shorter period if circumstances justify it. Upon receipt of the application, the returning officer must be satisfied that the applicant is indeed a voter on election duty; this verification may involve checking the applicant’s official designation or duty roster. If the verification is satisfactory, the returning officer must then issue a postal ballot paper to the voter.

The second part of rule 20, sub‑paragraph (2), addresses a slightly different situation in which a voter on election duty, who holds a position such as polling officer, presiding officer, or any other public servant assigned to election duty within his own constituency, prefers to vote in person at the polling station rather than by post. In that case the voter must submit a separate application using Form 12A, and the application must reach the returning officer at least four days before the poll, although again the officer may allow a shorter interval if needed. If the returning officer is convinced that the applicant is a public servant on election duty in the constituency in question, the officer must issue an election‑duty certificate in the form of Form 12B. This certificate serves as official proof that the individual has been granted permission to vote in person despite being on duty. In addition, the returning officer must annotate the marked copy of the electoral roll with the abbreviation “EDC” next to the voter’s name, indicating that an election‑duty certificate has been issued. Finally, the returning officer must take steps to ensure that the voter is not permitted to cast a vote at the polling station where he would normally be entitled to vote, thereby preventing any double‑voting or confusion about which ballot box should receive his vote.

Rule 21 deals with electors who are under preventive detention, a situation in which the individual is being held in custody for reasons of public safety before any trial has taken place, yet retains the constitutional right to vote. Sub‑paragraph (1) requires the appropriate Government—typically the state or central government responsible for the prison or detention facility—to, within fifteen days after the election is called, identify all electors who are being detained, and to inform the returning officer of each such person’s name, residential address, electoral roll number, and the details of the place of detention. This prompt reporting ensures that the returning officer can include these detained electors in the list of persons eligible for a postal ballot. Sub‑paragraph (2) provides that any elector who finds himself under preventive detention may, within the same fifteen‑day window, send an intimation to the returning officer expressing his wish to vote by post. The intimation must contain the elector’s name, residential address, electoral roll number, and the location where he is being detained. Sub‑paragraph (3) then obliges the returning officer, on the basis of either the government’s intimation under sub‑paragraph (1) or the individual’s own intimation under sub‑paragraph (2), to issue a postal ballot paper to every such detained elector, thereby ensuring that his vote can be cast despite his confinement.

The specifications for the actual ballot paper are laid down in rule 22. The first sub‑paragraph makes clear that every postal ballot paper must be accompanied by a counterfoil, which is a detachable portion that serves as a record for the election officials, showing that a ballot has been issued. Both the ballot paper itself and the counterfoil must conform to a format prescribed by the Election Commission, and the information on them must be presented in any language or languages that the Commission deems appropriate, thereby accommodating linguistic diversity. The second sub‑paragraph requires that the names of the candidates be printed on the ballot in exactly the same order in which they appear on the official list of contesting candidates, ensuring consistency and preventing any confusion about the order of names. The third sub‑paragraph addresses the rare but possible situation in which two or more candidates share the same name. In such cases the ballot must contain an additional identifier for each candidate—such as the candidate’s occupation, place of residence, or some other distinguishing feature—so that the voter can clearly differentiate between them when marking his or her choice.

Rule 23 sets out the complete package that must accompany each postal ballot when it is dispatched to the elector. The ballot, together with its counterfoil, must be sent by ordinary post, and the envelope must bear a certificate of posting, which serves as proof that the item was mailed on a particular date. Along with the ballot the elector must receive a declaration form in the shape of Form 13A, which the elector signs to confirm that the ballot is being used in accordance with the law; a cover sheet in Form 13B, which provides additional instructions and space for the elector’s details; a larger envelope addressed to the returning officer, known as Form 13C, which the elector will use to return the completed ballot; and a set of instructions compiled in Form 13D, which guide the voter through the entire process of marking, sealing, and sending back the ballot. The rule also includes a proviso that, for the special categories of voters—namely special voters and voters on election duty—the returning officer is permitted, and indeed may find it necessary, to deliver the ballot paper and all accompanying forms directly to the voter in person, rather than relying on ordinary postal delivery. This provision acknowledges the practical difficulties that might arise in delivering postal ballots to individuals who are in remote locations, detained, or otherwise hard to reach, and ensures that the integrity of the voting process is maintained for these specially protected groups.

The provision begins by stating that, when a postal ballot paper is to be sent to a voter, the Returning Officer may use an electronic method of transmission that has been approved and specified by the Election Commission. The Election Commission, which is the independent constitutional body tasked with supervising the conduct of elections in the country, has the authority to determine the particular electronic channels, such as secure email, encrypted web portals, or other digital delivery mechanisms, that may be employed for this purpose. The reference to “the persons specified in sub‑clause (ii) of clause (a) of rule 18” identifies the particular categories of electors who are entitled to receive a postal ballot, for example service voters, special voters, or other persons who are unable to attend a polling station in person. The language therefore allows the use of modern technology to reach these voters, provided that the method has been formally sanctioned by the Commission.

When a postal ballot paper is transmitted by electronic means, the rule makes clear that the same procedural requirements that apply to a paper ballot must also apply, albeit with the necessary modifications. The phrase “mutatis mutandis” is a Latin term meaning “the necessary changes being made” and signals that the substantive provisions of this rule as well as rules numbered 22, 24, and 27 will be applied to the electronic transmission in a manner that reflects the different medium. In practice this means that the same standards of confidentiality, verification, and record‑keeping that govern a physical ballot are to be observed for an electronic ballot, with adjustments for the fact that the ballot is delivered digitally rather than by post.

The Returning Officer, who is the official responsible for the overall administration of an election in a given constituency, must, at the very moment that a ballot paper is issued, carry out three related actions. First, on the counterfoil—a detachable part of the ballot paper that remains with the Returning Officer’s records—the officer records the electoral roll number of the voter. The electoral roll number is a unique identifier assigned to each registered elector and appears on the official list, known as the marked copy of the electoral roll, that shows who is entitled to vote. Second, the Returning Officer marks the name of the elector in the marked copy of the roll to indicate that a ballot paper has been issued. This entry is made without writing down the serial number of the specific ballot that has been sent, thereby preserving the secrecy of the ballot while still allowing the officer to track that the voter has received a ballot. Third, the officer must ensure that the same elector is not permitted to cast a vote at a physical polling station on the election day, because the voter is already voting by post; allowing a double vote would defeat the principle of one person, one vote.

Before any ballot paper is handed to an elector who is voting in a local authority constituency or by a member of a legislative assembly, the rule requires that the serial number printed on the ballot be concealed in a manner directed by the Election Commission. The serial number is a unique code that distinguishes one ballot from another and is essential for the administrative audit of the election, yet its visibility to the voter could potentially undermine the secrecy of the ballot. By ordering that the number be effectively hidden—perhaps by using a covering strip, a watermark, or an opaque overlay—the Commission seeks to balance the need for traceability with the constitutional guarantee that the content of a vote remains private.

All officers who have custody of a postal ballot paper, whether they are postal workers, clerks, or other officials who handle the paper during its journey, are required to deliver the ballot to the intended recipient without any unnecessary delay. This duty underscores the importance of timeliness in ensuring that the voter receives the ballot with enough time to mark it and return it before the deadline, thereby preserving the voter’s right to participate fully in the electoral process.

Once the Returning Officer has issued ballot papers to every elector who is entitled to vote by post, a series of closing steps must be undertaken to secure the electoral records. In the case of a parliamentary or assembly constituency election, the officer must, subject to the specific instructions of rule 27P, place the portion of the marked copy of the electoral roll that lists service voters into a sealed packet. A brief description of what the packet contains—such as “service voter roll entries”—and the date on which the sealing occurs must be written on the outside of the packet. The remaining relevant sections of the marked copy are then forwarded to the various presiding officers, who are the officials in charge of individual polling stations, so that they can continue with their responsibilities. In doing so, the officer may also note the names of electors who received ballot papers at polling stations, but again must refrain from recording the serial numbers of those ballots, preserving ballot secrecy. For any other type of election, such as a municipal or local body election, the entire marked copy of the electoral roll is sealed in a packet, labelled with a concise description of its contents and the sealing date, ensuring that the official record is safely archived.

In addition to sealing the roll, the Returning Officer must also seal a separate packet that contains the counterfoils of all the ballot papers that have been issued to postal voters. The counterfoil is the part of the ballot that stays with the election officials and serves as a receipt that a ballot was dispatched. As with the roll, a short description of what is inside the packet and the date of sealing must be recorded on its exterior. This dual sealing process creates a clear audit trail: one packet documents which voters were sent ballots, while the other shows which ballot papers were sent out, without linking any specific serial number to a particular voter.

The next rule deals with the act of recording a vote on a postal ballot. An elector who receives a postal ballot and wishes to cast a vote must mark his or her choice on the ballot paper following the specific instructions that appear on Part I of Form 13D. Form 13D is the official ballot template that contains the list of candidates or options, along with clear guidance on how to indicate a preference, for example by ticking a box or drawing a mark. After the vote has been marked, the elector places the completed ballot inside the protective envelope provided in Form 13B, which is designed to keep the ballot confidential and to protect it from damage during transit.

The elector must also sign a declaration contained in Form 13A. This declaration confirms that the voter has indeed cast the vote indicated on the ballot and attests to the authenticity of the process. The signature must be made in the presence of a qualified official, and that official must attest to the signature—that is, they must certify that the signature belongs to the voter. The rule specifies a hierarchy of officials who may fulfill this role, depending on the category of the voter. For a service voter—a member of the armed forces, paramilitary forces, or a government employee stationed away from their home constituency—the attesting officer may be appointed by the commanding officer of the unit, ship, or establishment where the voter is posted, or by the diplomatic or consular representative of India in the country where the voter resides, reflecting the need to accommodate voters who are abroad or in remote postings. For a special voter, who might be a person entitled to vote under special circumstances such as a caretaker government official, the attesting officer must be at least of the rank of Deputy Secretary to the Government, ensuring a senior level of oversight. In the case of a voter who is on election duty—someone assigned to assist with the conduct of the election at a polling station—a Group A or Group B officer, or alternatively the presiding officer of the polling station where the voter is serving, may act as the attesting official. If the voter is under preventive detention—a situation where a person is lawfully confined for reasons of national security or public order—the attestation may be performed by the Superintendent of the jail or the Commandant of the detention camp where the elector is held. For any other category of voter not expressly covered, the Election Commission may issue a notification naming an appropriate officer to act as the witness.

The final rule addresses the situation of electors who, because of illiteracy, blindness, or other physical infirmities, cannot independently fill out the ballot paper or sign the declaration. In such cases, the voter may bring the entire set of documents—the ballot paper, the declaration, and the envelopes—to an officer who is competent to attest the signature under the conditions described in sub‑rule (2) of rule 24. The voter requests that officer to record the vote on the ballot paper and to sign the declaration on the voter’s behalf. The officer is then required to mark the ballot in accordance with the voter’s expressed choice, do so in the voter’s presence to preserve transparency, sign the declaration as a witness, and complete the related certification that appears in Form 13A. This safeguard ensures that voters who are unable to write or sign due to disability are not disenfranchised, while still maintaining the integrity and authenticity of the voting process.

Taken together, these provisions create a comprehensive framework for conducting postal voting that balances accessibility, security, and secrecy. The use of electronic transmission where permitted modernizes the system, while the meticulous record‑keeping—such as noting electoral roll numbers on counterfoils, sealing rolls and counterfoil packets, and requiring attestation by designated officials—provides a robust audit trail. The special accommodations for service voters, special voters, election‑duty personnel, detainees, and persons with disabilities reflect the inclusive intent of the electoral law, ensuring that a wide range of citizens can participate in the democratic process without compromising the fundamental principle that each vote remains private and that no individual may vote more than once. By following these detailed steps, election administrators can uphold the credibility of the electoral outcome and maintain public confidence in the fairness of the election.

The provisions that follow describe in detail the procedures that must be observed when a postal ballot paper, together with any accompanying documents required under rule twenty‑three, fails to reach the intended voter, and the steps that are to be taken when such documents are returned, spoiled, or delayed. Under clause one of section twenty‑six, if a ballot paper that has been dispatched by post and the other papers that are required by rule twenty‑three are returned to the returning officer because they could not be delivered for any reason—whether the address was incorrect, the post office was unable to locate the voter, or some other circumstance prevented delivery—the returning officer is authorised to take remedial action. The officer may either re‑issue the entire set of papers by sending them again through the postal system, ensuring that a certificate of posting is obtained as proof that the items have been dispatched, or, if the voter makes a specific request, the officer may arrange for the papers to be delivered directly to the elector in person. This personal delivery may be carried out by the returning officer themselves or by a designated agent, and it is intended to guarantee that the elector receives the ballot materials in a timely manner despite the initial delivery failure.

Clause two of the same section deals with situations in which an elector, perhaps inadvertently, handles the ballot paper or any of the associated documents in such a way that they become unsuitable for use. For example, an elector might accidentally tear the ballot, spill a liquid on it, or otherwise damage it such that the paper can no longer be marked clearly or the accompanying forms cannot be completed as required. In such cases, the elector is required to return the spoiled items to the returning officer. Once the returning officer is satisfied that the damage was accidental and not the result of intentional tampering, the officer must issue a second set of the ballot paper and accompanying documents to the elector. This second set is intended to replace the unusable first set and to allow the elector to cast a valid vote without undue delay.

The third clause of section twenty‑six sets out the administrative duty of the returning officer with respect to the spoiled papers that have been returned. The officer must cancel those papers, which means marking them in a way that clearly indicates they are no longer valid for voting. After cancelling, the officer is required to keep the cancelled items together in a separate packet that is distinct from any other election material. On each packet, the officer must note the particulars of the election—such as the name of the constituency, the date of the election, and the type of election—and must also record the serial numbers of the ballot papers that have been cancelled. This record‑keeping ensures that there is a clear audit trail that can be inspected later to confirm that the cancelled papers have not been re‑introduced into the count.

Section twenty‑seven explains how a completed postal ballot must be returned to the returning officer. Once an elector has marked his or her choice on the ballot paper and has made the required declaration—either under rule twenty‑four, which generally covers a standard declaration of identity and intention, or under rule twenty‑five, which may involve additional statements for special circumstances—the elector must send the ballot and the declaration back to the returning officer. The return must be made in accordance with the instructions that are printed on Part II of Form thirteen D, which typically include the address to which the ballot should be mailed, any specific envelope requirements, and any deadlines that must be observed. The crucial requirement is that the ballot must reach the returning officer before the hour that has been fixed for the commencement of the counting of votes. This deadline ensures that all postal votes can be included in the first count and that the counting process can begin on schedule.

If a postal ballot arrives after the deadline specified in the first subsection, the returning officer is required to note the exact date and time at which the envelope was received. All such late‑received envelopes must then be placed together in a separate packet that is kept apart from ballots that arrived on time. By segregating the late ballots, the returning officer can later determine whether any of them may be counted, depending on the rules that apply to late submissions, and can also maintain a clear record for any potential disputes.

The returning officer must also keep all envelopes containing postal ballots in safe custody until the counting of votes actually begins. “Safe custody” implies that the envelopes are stored in a secure location—such as a locked room or a secure filing cabinet—under the supervision of authorized election staff, and that they are protected from loss, tampering, or damage. This safeguard is essential for preserving the integrity of the election and for ensuring that every valid ballot can be accounted for when the counting process starts.

The next part of the regulations, identified as Part Three A, deals specifically with the procedure for voting by a class of electors who have been specially notified by the Election Commission. In order to give clear meaning to the terms used in this part, a set of definitions is provided. The term “Assistant Returning Officer, for the notified class of electors,” refers to the assistant returning officer who has been specifically appointed by the Election Commission to manage the voting process for that particular group of voters. This appointment is made in accordance with the Commission’s rules and is intended to provide a point of contact who is knowledgeable about the special provisions that apply to the notified class.

The definition of “absentee voter” includes any person who belongs to a class of individuals that may be notified under clause (c) of section sixty of the Representation of the People Act. Such individuals are typically employed in essential services that are deemed critical for the functioning of society, such as health care workers, police officers, or members of the armed forces. The definition also expressly includes senior citizens, persons with disabilities, and, where applicable, individuals who are suspected of having COVID‑19 or who have been confirmed to be affected by the disease. In other words, an absentee voter is anyone who, because of their occupation, age, health condition, or pandemic‑related status, cannot attend the polling station in person and therefore is entitled to cast a vote by post.

The term “nodal officer” is used to denote an officer who has been given the authority to verify the claim of an absentee voter who is not a senior citizen or a person with a disability. This verification step is necessary to confirm that the individual’s request for postal voting is legitimate and complies with the eligibility criteria set out by the election authorities. The nodal officer’s role is thus a safeguard against fraudulent claims and ensures that the special provisions are applied only to those who truly qualify.

A “notified elector” is defined as an elector who belongs to a class of persons that has been formally notified by the Election Commission under clause (c) of section sixty, but who does not fall within the narrower category of absentee voters. In practice, this means that a notified elector may be a person who meets the criteria for special voting arrangements but does not belong to the essential‑service or pandemic‑related categories that are covered by the absentee voter definition. The distinction is important because different procedural rules may apply to absentee voters and to other notified electors.

The definition of “person with disability” aligns with the benchmark disability definition contained in clause (r) of section two of the Rights of Persons with Disabilities Act, 2016. A person with a benchmark disability is one whose disability meets a nationally recognised standard of severity, and such individuals are flagged in the electoral roll database as “persons with disability.” This flagging allows election officials to identify them quickly and to apply the appropriate voting accommodations, such as postal ballot issuance.

A “poll officer” in this context is the election official who has been deputed—meaning formally assigned—the specific task of issuing postal ballots to electors who belong to the class of senior citizens or persons with disabilities. This officer is responsible for ensuring that the ballot packets are correctly prepared, that the appropriate documentation accompanies each ballot, and that the dispatch is recorded in the election’s official registers.

The term “senior citizen” is used in this part to describe an elector who is a member of the absentee‑voter class and who is at least eighty years of age. By setting the age threshold at eighty, the regulations recognise that individuals in this age group may have mobility or health concerns that make it difficult for them to travel to a polling station, thereby justifying the provision of a postal ballot.

Finally, “COVID‑19 suspect or affected persons” are defined as electors who either have tested positive for the coronavirus disease according to a government‑approved hospital or a designated COVID‑19 hospital, or who are under home quarantine or institutional quarantine because of exposure to the virus. The quarantine status must be certified by a competent authority, such as a health department official, and the certification must be in accordance with any additional guidelines that may be issued by a state government or union territory administration. This definition ensures that individuals who are medically advised to remain isolated can still participate in the electoral process without endangering public health.

Section twenty‑seven B makes clear that, notwithstanding any other provisions contained in Part Three of the main electoral rules, the special provisions set out in this Part shall apply to any notified elector who wishes to vote by post in an election. In other words, if a person belongs to a notified class and chooses to use the postal voting system, the procedures detailed in this Part—such as the issuance of ballot papers, the handling of applications, and the roles of the various officers—take precedence over the general rules that govern ordinary postal voting.

Section twenty‑seven C deals with the manner in which a notified elector must inform the election authorities of his or her intention to vote by post. The elector is required to submit an application using Form twelve C, which is a prescribed form that collects all necessary information, such as the elector’s name, address, identification details, and the specific class to which the elector belongs. This application must be sent to the Assistant Returning Officer for the notified class of electors, and it must reach the officer at least ten days before the scheduled date of the poll. The ten‑day period provides sufficient time for the Assistant Returning Officer to verify the applicant’s eligibility, to prepare the appropriate ballot packet, and to dispatch the materials so that they can be returned in time for counting.

Upon receipt of a valid application, the Assistant Returning Officer is obligated to issue a postal ballot paper to the elector. This issuance includes not only the ballot itself but also any accompanying declaration forms and instructions that the elector needs to complete in order to cast a valid vote. However, the law also provides a safeguard: if an application fails to include all of the particulars required by Form twelve C, the Assistant Returning Officer may reject the application. This rejection is permissible only after the officer has made reasonable efforts to obtain the missing information, and only if it remains impossible to ascertain the required details. This clause prevents incomplete or ambiguous applications from delaying the electoral process while still giving applicants a fair opportunity to correct any deficiencies in their submission.

The law governing the conduct of elections by postal ballot sets out a series of detailed procedural requirements that are intended to guarantee both the integrity of the voting process and the equal treatment of all electors, including those who are unable to attend a polling station in person. One of the central provisions deals with the situation of an absentee voter, a person who, for reasons such as being away from the constituency at the time of the election, must cast his or her vote by post. In this context, the legislation specifies that the absentee voter must submit an application using a prescribed document known as Form 12D. This form is a standardized template that contains a series of fields where the applicant is required to provide particular pieces of information. The particulars that must be entered in Form 12D are enumerated in the form itself, and they typically include the voter’s name, age, address, the constituency to which the voter belongs, the reason for the absentee status, and any other identifying data that the election authorities deem necessary to verify the eligibility of the applicant.

Once the applicant has completed Form 12D, the document must be verified by a designated official referred to as the Nodal Officer. The Nodal Officer is a senior election official appointed by the Election Commission of India to oversee the processing of applications for postal voting within a particular jurisdiction. The verification carried out by the Nodal Officer consists of confirming that the information supplied by the applicant matches the records held in the electoral roll and that the applicant satisfies the legal criteria for being granted a postal ballot. It is important to note that the verification requirement does not apply to certain categories of voters who are already covered by separate provisions; namely, senior citizens, defined as persons aged sixty years or more, and persons with disability, defined as individuals who suffer from a physical or mental impairment that substantially limits one or more major life activities. For these two groups, the verification by a Nodal Officer is not required because they are entitled to a more streamlined process under other sections of the election law.

After the Nodal Officer has completed the verification, the duly verified Form 12D must be transmitted to the Returning Officer. The Returning Officer is the chief election official for a particular constituency and has the ultimate responsibility for ensuring that the electoral process is conducted in accordance with the law. The law mandates that the verified application must reach the office of the Returning Officer within a strict time limit of five days from the date on which the election has been formally announced, a date that is officially termed the notification of election. This five‑day window is intended to allow sufficient time for the election machinery to prepare the necessary postal ballot papers and related documents, while also preventing undue delays that could affect the timely conduct of the election.

In addition to the requirement for verification, the legislation also addresses the procedural consequences of submitting an incomplete or improperly certified application. Specifically, if an applicant files a Form 12C or a Form 12D without attaching the certificate that must be issued by either the authorized officer or the Nodal Officer as prescribed in Part II of the relevant form, the election authorities are empowered to reject the application outright. The certificate in question serves as an official endorsement confirming that the applicant has satisfied all the eligibility criteria for postal voting, and its absence is considered a material defect that precludes the application from being processed further.

The rules concerning the physical characteristics of the postal ballot paper itself are set out in Section 27D. Every postal ballot paper must be accompanied by a counterfoil, which is a detachable portion of the paper that remains with the election officials after the ballot is issued. The counterfoil is used to record information about the ballot and to maintain a trail that links the ballot to the specific elector who received it. Both the ballot paper and its counterfoil must conform to a particular format that the Election Commission may prescribe, and the language or languages used on these documents must also be specified by the Commission. This ensures that the ballot is understandable to the voter and that the official records are consistent across the country.

The names of the candidates who are contesting the election are printed on the postal ballot paper in a specific sequence. The sequence follows the order in which the candidates appear on the official list of contesting candidates, a list that is compiled by the Returning Officer after the nomination process has been completed. By arranging the names in this predetermined order, the election authorities aim to eliminate any ambiguity about which name corresponds to which candidate and to provide a uniform presentation that voters can rely upon.

In instances where two or more candidates share the same name, a potential source of confusion is addressed by requiring that each such candidate be distinguished by additional identifying information. This may take the form of indicating the candidate’s occupation, such as “teacher” or “farmer,” or by specifying the candidate’s place of residence, for example “resident of Delhi” or “resident of Mumbai.” Alternatively, another method of differentiation may be employed, provided it clearly separates the identities of the candidates and allows the voter to make an informed choice without the risk of mistakenly voting for the wrong individual.

Section 27E deals with the issuance of the postal ballot paper to the voter. The law stipulates that the ballot paper must be sent by post, and that the dispatch must be accompanied by a certificate of posting, which serves as proof that the postal service has accepted the item for delivery. Along with the ballot paper, the voter must receive a set of supplementary documents that together form a complete postal voting package. The first of these is a declaration in Form 13A, which the voter must sign to confirm that the vote being cast is indeed his or her own and that the voter complies with all the statutory requirements for postal voting. The second document is a cover in Form 13B, which is a sealed envelope designed to protect the ballot paper during transit and to ensure that the ballot remains confidential until it reaches the election officials. A larger envelope, identified as Form 13C, is addressed to the Returning Officer; this envelope is intended to hold the entire set of documents that the voter will return after marking the ballot, thereby facilitating the secure transport of the vote back to the election authority. Finally, the voter receives a set of instructions in Form 13E, which provides step‑by‑step guidance on how to correctly complete the ballot, how to sign the declaration, and how to package the materials for return. These instructions are crucial for preventing errors that could invalidate the vote.

A particular provision allows the Assistant Returning Officer, who is a deputy to the Returning Officer and is responsible for managing the group of electors who are entitled to vote by post, to deliver the ballot paper and its accompanying forms directly to the voter in person. This personal delivery is permissible when the Assistant Returning Officer determines that such a method is more appropriate, perhaps due to the voter’s circumstances or the lack of reliable postal service in the area. However, even when the ballot is delivered in this manner, the same procedural safeguards—such as the inclusion of the declaration, cover, large envelope, and instructions—must still be provided.

For absentee voters, an additional specification states that the ballot paper may be issued in a manner that is determined by the Election Commission. The Commission may, for instance, authorize the use of electronic transmission or allow the ballot to be collected from a designated office, depending on the logistical challenges faced by the voter. This flexibility is intended to accommodate the diverse situations of absentee voters while still preserving the essential safeguards of the postal voting system.

When the Assistant Returning Officer issues the ballot paper, a series of administrative steps must be undertaken simultaneously. First, the officer must record the elector’s unique electoral roll number on the counterfoil attached to the ballot paper. The electoral roll number is a distinct identifier assigned to each registered voter and is recorded in the official list of electors for the constituency. By inscribing this number on the counterfoil, the election officials create a direct link between the physical ballot and the specific voter who received it. Second, the officer must make a notation on the marked copy of the electoral roll—a version of the roll that is used for administrative purposes—to indicate that a postal ballot has been issued to the particular elector. This notation does not, however, include the serial number of the ballot paper, thereby preserving voter anonymity and preventing any possible correlation between a specific ballot and the voter’s identity. Third, the officer is required to ensure that the elector does not cast a vote at a traditional polling station on the election day, because each elector is allowed to vote only once, either in person or by postal ballot, but not both.

All officials who have custody of a postal ballot paper at any stage of the process, whether they are responsible for sending it out, handling it during transit, or receiving it back, are mandated to take all reasonable measures to guarantee that the ballot reaches the intended recipient without undue delay. This duty underscores the importance of timely delivery, as any significant postponement could prevent the voter from returning the ballot before the deadline for counting.

The Assistant Returning Officer also bears the responsibility of ensuring that all postal ballots are dispatched to the eligible electors whose applications have been received in accordance with rule 27C, which outlines the procedure for notifying the election authority of the need for a postal ballot. This issuance must be completed at least eight days before the date of the poll in the relevant constituency. The eight‑day period provides a buffer that allows voters sufficient time to receive, complete, and return their ballots while still enabling the election officials to process and count the votes in accordance with the prescribed timetable. Once the eight‑day window expires, the Assistant Returning Officer must take a series of steps to preserve the integrity of the electoral record. The marked copies of the electoral rolls that were used to track ballot issuance are to be placed in sealed envelopes. Each envelope must be labeled with a brief description of its contents and the date on which it was sealed. These sealed envelopes are then forwarded to the Returning Officer for safekeeping. In parallel, the counterfoils that were detached from the issued ballot papers must be gathered together and sealed in a separate packet. This packet, too, receives a brief description and a sealing date before being sent to the Returning Officer. The separation of the ballot papers from their counterfoils and the secure sealing of both sets of documents serve as a safeguard against tampering and help maintain a clear audit trail.

Section 27F deals with the act of recording a vote on the postal ballot paper. When a voter receives the ballot package, he or she must follow the instructions contained in Form 13E to mark the ballot correctly. The instructions typically explain how to indicate a choice—whether by placing a tick, a cross, or another designated mark—next to the name of the preferred candidate, and they may also provide guidance on how to handle any special situations, such as an invalid vote or a protest vote. After marking the ballot, the voter must place it inside the cover provided by Form 13B, which is then sealed to preserve the secrecy of the vote.

The voter is also required to sign the declaration found in Form 13A. This declaration is a statutory statement in which the voter affirms that the ballot has been filled out by the voter personally, that the voter is eligible to vote in the election, and that the voter has complied with all procedural requirements. The signature on the declaration must be witnessed and attested by an officer who is authorized under sub‑rule (2) of rule 27J. That officer is typically a government employee or a designated election official who has the legal authority to verify the identity of the voter and to certify that the signature is genuine. The attestation process adds an additional layer of verification, ensuring that the ballot cannot be fraudulently completed by an unauthorized person.

In the case of an absentee voter, the law provides a special provision for the place where the vote may be recorded. The Election Commission has the discretion to specify a particular centre—such as a designated office of the election authority, a consular office abroad, or any other approved location—where the absentee voter can go to fill out the ballot. This flexibility is meant to accommodate voters who may be located far from their constituency or who may have limited access to postal services. Furthermore, for absentee voters who belong to the senior citizen category or who are persons with disability, the requirement for the declaration to be attested by the officer specified in rule 27J is relaxed. In these cases, the attestation may be performed by the poll officer, who is the official responsible for overseeing the conduct of voting at a polling station. This accommodation acknowledges the practical challenges that older voters and voters with disabilities may face in obtaining an attestation from a higher‑level election official.

Overall, the series of rules and procedural steps outlined in these provisions are designed to create a robust system for postal voting that balances the need for accessibility with the imperative of safeguarding the electoral process. By mandating specific forms, verification by designated officers, clear timelines, and rigorous documentation of each stage—from the issuance of the ballot to its eventual return and counting—the legal framework seeks to ensure that every eligible voter, whether present in the constituency, temporarily absent, senior, or differently‑abled, can exercise the franchise in a manner that is both convenient and secure. The detailed specifications concerning the format of the ballot, the method of distinguishing candidates with identical names, the packaging of the ballot materials, and the record‑keeping obligations of election officials all serve to minimize the risk of fraud, reduce the possibility of administrative errors, and uphold public confidence in the democratic process.

Section 27G deals with the special assistance that must be provided to electors who, because of illiteracy, blindness or any other physical infirmity, are unable to mark their choice on a postal ballot paper and to sign the accompanying declaration. The provision recognises that a democratic election must be inclusive and that persons who cannot read, write or see the ballot paper should not be disenfranchised. In such circumstances the elector is required to bring the ballot paper together with the declaration form and any enclosing covers that have been sent to him to an officer who has been specifically authorised under sub‑rule (2) of rule 27J. The elector must make a clear request to that officer that the officer record the vote in his presence and also sign the declaration on his behalf. The officer, upon receiving the elector’s request, must act in accordance with the elector’s expressed wishes. He does this by marking the ballot paper in the exact manner the elector indicates, ensuring that the choice is recorded accurately. He must also sign the declaration where the elector is unable to do so, and he must fulfil every other procedural requirement that the law imposes for a valid postal vote. The purpose of this sub‑rule is to guarantee that the vote of a person who is physically unable to complete the ballot himself is counted, while preserving the integrity of the voting process by requiring the officer to act only after the elector’s explicit direction and in his presence.

Section 27H provides the mechanisms for re‑issuing a postal ballot paper when the original set of documents fails to reach the elector. Under sub‑rule (1) the Assistant Returning Officer who is responsible for the class of electors that have been notified to vote by post may, upon learning that the ballot paper and any accompanying forms have been returned undelivered for any reason, either send a fresh set of papers by post using a certificate of posting, or deliver them personally, or arrange for personal delivery by a courier or other means. This may happen either because the elector himself requests a new set or because the officer, acting on his own initiative, determines that the original papers have not been received. Sub‑rule (2) addresses a situation in which a notified elector, by accident, mishandles the ballot paper or any of the accompanying documents in a way that renders them unsuitable for use – for example, by tearing, spilling liquid on them, or otherwise spoiling them. In such a case the elector must return the damaged papers to the Assistant Returning Officer, explain the inadvertent mistake, and receive a second set of the ballot paper and related documents. Sub‑rule (3) requires the Assistant Returning Officer to formally cancel the spoiled papers that have been returned. The cancelled papers must be placed in a separate packet that is clearly labelled with the particulars of the election – such as the name of the constituency, the date of the election, and any other identifying information – together with the serial numbers of the cancelled ballot papers, so that there is a transparent record that those specific papers are no longer part of the ballot pool.

Section 27I sets out the obligations of a notified elector after he has completed his vote and signed his declaration, either under the ordinary procedure described in rule 27F or under the assisted procedure of rule 27G. Once the elector has recorded his choice and made the required declaration, he must return the ballot paper and the declaration to the Returning Officer who is responsible for that particular election. The return must occur before the hour that has been fixed for the commencement of the counting of votes; that is, the ballot must be in the Returning Officer’s possession before the counting begins. An exception is provided for absentee voters, for whom the postal ballot paper must be returned to the centre that has been designated for recording votes under sub‑rule (3) of rule 27F. This exception may be further modified by any direction that the Election Commission may issue in respect of absentee voting. If a cover containing a postal ballot paper arrives after the prescribed deadline, sub‑rule (2) obliges the Returning Officer to note the exact date and time at which the cover was received. The Returning Officer must then keep all such late‑arriving covers together in a separate packet, distinct from the packet of ballots that were received on time. Sub‑rule (3) requires that the Returning Officer retain all covers that contain postal ballot papers in safe custody until the counting of votes begins, thereby ensuring that the integrity of the ballot papers is protected and that there is a clear audit trail for any ballots that might be examined during the counting process.

Section 27J identifies the categories of officers who are authorised to perform certain functions under this part of the electoral regulations. Sub‑rule (1) states that the officers listed in sub‑rule (2) are the only individuals who may act as authorised officers for two specific purposes: first, for carrying out the duties described in sub‑rule (2) of rule 27G, which relates to the assistance that an officer provides to an illiterate or infirm elector; and second, for issuing a certificate that appears in Part II of Form 12C, a form that is used in the administration of postal voting. Sub‑rule (2) then enumerates the types of officers who may be designated as authorised officers for the purposes identified in sub‑rule (1). These include an officer in charge of a migrant camp or any area where migrants reside, an officer in charge of an office from which a migrant elector receives his salary as a migrant employee, an officer in charge of a treasury or bank from which a migrant elector draws his pension as a pensioner, and any gazetted officer – that is, an officer who has been formally listed in the Gazette and holds a position of official authority. By specifying these categories, the rule ensures that assistance to vulnerable electors is provided by officials who are likely to be familiar with the elector’s circumstances and who have the requisite authority to act on his behalf.

Section 27K deals with the handling of the marked copy of the electoral roll during the polling process. The Returning Officer is required to use the marked copy of the electoral roll that has been supplied to him by the Assistant Returning Officer for the notified class of electors. This marked copy contains an indication of which electors have already been sent a postal ballot paper. The purpose of using this marked roll during the poll is to prevent any elector who has already voted by post from attempting to cast a second vote in the polling station. In other words, the marked roll serves as a safeguard against double voting, ensuring that each eligible person can cast only one vote, whether it is by postal ballot or in person.

Section 27L gives the Election Commission a broad power to alter the way that postal ballot papers are handled in relation to ordinary ballot papers. Notwithstanding any other provision that may be found in rule 54A, the Commission may issue a notification in the Official Gazette directing that postal ballot papers be mixed together with the ordinary ballot papers at the stage when the ordinary ballots are being mixed under rule 59A. If the Commission makes such a direction, it may also prescribe, by further instructions to the Returning Officers, the exact method by which the mixing of postal and ordinary ballots should be carried out in each constituency. This power allows the Commission to adopt procedures that enhance the secrecy and integrity of the ballot counting process, for example by ensuring that postal ballots are not kept separate and thereby potentially identifiable.

Section 27M provides definitions that are essential for interpreting the provisions that follow in Part IIIB, which concerns voting by classified service voters through proxy. The first definition, under clause (a), clarifies that a “classified service voter” is any individual who falls within the category described in clause (a) of section 60 of the main electoral law and who has chosen to exercise his franchise by appointing a proxy. The second definition, also under clause (a), explains that a “proxy” is the person who has been appointed by a classified service voter, in accordance with rule 27N, to cast a vote on the elector’s behalf and in the elector’s name. The third definition, under clause (b), states that a “service voter” is any person who is listed in clause (a) of section 60 and who appears on the last part of the electoral roll for the relevant constituency. These definitions establish the terminology that will be used in the subsequent rules governing proxy voting for members of the classified services, ensuring that the legal language is clear and that the rights and obligations of both the voter and the appointed proxy are precisely delineated.

The regulations governing the appointment of a proxy by a voter who belongs to a classified service—such as members of the armed forces, police, or other uniformed organisations that are permitted to cast a special “service” vote—are set out in a series of detailed provisions. A classified service voter, who is a person enlisted in one of these categories and therefore entitled to a separate ballot, may choose not to vote in person but instead to entrust his or her vote to another individual. This option is provided for under rule 27N, which authorises the voter to give his vote by proxy, that is, by designating another person to cast the ballot on his or her behalf, provided that the appointment is carried out in accordance with the procedural steps described in sub‑rules (2) to (4) of the same rule.

The first condition laid down in sub‑rule (2) specifies who may be appointed as a proxy. Any service voter who wishes to vote by proxy is free to select any person to act as his or her proxy, but that person must satisfy a number of statutory requirements. The proxy must be an ordinary resident of the parliamentary or assembly constituency in which the election is being held; this means the individual must ordinarily live in the same geographic area as the voter and be known to the local electoral authorities as part of the ordinary community. In addition, the proxy must be at least eighteen years of age, the minimum legal age for a person to be recognised as an elector in India. Finally, the proxy must not be disqualified from being entered on an electoral roll under section sixteen of the Representation of the People Act, 1950, a piece of legislation that enumerates various disqualifications such as insolvency, unsound mind, or conviction for certain offences. These safeguards are intended to ensure that the person acting as a proxy is a legitimate elector who can be trusted to exercise the vote responsibly.

Having identified a suitable individual, the classified service voter must formalise the appointment by completing a prescribed document known as Form 13F. This form is the official instrument through which the voter records the name and details of the chosen proxy and declares his or her intention to have the proxy cast the ballot. The completion of Form 13F constitutes the legal act of appointing a proxy, and the form must be filed with the returning officer, who is the election official responsible for overseeing the conduct of elections in the relevant constituency.

The validity of the proxy appointment is addressed in sub‑rule (4). Once the voter has submitted Form 13F, the appointment remains in force for as long as the voter continues to be classified as a service voter. The appointment automatically terminates if the voter revokes it, if the voter dies, or if the proxy himself or herself dies, whichever occurs first. Revocation of a proxy must be made by filing another prescribed document, Form 13G, and the revocation takes effect from the date on which the returning officer receives the form. If the voter wishes to replace a deceased or revoked proxy while still retaining his status as a service voter, he may appoint a substitute proxy by filing a further Form 13G. The substitute proxy then assumes the role of the original proxy from the moment the returning officer records the new form. This mechanism ensures continuity of representation for the voter and avoids any lapse in the ability to cast a ballot.

The procedural obligations of the voter do not end with the filing of Form 13F or Form 13G. Rule 27‑O requires the voter to inform the returning office of the name of the appointed proxy as soon as practicable after the appointment has been made. The notification must reach the returning officer no later than the final date for the filing of nomination papers for the earliest election that is scheduled to take place in the constituency after the appointment. This deadline is critical because it determines whether the proxy will be recognised for the upcoming election or only for future contests. If, despite the rule, the notification arrives after the nomination deadline has passed, the notification will be considered ineffective for the election that is already under way, but it will remain valid for any subsequent election, provided the other conditions of rule 27N are satisfied.

Upon receipt of the voter’s intimation, the returning officer is required to take a series of administrative actions as set out in rule 27‑P. First, the officer must annotate the master electoral roll—the official list of all voters in the constituency—by writing the abbreviation “CSV” beside the name of the classified service voter. The letters stand for “Classified Service Voter” and serve as a clear indicator that the voter has appointed a proxy. The officer then follows one of two courses of action depending on the timing of the intimation. If the notice arrives before the deadline for nomination papers, the returning officer must ensure that no postal ballot paper is issued to the service voter, because the vote will be cast by the proxy in person at the polling station. Conversely, if the notice is received after the nomination deadline, the returning officer must issue a postal ballot paper to the classified service voter for the election that is currently in progress, in accordance with the provisions governing postal voting set out in Part III of the rules.

In addition to marking the electoral roll, the returning officer must compile and maintain a separate, up‑to‑date register that lists all classified service voters who have informed the office of their proxies, together with the full addresses of those proxies. The format of this register is determined by the Election Commission, which may issue specific guidelines from time to time. After the nomination deadline has passed, the returning officer uses this register to prepare, for each polling station, a sub‑list that groups classified service voters and their respective proxies according to the residential address recorded in the electoral roll. These sub‑lists are then attached, in the order prescribed by the Election Commission, to the relevant part of the electoral roll that will be used at each polling station. Once attached, the combined document—consisting of the original roll and the appended sub‑list—constitutes the “marked copy” of the electoral roll that will be used by polling officials during the conduct of the poll, as required by rule 33A or rule 49F, depending on the specific circumstances of the election. This systematic process ensures that the proxy’s vote can be accurately recorded and that the integrity of the electoral process is maintained for all classified service voters.

The provision identified as 27Q deals specifically with the manner in which a person who is authorized to cast a vote on behalf of another voter—known as a proxy—must act when the voter for whom the proxy is standing belongs to a classified service. A classified service voter is an individual who is employed in a particular category of public service that is recognized by the electoral regulations and whose name appears on a special segment of the electoral roll. The electoral roll is the official list of all persons who are entitled to vote in a given election, and it is organized by polling stations so that each voter knows the location where they are required to cast their ballot. In this context, sub‑rule (4) of rule 27P provides the mechanism for adding the name of a classified service voter to the appropriate part of the roll. The first part of rule 27Q therefore requires that the proxy, the person acting on the classified service voter’s behalf, must be physically present at the polling station that corresponds to the entry of the classified service voter in the roll. The presence of the proxy at the designated polling station ensures that the vote is recorded in the correct location and that the procedural safeguards that apply to ordinary electors are also applied to proxy votes.

The second subsection of 27Q elaborates on the exact process by which the proxy must record the vote. It states that the proxy is to mark the ballot in exactly the same way that any other elector who is assigned to that polling station would do. This means that the proxy must follow the same sequence of steps, use the same ballot paper, and abide by the same procedural rules that govern the act of voting for a regular elector. The reference to rules 34, 35 and 36 through to 43 indicates that these rules set out the detailed procedures for marking a ballot, handling the ballot paper, and ensuring that the vote is properly recorded and kept confidential. In addition, where special provisions are applicable—specifically rules 49G, 49H and the series from 49J to 49R—the proxy must also comply with those provisions in the same way that they apply to any other voter at the polling place. The inclusion of a “provided that” clause introduces a specific interpretative adjustment: any mention in rule 37, or where relevant rule 49K, of the “left forefinger of the elector” must be understood, for the purpose of a proxy vote, to refer to the “left middle finger” of the proxy. This adjustment reflects the practical reality that the elector whose vote is being cast is not physically present to place a finger on the ballot, and therefore the proxy must use a different finger to satisfy the requirement for a physical confirmation of the vote. By providing this clarification, the rule ensures that the act of voting by proxy remains consistent with the legal standards that apply to personal voting while accommodating the logistical differences inherent in proxy voting.

The text then proceeds to the heading of Part IV, which deals with voting in parliamentary and assembly constituencies. These constituencies are the geographical areas that elect representatives to the national Parliament or to a state legislative assembly. Within Part IV, Chapter I is concerned with the mechanics of voting by ballot, meaning the process by which a voter marks a piece of paper to indicate the candidate of choice and then deposits that paper into a ballot box. Section 28 supplies the definitions that are essential for interpreting the language of this chapter and the subsequent Chapter II. The term “candidate” is defined simply as a person who is contesting the election, that is, a person whose name appears on the ballot as an option for voters. The term “constituency” is clarified to mean either a parliamentary constituency, which sends a member to the national legislature, or an assembly constituency, which sends a member to a state legislature. The definition of “polling agent” is more elaborate: a polling agent is a person appointed by a candidate, under the authority granted by section 46 of the governing election law, to act on behalf of that candidate at a specific polling station. The definition explicitly includes the candidate themselves and the candidate’s election agent—another official who assists with the campaign—if they happen to be present at the polling station. This inclusive definition ensures that anyone acting in support of a candidate at the polling place is covered by the rules governing polling agents.

Section 29 deals with the design of ballot boxes, which are the containers that receive the completed ballot papers. The requirement is that every ballot box must conform to a design that has been approved by the Election Commission, the independent body responsible for overseeing the conduct of elections. By mandating Commission approval, the law seeks to guarantee that the boxes meet standards of security, durability, and tamper‑evidence, thereby protecting the integrity of the votes placed inside them.

Section 30 describes the form that ballot papers must take. Sub‑section (1) stipulates that each ballot paper must have a counterfoil attached to it. The counterfoil is a detachable portion that remains with the election officials and records the number of ballots printed, thereby providing an audit trail. Both the ballot paper and its counterfoil must be produced in a format and with the particulars—such as candidate names, symbols, and any required instructions—that the Election Commission decides. The language or languages used on the ballot must also be those approved by the Commission, ensuring that all voters can understand how to mark their choice. Sub‑section (2) requires that the names of the candidates be listed on the ballot in the same sequence as they appear on the official list of contesting candidates. This uniform ordering prevents any inadvertent bias that could arise from a random or arbitrary arrangement. Sub‑section (3) addresses the situation where two or more candidates share exactly the same name. To avoid confusion, the law mandates that such candidates be distinguished by adding an identifier, such as their occupation, their place of residence, or another distinguishing feature, thereby enabling voters to differentiate between them when marking their ballots.

Section 31 outlines the physical arrangements that must be present at each polling station on election day. The first requirement, set out in sub‑section (1), is that two notices be displayed prominently in a location visible to anyone entering the polling place. The first notice must specify the polling area—that is, the geographic zone whose residents are entitled to vote at that station. If the polling area is served by more than one polling station, the notice must also detail which voters are assigned to the particular station in front of them, thereby reducing confusion about where a voter should go. The second notice must be a copy of the list of contesting candidates, allowing voters to verify who is standing for election before they mark their ballot. Sub‑section (2) requires that each polling station be equipped with one or more voting compartments. These compartments are private spaces, often called voting booths, that allow electors to mark their ballots without being observed by others, thereby preserving the secrecy of the vote. Sub‑section (3) obliges the returning officer—the official responsible for overseeing the conduct of the election in that constituency—to supply each polling station with a sufficient number of ballot boxes, the relevant portion of the electoral roll (which lists the names of the electors eligible to vote at that station), the ballot papers themselves, the tools required to stamp a distinguishing mark on the ballot papers (such as a serial number or a unique identifier), and any other items that might be necessary for electors to make a clear mark on their ballots, such as pens or pencils. By ensuring that all these materials are available, the law seeks to prevent any procedural delays or obstacles that could impede voters from exercising their franchise.

Section 32 governs who may be admitted inside the polling station at any given time. The presiding officer, who is the official in charge of conducting the poll at a particular station, must manage the flow of people to avoid overcrowding, which could compromise order and the secrecy of the vote. The presiding officer must also exclude from the interior of the polling station anyone who does not fall within a specific set of categories. The first category includes polling officers, who are the staff members assigned to manage the voting process. The second category comprises public servants who are on duty in connection with the election, such as security personnel or administrative staff. The third category includes individuals who have been specifically authorized by the Election Commission, perhaps for special tasks such as observation or technical assistance. The fourth category consists of candidates, their election agents, and, subject to the provisions of rule 13, one polling agent for each candidate; this ensures that each candidate has a limited but sufficient presence at the station to monitor the process without causing congestion. The fifth category allows a child who is being carried by an elector to accompany that elector, recognizing that many voters travel with young children. The sixth category permits a person to accompany a blind or infirm elector who requires assistance in moving to or within the polling place. Finally, the seventh category covers any other persons who may be employed by the returning officer or the presiding officer under the authority granted by sub‑rule (2) of rule 34 or sub‑rule (1) of rule 35. These provisions collectively aim to maintain a controlled environment within the polling station, protecting the secrecy and integrity of the vote while allowing necessary assistance and oversight.

Section 33 details the procedure for preparing ballot boxes for the actual casting of votes. When a paper seal is employed as a security measure to lock a ballot box, the presiding officer must first affix his own signature to the seal. In addition, the presiding officer must obtain the signatures of any polling agents who are present and who wish to affix their signatures to the same seal. This practice creates a record of who was involved in securing the ballot box and provides an additional layer of accountability. After the signatures have been collected, the presiding officer places the paper seal into the designated slot on the ballot box, thereby locking the box in a manner that still leaves the slit for inserting ballot papers open. This arrangement allows electors to deposit their completed ballots while preventing any unauthorized opening of the box. The final sub‑section mandates that the seals used to secure a ballot box must be applied in such a way that, once the box is closed, it cannot be opened without breaking the seal. This tamper‑evident characteristic ensures that any attempt to access the contents of the box after the poll has begun would be immediately apparent, thereby deterring fraud and preserving confidence in the electoral process.

Taken together, these provisions construct a comprehensive framework that governs how votes are cast, recorded, and protected in the context of parliamentary and assembly elections. By specifying the responsibilities of proxies for classified service voters, defining the essential terms, prescribing the design and handling of ballot boxes, detailing the form and layout of ballot papers, outlining the physical and administrative arrangements at polling stations, and establishing strict protocols for securing ballot boxes, the law seeks to uphold the principles of secrecy, fairness, and integrity that are fundamental to a democratic election. Each rule interacts with the others to create a system in which every eligible voter—whether voting in person, through a proxy, or with assistance—can exercise their franchise in a manner that is transparent, verifiable, and protected against undue influence or manipulation. The meticulous attention to procedural detail reflects the broader objective of the electoral framework: to ensure that the will of the electorate is accurately captured and that the outcome of each election is accepted as legitimate by all participants and observers.

The presiding officer, who is the official responsible for overseeing the conduct of the poll at a particular polling station, must take special care when the ballot boxes used for the election do not require the traditional paper seals that are often employed to guarantee that the containers remain unopened. In such circumstances the presiding officer is required to secure and seal the ballot box in a manner that leaves the narrow slit through which ballot papers are to be inserted open, thereby allowing the physical entry of votes while still preventing any unauthorised removal of the contents. In addition, the presiding officer must permit any polling agents who are present at the polling place and who wish to do so to affix their own seals to the box; these seals, which are typically small pieces of wax or other tamper‑evident material bearing the agent’s identifier, serve as an additional layer of assurance that the box has not been interfered with after it has been closed.

Every ballot box that is to be used at a polling station must carry identification labels on both its interior surface and its exterior surface. These labels are required to display several pieces of information that together allow the box to be uniquely recognised and tracked throughout the election process. First, the label must show the serial number, if one has been assigned, together with the name of the constituency – that is, the larger electoral district in which the polling station is situated. Second, the label must also contain the serial number and the name of the specific polling station, which distinguishes this location from other stations within the same constituency. Third, a separate serial number that is unique to the ballot box itself must be recorded; this particular number is to be entered on the outside label only after the poll has concluded, thereby providing a clear record of which box contained which votes. Finally, the label must bear the date of the poll, allowing anyone who examines the box later to confirm the exact day on which the votes were cast. By requiring both an inside and an outside label, the rule ensures that the identification of each box is evident whether it is opened for inspection or simply observed from the outside.

Immediately before the poll is opened to voters, the presiding officer has a duty to demonstrate in front of the polling agents and any other individuals who are present that the ballot box is empty. This demonstration includes showing that the box contains no stray papers, debris, or any other objects that might suggest tampering, and also confirming that the labels described in the preceding rule are correctly affixed and contain the appropriate information. Once this verification has been completed, the presiding officer proceeds to close the ballot box, to seal it according to the prescribed method, and to secure it so that it cannot be opened without proper authority. The sealed box must then be placed in a location where it remains fully visible to both the presiding officer and the polling agents throughout the voting period, thereby allowing continuous visual monitoring and reducing the risk of secret interference.

At the same time, just before voting commences, the presiding officer must also exhibit the marked copy of the electoral roll that will be used to verify the identity of each voter. The electoral roll is a printed list of all persons who are entitled to vote in the constituency, and the marked copy is a version in which the names of voters who have already cast their ballots are crossed off or otherwise indicated. The presiding officer must show that this marked copy does not contain any entries that fall outside the narrow categories allowed by the law. Specifically, the roll must not include any entry that is not the result of a correction made under clause (b) of sub‑rule (2) of rule 20, which deals with the removal of duplicate or erroneous entries, nor any entry that is not a correction made under clause (b) of sub‑rule (2) of rule 27E, which concerns the updating of addresses and other details. In addition, the roll must not bear any marks other than those that are expressly permitted: a mark made in accordance with clause (b) of sub‑rule (2) of rule 23, which allows for the indication of a voter who has already voted, or a mark made under clause (b) of sub‑rule (2) of rule 27E, which permits a specific notation for voters whose names have been amended. By ensuring that the roll is free from extraneous entries and unauthorized markings, the presiding officer helps to maintain the integrity of the voting register and to prevent confusion or fraud at the polling station.

When a polling station is intended to serve both male and female electors, the presiding officer has the discretion to organise the order in which voters enter the station so as to respect cultural sensitivities and to provide a comfortable environment for all participants. The officer may direct that men and women be admitted alternately, in separate groups or batches, thereby avoiding a situation in which men and women are mixed together in the same queue. This practice is intended to accommodate societies in which gender segregation is customary or required, while still ensuring that the poll proceeds efficiently. Moreover, the returning officer, who is the senior official responsible for the overall conduct of the election in the constituency, or the presiding officer at a particular station, may appoint a woman to serve as an attendant at any polling place. The female attendant’s duties include assisting women electors with any special needs they may have, helping them locate their names on the roll, and generally supporting the presiding officer in matters that pertain specifically to female voters. One of the attendant’s important responsibilities is to assist in searching a woman elector should it become necessary to verify her identity more thoroughly, for example if there is a question about the authenticity of her enrolment details.

The presiding officer is authorised to enlist the help of any persons he deems suitable in order to facilitate the identification of electors or to provide assistance in other aspects of conducting the poll. These assistants may be volunteers, election officials, or other individuals who are trusted to act impartially. As each voter arrives at the polling station, the presiding officer – or a polling officer who has been delegated this authority – must compare the name and other personal particulars of the voter, such as age, address, and any identifying symbols, with the corresponding entry in the official electoral roll. After making this verification, the official calls out the serial number, which is the unique number assigned to each entry on the roll, together with the voter’s name and any other relevant particulars, thereby providing a transparent confirmation that the correct person is being served. In constituencies where voters have been issued identity cards under the Registration of Electors Rules of 1960, the voter is required to present this card to the presiding officer or to the authorised polling officer before a ballot paper is issued. The identity card contains the voter’s photograph and other biometric data, which further confirms that the individual presenting the card is indeed the person named on the roll. When deciding whether to issue a ballot paper, the presiding officer or polling officer is allowed to overlook minor clerical or printing mistakes in the electoral roll – such as a misspelled name or an incorrect date of birth – provided that the official is convinced that the person standing before him is the same individual to whom the entry pertains. This discretion helps to avoid disenfranchising voters because of trivial errors that do not affect the substantive identity of the elector.

A special provision applies to persons who are public servants engaged in election duty, such as police officers, election observers, or other officials who have been assigned to assist with the conduct of the poll. The general rules of identification under rule 35 do not apply to these individuals if they produce an election duty certificate in Form 12B, which serves as proof that they are performing official duties on election day. Even if the polling station where they present the certificate is not the same station at which they are entitled to cast their own vote, the presiding officer must still accommodate them. Upon receipt of the certificate, the presiding officer first obtains the signature of the person presenting it, thereby creating a record of the interaction. The officer then records the name and the electoral roll number of the individual, as indicated on the certificate, on the marked copy of the electoral roll at the end of the list, ensuring that the person’s presence is logged. Finally, the presiding officer issues a ballot paper to the public servant and allows him or her to vote in exactly the same manner as any other elector who is entitled to vote at that polling station, thereby respecting the right of election workers to participate in the democratic process while maintaining procedural consistency.

If a polling agent – a person appointed by a candidate or political party to observe the conduct of the poll at a particular station – believes that a person who has arrived to vote is not, in fact, the elector named in the roll, the agent may raise a formal challenge to the person’s identity. To do so, the agent must first place a cash deposit of two rupees with the presiding officer for each challenge that is made. This modest monetary requirement serves as a deterrent against frivolous or malicious challenges while still allowing legitimate concerns to be raised. Once the deposit has been made, the presiding officer is obligated to follow a prescribed procedure. He must first warn the individual who is being challenged that making a false claim about one’s identity – an offence known as personation – carries a penalty under the law. The presiding officer then reads aloud, in full, the entry from the electoral roll that corresponds to the challenged individual, including the serial number, name, address, and any other details recorded. The individual is asked directly whether he or she is the person described in that entry. Regardless of the outcome, the presiding officer must enter the name and address of the person who was challenged into the official list of challenged votes, which is maintained on Form 14. This record ensures that every challenge is documented, providing a transparent trail that can be reviewed later in case of disputes or allegations of irregularities.

The requirement that a person who is being challenged in an election be asked to affix his signature in the list of electors is intended to create a written record of his participation in the verification process. By placing his signature next to his name, the official creates an additional piece of evidence that can be examined later if any dispute arises about whether the individual who presented himself at the polling station was indeed the person whose name appeared on the electoral roll. The act of signing serves as a personal acknowledgment that the individual is the same person listed, and it also provides a trail for auditors and courts to follow if the matter is taken beyond the polling station.

When a challenge to an elector’s identity is raised, the presiding officer—who is the chief official responsible for conducting the poll in a particular booth—must immediately conduct a concise, or “summary,” inquiry. This inquiry is not a full trial but rather a rapid examination designed to determine whether the challenge has merit. To that end, the officer may first ask the challenger, that is, the person who raised the objection, to produce any documentary or testimonial evidence that supports the claim that the person claiming to be a voter is not, in fact, the registered elector. At the same time, the officer may require the individual who is being challenged to present evidence that proves his identity. Such proof might include an identity card, a passport, a driver’s licence, or any other government‑issued document that matches the details on the electoral roll.

In addition to documentary proof, the presiding officer is authorised to pose a series of questions directly to the person whose identity is in doubt. These questions are designed to elicit factual answers that can confirm or refute the claim of impersonation. For example, the officer might ask about the elector’s date of birth, place of residence, occupation, or the names of close relatives, all of which are information typically recorded on the electoral roll. The answers must be given under oath, meaning that the person must swear to tell the truth, a legal promise that carries penalties for falsehood. By administering an oath, the officer underscores the seriousness of the inquiry and ensures that the responses are given with the weight of legal responsibility. The officer may also require any other witness who wishes to offer testimony about the identity of the challenged individual to take an oath, thereby extending the same level of solemnity to additional evidence.

Once the presiding officer has gathered the evidence and heard the answers, he must reach a conclusion. If, after considering all the material presented, the officer decides that the challenge has not been established—that is, there is insufficient proof that the person is not the genuine elector—he must allow that individual to cast his vote. Conversely, if the officer is satisfied that the challenge is valid and the person attempting to vote is indeed a substitute or impostor, he must prevent that person from voting. This act of exclusion is commonly referred to as “debarment,” and it is a protective measure designed to preserve the integrity of the election by ensuring that only eligible voters are able to exercise their franchise.

The presiding officer also has discretion to evaluate the motive behind the challenge itself. If he believes that the objection was made in bad faith—perhaps as an attempt to disrupt the voting process, to harass a legitimate voter, or simply because the challenger is seeking to delay the poll without any genuine concern—he may deem the challenge “frivolous.” In such cases, the rule provides that any deposit that the challenger was required to make when lodging the objection must be forfeited to the government. This financial penalty is intended to deter people from making baseless or malicious challenges. On the other hand, if the challenge is made in good faith and the presiding officer ultimately decides that it is not upheld, the officer must return the deposit to the challenger after the inquiry has concluded, thereby restoring the challenger’s funds.

The statutes also contain specific safeguards designed to prevent “personation,” which is the illegal practice of voting in the name of another registered elector. One of the most visible safeguards is the requirement that every elector whose identity has been satisfactorily verified by the presiding officer or the polling officer must present his left forefinger for inspection. The officer inspects the finger to confirm that it is the correct one, and then he applies a mark of indelible ink to the same finger. Indelible ink is a special type of pigment that is resistant to washing or rubbing, and it remains visible for several days, thereby providing a clear visual indication that the individual has already voted. By marking the left forefinger, the election officials create a simple yet effective system that prevents the same person from receiving more than one ballot.

If an elector refuses to allow his left forefinger to be inspected, or refuses to let the ink be applied, or attempts to remove the ink after it has been placed, the law treats this refusal as a serious violation. Likewise, an elector who does not produce his identity card when it is required—such as when the rules stipulate that a photo identification document must be shown—will also be denied a ballot. In either circumstance, the election official is empowered to withhold the ballot paper and to prevent the individual from voting. This strict approach underscores the importance placed on verifying voter identity to prevent fraud.

There are special provisions for elections that are conducted simultaneously for two different levels of government, such as a parliamentary constituency and a state assembly constituency. In such cases, an elector who has already had his left forefinger marked for one of the elections, or who has already produced his identity card for one of them, is allowed to receive a ballot paper for the other election. This rule acknowledges the practical reality that a voter may need to cast two separate votes on the same day and ensures that the verification process does not become a barrier to exercising both votes.

The legislation also anticipates situations in which an elector may be missing a left forefinger, or even all the fingers on his left hand. In these circumstances, the requirement to mark the left forefinger is interpreted flexibly. If the left forefinger is absent, any other finger on the left hand may be used for the ink mark. If all the fingers on the left hand are missing, then any finger on the right hand may serve as a substitute. In the extreme case where the person has lost all fingers on both hands, the law permits the ink to be applied to the extremity of whichever arm the person still possesses. This inclusive language ensures that the verification process accommodates individuals with disabilities while still maintaining the underlying purpose of preventing double voting.

The handling of ballot papers themselves is governed by detailed procedural rules intended to guarantee both accountability and secrecy. Before any ballot paper is handed to a voter, the Election Commission— the independent body that oversees the conduct of elections—must prescribe a distinguishing mark that is stamped on the back of each ballot. This mark may consist of a serial number, a colour code, or any other identifier that allows officials to track the paper without revealing the content of the vote. In addition, the presiding officer must sign the entire back of each ballot paper in full, thereby attesting that the ballot is genuine and has been issued in accordance with the prescribed procedures.

When a polling officer actually issues a ballot to a voter, he must record the voter’s electoral roll number on the counterfoil, which is the detachable portion of the ballot that remains with the election officials. The electoral roll number is a unique identifier assigned to each registered voter and is printed on the official list of electors. The officer must also obtain either the voter’s signature or a thumb impression on the counterfoil, which serves as a personal acknowledgement that the ballot has been received. The thumb impression is a simple imprint of the voter’s thumb that can be taken quickly and is especially useful for voters who are illiterate or otherwise unable to sign their name. After the signature or thumb impression is obtained, the officer must mark the voter’s name in the official copy of the electoral roll to indicate that a ballot has been issued to that individual. This marking is made without noting the specific serial number of the ballot, a practice that protects the secrecy of the vote by ensuring that the ballot cannot later be linked to a particular voter through the serial number.

A safeguard is built into the system that prohibits the delivery of any ballot paper unless the voter has first provided his signature or thumb impression on the counterfoil. This requirement prevents the accidental or intentional issuance of unclaimed ballots, which could otherwise be misused. However, the rules also recognise that it is not necessary for a presiding officer, polling officer, or any other election official to certify the thumb impression itself. In other words, the thumb impression does not need to be formally attested, because the act of taking the impression and recording it on the counterfoil is deemed sufficient evidence of receipt.

Importantly, no one present in the polling station is allowed to record the serial numbers of the individual ballot papers that have been handed out to specific voters. This prohibition is a critical element of the secret ballot system. By ensuring that the serial numbers cannot be matched to particular voters, the law protects voters from any possible coercion or retaliation based on how they voted.

The secrecy of the vote is further reinforced by a set of procedural steps that each voter must follow once a ballot has been issued. After receiving the ballot paper, the elector must immediately proceed to a voting compartment, a private enclosure that shields the voter from observation by other individuals. Inside this compartment, the voter is expected to mark his choice in accordance with the instructions printed on the ballot, then fold the ballot in a prescribed manner, and finally place it in the sealed ballot box. This process is designed to make it impossible for anyone else to see how the voter marked the ballot, thereby preserving the confidentiality that is a cornerstone of democratic elections. The requirement to move quickly to a voting compartment also reduces the likelihood of disturbances or interruptions that could compromise the secrecy of the vote.

Together, these detailed provisions—ranging from the requirement for a signature on the electoral list, through the presiding officer’s authority to conduct a concise inquiry and to impose penalties for frivolous challenges, to the use of indelible ink on the left forefinger and the careful handling of ballot papers—form a comprehensive framework intended to safeguard the integrity, transparency, and privacy of the voting process. By articulating each step in clear, procedural language, the law seeks to minimise opportunities for fraud, to ensure that each eligible voter can cast a single, confidential vote, and to provide a clear audit trail that election officials and, if necessary, courts can examine after the poll has concluded. This multilayered approach reflects a longstanding commitment to democratic principles and underscores the importance of meticulous administrative safeguards in the conduct of free and fair elections.

In the conduct of a voting procedure each elector who reaches the voting compartment is required to follow a series of prescribed steps that together ensure the secrecy and integrity of the vote. First, after the elector has entered the compartment and taken the ballot paper that has been supplied for the purpose, he must indicate his choice by making a mark, usually a tick or a cross, on the ballot paper in close proximity to the symbol that represents the candidate for whom he wishes to cast his vote. The instrument supplied for the purpose of marking the paper may be a pen, a pencil, or any other approved writing device that the election officials have provided in order to avoid the introduction of unregulated materials. Once the mark has been made, the elector must then fold the ballot paper in such a way that the side on which the vote is recorded is concealed, thereby preventing any other person from seeing how the elector has voted. The folding technique is prescribed in the regulations to create a uniform shape that can be easily handled and placed into the ballot box without revealing the contents.

If, at any point during this process, the presiding officer—who is the official responsible for overseeing the conduct of the poll at a particular polling station—asks to see the distinguishing mark that the elector has placed on the paper, the elector is obliged to comply and show the mark to the presiding officer. This requirement may arise, for example, when the presiding officer wishes to verify that the marking has been made correctly or that the elector has not attempted to deface the ballot in a way that would invalidate it. After the mark has been shown, if required, the elector must then insert the folded ballot paper into the ballot box, a secure container that is sealed for the duration of the election and later opened only for the purpose of counting the votes. Finally, having completed the act of voting, the elector is required to leave the polling station, thereby allowing the next eligible voter to proceed to the voting compartment.

The regulations also impose a duty on every elector to cast his or her vote without undue delay. This principle is intended to keep the flow of voters moving efficiently, preventing long queues and ensuring that the polling station can accommodate all eligible voters within the allotted hours of operation. In order to maintain order and protect the confidentiality of each vote, the rules expressly forbid more than one elector from being inside a voting compartment at the same time. The voting compartment is a small, private enclosure that shields the voter from observation; allowing two people inside simultaneously would compromise the secrecy of the ballot and could lead to undue influence or intimidation.

If an elector who has already been handed a ballot paper refuses, after a clear warning has been given by the presiding officer, to follow the procedure outlined above, the presiding officer, or a polling officer acting under the presiding officer’s direction, is authorized to retrieve the ballot paper from the elector. This retrieval applies whether the elector has already marked the ballot or not; the mere refusal to comply with the prescribed method is sufficient to trigger the cancellation. Once the ballot paper has been taken back, the presiding officer must make a notation on the reverse side of the paper stating, in clear language, “Cancelled: voting procedure violated.” Beneath this statement the presiding officer must sign his name, thereby providing a record of who authorized the cancellation and confirming that the proper procedural steps have been observed.

All ballot papers that bear the inscription “Cancelled: voting procedure violated” are to be collected and stored together in a separate envelope or cover. The cover itself must be clearly labeled on its front surface with the words “Ballot papers: voting procedure violated” so that anyone reviewing the materials later can instantly identify the purpose of that particular packet. This segregation ensures that the cancelled ballots are not mixed with the valid votes that will later be counted, thereby preserving the integrity of the count.

In addition to the cancellation procedure, the rules recognize that an elector whose ballot has been taken back under the foregoing provision may also be subject to further penalties, such as fines or other sanctions, as prescribed by the broader electoral legislation. However, irrespective of any additional penalties that might be imposed, the vote that may have been recorded on that particular ballot paper—whether it was marked or left blank—must not be included in the final tally. In effect, the ballot is treated as null and void, and its contents are disregarded for the purpose of determining the outcome of the election.

Special provisions are made for electors who, because of blindness or another physical infirmity, are unable to recognize the symbols that denote the candidates on the ballot paper or to make a mark without assistance. If the presiding officer is satisfied, after a reasonable assessment, that an elector falls within this category, the presiding officer may allow the elector to bring a companion into the voting compartment. The companion must be at least eighteen years of age, which is the legal age of majority in most jurisdictions, and must be able to assist the elector in recording the vote exactly as the elector wishes. The companion’s role includes marking the ballot paper on behalf of the elector, folding the paper to conceal the vote, and placing the folded ballot into the ballot box. The regulation expressly limits each companion to assisting only one elector at any given polling station on the same day, thereby preventing any potential conflict of interest or the possibility that a single person might influence multiple voters. Before a person may act as a companion, he or she must sign a declaration affirming two commitments: first, that the companion will keep the elector’s vote secret and will not disclose it to anyone; and second, that the companion has not already served in that capacity for another elector at any polling station on that day. This declaration is a safeguard designed to protect the confidentiality of the vote and to ensure that the assistance is provided in a transparent and accountable manner.

For the purpose of maintaining a reliable administrative record, the presiding officer is required to document every instance in which a companion has been permitted to assist an elector under these special provisions. This documentation is made in a standardized form known as Form 14A, which captures details such as the name of the elector, the nature of the infirmity, the identity of the companion, and the time at which assistance was rendered. By keeping this record, election officials can later verify that the assistance was provided in accordance with the law and can address any disputes that might arise concerning the validity of the assisted votes.

The rules also address situations in which a ballot paper becomes unusable through no intentional act of the elector. If an elector inadvertently handles the ballot in a way that renders it unsuitable for casting a valid vote—perhaps by tearing it, spilling liquid on it, or otherwise damaging it—the elector may return the compromised ballot to the presiding officer. Upon receiving the ballot, the elector must explain the inadvertent mistake, and the presiding officer, satisfied that the error was not deliberate, will issue a fresh ballot paper to the elector so that the voting process can continue. The ballot paper that has been returned, together with its accompanying counterfoil—a small attached slip that records the serial number of the ballot for accounting purposes—must be clearly marked by the presiding officer with the words “Spoilt: cancelled.” This marking indicates that the ballot has been rendered void and will not be counted, while also preserving an audit trail that shows how many ballots were spoiled during the polling process.

A related circumstance arises when an elector, after receiving a ballot paper, decides not to use it at all. In this case the elector may simply hand the ballot back to the presiding officer. The presiding officer will then mark both the ballot and its counterfoil with the inscription “Returned: cancelled.” This designation distinguishes a voluntarily returned ballot from one that was unintentionally damaged. All ballot papers that have been cancelled either because they were spoilt or because they were returned are to be gathered together in a separate packet, distinct from the ballots that will be counted. Keeping these cancelled papers apart helps election officials maintain a clear and orderly tally and prevents any accidental inclusion of invalid votes in the final count.

The regulations further provide for the handling of “tendered votes,” which occur when an individual claiming to be a particular elector arrives at the polling station after another person has already voted under that same name. In such cases the presiding officer is empowered to ask a series of verification questions designed to establish the claimant’s true identity. These questions might include inquiries about the elector’s date of birth, address, or any other personal details that are recorded on the electoral register. If the claimant satisfactorily answers these questions, the presiding officer may, subject to the conditions set out in this rule, allow the claimant to mark a ballot paper. This ballot is specially designated as a “tendered ballot paper” to differentiate it from the standard ballots issued at the beginning of the polling process.

Before a tendered ballot paper can be handed to the claimant, the claimant is required to sign his or her name on a list that is maintained in a form known as Form 15. This signature is entered opposite the entry that corresponds to the claimant’s name, thereby creating a written record that the individual has received a tendered ballot. The purpose of this signature is to provide an additional layer of accountability and to ensure that there is a clear paper trail linking the person who received the ballot to the ballot itself.

A tendered ballot paper is physically identical to the other ballots used at the polling station, with the only distinction being a set of procedural markings that identify it as a tendered ballot. Specifically, the tendered ballot is placed as the last item in the serially numbered bundle of ballots that are issued for use at the polling station. By positioning it at the end of the bundle, officials can easily track its issuance and prevent any confusion with the regular ballots that are distributed earlier in the voting process. Moreover, both the tendered ballot and its accompanying counterfoil are endorsed on the back with the words “tendered ballot paper” written in the hand of the presiding officer. The presiding officer must also sign his name beneath this endorsement. This handwritten endorsement serves as an official seal, confirming that the ballot has been properly authorized for use in the special circumstances described, and it provides a clear visual cue for later auditors who may need to verify that the tendered ballot was handled in accordance with the rules.

The person who is entitled to vote, known in the legislation as the elector, after he or she has made a mark on a ballot paper that has been handed out to the voter and after the paper has been folded so that the choice cannot be seen, is not required to place the paper directly into the ballot box. Instead, the elector must hand the folded ballot paper to the individual who is in charge of the voting place, the presiding officer. The presiding officer then takes the tendered ballot paper and puts it into a special protective cover that is kept solely for the purpose of holding such papers. This cover is designed to keep the ballot secure and to maintain the secrecy of the vote until the time comes for counting.

A particular exception is built into the rule for members of a political party who are voting in an election that is intended to fill one or more seats in the Council of States, which is the upper chamber of the Federal Parliament. In such cases, before the presiding officer places the voter’s folded ballot paper into the special cover, the presiding officer must give the authorised agent of the political party an opportunity to verify which candidate the elector has chosen. The term “authorised agent” is defined by the legislation as a person who has been appointed by the political party in accordance with sub‑rule (2) of rule 39AA, as it has been made applicable, and who is also named under clause (ii) of rule 70. The appointment must have taken place in the context of a council election, that is, an election for the Council of States, and the agent must have been selected by the party’s assembly members rather than by a postal ballot, as required by clause (a) of rule 68. This provision ensures that the party’s representative can confirm that the vote has been cast for the intended candidate while still preserving the overall confidentiality of the ballot.

The closing of the poll is governed by rule 43. At the hour that has been fixed for the termination of voting under section 56 of the Elections Act, the presiding officer must shut the polling station. From that moment onward, no further electors may be admitted into the voting location. However, there is a safeguard for any voters who are already inside the polling station when the closing time arrives: they are permitted to complete their voting process and cast their ballots. If any dispute arises regarding whether a particular elector was already present in the polling station before it was closed, the decision rests solely with the presiding officer, and his or her determination is final and not subject to further appeal.

Once the poll has been closed, the presiding officer must act promptly to seal the ballot box, as set out in rule 44. The first step is to close the slit through which ballots are normally deposited. In ballot boxes that are equipped with a mechanical closing device, the presiding officer activates that mechanism; where no such device exists, the officer must manually seal the slit, typically by applying a tamper‑evident seal. Any polling agent who is present at the time may also affix his or her own seal to the same slit, providing an additional layer of security. After the slit has been sealed, the entire ballot box is to be locked and otherwise secured to prevent any unauthorised access. In the event that the volume of votes exceeds the capacity of the first ballot box, a second box may be brought into use. Before the second box is employed, the first box must be closed, sealed and secured in exactly the same manner as described, ensuring continuity of the chain of custody.

There is an exception to the standard sealing procedure for those polling stations that have received a specific direction from the Election Commission, the independent body that administers federal elections. When such a direction is in force, the presiding officer must follow the alternative steps laid out in sub‑rule (5). Under this alternative procedure, as soon as practicable after the poll closes, the presiding officer must transfer all ballot papers that are inside the ballot box or boxes to a cloth bag or a cover that is lined with cloth. This transfer must be done without opening or counting the ballots, thereby preserving the secrecy of each vote. The presiding officer must first demonstrate to any polling agents present that the bag or cover is empty, thereby establishing a clear starting point. After the ballots have been placed inside, the presiding officer must allow the agents to inspect each ballot box to confirm that it has indeed been emptied. The presiding officer then records, on the outside of the bag or cover, the name of the constituency, the name of the polling station, and the date on which the poll was held. Finally, the bag or cover is sealed, and any polling agent who wishes may affix his or her own seal to the container, again creating a visible record that the container has not been tampered with.

The preparation of an accounting record of all ballot papers is the next required step, as stipulated in rule 45. At the close of the poll, the presiding officer must complete a document known as Form 16, which is the official ballot paper account. This form lists, for example, the total number of ballot papers issued, the number of ballot papers that were tendered, any ballot papers that were rejected, and other relevant figures. The completed Form 16 must be placed in a separate envelope that is clearly marked with the words “Ballot Paper Account” printed on its front, thereby distinguishing it from other election materials. Furthermore, the presiding officer must provide each polling agent who is present at the close of the poll with a true copy of the entries made in the ballot paper account. The agent must sign a receipt acknowledging that he or she has received a true copy, and the presiding officer must also attest that the copy is accurate and faithful to the original.

Rule 46 deals with the sealing of a variety of other election documents and paper packets that must be kept separate from the ballot boxes but still require strict protection. The presiding officer is required to assemble several distinct packets, each of which contains a specific category of material. One packet holds the marked copy of the electoral roll, which is the official list of all registered voters that has been annotated to show who actually voted. Another packet contains the counterfoils of the used ballot papers; these are the blank backs of the ballot sheets that were printed on the same sheet as the ballot paper and are retained as a means of verifying the authenticity of the ballots. A further packet includes any ballot papers that have been signed in full by the presiding officer under sub‑rule (1) of rule 38 but that have not been handed out to voters, as well as any other ballot papers that were not issued to voters for any reason. Yet another packet comprises ballot papers that have been cancelled because the voter violated the prescribed voting procedure, as defined in rule 39, together with any other cancelled ballots that may exist for miscellaneous reasons. An additional packet contains the cover that holds all the tendered ballot papers, along with the accompanying list that is prepared on Form 15, which records the number and identification of those tendered ballots. The presiding officer must also prepare a packet that holds the list of challenged votes, that is, votes that have been disputed or questioned during the voting process. Finally, any other papers that the Election Commission has directed to be kept in a sealed packet must also be placed in their own sealed packet.

Each of these packets is to be sealed in a manner that creates a clear evidential trail. The presiding officer must affix his or her own official seal to each packet. In addition, the seal of the candidate whose election is being contested, or the seal of the candidate’s election agent or polling agent—any of whom may be present at the polling station and may wish to place a seal on the packet—must also be affixed. By requiring the seals of both the presiding officer and the relevant party representatives, the regulations establish a robust system of checks and balances that helps to prevent tampering, to preserve the integrity of the voting process, and to provide a transparent record for any later audit or recount. This comprehensive set of procedures, from the handling of a single tendered ballot to the sealing of all election documents, is designed to protect the secrecy of the vote while simultaneously ensuring that every step can be verified and trusted by all parties involved in the democratic process.

The duties that fall upon the presiding officer at the close of a voting day are set out in a series of detailed provisions that aim to guarantee the integrity of the electoral process from the moment the polling station ceases to receive votes until the moment the votes are finally counted. Under subsection one of Rule forty‑seven the presiding officer is required, after the poll has formally ended, to deliver, or to arrange for the delivery of, a number of specific items to the returning officer. The returning officer is the official who has overall responsibility for the conduct of the election in the constituency or district, and may designate a particular location for the hand‑over. The items that must be transferred include the ballot boxes themselves, or if the election is being conducted under a different method, the bags or protective covers that have been used in accordance with Rule forty‑four; the ballot paper account, which is a written record that lists the number of ballot papers issued, used, and remaining, thereby providing a paper trail that can be checked against the physical boxes; the sealed packets that are described in Rule forty‑six, which typically contain the marked copies of the electoral roll together with any other documents that have been sealed to prevent tampering; and finally all other papers that were used at the poll, such as any forms, notices, or procedural checklists that the officials might have completed during the course of the election. By enumerating these items, the legislation ensures that no piece of evidence that could affect the outcome of the vote is left behind, misplaced, or altered.

Once the items have been transferred, the responsibility for their safe handling passes to the returning officer. Subsection two of Rule forty‑seven obliges the returning officer to make “adequate arrangements” for the safe transport of the ballot boxes, the sealed packets, and any other related papers. The phrase “adequate arrangements” is intentionally broad, allowing the returning officer to choose the most appropriate method of transport given the circumstances, whether that be a secure police‑escorted vehicle, a specially designed courier service, or a sealed transport container with tamper‑evident seals. Moreover, the returning officer must ensure that the items remain under secure custody until the moment when the counting of votes begins. This custody requirement is crucial because any interruption or breach of the chain of custody could lead to doubts about the authenticity of the ballots and, consequently, to challenges against the election result. The law therefore mandates that the returning officer not only arrange for transport but also maintain a continuous record of who has had access to the ballot boxes and when, often through a signed logbook that is kept in a secure location.

The next set of rules deals with the situation in which a poll at a particular polling station cannot be completed on the scheduled day and must be adjourned. An adjournment may be necessary for a variety of reasons, such as a natural disaster, a serious technical malfunction of voting equipment, or an incident that threatens the safety of the electors and officials. Under subsection one of Rule forty‑eight, if a poll at any polling station is adjourned under subsection one of Section fifty‑seven, the procedural requirements laid down in Rules forty‑four through forty‑seven are to be applied, as far as practicable, as though the poll had been closed at the precise hour that was originally fixed under Section fifty‑six. In other words, the administrative steps that would normally follow the formal closing of a poll—such as the sealing of ballot boxes, the preparation of the ballot paper account, and the hand‑over to the returning officer—must still be performed, even though the actual voting has not been completed. This requirement preserves the continuity of the process and prevents any ambiguity about the status of the materials that have already been handled.

When the adjourned poll is later recommenced, subsection two of Rule forty‑eight specifies that any elector who has already cast a vote during the earlier part of the poll must not be permitted to vote again. This safeguard is essential to uphold the principle of one person, one vote, and it prevents the possibility of double voting that could otherwise arise if the same voter were allowed to cast a ballot in both the original and the adjourned phases. The returning officer, acting on the basis of the marked copy of the electoral roll that was sealed at the time of adjournment, must verify each voter’s status before issuing a ballot paper in the resumed poll.

To facilitate the continuation of voting after an adjournment, subsection three of Rule forty‑eight requires the returning officer to supply the presiding officer at the affected polling station with two specific items: the sealed packet that contains the marked copy of the electoral roll, and a new ballot box. The marked copy of the electoral roll is a version of the voter list on which the names of individuals who have already voted are marked, typically with a tick or a stamp, thereby providing a clear record of who has exercised their franchise. By providing a new ballot box, the legislation ensures that any ballots already cast are kept separate from those that will be cast after the poll resumes, reducing the risk of confusion or accidental mixing of votes.

The presiding officer, upon receipt of the sealed packet, must open it in the presence of any polling agents who have been appointed by the candidates or political parties, as stipulated in subsection four of Rule forty‑eight. This opening must be done in a transparent manner, with all agents observing, to confirm that the contents have not been tampered with. The presiding officer then uses the marked copy of the electoral roll to record the names of electors to whom ballot papers are issued during the adjourned poll, but crucially, the serial numbers of the ballot papers are not recorded on the roll at this stage. The omission of serial numbers reflects the fact that the serial numbers are generally used only to track the physical ballot papers themselves, whereas the roll is primarily a tool for confirming voter eligibility and ensuring that each voter receives only one ballot.

Finally, subsection five of Rule forty‑eight reiterates that the general procedural framework set out in Rules twenty‑eight through forty‑seven continues to apply to an adjourned poll in exactly the same way it applied before the poll was interrupted. This means that all the safeguards, checks, and balances designed to protect the voting process—such as the presence of polling agents, the sealing of ballot boxes, the preparation of the ballot paper account, and the hand‑over to the returning officer—remain in force throughout the entire duration of the adjourned poll, ensuring that the integrity of the election is not compromised by the interruption.

The third major provision in this segment of the electoral regulations concerns the introduction of a different method of voting known as “voting by ballot” at certain polling stations that have been specially designated for this purpose. Under subsection one of Rule forty‑nine, the Election Commission, which is the independent body charged with overseeing all elections, has the authority to issue a notification in the Official Gazette at least fifteen days before the scheduled date of the poll, or before the first of the dates on which the poll is to be held. This notification may direct that, at specific polling stations identified in the notice, the method of voting by ballot shall be employed. The phrase “voting by ballot” in this context refers to a system in which voters cast their votes by inserting a paper ballot into a sealed container, rather than by marking a register or using an electronic device. The purpose of allowing this method at designated stations is often to accommodate special circumstances, such as the need for a more discreet voting process, logistical considerations in remote areas, or to provide an alternative in the event of equipment failure.

Any polling station that is singled out in the Election Commission’s notification is thereafter referred to in these rules as a “notified polling station”. This terminology is used to differentiate these locations from ordinary polling stations that operate under the standard set of rules. The designation carries with it a series of modifications to the usual procedural requirements, as set out in subsection three of Rule forty‑nine. The first modification replaces Rule thirty with a new rule, numbered thirty‑A, which simply states that every ballot paper shall be of a design that the Election Commission may decide. This provision grants the Commission flexibility to determine the appearance, size, security features, and any other characteristics of the ballot paper, such as watermarks or serial numbers, in order to prevent forgery and to make the paper easily recognizable to voters and officials alike.

The second set of modifications concerns the physical arrangement of the voting area within a notified polling station. Sub‑rules two and three of Rule thirty‑one are superseded by new provisions that prescribe that each notified polling station must have a single voting compartment. Inside this compartment, there shall be a separate ballot box for each candidate contesting the election, and these boxes must be arranged so that a voter can place a ballot paper into any of the boxes without being observed by anyone outside the compartment. This arrangement is intended to preserve the secrecy of the vote, a cornerstone of democratic elections, by preventing any onlooker from seeing which box a particular ballot is being placed into. The returning officer is tasked with supplying, for each notified polling station, a sufficient number of ballot boxes, copies of the relevant part of the electoral roll (the section of the voter list that pertains to that station), the ballot papers themselves, and any other election materials that might be required, such as pens, ink pads, or ballot papers with special security features.

The third set of modifications replaces sub‑rules five, six, and seven of Rule thirty‑three with detailed instructions on how the ballot boxes are to be identified, inspected, and prepared before the poll begins. First, the symbol that has been allotted to each candidate under Rule ten must be printed on labels that are affixed both inside and outside the corresponding ballot box. By placing the symbol on both sides, election officials and observers can quickly verify that the ballot papers intended for a particular candidate are being placed in the correct container. Once the symbols have been affixed, each ballot box may also be marked with additional distinguishing marks as directed by the Election Commission, which could include color codes, serial numbers, or other visual cues designed to further reduce the likelihood of confusion. Immediately before voting commences, the presiding officer must allow the polling agents—representatives appointed by the candidates—to inspect each ballot box. During this inspection, the presiding officer must demonstrate that each box is empty, that the proper labels have been affixed both inside and outside, and that any additional distinguishing marks required by the Commission are present. After the agents are satisfied that the boxes meet the required standards, they are to be secured and sealed, typically with tamper‑evident seals, and then placed side by side within the voting compartment in the same order in which the candidates’ names appear on the official list of contesting candidates. This ordering ensures that there is a clear visual correlation between each candidate’s name and the corresponding ballot box, thereby facilitating an orderly voting process.

The final modification introduced by Rule forty‑nine, in place of Rule thirty‑eight, concerns the actual issue of ballot papers to electors. A new rule, numbered thirty‑eight‑B, stipulates that before a ballot paper can be handed to an elector, it must meet certain conditions, although the text excerpt stops short of enumerating those conditions. In practice, these conditions typically require that the ballot paper be unmarked, that it bear the official design approved by the Election Commission, that it be accompanied by instructions that are clear and understandable to voters, and that it be handed over in a manner that maintains the secrecy of the vote. The underlying purpose of this rule is to ensure that each elector receives a ballot paper that is authentic, free from any pre‑existing marks that could influence the voter’s choice, and that the process of issuing the ballot does not inadvertently reveal the voter’s identity or political preference.

Taken together, the provisions contained in Rules forty‑seven, forty‑eight, and forty‑nine form an integrated framework designed to safeguard the handling of ballot boxes and related documents, to provide clear procedures for dealing with unexpected interruptions to the polling process, and to accommodate a special method of voting that relies on the physical separation of ballot papers into distinct boxes for each candidate. By prescribing detailed steps for the delivery, transport, custody, and inspection of electoral materials, the legislation seeks to eliminate opportunities for fraud, tampering, or accidental error. Moreover, the rules concerning adjourned polls ensure that the continuity of the election is maintained even when unforeseen events force a temporary suspension of voting, while the provisions for notified polling stations introduce a flexible yet tightly controlled alternative voting method that can be deployed where circumstances demand it. The overall design of these rules reflects a balance between the need for procedural rigor—necessary to uphold public confidence in the electoral system—and the need for adaptability, allowing election officials to respond effectively to a wide range of practical challenges that may arise during the conduct of an election.

The law requires that every ballot paper which is to be used in an election must bear a special identifying feature that is prescribed by the Election Commission, the independent constitutional body that oversees the conduct of elections and ensures that the process is fair, transparent and consistent with the legal framework. This distinguishing mark, which may be a stamp, a seal, a watermark or any other form of official imprint, serves the purpose of preventing the use of counterfeit or unauthorized ballot papers and allows election officials to verify at a glance that the paper they are handling has been duly sanctioned by the authority that controls the electoral machinery. In addition to this official marking, the ballot paper must also carry a full signature on its reverse side, that is, on the back, which must be provided by the presiding officer who is the senior official responsible for supervising the polling station on the day of the vote. The signature of the presiding officer acts as a further safeguard, confirming that the paper has been issued under the direct supervision of the person accountable for the conduct of the poll at that particular location. By requiring both an official stamp and a signature, the rule creates a dual layer of authentication that makes it exceedingly difficult for any irregularities to go undetected.

When a ballot paper is handed over to a registered voter, the polling officer – the election official who is tasked with the day‑to‑day management of the voting process at a specific polling place – is required to note the unique serial number that appears on the ballot paper in the official electoral register. The electoral register is a printed or electronic list that contains the names of all eligible electors in the constituency, together with their personal details and, in many jurisdictions, a photograph and a unique identification number. The “marked copy” of the electoral roll is a version of this register on which the polling officer makes entries in ink or by electronic entry to record the occurrence of each voting act. By recording the serial number of the ballot paper against the entry that corresponds to the particular elector, the officer creates a traceable link between the voter and the specific piece of paper that the voter will later use to express a choice. This practice is intended to provide an audit trail that can be consulted in the event of an allegation of ballot‑paper substitution, duplication, or other forms of fraud, while still preserving the secrecy of the vote because the serial number alone does not reveal the candidate selected.

Except as expressly permitted by the provision that deals with the recording of serial numbers in the marked copy of the electoral roll, no individual who is present in the polling station – whether a polling agent, a supporter of a candidate, a member of the public, or any other person – is allowed to write down, keep a note of, or otherwise record the serial numbers of the ballot papers that have been issued to particular electors. This prohibition is designed to prevent the creation of a secondary record that could be used to match a specific ballot paper with a specific voter, which would undermine the principle of secret ballot that lies at the heart of democratic elections. The rule therefore creates a clear line between the official, controlled record kept by the polling officer and any informal notes that might be taken by other people, reinforcing the confidentiality of the individual’s vote.

In certain circumstances the rule that ordinarily requires the recording of serial numbers in the marked copy of the electoral roll is set aside, and a different sub‑rule takes its place. This substitution is indicated by the phrase “in lieu of sub‑rule (2) of rule 39,” which means that instead of the standard procedure, a special instruction applies. The substituted sub‑rule directs that as soon as an elector receives a ballot paper, the elector must immediately proceed to the voting compartment – a private enclosure where the voter can mark the ballot in secrecy – and insert the ballot paper through a narrow slit into the ballot box that has been assigned to the candidate for whom the voter wishes to cast a vote. The language “forthwith go into the voting compartment” emphasizes the urgency and the requirement for the voter not to linger with the ballot paper outside the privacy of the compartment, thereby reducing the risk that the ballot could be observed, intercepted, or otherwise compromised. The slit serves as a physical barrier that prevents the voter from seeing the contents of other ballot boxes and ensures that the ballot can only be deposited in the correct box.

A further amendment to the standard rule is introduced by the provision that replaces sub‑rule (1) of rule 40. This amendment acknowledges that some electors may be unable to see the symbols, colors, or other visual identifiers that are attached to the ballot boxes because of blindness or other physical disabilities that affect vision or manual dexterity. In such cases, the presiding officer – who is the chief election official at the polling station – is required to allow the voter to be accompanied by a companion who is at least eighteen years of age. The companion is permitted to accompany the elector into the private voting compartment for the sole purpose of hearing the voter state the name of the candidate they wish to support and then, under the direction of the voter, placing the ballot paper into the appropriate ballot box. The rule includes a series of safeguards to prevent abuse of this assistance: first, a single companion may not serve more than one elector on the same day at the same polling station, thereby limiting the opportunity for collusion or for the companion to learn the voting choices of multiple voters. Second, before a person may act as a companion, he or she must make a formal declaration that the identity of the candidate chosen by the elector will be kept strictly confidential, and the companion must also affirm that he or she has not already acted as a companion for any other elector on that day. These declarations are intended to protect the secrecy of the ballot and to ensure that the assistance is provided in a manner that does not compromise the integrity of the voting process.

Another set of provisions replaces the original rule 42 with a new series of rules, labelled 42A and 42B, which deal with a specific type of vote known as a “tendered vote.” A tendered vote occurs when a person who claims to be a particular elector arrives at the polling station after another individual has already voted using that elector’s name or identification. The presiding officer, exercising his discretionary power, may ask the late‑arriving person a series of questions designed to verify his or her identity. If the presiding officer is satisfied that the person is indeed the rightful elector, he or she must issue a ballot paper that is designated as Form 17, and this ballot paper is thereafter referred to in the rules as a “tendered ballot paper.” Before the tendered ballot paper can be handed to the elector, the person must sign his or her name in a dedicated register, known as Form 15, which records the names of all individuals who have received tendered ballots. This signature creates a paper trail that links the tendered ballot to a specific individual, thereby providing accountability while still preserving the secrecy of the vote.

The elector who receives the tendered ballot is then required to write the name of the candidate of his or her choice directly on the ballot paper. If the elector is illiterate, blind, physically unable to write, or otherwise incapable of marking the ballot himself, the presiding officer must act on the elector’s instructions to make the entry, ensuring that the voter’s intention is faithfully recorded. The rule expressly requires that the marking of the tendered ballot be carried out with “due regard to secrecy,” meaning that the process must be conducted in such a way that no one else can see which candidate is being selected. Once the tendered ballot paper has been marked, it must be placed immediately into a specially designed cover, a sealed container that is kept separate from the ordinary ballot boxes and is used only for the purpose of holding tendered votes until they can be counted. This separate handling reduces the risk that tendered ballots might be mixed with regular ballots and thus preserves the integrity of the overall count.

A further proviso applies when the tendered voter is a member of a political party that is contesting an election to fill a seat or seats in the Council of States, which is the upper chamber of the legislature. In that situation, even though the rule generally requires secrecy, the presiding officer is allowed, contrary to the usual requirement, to permit an authorised agent of the political party to verify how the tendered ballot was cast before the ballot is placed in the secure cover. The term “authorised agent” is defined in the explanatory notes that follow the rule: it refers to a person who has been officially appointed by a political party under the authority of sub‑rule (2) of rule 39AA, as it applies in a council constituency, and who has been designated according to clause (ii) of rule 70. The appointment of such an agent is made in the context of an election for a council constituency, and the agent’s role is limited to verifying the content of the tendered ballot, not influencing the voter’s choice. The rule also clarifies that the authorised agent must be appointed by the political party rather than by members of the assembly or through a postal ballot, ensuring that the verification process remains under the control of the party that is directly affected by the tendered vote.

Rule 42B deals with the circumstances under which the presiding officer may enter the private voting compartment while the poll is in progress. The voting compartment is a sealed area where each elector can mark a ballot in secrecy, and it is generally off‑limits to anyone other than the elector themselves. However, the presiding officer is empowered, at his or her discretion, to step inside the compartment whenever it is deemed necessary to protect the integrity of the ballot boxes. This might include situations where there is a suspicion that the ballot boxes could be tampered with, moved, or otherwise interfered with. The presiding officer’s entry is intended to allow swift corrective action, such as securing the boxes, checking that they are properly sealed, or otherwise ensuring that no unauthorized person can gain access to the ballots.

If the presiding officer observes or receives a report that an elector who has entered the voting compartment is attempting to interfere with a ballot box – for example, by trying to remove a ballot, add an extra ballot, or otherwise manipulate the contents – or if the elector remains inside the compartment for an unusually long period, the presiding officer must act promptly. The presiding officer is required to re‑enter the compartment to investigate the situation, to take steps that will restore the orderly flow of the poll, and to prevent any further obstruction or misconduct. In order to maintain transparency and to avoid any perception of bias, the presiding officer must allow any polling agents who are present – representatives of the candidates or parties who are monitoring the conduct of the poll – to accompany him or her when entering the compartment. This accompaniment ensures that the actions taken by the presiding officer are witnessed by neutral observers and that the integrity of the polling process is upheld.

Overall, the collection of rules described above is designed to balance two fundamental principles that underpin modern democratic elections: the need for rigorous procedural safeguards that prevent fraud, duplication, or tampering, and the equally important requirement that each voter’s choice remain secret and free from undue influence. By mandating official markings and signatures on ballot papers, by requiring the systematic recording of serial numbers, by limiting who may note those numbers, and by providing special procedures for voters who are blind, illiterate, or otherwise physically unable to mark a ballot themselves, the legislation seeks to create an inclusive, transparent, and trustworthy electoral environment. The provisions for tendered votes recognize that errors or disputes may arise concerning an elector’s identity, and they provide a controlled mechanism for resolving such disputes without compromising secrecy. Finally, the rules governing the presiding officer’s authority to enter the voting compartment underscore the importance of vigilance and rapid response to any irregularities, while also ensuring that such powers are exercised in a manner that is open to observation by party agents. Taken together, these detailed instructions form a comprehensive framework that guides election officials, poll workers, candidates, and voters in the conduct of free and fair elections.

The provisions that govern the handling of ballot papers that are discovered either wholly or partly outside the designated ballot boxes are set out in rule 42C, and they are intended to preserve the integrity of the voting process while providing clear guidance to election officials on how to treat such irregularities. Under the first subsection of rule 42C, whenever a ballot paper that has already been issued to a registered voter fails to be placed by that voter into any of the official ballot boxes, and that paper is subsequently found lying somewhere within the precincts of the polling station – whether it is situated inside the voting compartment, in the hallway, on the floor, or in any other area that is part of or adjacent to the polling venue – the law treats this situation as though the ballot paper had been formally returned to the presiding officer. The presiding officer is the individual appointed to supervise the conduct of the poll at each station, and the reference to sub‑rule (2) of rule 41 indicates that the returned paper must be processed in the same manner as any other ballot that is handed back to the officer for a legitimate reason, such as a spoiled vote or an accidental duplication. In effect, the rule creates a legal fiction that the missing ballot has been submitted back to the official in order to ensure that the paper is not simply discarded or ignored, thereby maintaining a complete record of every ballot that left the voter’s hand.

The second subsection of rule 42C deals with a more nuanced circumstance in which a ballot paper is discovered only partly inserted into the ballot box that belongs to a particular candidate. In such cases the regulation presumes that the voter’s intention was to cast the vote for that candidate, despite the incomplete insertion. The presumption of intent is a practical solution that prevents the disenfranchisement of the voter due to a technical mishap, such as a paper snagging on the edge of the slit or the voter accidentally pulling the paper back out after starting the insertion. Accordingly, the presiding officer is directed to complete the act that the voter began, namely to push the partially inserted ballot fully into the box, thereby ensuring that the vote is counted as intended. This approach balances the need for procedural rigor with a compassionate recognition that human error should not invalidate a legitimate expression of political preference.

In the same vein, the legislation replaces the earlier rule 44 with a new rule designated 44A, which specifies the procedures for sealing ballot boxes once the polling has concluded. The first paragraph of rule 44A requires the presiding officer, as soon as it is reasonably practicable after the closing time of the poll, to close the slit through which voters have deposited their ballots. If the ballot box is equipped with a mechanical device – such as a latch or a lock – that automatically secures the slit, the officer must engage that mechanism. Where no such device exists, the officer must manually seal the opening, typically using a tamper‑evident seal, and must also allow any polling agents present – who are representatives of the contesting candidates or their parties – to affix their own seals. This collaborative sealing process serves as a safeguard against tampering, as the presence of multiple independent seals makes it evident if any unauthorized opening occurs after the poll has ended. The second paragraph of rule 44A then mandates that all ballot boxes, once sealed, must be kept under secure conditions until they are transported to the counting centre, thereby preserving the confidentiality and authenticity of the votes contained within.

Further modifications to the electoral code are noted in the accompanying parenthetical clauses. The first of these, identified as clause (aa) of sub‑rule (1) of rule 46, is expressly excluded from application in the present context, meaning that any procedural requirement that might have been imposed by that clause does not apply to the handling of ballot boxes under rule 44A. The second modification replaces sub‑rules (3) and (4) of rule 48 with a new sub‑rule that governs the conduct of an adjourned poll. An adjourned poll occurs when, for any reason – such as a disruption, a natural disaster, or a technical failure – the original poll cannot be completed at the scheduled time and must be resumed at a later date. Under the new sub‑rule, the returning officer – the senior official responsible for overseeing the election in a given constituency – must supply the presiding officer at the location of the adjourned poll with a sealed packet that contains a marked copy of the electoral roll (the official list of eligible voters) and a fresh set of ballot boxes. The presiding officer is then required to open this sealed packet in the presence of any polling agents who are on site, a practice that ensures transparency and prevents accusations of tampering. Once the packet is opened, the presiding officer uses the marked copy of the electoral roll to record the serial numbers of the ballot papers that are issued to voters during the adjourned poll, thereby creating a reliable audit trail that links each ballot to a specific voter without compromising the secrecy of the vote.

The text then moves to Chapter II, which deals specifically with voting by electronic voting machines – a modern alternative to the traditional paper ballot system. Rule 49A defines the essential design elements of an electronic voting machine, hereafter referred to as the voting machine. Each machine must consist of two principal components: a control unit and a balloting unit. The control unit is typically a secure, tamper‑resistant device that stores the configuration data, including the list of candidates and the total number of votes cast, while the balloting unit is the interface through which the voter records his or her choice. Both components must be constructed in accordance with specifications that have been approved by the Election Commission, the independent body charged with supervising elections and ensuring that all technical standards are met. The provision also contains a proviso that allows for the attachment of a printer and a drop‑box to the voting machine, provided that these accessories conform to a design also approved by the Election Commission. The printer, when installed, produces a paper trail of each vote – a physical record that can be used for verification or recount purposes – and the drop‑box serves as a secure receptacle for the printed receipts. The Election Commission retains the authority to direct the use of this paper‑trail system in particular constituencies or parts of constituencies, depending on the assessed need for additional auditability.

Rule 49B outlines the steps that the returning officer must follow to prepare each voting machine before it is placed at a polling station. The first paragraph requires that the balloting unit contain all the necessary particulars – such as the names of the candidates, their symbols, and any other information required to identify them – and that these particulars be presented in the language or languages specified by the Election Commission, reflecting the linguistic diversity of the electorate. The second paragraph stipulates that the names of the candidates be arranged on the balloting unit in exactly the same order in which they appear on the official list of contesting candidates, thereby avoiding any confusion that could arise from a different sequence. The third paragraph deals with the situation where two or more candidates share an identical name; in such cases, the returning officer must add distinguishing details, such as each candidate’s occupation, place of residence, or another differentiating descriptor, so that voters can clearly tell the candidates apart.

The fourth paragraph of rule 49B sets out the detailed sealing procedures that must be observed once the balloting unit has been prepared. The returning officer first affixes a label that displays the names and symbols of all contesting candidates onto the balloting unit, and then applies his own seal to the unit. In addition, any candidate or election agent who wishes to add his own seal may do so, providing an extra layer of verification that the unit has not been altered after the returning officer’s inspection. The returning officer then proceeds to the control unit, where he enters the total number of contesting candidates and locks the section of the control unit that records this figure, again sealing it with his own seal and, if desired, with the seals of the candidates or their agents. If a printer for generating a paper trail is being employed, as allowed by the proviso to rule 49A, the returning officer must also configure the printer in accordance with the number of candidates set in the control unit. This configuration involves loading the printer with the serial numbers and names of each candidate as they appear on the balloting unit, loading the appropriate paper, and sealing the printer in the manner prescribed by the Election Commission, thereby ensuring that the printer cannot be tampered with after the election begins.

Rule 49C addresses the physical arrangements that must be present at each polling station to facilitate both transparency and voter privacy. The first sub‑paragraph requires that, outside the building where the poll is being conducted, a prominently displayed notice be placed. This notice must state the geographical polling area that the station serves, identifying the electors who are entitled to cast their votes there. In instances where a single polling area is served by multiple polling stations, the notice must also specify which electors belong to each individual station, thereby preventing confusion about where a voter should go. The same notice must also include a copy of the list of contesting candidates, giving voters immediate access to the information they need to make an informed choice. The second sub‑paragraph mandates that each polling station be equipped with one or more voting compartments – enclosed spaces that shield the voter from observation by others – where electors can record their votes without fear of intimidation or undue influence. Finally, the third sub‑paragraph obliges the returning officer to supply each station with at least one voting machine, together with the relevant portion of the electoral roll (the list of voters for that specific station) and any additional election materials that may be required to conduct the poll efficiently, such as ballot paper dispensers, pens, or signage. By establishing these requirements, the law seeks to ensure that the transition from paper ballots to electronic voting does not compromise the fundamental principles of secrecy, fairness, and accountability that underpin the democratic process.

The rule identified as sub‑paragraph (4) of the provision concerning the use of voting machines clarifies that, notwithstanding the requirements set out in sub‑rule (3), the returning officer – that is, the official appointed by the Election Commission to oversee the conduct of the election in a particular constituency – is permitted to deploy a single common voting machine for use by two or more distinct polling stations, provided that those stations are physically located within the same building or complex. This permission is not automatic; it must be obtained in advance through a formal approval process that involves the Election Commission, the autonomous body responsible for administering elections throughout the country. The purpose of allowing a common machine in a shared premises is to promote efficiency and to reduce the logistical burden of transporting and maintaining separate equipment for each polling station when the physical layout makes it feasible to serve several stations from a single device.

The next set of regulations, catalogued under rule 49D, deals with the admission of persons into the polling station at the moment the poll is in progress. The presiding officer – the individual who is in charge of the polling station on the day of the election – has the responsibility to control the flow of electors so that only a manageable number of voters are inside the enclosure at any one time. This control is essential to avoid overcrowding, to preserve order, and to maintain the secrecy and security of the ballot. The presiding officer must exclude from entry any individual who does not fall within a specific list of permitted categories. Those allowed to be present inside the polling station, besides the electors themselves, include the polling officers who are appointed to assist with the technical and procedural aspects of the vote; any public servants who are on duty in connection with the election, such as police personnel or security staff; persons who have been expressly authorised by the Election Commission, for example, observers or auditors; the candidates who are contesting the election together with their election agents – the people designated to manage the candidate’s campaign activities at the poll – and, subject to the conditions laid down in rule 13, one polling agent for each candidate, who acts as a liaison between the candidate and the presiding officer; a child who is being carried in the arms of an elector, since the child is not a separate voter but merely accompanying the adult; a helper who is assisting a blind or infirm elector who cannot move unassisted, thereby ensuring that persons with disabilities are not disenfranchised; and finally, any other individual whom the returning officer or the presiding officer may deem necessary to employ under the specific provisions of sub‑rule (2) of rule 49G or sub‑rule (1) of rule 49H, which cover special assistance for women electors and identification assistance respectively.

Rule 49E sets out the precise manner in which a voting machine must be prepared for the poll. Each electronic voting device consists of a control unit – the part of the machine that stores the votes, controls the operation, and often displays the results – and a balloting unit – the part that the voter interacts with, usually a touch‑screen or a set of buttons that records the choice. In jurisdictions where a paper‑trail printer is attached to the electronic system, that printer must also be prepared and displayed. The law requires that both the control unit and the balloting unit carry a permanent label that contains several pieces of identifying information. The label must show, first, the serial number of the device, if such a number has been assigned, together with the name of the constituency, which is the geographic area that the election is being held in. Secondly, the label must indicate the serial number and the name of the specific polling station or stations that the machine is intended to serve. Thirdly, the label must contain the serial number of the particular unit itself, which distinguishes it from other units of the same model. Finally, the label must bear the date on which the poll is to be held, thereby linking the device unequivocally to a single election event.

Immediately before voting commences, the presiding officer is required to conduct a demonstration for the polling agents – the representatives of each candidate who observe the conduct of the poll – and for any other persons who are present in the polling station. During this demonstration the presiding officer must show that the voting machine is in a pristine condition, meaning that no votes have yet been entered into its memory, and that the label described above is correctly affixed to both the control and balloting units. If a paper‑trail printer is being used, the presiding officer must also verify that the printer’s drop box, the compartment that collects the printed receipts, is empty, thereby confirming that no prior voting records exist.

To protect the integrity of the machine, a paper seal is employed to lock the control unit. The presiding officer must place his own signature on this seal, and then invite any polling agents who wish to do so to also sign the seal. This practice creates a visible record of who was present when the seal was applied and serves as a deterrent against tampering. Once the signatures have been collected, the seal is affixed in the specific slot that has been designated for that purpose on the control unit. The seal must be applied in such a way that, after it is in place, it becomes physically impossible to press the “result button” – the button that would allow the control unit to display the stored vote totals – without breaking the seal. In other words, any attempt to access the results before the poll has been closed would necessarily destroy the seal, thereby providing evidence of an unauthorized intrusion. After the seal is applied, the control unit is closed and positioned in full view of both the presiding officer and the polling agents, while the balloting unit is placed inside the voting compartment, the area where electors actually cast their votes. If a paper‑trail printer is part of the system, it is kept together with the balloting unit in the same voting compartment, and it is connected to the electronic voting machine in accordance with the detailed technical instructions issued by the Election Commission, ensuring that each vote recorded electronically is simultaneously printed on a paper receipt that can later be used for audit purposes.

Rule 49F addresses the marked copy of the electoral roll – the official list of voters that is used on election day to verify each elector’s eligibility. Just before the poll begins, the presiding officer must again make a public demonstration, this time to the polling agents and any other observers, showing that the marked copy of the electoral roll does not contain any unauthorized entries. Specifically, the roll must be free of any entry that has not been made in accordance with clause (b) of sub‑rule (2) of rule 20, which governs the addition of names to the roll for reasons such as a change of residence or the correction of an error. Likewise, the roll must not contain any extraneous markings except for those that are permitted under clause (b) of sub‑rule (2) of rule 23, which relates to the notation of a voter’s death, removal, or other legal disqualification. By confirming the purity of the roll, the presiding officer reassures the candidates and their agents that the list of voters is accurate and has not been tampered with.

The provisions collected under rule 49G provide special facilities for women electors. In polling stations that serve both male and female voters, the presiding officer has the discretion to organize the entry of electors in alternating batches, thereby ensuring that men and women are admitted to the voting area in separate groups. This arrangement can help to respect cultural norms that require gender segregation, while still allowing the poll to proceed efficiently. Moreover, the returning officer or the presiding officer may appoint a woman to serve as an attendant at any polling station. The attendant’s duties include assisting women voters with any procedural requirements, such as helping them navigate the voting compartment, and providing general support to the presiding officer in managing the poll with regard to women electors. One of the attendant’s specific responsibilities is to assist in frisking a woman elector, if the circumstances make such a search necessary for security reasons, thereby ensuring that the search is conducted by a female official in accordance with the principle of modesty and respect for the voter’s privacy.

Rule 49H deals with the identification of electors at the polling station. The presiding officer is empowered to employ, at his discretion, any persons he deems suitable to help verify the identity of voters or to otherwise assist in the conduct of the poll. As each voter approaches the entrance to the polling station, either the presiding officer himself or a polling officer whom he has authorized for that purpose must compare the voter’s name and other relevant particulars – such as the voter’s address, age, or unique identification number – with the corresponding entry in the electoral roll. After confirming the match, the officer announces the serial number, the name, and any other relevant particulars of the elector, thereby providing a public verification that the person entering the booth is indeed the registered voter for that entry. In constituencies where voters have been issued identity cards under the Registration of Electors Rules, 1960, the process includes an additional step: the elector must produce his or her official identity card to the presiding officer or the authorized polling officer. The officer then checks that the details on the card correspond to the entry in the roll, providing an extra layer of security against impersonation or fraudulent voting. This systematic identification procedure is designed to preserve the sanctity of the ballot by ensuring that only eligible voters are allowed to cast a vote, and that each eligible voter may do so only once.

In the conduct of an election the official charged with overseeing the voting process, whether the presiding officer of a polling station or the polling officer appointed for the particular duty, must be prepared to resolve occasional discrepancies that appear on the electoral roll. The electoral roll is the official list of all persons entitled to cast a vote in a given constituency, and it is printed in a large book‑let that is made available at each polling place. Because the roll is prepared by clerks who transcribe names, addresses, ages and other particulars from earlier records, occasional clerical mistakes or printing errors can arise. For example, a name may be misspelled, an address may be recorded incorrectly, or a numeral may be transposed. The rule identified as paragraph (4) authorises the presiding officer or the polling officer, as appropriate, to disregard such minor errors when determining whether a person standing before the polling station is in fact the elector to whom the entry on the roll refers. The officer is required to be satisfied, after a reasonable examination, that the individual who has presented himself or herself is indeed the same person that the erroneous entry describes. This provision reflects the practical need to avoid disenfranchising voters over trivial typographical faults, while still preserving the integrity of the electoral register.

The next set of provisions, labeled as rule 49‑I, deals specifically with public servants who are on election duty. In many jurisdictions public officials—such as police officers, election officers, or other government employees—are required to work at polling stations on election day. Because they are engaged in official duties, they are often unable to vote at the ordinary polling place to which they are registered. To accommodate this situation, a special election duty certificate, known as Form 12B, may be issued to the public servant. The rule clarifies that the general provisions contained in rule 49H, which might otherwise restrict a person’s ability to vote at a station other than his or her designated one, do not apply when the individual produces a valid Form 12B. In other words, a public servant who is stationed at a polling place different from his or her normal constituency may still be permitted to cast a ballot there, provided that the certificate is shown. Upon presentation of the certificate, the presiding officer must first obtain the signature of the person producing the document; this signature is recorded as evidence that the individual indeed presented the certificate voluntarily and is not being impersonated. The officer then records the name and the electoral roll number that appear on the certificate at the end of the “marked copy” of the electoral roll—a version of the roll in which the names of those who have already voted are marked so as to prevent duplicate voting. Finally, the public servant is allowed to vote in exactly the same way as any other elector who is entitled to vote at that particular polling station. The rule thereby balances the necessity of allowing election officials to fulfill their duties with the principle that every eligible citizen must be able to exercise the franchise.

Rule 49J sets out the procedure by which a polling agent—an individual appointed by a candidate or a political party to observe the conduct of voting—may challenge the identity of a person who claims to be a specific elector. The purpose of this provision is to provide a formal mechanism for preventing “personation,” which is the fraudulent act of voting in the name of another person. The rule begins by requiring the polling agent to deposit a nominal sum of two rupees in cash with the presiding officer for each challenge that is raised. This small financial stake serves two purposes: it creates a minimal barrier that discourages frivolous or vexatious challenges, and it also provides a source of funds that can be forfeited if the challenge is found to be baseless. Once the deposit has been made, the presiding officer follows a prescribed sequence of steps. First, the officer must warn the person who is being challenged that a penalty exists for the offence of personation; the warning makes clear that if the individual is later found to have voted fraudulently, criminal sanctions may be imposed. Next, the officer reads aloud the full entry from the electoral roll that pertains to the claimed elector—this includes the name, age, address and any other identifying information that appears in the roll. The officer then asks the individual directly whether he or she is the person referred to in that entry, thereby giving the individual an opportunity to affirm or deny the claim. The officer records the name and address of the challenged individual in a special register known as Form 14, which is specifically designed to keep track of all votes that are subject to challenge. In addition, the challenged person is required to sign this list, thereby acknowledging that his or her identity has been formally disputed.

After the initial formalities, the presiding officer conducts a summary inquiry, which is a brief but structured investigation aimed at resolving the matter without unduly delaying the voting process. During this inquiry the presiding officer may request that the challenger present any evidence that supports the allegation—such as photographs, affidavits, or testimony from witnesses who can attest that the person attempting to vote is not the genuine elector. Simultaneously, the person whose identity is being challenged is invited to present proof of his or her own identity. This could include official documents such as a voter’s identity card, a driver’s licence, or any other government‑issued proof of residence and age that matches the details on the electoral roll. The presiding officer may also ask the challenged individual a series of questions designed to confirm personal details, for instance, questions about the person’s date of birth, the street name of his or her residence, or the names of family members listed on the roll. The answers are to be given on oath, meaning the individual swears that the information provided is true, under the penalty of perjury. The officer is also empowered to administer an oath not only to the person being challenged but also to any other witness who offers testimony, ensuring that all statements are made with legal solemnity.

Following the inquiry, the presiding officer makes a determination based on the evidence presented. If the officer concludes that the challenge has not been substantiated—that is, there is insufficient proof that the individual is not the rightful elector—then the challenged person is allowed to cast his or her ballot without further impediment. Conversely, if the presiding officer is satisfied that the challenge is well‑founded and that the person is attempting to vote fraudulently, the officer must debar the individual from voting, effectively disallowing the ballot. The rule also addresses the disposition of the two‑rupee deposit. If the presiding officer believes that the challenge was made in bad faith or was frivolous—meaning the challenger had no reasonable basis for the allegation—the deposit is forfeited to the Government as a penalty for the misuse of the challenge process. In all other circumstances, once the inquiry is concluded, the deposit must be returned to the challenger, thereby restoring the minimal financial stake that was originally placed.

Rule 49K introduces safeguards designed to further prevent personation by requiring a physical verification step that is difficult to replicate. Once a presiding officer or polling officer is satisfied that an individual is indeed the elector whose name appears on the roll, the officer must inspect the elector’s left forefinger. The left forefinger is the customary finger used for fingerprinting and for applying indelible ink marks because it is most visible and most consistently used by voters. The officer then applies a small dot of indelible ink to that finger. The ink is formulated to be permanent for the duration of the election, typically lasting at least a day or more, thereby providing a visual indicator that the person has already voted. This measure helps to prevent a voter from casting multiple ballots at the same or different polling stations. The rule also enumerates specific circumstances under which a person may be denied the right to vote. If an elector refuses to allow the left forefinger to be inspected or refuses to have the ink applied, or if the elector already has an ink mark on that finger from a previous vote, the officer may deem that the person is attempting to evade detection and may deny voting rights. Likewise, if an elector fails or refuses to produce an identity card as mandated by sub‑rule (3) of rule 49H—a separate rule that requires presentation of a government‑issued identity document for verification—then the elector may be barred from voting. The rule also anticipates situations where two elections are conducted simultaneously, such as a general parliamentary election and a state assembly election held on the same day. In such cases, if a voter has already had the left forefinger marked, or has shown the required identity card for one of the elections, the elector is nevertheless permitted to cast a ballot for the other election, despite the fact that the same safeguard has already been applied. This provision recognizes that the physical marking is intended to prevent duplicate voting within a single election, not across distinct elections occurring concurrently.

The rule also contemplates individuals who, for medical or physical reasons, do not have a left forefinger. In such instances, the reference to the left forefinger is interpreted to mean any other finger on the left hand. If all fingers on the left hand are absent, the rule extends the requirement to the forefinger or any other finger on the right hand. In the extreme case where an individual has lost all fingers on both hands, the rule adapts further, allowing the verification to be performed on the extremity of either arm that the person still possesses—essentially, the remaining part of the limb is used as a surrogate for the finger. These accommodations ensure that the safeguard does not unfairly disenfranchise persons with disabilities while still preserving the underlying objective of preventing multiple voting.

Finally, rule 49L outlines the specific steps that must be followed when an electronic voting machine (EVM) is used instead of the traditional paper ballot. The polling officer, who is the official responsible for the overall conduct of voting at a particular station, must first record the elector’s roll number—the unique identification number assigned to each voter in the marked copy of the electoral roll—into a separate register known as Form 17A. This register serves as a log of every person who has accessed the machine, creating an audit trail that can be examined later if any dispute arises. After entering the roll number, the officer must obtain either the elector’s signature or a thumb impression on the same register. The signature provides a handwritten affirmation of the voter’s identity, while a thumb impression serves as a biometric alternative for individuals who are unable or unwilling to sign. The inclusion of either a signature or thumb impression ensures that there is a physical record that can be cross‑referenced with the electronic data stored on the machine, thereby enhancing the transparency and accountability of the electronic voting process. By mandating these steps, the rule seeks to combine the efficiency and speed of electronic voting with the reliability of traditional paper‑based verification methods, ensuring that the integrity of the election is maintained even as technology evolves.

Collectively, these provisions illustrate a comprehensive framework designed to safeguard the sanctity of the electoral process. They balance the need for accessibility—allowing public servants on duty to vote, providing accommodations for voters with physical disabilities, and integrating modern voting technology—with the imperative to prevent fraud, ensure that each citizen votes only once, and that any challenges to a voter’s identity are handled in a fair, transparent, and procedurally sound manner. The detailed steps, from the inspection of a forefinger to the recording of a two‑rupee deposit, reflect a careful calibration between administrative practicality and the fundamental democratic principle that every eligible individual should be able to cast a free and fair vote without undue obstruction, while simultaneously protecting the ballot from manipulation or deceit.

The procedure for recording a voter’s name on the official list of electors, commonly called the electoral roll, requires that the officer in charge of the polling station place a clear mark beside the entry of each person who has been granted permission to cast a ballot. This marking serves as an immediate visual indication that the individual has satisfied all preliminary requirements and is therefore entitled to vote. In addition to this notation, the officer must also record, in a prescribed manner, the particulars of the identity document that the voter has produced to prove his or her identity. Such documents may include a national identity card, a driver’s licence, a passport or any other form of government‑issued identification that contains a photograph, name and unique number. The purpose of noting the document type and number is to create a verifiable trail that can be consulted later in the event of an electoral dispute.

A fundamental safeguard built into the system is that no person shall be allowed to vote unless his or her signature, or in the case of an illiterate voter, a thumb impression, has been taken on the register of voters. The signature or thumb impression is a personal biometric marker that confirms that the person who is standing at the polling place is indeed the same individual whose name appears on the roll. This requirement applies uniformly to all electors, regardless of age, gender or place of residence, and it is intended to prevent impersonation and multiple voting.

Even though rule 2, sub‑rule 2, contains provisions concerning the recording of signatures or thumb impressions, a higher‑level rule makes clear that any presiding officer, any polling officer, or any other official who is responsible for overseeing the voting process must also attest—that is, formally certify—the thumb impression that appears on the register of voters. The act of attestation involves the officer writing his or her own name, designation and signature alongside the thumb impression, thereby confirming that the impression has been correctly captured and that it belongs to the person named on the roll. This dual verification, consisting of the voter’s own biometric mark and the officer’s attestation, creates a robust chain of custody for each vote.

The principle of secrecy of the ballot is enshrined in rule 49M, which obliges every voter who has been lawfully permitted to vote, as set out in rule 49L, to preserve the confidentiality of his or her choice while inside the polling station. To achieve this, the law prescribes a precise sequence of actions that each elector must follow from the moment permission is granted until the voter exits the voting area. Immediately after the presiding officer or the polling officer in charge of the voting machine’s control unit signals that the voter may proceed, the officer activates the ballot‑recording component of the machine by pressing a designated button on the control unit. This activation readies the balloting unit, which is the part of the machine that actually registers the voter’s selection.

Once the balloting unit is activated, the voter must promptly move to the voting compartment, a private enclosure designed to shield the voter from observation by others. Inside this compartment the voter indicates his or her choice by pressing the button that corresponds to the name and the symbol of the preferred candidate. Modern electronic voting machines are typically arranged with a row of buttons, each bearing the candidate’s printed name and a graphic symbol that helps voters, including those with limited literacy, to identify their preferred option. When the voter depresses the appropriate button, the machine records the selection in its internal memory.

If the voting machine is equipped with a paper‑trail printer, an additional visual confirmation is provided at the moment the vote is cast. The printer, located next to the balloting unit within the privacy of the voting compartment, prints a small slip of paper that contains three key pieces of information: a unique serial number that identifies the specific transaction, the name of the candidate, and the candidate’s symbol. The voter can see this printed slip through a transparent window before the paper is automatically cut and dropped into a sealed drop box inside the printer. This allows the voter to verify that the machine has captured the intended choice while still maintaining secrecy, because the slip is immediately destroyed in a manner that prevents anyone else from seeing its contents.

After the vote has been recorded, the voter must leave the voting compartment without delay, thereby ensuring that the next person in line can proceed without unnecessary waiting. The law expressly requires that each voter complete the entire process—moving from the presiding officer to the control unit, entering the compartment, pressing the appropriate button, and exiting—without undue delay. This clause is intended to keep the flow of voters moving efficiently, especially in constituencies with large electorates.

To further protect the integrity of the voting process, the regulations prohibit more than one person from occupying the voting compartment at the same time. If an elector attempts to enter the compartment while another voter is still inside, the presiding officer or a polling officer must deny entry until the compartment is vacant. This rule prevents any possibility of a voter seeing another’s ballot or influencing another’s choice, thereby reinforcing the secrecy and independence of each vote.

In the event that a voter, having already been given permission to vote, refuses to follow the prescribed steps—such as refusing to press the button that corresponds to his or her chosen candidate after being warned by the presiding officer—the officer, acting under the direction of the presiding officer, must refuse to allow that person to vote. The law therefore empowers the presiding officer to enforce compliance with the voting procedure and to protect the orderly conduct of the election.

When a voter is denied the right to vote because of non‑compliance with the required procedure, the presiding officer must make a specific entry in the official register of voters, which is recorded on Form 17A. This entry, made under the presiding officer’s signature, notes that the voting procedure was violated and includes the name of the elector so that a clear record exists of the denial. This documentation ensures transparency and provides a basis for any subsequent review or appeal.

A separate set of rules, designated as 49MA, deals with complaints that arise when a paper‑trail printer is used. If a voter, after having pressed the button that records his or her vote, claims that the printed slip shows the name or the symbol of a different candidate than the one intended, the presiding officer must first obtain a written declaration from the voter outlining the allegation. Before the voter signs this declaration, the officer must warn the voter that making a false statement could lead to legal consequences, thereby discouraging frivolous or dishonest claims.

If the voter supplies the written declaration, the presiding officer records a second entry for that voter on Form 17A and then permits the voter to perform a test vote. This test vote is conducted in the presence of the presiding officer, any candidates or their agents who may be present, and the voting machine itself. The purpose of the test vote is to generate a new paper slip under controlled conditions, allowing all parties to observe whether the printer accurately reflects the button that is pressed.

Should the test vote confirm the voter’s original allegation—that the printer printed an incorrect name or symbol—the presiding officer must immediately inform the returning officer, who is the official responsible for overseeing the entire election in the constituency. The presiding officer must also halt any further voting on that particular machine to prevent additional errors, and must follow any instructions that the returning officer issues in response to the malfunction.

Conversely, if the investigation determines that the allegation is unfounded and that the paper slip produced during the test vote matches the button that was pressed, the presiding officer must make a specific remark on the second entry in Form 17A. This remark must include the serial number of the test vote slip and the name of the candidate for whom the test vote was recorded. The elector must then provide a signature or thumb impression opposite this remark, confirming that he or she acknowledges the outcome of the test. Finally, the presiding officer records the details of the test vote in item five of Part I of Form 17C, which is the official log used to track votes cast on each electronic machine. This comprehensive documentation creates a transparent audit trail that can be examined by election observers, auditors or courts if any dispute arises later.

Through these detailed procedures—ranging from the marking of the electoral roll, the capture of biometric identifiers, the activation and use of electronic voting equipment, the maintenance of secrecy within the voting compartment, and the handling of complaints about printed paper slips—the legal framework aims to ensure that every vote is cast freely, recorded accurately, and protected from fraud or manipulation. The layered safeguards, which involve multiple officials, written records, biometric verification and optional paper‑trail evidence, collectively uphold the democratic principle that elections must be both fair and perceived to be fair by all participants.

The provision identified as rule 49N deals with the special accommodation that must be given to electors who are blind or who suffer from other physical infirmities which prevent them from seeing the symbols printed on the voting machine’s balloting unit or from operating the machine’s buttons without help. When the person in charge of the polling place, known as the presiding officer, is convinced after a reasonable inquiry that an elector cannot independently recognise the candidate symbols or cannot press the appropriate button, the officer is required to allow that elector to bring a companion into the voting compartment. The companion must be an adult of at least eighteen years of age, and his role is to record the vote exactly as the elector wishes, acting solely as a conduit for the elector’s expressed choice. The rule imposes a safeguard that a single companion may not serve more than one elector at the same polling station on the same day, thereby limiting the possibility of undue influence or the appearance of impropriety. In addition, before a person may act as a companion, he must make a formal declaration that he will keep the content of the vote strictly confidential and that he has not already served as a companion for any other elector elsewhere on that day. The presiding officer is also required to document every instance in which this rule is applied by entering the details in a prescribed record sheet known as Form 14A, which serves as an official log of all such accommodations made during the election.

Rule 49O addresses the situation in which an elector, after having his name entered in the official register of voters on the designated sheet called Form 17A and after affixing his signature or thumb impression as required by an earlier rule, decides not to cast a vote. In that case the presiding officer must make a clear note on the same entry in Form 17A indicating that the elector chose not to vote. The elector’s signature or thumb impression must then be taken again, this time beside the remark that records his decision not to vote, so that there is a documented, verifiable record of the elector’s conscious choice to abstain after having been formally recognised as a registered voter for that polling session.

The provisions labelled as rule 49P concern “tendered votes,” which are ballots provided to a person who claims to be a particular elector after another individual has already cast a vote in that elector’s name. When such a claim is made, the presiding officer may ask the claimant a series of verification questions to establish his identity; if the claimant answers satisfactorily, he is not allowed to use the electronic balloting unit but is instead given a paper ballot specially designed for this purpose. The design and the language or languages used on the tendered ballot are determined by the Election Commission, the central body that oversees the conduct of elections. Before the paper ballot is handed over, the elector must write his own name in the entry that corresponds to him on another official register known as Form 17B, thereby linking the paper ballot to his identity in the official record. Upon receiving the paper ballot, the elector must promptly proceed to the voting compartment, mark his choice by making a clear cross (the letter “X”) next to the symbol of the candidate he wishes to support, using the writing instrument supplied for that purpose. He must then fold the ballot in a manner that hides the mark, and if the presiding officer asks, he must show a distinguishing feature on the ballot that identifies it as a tendered ballot. After that, the elector hands the folded ballot to the presiding officer, who places it into a special envelope kept for the secure storage of such votes, and finally the elector is required to leave the polling station. If the elector who is using a tendered ballot is also blind or otherwise physically unable to mark the paper without help, the presiding officer must apply the same procedure set out in rule 49N, allowing the elector to bring an adult companion into the voting compartment under the identical conditions of confidentiality and exclusivity, so that the companion can assist in marking the ballot in accordance with the elector’s wishes.

The rule numbered 49Q gives the presiding officer the authority to enter the voting compartment at any time during the polling period if he believes it is necessary to protect the integrity of the voting process. The presiding officer may step inside the compartment to take any measures that are required to ensure that the electronic balloting unit is not tampered with, altered, or interfered with in any way that could compromise the secrecy or accuracy of the vote. If the presiding officer observes or suspects that an elector who has entered the compartment is attempting to manipulate the machine, or if the elector remains inside for an unusually long period, the presiding officer must intervene, re‑enter the compartment and take appropriate action to restore order and ensure that the poll proceeds smoothly and without obstruction. Whenever the presiding officer decides to go inside the voting compartment under these circumstances, he must allow any polling agents who are present at the station to accompany him if they wish, thereby providing transparency and allowing the agents to witness the actions taken inside the compartment.

Finally, rule 49R deals with the formal closing of the polling station. The presiding officer is required to shut the poll at the exact time prescribed by the higher authority, specifically the time set out in section 56 of the governing election statutes. Once that time has passed, no additional electors may be admitted to the polling place. However, there is an explicit provision that any electors who are already inside the polling station at the moment it is closed must be permitted to finish casting their votes, so that no voter who has arrived before the deadline is denied the opportunity to vote. In the event that a dispute arises concerning whether a particular elector was already present inside the polling station before it was closed, the presiding officer has the final authority to make a determination, and his decision is conclusive and not subject to further appeal within the polling process. This ensures that the closing of the poll is handled in an orderly fashion while respecting the rights of all voters who were present at the designated cutoff time.

The presiding officer, who is the official in charge of overseeing the conduct of the poll at a particular polling station, is required at the conclusion of voting to compile a formal summary of all the votes that have been recorded. This summary must be entered on a specific document known as Form 17C, which serves as the official account of the votes counted by the electronic voting apparatus. Once the poll has been closed, the presiding officer must place this completed Form 17C into a separate envelope that is clearly marked with the words “Account of Votes Recorded” so that anyone handling the envelope can immediately recognize its contents. The purpose of this separate cover is to protect the integrity of the vote‑recording sheet and to ensure that it is not confused with any other paperwork that may be present at the polling station.

In addition to preparing the official account, the presiding officer must provide every polling agent who is present at the polling station with a true copy of the entries that appear on Form 17C. A polling agent is a person appointed by a candidate or his election agent to observe the polling process and to safeguard the interests of that candidate. The presiding officer must first obtain a receipt from each polling agent acknowledging receipt of the copy, and then must sign or otherwise attest the copy as a true and accurate reproduction of the original entries. This step creates a documented chain of custody for the vote record, allowing the agents to later verify that the figures they received correspond exactly to those originally recorded by the voting machine.

Immediately after the poll has been closed, the presiding officer must act without undue delay to prevent any further votes from being entered into the electronic system. To achieve this, the presiding officer closes the control unit of the voting machine, which is the central component that governs the operation of the device and stores the electronic tally of votes. By closing this unit, the officer ensures that the machine can no longer accept new inputs. The presiding officer must then physically detach the balloting unit, which is the part of the machine where voters actually cast their ballots, from the control unit. If the machine includes a printer that produces paper slips as a physical record of each vote, the presiding officer must also separate the printer from the rest of the apparatus, taking care that the stack of paper slips that have already been deposited in the printer’s drop box remains untouched and intact. This careful handling guarantees that the electronic and paper records are both preserved in the condition they were in at the moment voting ended.

After the components have been separated, each part—the control unit, the balloting unit, and the printer where applicable—must be sealed in a manner prescribed by the Election Commission, the independent body that administers elections and sets the standards for security and transparency. The seal used to close each unit must be applied in such a way that it cannot be removed without breaking the seal, thereby providing a clear indication if any tampering occurs. The sealing process may involve the use of tamper‑evident stickers, wax seals, or other approved mechanisms that leave a visible sign of interference if someone attempts to open the unit. In addition to the seals placed by the presiding officer, any polling agents who are present at the station and who wish to affix their own seals are permitted to do so, thereby adding an extra layer of assurance that the devices have not been altered after the poll’s closure.

The presiding officer must then proceed to prepare a series of sealed packets that contain various documents and materials used during the poll. One packet must hold the marked copy of the electoral roll, which is the official list of all registered voters for that constituency, with marks indicating who actually voted. Another packet must contain the register of voters, known as Form 17A, which records the names of voters as they sign in at the polling station. A third packet must enclose the cover that held the ballot papers that were actually tendered, together with the accompanying list compiled on Form 17B, which details the distribution of ballot papers to the presiding officer. A fourth packet must contain the list of challenged votes, that is, any ballots that were disputed or set aside for further examination because of irregularities. Finally, any additional papers that the Election Commission has directed to be kept in a sealed packet must also be placed in a separate packet. Each of these packets is to be sealed first with the seal of the presiding officer and then, if a candidate, his election agent, or a polling agent who is present wishes to add their own seal, that seal must also be affixed. This dual‑sealing system provides a clear audit trail and assures all parties that the documents have remained untouched since the poll ended.

Having completed the sealing of the voting machine components and the various document packets, the presiding officer must deliver all of these items to the returning officer, who is the official responsible for overseeing the entire election process within the constituency. The delivery may be made directly by the presiding officer or by an authorized courier, and it must be to the location specified by the returning officer. The items to be handed over include the voting machine itself, the Form 17C account of votes, all of the sealed packets prepared under the previous rule, and any other papers that were used at the polling station, such as receipts, notices, or supplementary forms. Once the returning officer receives these materials, he must make suitable arrangements to ensure their safe transport and storage. This typically involves using secure, tamper‑evident containers, assigning trusted personnel to guard the items, and maintaining detailed logs of every movement, all with the aim of preserving the integrity of the evidence until the counting of votes begins.

If, for any reason, the poll at a particular polling station has to be adjourned—that is, temporarily suspended and resumed at a later time—under the circumstances described in the relevant section of the election law, the provisions that govern the closing of the poll, the sealing of machines, and the handling of documents are to be applied as far as possible as if the poll had been closed at the scheduled time. In other words, even though the poll has not formally ended, the officials must treat the situation as if it had, following the same procedures for sealing and documenting. When an adjourned poll is called for a second time, the electors who have already cast a vote in the earlier part of the poll are not permitted to vote again, to prevent duplicate voting. The returning officer must supply the presiding officer at the station where the adjourned poll will take place with a new sealed packet that contains a fresh marked copy of the electoral roll, a new register of voters (Form 17A), and a new voting machine, all sealed in accordance with the standard rules. The presiding officer then opens this sealed packet in the presence of any polling agents who are present, and uses the newly provided marked electoral roll to indicate which electors are still eligible to vote at the rescheduled poll. The same set of rules that apply to an ordinary poll—including those governing the handling of voting machines, the preparation of accounts, and the sealing of documents—continue to apply to the adjourned poll, ensuring consistency and fairness throughout the entire electoral process.

In the event that the presiding officer believes that a phenomenon known as booth capturing is occurring, which refers to the illegal practice of forcibly taking control of a polling station in order to cast fraudulent votes, the officer must act immediately to stop any further voting. This is done by promptly closing the control unit of the voting machine, thereby preventing the machine from recording any additional votes, and by detaching the balloting unit from the control unit so that it can no longer be used. This rapid response is intended to protect the integrity of the election by halting the illicit activity as soon as it is detected.

The section that follows introduces a series of definitions that are used throughout the part of the law dealing with the counting of votes in parliamentary and assembly constituencies. The term “candidate” is defined simply as any individual who is contesting the election. The word “constituency” refers to the geographical area that elects a member to either the parliament or a state legislative assembly. A “counting agent” is a person who has been formally appointed under the relevant provision of the election statutes to observe the counting of votes; this category includes the candidate himself as well as any election agent representing the candidate, provided that they are present at the counting venue. The phrase “notified polling station” denotes a polling station that has been officially designated for use in the election under the specific rule governing such notifications, while the broader term “polling station” refers to any polling place established under the general provision of the election law, except for those that have been specifically notified. These definitions establish a common vocabulary that ensures all participants in the electoral process—officials, candidates, agents, and observers—share a clear and uniform understanding of the terms used in the subsequent procedural rules.

The officer charged with the responsibility of returning the election result must, as a matter of procedure, determine in advance the venue or venues at which the ballot papers will be examined and tallied, and must also set the precise moment when the counting process will begin. This decision is required to be taken no later than one week before the first day on which the poll is scheduled to take place, thereby giving sufficient time for all interested parties to be informed. Once the place or places and the date and time have been fixed, the returning officer is obligated to communicate these particulars in writing to each candidate who is standing for election, as well as to each candidate’s appointed election agent, who acts on the candidate’s behalf in matters of election administration. The written notice must contain all the essential details so that the candidates and their representatives can make appropriate arrangements to be present at the counting. The legislation also contains a safeguard that allows the returning officer, should an unforeseen circumstance arise that makes the original arrangement impracticable, to change the date, the time, or the location of the counting, or any combination of these elements. Even when such a change is deemed necessary, the officer must still provide a written notice of the new arrangements to each candidate and to each election agent, ensuring that the parties are kept fully informed of any modification that could affect their ability to observe the count.

In respect of the people who may be present at the counting venue to represent the interests of the candidates, the law sets out a maximum number of counting agents that each candidate may appoint. This limit, which is generally sixteen agents per counting location, may be varied only if the Election Commission, the body that supervises the conduct of elections, issues a specific direction that either raises or lowers the ceiling for a particular circumstance. The appointment of each counting agent must be made on the prescribed Form 18, and the candidate must prepare two identical copies of this form. One copy is to be forwarded to the returning officer so that the officer has a record of who has been designated to act as a counting agent, while the other copy is to be handed to the counting agent himself. The agent must then present this second copy to the returning officer not later than one hour before the scheduled start of the counting, thereby confirming his or her identity and authority to be present at the count. Admission to the counting venue is contingent upon the agent delivering this second copy, having completed and signed the declaration that is part of the form, and receiving from the returning officer an official authority that permits entry. If at any stage a candidate decides to withdraw a counting agent, the revocation must be documented on Form 19 and lodged with the returning officer. Should such a revocation occur before the counting actually begins, the candidate retains the right to appoint a replacement agent, provided that the new appointment follows the same procedural steps set out in the rule governing the use of Form 18.

The returning officer also has a duty to control who may be present inside the counting room, which is the place fixed for the tally of votes. By default, every person who is not expressly authorised is to be excluded from the counting area. The only individuals who may be allowed entry are those that the returning officer himself appoints to assist in the counting process—these individuals are commonly known as counting supervisors and counting assistants—the persons who have received authorisation directly from the Election Commission, public servants who are on duty in connection with the election, and the candidates together with their election agents and counting agents. A further restriction is placed on the appointment of counting supervisors and assistants: anyone who has been employed by a candidate, or who has performed any work on behalf of a candidate in connection with the election, is prohibited from being appointed to these supervisory roles, thereby preventing a conflict of interest. Within the counting room, the returning officer determines which counting agent will oversee the work at each individual counting table or at any group of tables, ensuring that the monitoring of the count is organised and that each agent knows precisely where his or her responsibilities lie. The legislation also provides that if any person present in the counting area behaves in a disorderly manner, refuses to follow a lawful direction issued by the returning officer, or otherwise disrupts the orderly conduct of the count, that person may be removed from the premises. The authority to remove a disruptive individual may be exercised by the returning officer himself, by a police officer who is on duty at the time, or by any other person who has been given the power to do so by the returning officer.

To preserve the confidentiality of the ballot and to ensure that the secrecy of each voter’s choice is protected, the returning officer must, before the counting begins, read aloud the provisions of section one hundred twenty‑eight to everyone who is present in the counting room. Section one hundred twenty‑eight sets out the legal principles that guarantee the anonymity of the vote and the penalties that apply to anyone who attempts to disclose how an individual voted. By publicly reciting these provisions, the returning officer reminds all participants of their legal obligations and the seriousness with which the law treats any breach of ballot secrecy, thereby reinforcing the integrity of the electoral process.

When ballots have been submitted by post, a separate set of rules applies to their handling before they are mixed with the ordinary ballot papers. The returning officer must first deal exclusively with the postal ballot papers, following a precise sequence of steps. Any envelope that contains a postal ballot and is labelled as Form 13C, if it arrives after the deadline that has been set for the receipt of postal votes, must remain sealed and must not be opened; consequently, any vote that might be contained in such an overdue envelope is to be excluded from the count. All other envelopes that have been received within the prescribed time are opened one after another. As each envelope is opened, the returning officer must first examine the accompanying declaration, which is recorded on Form 13A, to verify that the declaration is present, that it has been properly signed and attested, and that the information it contains is consistent with the other documents. If the declaration is missing, is not signed, contains substantial defects, or if the serial number of the ballot paper recorded on the declaration does not match the serial number that is printed on the cover labelled as Form 13B, the envelope is not to be opened further. Instead, the returning officer makes a note on the envelope indicating the reason for rejection and then sets the ballot paper aside as a rejected vote. For each envelope that is therefore rejected, the envelope together with its declaration is placed back into a new Form 13C envelope. All of these sealed Form 13C envelopes, containing either accepted or rejected postal ballots, are then gathered into a separate packet that is sealed and clearly marked. The seal is accompanied by a record that lists the name of the constituency, the date on which the counting is taking place, and a brief description of the contents of the packet, thereby creating a transparent audit trail that can be examined later if any dispute arises about the handling of postal votes. This careful procedure ensures that the counting of postal ballots is carried out with the same level of scrutiny and fairness as the counting of ordinary ballot papers, thereby upholding the overall integrity of the election.

The officer who has been appointed to oversee the conduct of the election, commonly referred to as the returning officer, must first gather together every declaration that has been completed on Form 13A and that he has examined and found to be in proper order. These declarations, which constitute the formal statements submitted by voters who have voted by post, are to be placed in a distinct packet that is kept separate from any other materials. Before any of the envelopes or covers that are prepared on Form 13B are opened, this packet containing the Form 13A declarations must be securely sealed. The sealing is intended to preserve the integrity of the documents and to prevent any tampering before the subsequent stages of the counting process begin. On the outside of this sealed packet the returning officer is required to record certain particulars that are specified in sub‑rule (5); these particulars typically include information such as the name of the constituency, the date on which the declarations were received, and any reference numbers that identify the batch of postal votes.

Once the packet of Form 13A declarations has been sealed, the returning officer proceeds to the next phase, which involves opening the covers that have been prepared on Form 13B. The covers in Form 13B that have not already been dealt with by the earlier provisions of this rule are to be opened one after another in a systematic sequence. For each cover that is opened, the returning officer must carefully examine the ballot paper contained within. This examination is commonly called a scrutiny, and its purpose is to determine whether the vote recorded on the ballot paper complies with all of the legal requirements for a valid postal vote. The officer must decide, in each case, whether the vote can be counted as a legitimate expression of the voter’s choice or whether it must be set aside as invalid.

A postal ballot paper is to be rejected, that is, it must be excluded from the count, if any of a number of specific conditions is present. First, the ballot paper must be rejected if it bears any mark or any form of writing other than the mark that indicates the voter’s choice, and that extraneous mark or writing would enable the identity of the elector to be ascertained. The principle behind this prohibition is the requirement for secrecy of the vote; any identifying mark would compromise the confidentiality that is guaranteed by law. Second, the ballot paper must be rejected if it contains no indication of a vote at all, meaning that the voter has failed to mark any candidate or option on the paper. Third, the ballot paper must be rejected if the notes on it show that the voter has expressed support for more than one candidate; in other words, the ballot must contain a vote for only a single candidate, and any indication that the voter has voted for multiple candidates invalidates the ballot. Fourth, the ballot paper must be rejected if it is determined to be spurious, which means that it is not an official ballot issued by the returning officer but rather a counterfeit or unauthorized document. Fifth, the ballot paper must be rejected if it has been so severely damaged or mutilated that it can no longer be positively identified as a genuine ballot paper; damage of this sort could render the paper illegible or could raise doubts about its authenticity. Finally, a ballot paper must be rejected if it is not returned inside the envelope that was originally sent to the elector along with the ballot; the envelope, prepared on Form 13B, serves as a tamper‑evident container, and any ballot that arrives outside of that container is considered suspect.

In addition to the foregoing grounds for rejection, a vote that is recorded on a postal ballot paper must also be rejected if the mark that is intended to indicate the voter’s choice is placed on the ballot in such a way that it creates genuine doubt about which candidate the vote was meant for. This situation arises when the mark is ambiguous, for example when it straddles the boundary between two candidates’ names or when it is placed in a position that does not clearly correspond to any single candidate. In such cases the returning officer must treat the ballot as invalid because the intention of the voter cannot be ascertained with certainty.

Conversely, the returning officer is not permitted to reject a ballot merely because the mark indicating the vote is faint, indistinct, or has been made more than once, provided that the overall appearance of the ballot still makes clear the voter’s intention to support a particular candidate. The law recognises that voters may occasionally make a mark that is not perfectly neat, and as long as the intended choice can be identified without doubt, the vote must be counted. This provision is intended to uphold the principle that the expression of the voter’s will should not be discounted on the basis of minor imperfections in the physical marking.

Having completed the scrutiny of each postal ballot and having identified which ballots are valid and which are to be rejected, the returning officer must then proceed to tally the valid votes. For each candidate who is contesting the election, the returning officer adds together all of the valid postal votes that have been recorded in favour of that candidate. The total number of votes for each candidate is then entered onto the official result sheet, which is prepared on Form 20. This result sheet constitutes the formal record of the election outcome for the constituency. Once the totals have been entered, the returning officer makes a public announcement of the results, thereby informing the electorate, the candidates, and any interested parties of the final vote counts as derived from the postal ballot process.

Following the announcement of the results, the returning officer must take steps to preserve the evidence of the counting process. All of the ballot papers that have been judged to be valid, together with all of the ballot papers that have been rejected, are to be placed into separate bundles. These bundles must be kept apart from one another to maintain a clear distinction between the votes that contributed to the final tally and those that were excluded. The two bundles are then placed together inside a single packet, which is to be sealed. The sealing must bear the official seal of the returning officer, and it may also bear the seals of any candidates, their election agents, or the counting agents who wish to affix their own seals to the packet as a further safeguard. On the outside of this sealed packet the returning officer must record, in plain language, the name of the constituency, the date on which the counting took place, and a concise description of what the packet contains—in other words, a statement that it holds the bundles of valid and rejected postal ballot papers. This sealed packet serves as the official archive of the postal voting process, ensuring that the materials can be inspected later if any dispute arises and providing a transparent chain of custody.

In the broader context of election administration, the law also provides that the returning officer may, when circumstances warrant, open more than one ballot box at the same time. A ballot box is the container that holds the ordinary ballot papers that are cast at a polling station, and in many elections there are separate boxes for each polling station within a constituency. The returning officer has the discretion to have the contents of these boxes opened and counted simultaneously, rather than one after another. This simultaneous opening can be useful in situations where there are multiple polling stations that need to be processed quickly, or where the returning officer wishes to ensure that the counting at all locations proceeds in a coordinated manner. By allowing the concurrent opening and counting of multiple boxes, the rule seeks to promote efficiency while still preserving the integrity and accuracy of the count. The returning officer must still follow all of the procedural safeguards, such as the presence of candidates’ agents and the use of sealed containers, to guarantee that the simultaneous counting does not compromise the fairness of the election.

Before any ballot box is opened at a counting table, the individuals who have been appointed as counting agents and who are present at that particular table are entitled to a preliminary examination of the security device that has been affixed to the box. The security device in question is most commonly a paper seal, although the law allows for the use of any other type of seal that might have been placed on the box in order to guarantee its integrity. The purpose of this inspection is to enable the counting agents to verify, by visual inspection and by feeling the seal if appropriate, that the seal has not been broken, torn, or otherwise compromised. In other words, they must be satisfied that the seal is intact, which provides a presumption that the contents of the box have not been accessed or altered since the time the seal was originally applied at the close of voting.

Having allowed the counting agents to carry out this verification, the responsibility then passes to the returning officer, who is the chief official in charge of the entire electoral process at the polling station. The returning officer must personally confirm, based on the observations of the counting agents and his own inspection, that none of the ballot boxes has in fact been tampered with. “Tampered with” in this context means any unauthorized opening, removal, addition, or alteration of the ballot papers that are contained within a box after the seal has been applied. The returning officer’s satisfaction is a legal requirement; it is not sufficient for him merely to assume that the boxes are secure. He must be convinced, through the evidence presented by the intact seals and any other relevant observations, that the boxes have remained sealed and untouched.

If, after this careful examination, the returning officer comes to the conclusion that a particular ballot box has indeed been tampered with – that is, if he is satisfied that the seal has been broken, that the box has been opened without proper authority, or that there is any other indication of interference – the law directs a specific course of action. In such a case, the ballot papers that are inside the compromised box must not be counted at all. Instead, the returning officer must follow the special procedure that is set out in section fifty‑eight of the electoral regulations. Section fifty‑eight deals with the handling of ballot papers that have been transferred to bags or cloth‑lined covers under an earlier rule, and it provides a detailed method for dealing with the situation where the integrity of a box cannot be guaranteed. In effect, the compromised box is set aside, its contents are excluded from the ordinary counting process, and a separate, more stringent set of rules is applied to ensure that any votes that might still be salvageable are treated in a way that preserves the overall fairness of the election.

The counting of votes proceeds under the heading “Counting of Votes,” which is set out in rule fifty‑six. The first step, as indicated in the original text, is to remove all ballot papers from each ballot box and to arrange them into convenient bundles. This bundling is intended to make the subsequent examination and counting more orderly, allowing the officials to handle a manageable number of papers at a time rather than dealing with a single, unwieldy pile. Once the papers have been grouped, the returning officer must examine each ballot paper individually to determine whether it meets the criteria for a valid vote. The law enumerates several specific grounds upon which a ballot paper must be rejected, and each ground is explained in plain language to ensure that the officials understand exactly what disqualifies a ballot.

The first ground for rejection is that the ballot paper bears any mark or writing that could identify the elector who cast the vote. The principle behind this rule is the secrecy of the ballot; the voter’s identity must remain unknown to protect the voter from intimidation or retaliation. If a name, address, initials, or any other personal identifier appears on the paper, the ballot is automatically invalid. The second ground for rejection concerns the location and nature of the mark that indicates the voter’s choice. A ballot must show a clear indication of the voter’s selection by placing a mark directly on or very near the symbol of one of the candidates that appears on the face of the ballot paper. If the ballot bears no mark at all, or if the mark is placed elsewhere on the paper – for example, in a marginal area that does not correspond to any candidate’s symbol – the ballot is rejected. Likewise, if the voter uses an instrument other than the official ballot marker supplied for the purpose – such as a pen, pencil, or any improvised tool – the ballot is also deemed invalid because the use of an unauthorized instrument could affect the legibility or authenticity of the vote.

A third ground for rejection occurs when a ballot paper shows votes for more than one candidate. The voting system in question is designed for a single‑choice vote, meaning each voter may select only one candidate. If a voter marks two or more candidates, the ballot is considered ambiguous and is therefore rejected. The fourth ground is closely related but distinct: it applies when the mark indicating the vote is placed in such a way that it is doubtful which candidate was intended. For example, a mark that straddles the boundary between two candidate symbols, or that is so faint or smudged that the election officials cannot confidently attribute it to a particular candidate, must be rejected because the voter’s intention cannot be ascertained with certainty.

The fifth ground for rejection is the presence of a spurious ballot paper. A spurious ballot is one that has not been officially issued by the election authority; it may be a counterfeit or a duplicate that was introduced into the counting process. Such papers are automatically excluded because they do not represent a legitimate vote. The sixth ground concerns ballots that are so damaged or mutilated that the officials cannot confirm that they are genuine ballot papers. Damage might include tears, water damage, missing sections, or any alteration that makes it impossible to verify that the paper originated from the official supply of ballots.

The seventh ground for rejection deals with the serial number and design of the ballot paper. Official ballots are printed with a unique serial number and a specific design that is consistent across all ballots used at a particular polling station. If a ballot paper bears a serial number that does not correspond to the range of numbers assigned for that election, or if its design differs from the authorized format – for instance, if the paper is of a different size, color, or layout – the ballot must be rejected because its authenticity cannot be confirmed. The eighth ground for rejection refers to the requirement that each ballot paper must bear both a specific mark and the signature of the presiding officer, as mandated by sub‑rule (1) of rule thirty‑eight. If a ballot paper fails to display these required identifiers, it is to be rejected.

However, the law also provides important safeguards to prevent the automatic dismissal of ballots that contain minor procedural errors. First, if the returning officer determines that a defect described in the seventh or eighth ground – namely, a problem with the serial number, design, or missing signature – arose from a mistake or oversight on the part of the presiding officer or another polling officer, the ballot paper should not be rejected solely on that basis. In other words, an administrative error does not automatically invalidate a voter's choice, provided that the ballot can otherwise be verified as genuine. Second, the law adds a further qualification concerning the clarity of the voter’s mark. Even if the mark is indistinct or there are multiple faint marks, the ballot should not be rejected if the intention of the voter to vote for a particular candidate is evident from the overall pattern of the marking. This provision acknowledges that voters may sometimes make imperfect marks, yet their preference can still be discerned.

Before the returning officer is permitted to reject any ballot paper on any of the grounds just described, he must give each counting agent who is present at the counting table a reasonable opportunity to examine the ballot. This examination must be limited to visual inspection; the counting agents are not allowed to touch the ballot paper or to handle any other ballot papers. The purpose of this rule is to ensure transparency and to allow the agents to verify that the rejection is justified, while also preserving the integrity of the ballot by minimizing handling that could cause additional damage or contamination.

Once a ballot paper has been formally rejected, the returning officer is required to make a clear, written record on the ballot itself. He must write the word “Rejected” and also note, in an abbreviated form, the specific ground for rejection. This endorsement may be written by hand or applied with a rubber stamp, and the returning officer must initial the endorsement to confirm that he has personally authorized the rejection. All ballots that have been marked in this way are then placed together in a single bundle. This bundling of rejected ballots serves two purposes: it keeps them separate from the valid votes for counting, and it creates an easily identifiable set of papers that can be reviewed later if any dispute arises.

Every ballot paper that does not fall under any of the rejection criteria is counted as one valid vote. The law makes a further clarification that no cover containing tendered ballot papers – meaning any envelope, bag, or other container that may have been used to transport the ballots to the counting table – may be opened for the purpose of counting votes. The contents of such a cover must remain sealed, and any ballots found inside an unopened cover are not to be counted. This rule prevents the possibility of tampering with ballots after they have been placed in a secure container.

After the counting of all valid ballot papers from all of the ballot boxes used at a particular polling station has been completed, a two‑step documentation process is carried out. First, the counting supervisor – the official who oversees the counting process – completes Part Two of Form Sixteen, which is titled “Result of Counting.” This form records the total number of votes for each candidate, the number of rejected ballots, and any other pertinent statistical information. The counting supervisor must sign this part of the form to attest to its accuracy, and the returning officer must also sign it, thereby providing a joint certification of the results. Following this, the returning officer enters the final figures into a separate result sheet known as Form Twenty. This sheet serves as the official record of the election outcome for that polling station, and the returning officer subsequently announces the particulars of the result to the public or to the appropriate electoral authority.

The next stage in the post‑counting process concerns the sealing of the used ballot papers. After the votes have been counted, the valid ballots for each candidate – that is, the ballots that were correctly marked in favor of a particular candidate – are bundled separately from the bundle of rejected ballots. These separate bundles are then placed together into a larger packet, which is sealed with the official seal of the returning officer. In addition, any candidates, their election agents, or the counting agents who wish to do so may also affix their own seals to the packet, thereby providing additional layers of verification that the packet has not been opened or altered after sealing. On the exterior of each sealed packet, a set of particulars is recorded. These particulars include the name of the constituency in which the election took place, the specific polling station where the ballots were used, and the date on which the counting was conducted. Recording these details ensures that the packet can be traced back to its origin and that the chain of custody is clear.

Finally, the law addresses the situation where ballot papers have been transferred from ballot boxes to cloth bags or cloth‑lined covers, as allowed under sub‑rule five of rule forty‑four. In those cases, the provisions of rules fifty‑five, fifty‑six, and fifty‑seven apply in exactly the same manner as they would if the ballots had remained in the original boxes. This means that the same procedures for inspecting seals, verifying that no tampering has occurred, rejecting invalid ballots, recording rejections, bundling valid and rejected papers, and sealing the final packets must all be followed even when the ballots have been moved to a different type of container for counting. The purpose of this cross‑reference is to ensure that the integrity of the electoral process is maintained uniformly, regardless of the specific method used to transport the ballots from the polling station to the counting table. By applying the same rigorous standards to all ballot handling scenarios, the regulations safeguard the credibility of the election and help to assure voters that their votes are counted accurately and fairly.

The legislation makes clear that whenever the rules speak of a “ballot box,” the reference is to be understood in a broader sense. In practical terms, the term does not limit itself to a wooden or metal container that might traditionally hold the papers; rather, it also includes any bag, sack, or other type of cover into which the contents that would normally be placed in a ballot box have been transferred. This broader construction is intended to cover all possible means by which the papers are collected and stored after the polling day, ensuring that the rules apply equally whether the material is kept in a rigid box, a flexible sack, or any other secure receptacle that serves the same purpose of safeguarding the votes.

The next set of provisions deals specifically with the process of counting votes at what are termed “notified polling stations.” A notified polling station is a voting place that has been formally identified and announced by the election authority, typically because of its significance or the volume of voters it serves. When the ballots that have been placed in ballot boxes at such stations must be counted, the general rules that govern ballot handling—originally enumerated as rules 50 through 54—continue to apply, but a special set of instructions replaces the ordinary rules numbered 55, 56 and 57. These special instructions are set out in what is known as Rule 55A and Rule 56A, and they provide a detailed framework for how the boxes are to be opened, inspected, and how the individual ballot papers are to be examined and counted.

Rule 55A, which concerns the scrutiny and opening of ballot boxes, begins by stating that all ballot boxes used at a notified polling station must be opened at the same moment. This simultaneous opening is designed to prevent any opportunity for manipulation that could arise if boxes were opened at different times. However, the rule also mandates that each ballot box must be handled in a way that guarantees its contents remain separate from the contents of any other box; in other words, the papers from one box must never become mixed with those from another. The purpose of this separation is to preserve the integrity of the vote for each individual candidate or party, which is often indicated by a unique symbol placed inside the box.

The rule further allows the returning officer—the official appointed to oversee the election process in a particular constituency—to open and count the contents of more than one ballot box at the same time, provided that the first condition of simultaneous opening and strict separation of contents is met. This flexibility is practical in situations where many boxes need to be processed quickly, such as in a large urban polling station, but it does not compromise the safeguards that keep each box’s contents distinct.

Before any box is opened, the rule requires that the counting agents who are present at the station be permitted to examine the seal that has been placed on the box. The seal may be a paper seal, a wax seal, a tamper‑evident tape, or any other type of seal that is intended to demonstrate that the box has not been opened since the close of voting. The agents must be given a reasonable opportunity to verify that the seal is intact and has not been broken or altered. This step provides a visual confirmation that the ballot papers inside have not been subject to unauthorized access.

Following the agents’ inspection, the returning officer must also be satisfied that none of the ballot boxes have been tampered with. The officer’s duty is to conduct a careful visual and, if necessary, a tactile examination of the seals and the boxes themselves, looking for any signs of interference such as broken seals, unusual marks, or evidence that a box may have been opened and resealed. If the returning officer concludes that a particular box appears to have been tampered with, the rule dictates that the ballot papers contained in that box must not be counted. Instead, the procedure set out in section 58 of the broader electoral code must be followed for that polling station, which typically involves a more detailed investigation and possibly a recount or a fresh tally under stricter supervision.

Assuming the returning officer is satisfied that the seals are unbroken, each ballot box is then opened in turn. At that moment, the counting agents are again allowed to inspect the interior of the box. They must confirm that the box bears the proper election symbol inside, which is a distinctive emblem that indicates which candidate or political party the box is associated with. The rule also requires that the box have been marked in accordance with sub‑rule (6) of rule 33, as modified by clause (c) of sub‑rule (3) of rule 49. Those references to other rules essentially set out the precise way in which the symbol should be placed, such as the size, color, and position, to avoid any ambiguity.

If a dispute arises concerning the identity of the candidate to whom a particular ballot box belongs—perhaps because the symbol is missing or unclear—the returning officer must resolve the issue by referring to the symbol that is inside the box. The legislation anticipates three specific problem situations. First, if there is no symbol inside the box at all, the officer must look for other evidence. Second, if the symbol is present but has been damaged, defaced, or otherwise rendered unrecognizable, the officer again must seek alternative clues. Third, if the same symbol appears on two or more boxes at the same polling station, the officer must determine which box belongs to which candidate by examining all relevant circumstances. These circumstances might include distinguishing marks such as serial numbers, unique stickers, or any other identifying features that were applied to the box at the time of its sealing. If, after considering all these factors, the returning officer still cannot determine the correct allocation, the matter must be immediately referred to the Election Commission, the independent body that has ultimate authority over election disputes. The Commission will then make a binding decision based on the evidence presented.

The second special provision, Rule 56A, deals with the counting of the individual ballot papers themselves. Once a ballot box has been opened, the papers that are taken out must be organized into convenient bundles. This bundling makes it easier for the counting agents to handle the papers systematically and to keep track of how many have been examined. The rule then sets out the circumstances under which a ballot paper may be rejected and therefore not counted as a valid vote. The returning officer is empowered to reject a paper if it bears any mark or writing that could identify the voter, because the secrecy of the ballot is a fundamental principle of democratic elections. Likewise, any ballot that is determined to be spurious—that is, a fake or counterfeit document that was not issued by the election authority—must be discarded. If a ballot is so heavily damaged, torn, or mutilated that its authenticity as an official voting paper cannot be ascertained, it too must be rejected.

Additional grounds for rejection include a mismatch in the serial number or design of the ballot paper. Official ballot papers are printed with a unique serial number and a specific design that is approved for use at a given polling station. If a paper carries a serial number that does not fit the sequence, or if its design differs from the authorized template—perhaps because it is a leftover from a previous election or a counterfeit—the returning officer must reject it. Finally, a ballot that lacks both the required official mark and the signature prescribed by sub‑rule (1) of rule 38 must also be set aside. The required mark might be a watermark or an embossed seal, while the signature could be that of the presiding officer, confirming that the ballot was issued under proper authority.

Nevertheless, the rule contains a safeguard for situations where the defect described in the previous points (the serial number or design error, or the missing mark or signature) is the result of an honest mistake or a failure on the part of the presiding officer or the polling officer. In such cases, the returning officer is not to reject the ballot solely because of that technical defect. This exception acknowledges that human error can occur during the complex process of issuing ballots, and it prevents voters from being disenfranchised for mistakes that are not their own.

Before a ballot paper is officially rejected, the returning officer must give the counting agents a reasonable opportunity to inspect the paper. This inspection allows the agents to see for themselves why the paper is being set aside, thereby ensuring transparency. However, the agents are not permitted to handle the rejected paper or any other ballot paper themselves; they may only view it. This restriction minimizes the risk of accidental damage or contamination of the ballots during the inspection.

Whenever a ballot is rejected, the returning officer must clearly mark it with the letter “R” and must note the specific ground for rejection in an abbreviated form. This marking can be done by hand or by using a rubber stamp, and it serves as an official record that the paper was examined and found unsuitable for counting. All rejected ballots that come from the same ballot box must be placed together in a separate bundle, keeping them distinct from the valid ballots that will be counted.

The rule further explains that every ballot paper that is not rejected under these provisions shall be counted as one valid vote. However, there is an important proviso: no cover, meaning no bag, sack, or other container that holds the ballot papers, shall be opened unless the counting process requires it, and no ballot paper shall be counted unless it has been properly extracted from such a cover in accordance with the prescribed procedures. This safeguard ensures that the chain of custody for the ballots remains intact and that the counting process respects the secrecy and integrity of each vote.

In sum, these detailed provisions create a rigorous framework for the handling of ballot boxes and the counting of ballot papers at notified polling stations. By requiring simultaneous opening, careful inspection of seals and symbols, clear procedures for resolving disputes over box allocation, and strict criteria for rejecting invalid ballots, the legislation seeks to protect the fundamental democratic principle that each citizen’s vote is counted accurately, securely, and without undue influence. The involvement of multiple officials—the returning officer, the counting agents, and, when necessary, the Election Commission—provides multiple layers of oversight, thereby reinforcing public confidence in the electoral process.

In the final stage of the electoral count, once every ballot paper that has been placed in each of the ballot boxes at a particular polling station has been examined and its tally recorded, a series of formal procedures must be carried out in strict accordance with the statutory requirements. The individual who has been appointed as the counting supervisor – the person tasked with overseeing the physical act of counting the votes at the station – is required to complete the second part of the official counting form, known as Part II, which records the result of the counting. This entry must be filled out with the exact numbers of valid and rejected ballots, and the counting supervisor must then sign that part of the form to attest that the figures are correct. Immediately after the supervisor’s signature, the returning officer – the senior election official who is responsible for the overall conduct of the election in the constituency – must also sign the same Part II, thereby confirming that the supervisor’s tally has been accepted and that the result is officially endorsed. In addition to this dual signature on the result form, the returning officer must transcribe the same figures onto a separate official result sheet, identified as Form 20, and must publicly announce the particulars of the count, thereby providing transparency to the electors, candidates and any observing parties.

The handling of the ballot papers after the count is completed is governed by a detailed sealing procedure designed to preserve the integrity of the paper evidence. All ballot papers that have been judged to be valid – that is, those that meet the legal definition of a proper vote – are to be gathered together in a single bundle. If any ballots have been identified as rejected because they fail to meet the prescribed standards, those rejected papers are also placed in the same bundle, so that each ballot box yields one combined packet containing both the valid and the rejected papers that originated from that box. This packet must then be sealed in a manner that provides a tamper‑evident closure: the seal is affixed by the returning officer and, if any of the candidates, their election agents or the agents appointed to assist in the counting wish to add their own seal for additional assurance, they may do so. On the outside of each sealed packet a record must be made that includes four essential pieces of information. First, the name of the constituency – the larger electoral district in which the polling station is located – must be noted. Second, the specific polling station where the ballot box was used must be identified, thereby linking the packet to its place of origin. Third, the name of the candidate to whom that particular ballot box was allotted – because in many electoral systems each candidate is assigned a distinct box at a polling station – must be recorded. Fourth, the date on which the counting took place must be entered, providing a chronological reference for any future audit or inquiry.

After each individual packet has been sealed and labelled, the returning officer proceeds to gather all the packets that relate to the same candidate into a larger container. This container, which may be a secure box or a similar storage unit, is also sealed with the signature seal of the returning officer and any additional seals that the candidate, the candidate’s election agent, or the candidate’s counting agents elect to attach. The purpose of this second level of sealing is to create a chain of custody that extends from the individual ballot box to the final aggregation of all ballots associated with a particular candidate across the entire constituency. On the exterior of this larger sealed container, three further items of information must be recorded. The first is again the name of the constituency, ensuring that the container can be identified even if it is moved from one location to another. The second is the list of all candidates whose ballots are included within the container, thereby providing a clear inventory of whose votes are being held together. The third is the date of the counting, which again supplies a temporal marker for the entire process.

In circumstances where the Election Commission – the independent body charged with administering elections – becomes aware of credible threats of intimidation or victimisation of voters in a particular constituency, it has the authority to intervene in the normal counting procedure in order to protect the fairness of the election. If the Commission judges that it is absolutely necessary to prevent any undue influence on the outcome, it may issue a formal notification in the Official Gazette – the government’s official public journal – specifying the constituency that is to be subject to special counting rules. This notification triggers the application of a distinct set of procedural rules that replace the ordinary rules normally set out in sections 55, 56, 57 and 59 of the election regulations. The replacement rules are designed to ensure that all ballot papers removed from the various ballot boxes used throughout the affected constituency are mixed together before any counting takes place, thereby eliminating any possibility that the location of a particular box could be used to infer the voting patterns of a specific group of voters.

Under the first of these special provisions, known as rule 55B, the returning officer is required to open, either personally or by delegating the task, all of the ballot boxes that were used at more than one polling station at the same time, rather than opening them one by one in the usual sequential manner. The total number of ballot papers found in each box must be counted and the figures recorded in Part II of Form 16, which is the same result form used in the ordinary counting process. In addition, if there is any mismatch – a discrepancy – between the total number of ballots recorded in this part of the form and the total number of ballots that had previously been listed in item number five of Part I (the part of the form that records the number of ballots issued to the station), that difference must also be noted in Part II. This double‑checking mechanism is intended to highlight any irregularities that might indicate missing or extra ballots. Before any ballot box is opened on the counting table, the counting agents – individuals appointed by the candidates to observe the counting – must be allowed to examine the paper seal or any other type of seal that was placed on the box, to verify that the seal has not been broken or altered. The returning officer must be satisfied that none of the seals show signs of tampering, which would suggest that the contents of the box might have been interfered with. Should the returning officer determine, after a careful inspection, that a particular box has indeed been tampered with, the law obliges him not to count the ballots contained in that compromised box. Instead, the returning officer must follow the special procedure set out in section 58 of the electoral regulations, which deals with the handling of ballots from a polling station where a breach of security has been identified.

The subsequent rule, identified as 56B, governs the actual counting of the votes once the ballots from all the boxes have been mixed together. Subject to any general or special directions that the Election Commission may issue – directions that might further clarify the process or adapt it to local circumstances – the mixed ballots are to be arranged into convenient bundles, which facilitates systematic scrutiny. The returning officer, acting as the chief officer responsible for the count, must then examine each ballot paper to determine whether it is to be accepted as a valid vote or rejected. The law provides a detailed list of the circumstances under which a ballot paper must be rejected. First, any ballot that bears a mark or any form of writing that could enable the identification of the individual voter – for example, a name, a signature, or any unique personal characteristic – must be set aside, because such identification would violate the principle of secret voting. Second, a ballot that either contains no marking at all, or contains a marking that is placed in a location other than on or immediately adjacent to the symbol of one of the candidates printed on the front of the ballot paper, must be rejected. This also includes any ballot on which the marking has been made with an instrument other than the approved ballot pen supplied for the purpose, because the use of an unauthorized instrument could affect the legibility or authenticity of the vote. Third, if a ballot shows votes for more than one candidate – a situation sometimes called an over‑vote – it must be rejected, as the voter has not expressed a clear, singular choice. Fourth, any ballot on which the mark indicating the vote is placed in a manner that makes it uncertain which candidate the voter intended to support – for example, a stray tick that falls between two symbols – must also be rejected, because the ambiguity undermines the ability to determine the voter’s intention. Fifth, a ballot that is determined to be spurious – that is, a counterfeit or otherwise forged ballot that was not legitimately issued by the election authorities – must be rejected to prevent fraud. Finally, any ballot that is so badly damaged, torn, or mutilated that it cannot be positively identified as an official ballot paper must be set aside, because its condition precludes a reliable assessment of its validity. By applying these rejection criteria rigorously, the returning officer ensures that only those ballots that meet the strict standards of a valid, secret, and unambiguous expression of voter intent are counted, thereby upholding the integrity of the electoral result.

The rules governing the handling of ballot papers at the conclusion of an election set out a series of detailed requirements that must be observed by the returning officer, the official appointed to oversee the counting process, as well as by the presiding officers and polling officers who supervise voting at each polling station. In particular, a ballot paper may be refused acceptance if it exhibits certain defects that are enumerated in the regulations. One such defect arises when a ballot paper bears a serial number, or a design, that differs from the serial numbers or the prescribed design that have been authorized for use at the specific polling station. The serial number is a unique identifying mark printed on each ballot paper to prevent duplication and to enable the tracking of each paper through the counting process, while the design refers to the overall layout, including the placement of candidate names, party symbols, and any security features that have been approved for that election. If a ballot paper is found to carry a number that does not match the official series, or if its visual format deviates from the standardized pattern, it is considered defective under clause (g). Another category of defect is described in clause (h), which deals with the absence of mandatory markings and signatures that a ballot paper should display according to the provisions of sub‑rule (1) of rule 38. Sub‑rule (1) of rule 38 ordinarily requires that each ballot paper carry a specific official mark—often a stamp or emblem of the electoral authority—and the signature of the presiding officer who supervised the voting at that station, thereby authenticating the paper. If either of these elements is missing, the ballot paper falls within the defect described in clause (h).

Nevertheless, the legislation recognizes that some defects may not be the fault of the voter but rather the result of an error or omission on the part of a presiding officer or a polling officer. In such circumstances, the returning officer is required to exercise discretion and may decide not to reject the ballot paper solely because of the defect identified in clause (g) or clause (h). This provision serves to protect the voter’s intention when the mistake originates from election officials rather than from the voter themselves. Moreover, the rules provide an additional safeguard concerning the clarity of the voter’s mark. Even if the indication of the chosen candidate is faint, smudged, or appears more than once on the ballot paper, the paper must not be rejected if the intended candidate can be unmistakably identified from the way the paper has been marked. This acknowledges that voters may sometimes press too lightly with a pen or may inadvertently make an extra mark, and it ensures that their vote is counted provided the choice is clear.

Before the returning officer may reject any ballot paper on the basis of the defects described, the officer must first give each counting agent who is present a reasonable opportunity to examine the paper. Counting agents are representatives appointed by the candidates or their agents to observe the counting process and to verify that it is conducted fairly. The returning officer may allow these agents to look at the ballot paper closely, but the agents are expressly forbidden from handling the paper or any other ballot papers. This restriction is intended to preserve the integrity of the ballot papers and to prevent any accidental damage or tampering while the agents are reviewing the evidence of a defect. After the inspection, if the returning officer decides that the ballot paper must be rejected, the officer must mark the paper in a prescribed manner. The officer is required to write or stamp the word “Rejected” on the paper, along with a brief statement of the reason for rejection, using either his own handwriting or a rubber stamp that bears the same information. The officer must also initial this endorsement, thereby providing a clear record of who made the decision and on what grounds. Once marked, all rejected ballot papers are to be collected together in a single bundle, separate from the papers that are deemed valid.

Every ballot paper that does not fall under the conditions for rejection is to be treated as a valid vote and counted accordingly. However, the rule also stipulates that no cover—meaning the envelope or protective wrapper that may have been used to hold the ballot paper during transport—may be opened, and any ballot paper still contained in such a cover must not be counted. This measure prevents the accidental inclusion of ballots that have not been properly processed or verified. After the counting of all the ballot papers that have been placed in the ballot boxes for a particular constituency is completed, the returning officer is obligated to record the results on the official result sheet identified as Form 20A. The returning officer then announces the particulars of the result, which include the number of votes each candidate has received. For the purposes of this rule, the term “constituency” is defined to mean, in the case of a parliamentary election, the assembly constituency that lies within the larger parliamentary constituency. This definition clarifies the geographic scope of the counting and ensures that the results are reported for the correct electoral area.

Following the initial counting, the process of handling the physical ballot papers continues with a step called sealing. The valid ballot papers that belong to each candidate, as well as the bundle of rejected ballot papers, must each be placed in separate packets. These packets are then sealed using the official seal of the returning officer and, if desired, the seals of the candidates, their election agents, or their counting agents. The use of these seals provides a tamper‑evident closure that can be inspected later to verify that the contents have not been altered. On each sealed packet the returning officer must record two essential pieces of information: the name of the constituency and the date on which the counting was conducted. This labeling ensures that the packets can be identified unambiguously at any later stage, such as during a recount or an audit.

The regulations further require that the counting be carried out in a continuous manner as far as practicable. This means that, once counting begins, the returning officer should proceed without unnecessary interruptions, thereby reducing the risk of errors or tampering that might arise during prolonged pauses. If a temporary suspension of counting is unavoidable—for example, because of a break for lunch or a technical problem—the returning officer must keep all ballot papers, packets, and any other election‑related documents securely sealed with his own seal and, where appropriate, the seals of the candidates or their agents. The officer must also take sufficient precautions to ensure the safe custody of these materials during the interval, which might include storing them in a locked room or under the supervision of trustworthy staff.

In the event that a fresh poll must be conducted, as provided for under section 58 of the broader electoral code, the returning officer has a duty to resume the counting of votes after the new poll has been completed. The resumption must occur at a date, time, and place that the returning officer has previously fixed, and notice of this schedule must have been given in advance to all candidates and their election agents. The same procedural rules that apply to the original counting—namely rules 56 and 57—continue to apply to the subsequent counting, ensuring consistency and fairness across both the initial and any additional polls.

After the counting has been finalized, the returning officer records the total number of votes each candidate received on the standard result sheet known as Form 20, and then publicly announces these totals. Once this announcement has been made, any candidate—or, if the candidate is not present, his election agent or one of his counting agents—may submit a written application to the returning officer requesting a recount, either of the entire ballot or of a specific portion of it. The application must set out the grounds on which the candidate believes a recount is justified, such as alleged irregularities, procedural errors, or doubts about the accuracy of the original count. Upon receiving such an application, the returning officer must consider it and decide whether to permit the recount in whole or in part, or to reject the request entirely. The returning officer is empowered to dismiss an application if it appears frivolous, unreasonable, or without merit, thereby preventing the counting process from being unduly delayed by baseless demands. Any decision made by the returning officer in response to a recount request must be documented in writing and must contain a clear statement of the reasons for the decision, providing transparency and a record that can be reviewed if the matter is later challenged.

If the returning officer decides to allow a recount, the recount must be carried out in accordance with the applicable procedural rules, which may be rule 54A, rule 56, or rule 56A, depending on the specific circumstances of the election. These rules set out the detailed steps for handling the ballot papers during a recount, including how to separate, examine, and re‑tally the votes, as well as how to address any rejected ballots that may need to be reconsidered. After the recount is completed, the returning officer must amend the result sheet, Form 20, to reflect any changes in the vote totals that result from the recount. This ensures that the final, official record accurately captures the outcome of the election after all legitimate challenges have been resolved.

The officer who is designated to oversee the conduct of the election, commonly referred to as the returning officer, is required, after the total number of votes that have been cast for each individual candidate has been publicly announced in accordance with the provisions set out in sub‑rule (1) or, where applicable, sub‑rule (5), to proceed to the final stage of the counting process. At this stage the returning officer must fill out the official record of the poll, known as the result sheet, which is prescribed in the statutory Form 20. The result sheet must be completed in full, signed by the returning officer as an affirmation of its accuracy, and thereafter no further request for a recount of the votes may be accepted. This prohibition on recounts, however, is subject to an important safeguard: the returning officer may not move to finalize the counting until those candidates and their duly appointed election agents who are physically present at the counting venue have been afforded a reasonable opportunity to make use of the right granted to them by sub‑rule (2). In practice this means that each candidate and his or her agent must be given sufficient time to examine the numbers, raise any immediate objections, and verify that the tally reflects the votes as they were announced, before the officer can lawfully close the books on the count.

Once the result sheet has been sealed by the returning officer, the next statutory duty is the formal declaration of the election outcome and the preparation of the return of election. Subject to the conditions laid down in section 65, which may impose additional requirements in certain circumstances, the returning officer must issue a declaration that identifies the candidate who has obtained the greatest number of valid votes – that is, votes that have been cast in accordance with the law and are free from any disqualifying defect. This declaration is recorded on a prescribed form, either Form 21C or Form 21D, depending on the specific type of election and the relevant legal provisions. The returning officer must then dispatch signed copies of this declaration to the appropriate authority, to the Election Commission, and to the chief electoral officer, ensuring that all relevant bodies receive official notice of the result. In addition to the declaration, the officer is required to complete and certify the return of election on Form 21E, which serves as the formal record that the election has been conducted and concluded according to law. Certified copies of the return are likewise sent to the Election Commission and the chief electoral officer, thereby completing the administrative loop that confirms the election’s legality and finality.

When the counting of ballot papers is carried out at more than one location – a situation that can arise in large constituencies or in elections that involve multiple phases – the statutory framework provides detailed guidance on how the process must be coordinated. The general rules governing counting, which are enumerated in rules 53, 54 and 55 through 60 of the election regulations, apply independently at each counting venue. This means that each place where ballot papers are opened, examined, and tallied must follow the same procedural safeguards, such as the verification of the number of ballot papers, the scrutiny of rejected ballots, and the recording of vote totals. However, a subset of the rules – specifically rules 54A, 63 and 64 – are designed to apply only at the final counting location, that is, the last place where counting takes place. These particular rules address matters such as the final reconciliation of totals, the preparation of the ultimate result sheet, and the formal announcement of the outcome, ensuring that the culmination of the counting process is uniform and that any discrepancies identified at earlier sites are resolved before the final declaration is made.

Having declared a candidate as elected, the returning officer must also issue a certificate of election to the successful candidate. This certificate, which is prepared on Form 22, serves as the official proof that the individual has been duly elected to the legislative body, whether that body is the House of the People (the lower house of the national parliament) or a state Legislative Assembly. The officer is required to furnish the certificate as soon as practicable after the declaration, and the elected person must sign an acknowledgment confirming receipt of the certificate. The signed acknowledgment must then be forwarded by registered post – a postal service that provides a receipt and proof of delivery – to the Secretary of the House of the People or, where the election pertains to a state legislature, to the Secretary of the Legislative Assembly. This step creates a formal paper trail that links the election result to the official records of the respective legislative body and ensures that the elected representative’s status is properly documented in the official registers.

In elections where electronic voting machines (EVMs) have been employed instead of traditional paper ballots, a distinct set of procedural rules governs the counting of votes. The general provisions that apply to the counting of paper ballots – those contained in rules 50 through 54 – continue to apply, but they are supplemented by specialized rules that replace the usual procedures for ballot paper scrutiny, counting, and verification. The first of these specialized rules, designated as rule 55C, authorizes the returning officer to bring the control units of the electronic voting machines – the electronic components that store the vote totals for each polling station – together for collective scrutiny and inspection. This means that the votes recorded in several machines can be examined and counted at the same time, streamlining the process while maintaining transparency. Before any votes are tallied from a control unit, any candidate, his election agent, or the counting agent who is present at the counting table must be allowed to inspect the physical security features of the machine, notably the paper seal and any other vital seals that were affixed to the unit when it left the polling station. The purpose of this inspection is to verify that the seals remain intact, thereby providing assurance that the machine has not been opened or altered since the voting took place. The returning officer is also required to make a personal determination that none of the electronic voting machines has been tampered with; this involves a careful assessment of the seal condition, the physical state of the machine, and any other indicators of potential interference.

If, after this inspection, the returning officer concludes that a particular voting machine appears to have been tampered with – for example, if a seal is broken or the machine shows signs of unauthorized access – the officer is mandated to exclude the votes recorded in that machine from the count. In such a circumstance, the officer must follow the procedures set out in section 58, section 58A or section 64A of the election law, depending on which provision is applicable to the specific polling station or stations involved. These sections provide detailed guidance on how to handle votes from a compromised machine, including the possible conduct of a fresh count using paper ballots, the issuance of a fresh election, or other remedial actions designed to preserve the integrity of the electoral outcome.

Assuming that the returning officer is satisfied that a voting machine has not been tampered with, the next step is to count the votes stored within the machine. This is governed by rule 56C, which prescribes a straightforward electronic method for obtaining the vote totals. The returning officer activates the “Result” button on the control unit, a function that causes the machine to display the total number of votes cast as well as the breakdown of votes for each individual candidate on a dedicated display panel. As each candidate’s vote total appears on the screen, the returning officer records the figure in Part II of Form 17C, which is the official record used to capture the vote count for each candidate at that particular counting location. If there is a test vote – a dummy vote that may have been recorded during a trial run of the machine – this test vote is listed in item 5 of Part I of Form 17C and must be subtracted from the total displayed for the candidate concerned, ensuring that only genuine votes are counted. Once the numbers have been entered, Part II of Form 17C is completed in all other respects and is signed both by the counting supervisor – the official responsible for overseeing the counting operation – and by the candidates or their election agents or counting agents who are present at the table. In parallel, the same figures are entered in the result sheet that is prepared on Form 20, and those entries are publicly announced, thereby providing transparent communication of the results to all interested parties. This comprehensive process of electronic vote counting, coupled with rigorous inspection and documentation, is intended to combine the efficiency of modern technology with the safeguards traditionally associated with paper‑based elections, ensuring that the final tally accurately reflects the will of the electorate.

The provisions that deal with the scrutiny of the paper trail generated by an electronic voting system are set out in detail to ensure that any doubts about the accuracy of the vote count can be resolved through a transparent and orderly procedure. When a printer that produces a paper record of each vote – commonly referred to as a “paper trail” – is employed at a polling place, the first step after the electronic tally has been entered on the result sheet is the public announcement of those entries. At that moment, any candidate who wishes to challenge the electronic figures, or, if the candidate is not present, the candidate’s election agent or any of the agents who have been appointed to count the votes, is entitled to submit a written request to the returning officer. This request must specifically ask that the printed slips that have been stored in the drop box of the printer be counted, and it may refer to a single polling station or to several stations, depending on the scope of the concern raised. The requirement that the request be in writing creates a clear documentary trail of who is seeking a recount and why, which helps the election officials to manage the process efficiently.

Once such an application has been received, the returning officer – the official who is responsible for overseeing the conduct of the election in the particular constituency – must consider the request in accordance with any general or special guidelines that may have been issued by the Election Commission, the body that supervises the entire electoral system. The returning officer has discretion to either grant the request in full, grant it in part, or reject it altogether. However, the law requires that the officer only reject a request if it is clearly frivolous, meaning it lacks any serious basis, or unreasonable, meaning it would impose an undue burden on the election process. In exercising this discretion, the officer must balance the need to preserve the integrity of the vote count with the practical considerations of time, resources, and the potential for unnecessary disruption.

Whenever the returning officer reaches a decision under these circumstances, the decision must be recorded in writing and must contain a clear explanation of the reasons for granting or denying the request. This written rationale serves several purposes: it provides transparency to the candidates and the public, it creates a record that can be reviewed by higher authorities if an appeal is lodged, and it reinforces the principle that electoral officials must act on an objective basis rather than on personal preference. The requirement for a written decision also helps to prevent arbitrary or capricious actions, thereby strengthening confidence in the fairness of the electoral process.

If the returning officer decides that the paper slips should be counted, whether in whole or in part, a series of steps must be followed to ensure that the recount is conducted in a manner that is consistent with the standards set by the Election Commission. First, the counting must be carried out using the method that the Commission has directed, which may involve specific procedures for handling the slips, verifying their authenticity, and tabulating the numbers. Second, if the count of the paper slips produces a result that differs from the number of votes displayed on the electronic control unit – the device that stores the electronic tally – the returning officer is required to correct the official result sheet, known as Form 20, to reflect the numbers derived from the paper slips. This amendment must be made in accordance with the precise format prescribed for Form 20, ensuring that the record of the vote count is accurate and reliable. Third, after the amendment has been made, the returning officer must publicly announce the changes so that all interested parties are aware of the revised figures. Finally, the returning officer must complete the result sheet by signing it, thereby certifying that the document now contains the correct, final numbers for the contested polling station(s).

The handling of the electronic voting machines themselves after the counting is concluded is governed by a separate set of rules that focus on sealing and preserving the integrity of the devices. Once the votes recorded in the control unit – the electronic component that stores the tally – have been verified on a candidate‑by‑candidate basis and entered into the appropriate sections of Form 17C and Form 20, the returning officer must seal the unit. This sealing is performed by affixing the official seal of the returning officer, and, if any candidates or their agents wish to do so, their own seals may also be placed on the unit. The sealing must be done in a way that does not erase or otherwise damage the memory of the unit, thereby preserving the electronic record of the vote. If a printer that produces paper slips has been used, the returning officer must also seal the collection of slips in the manner prescribed by the Election Commission, ensuring that the physical evidence of each vote remains intact and tamper‑proof.

After the sealing process is complete, the sealed control unit, together with any sealed paper slips, must be stored in specially prepared boxes that are designed to protect the equipment and maintain a clear chain of custody. The returning officer is required to label each box with a set of specific particulars that together constitute a complete identification of the contents. These particulars include the name of the constituency in which the unit was used, the details of the polling station or stations where the unit was deployed, the serial number assigned to the control unit and, where applicable, the serial number of the printer, the date on which the poll was held, and the date on which the counting took place. By recording these details, the election administration creates a comprehensive inventory that can be consulted in the future, for example, if an audit or a legal challenge requires examination of the sealed equipment.

In addition to the specific provisions concerning voting machines, the broader legal framework includes a series of rules, numbered 60 through 66, that were originally drafted for traditional ballot‑paper voting but are expressly made applicable to voting by electronic machines as far as practicable. Wherever those rules refer to a “ballot paper,” the term is to be interpreted as also encompassing any vote recorded by an electronic voting machine, thereby extending the same safeguards and procedural requirements to the newer technology. Moreover, any rule that mentions another rule in the earlier part of the election law is to be read as referring to the corresponding rule in Chapter II of Part IV, or, where relevant, to the specific rules dealing with electronic voting, namely rules 55C, 56C, and 57C. This cross‑referencing ensures that the legal regime remains coherent and that the same standards of fairness, transparency, and accountability apply regardless of the medium used to capture the vote.

The portion of the legislation that deals with elections involving assembly members and council constituencies introduces a few additional concepts that are important for understanding how these particular types of elections are conducted. The term “authorized agent,” as used in the relevant rules, refers to a person who has been officially appointed by a political party to act on its behalf in a council constituency election. The appointment is made under a specific sub‑rule that applies to council elections, and the authorized agent may also be appointed to act on behalf of the party in elections conducted by assembly members, provided that the election does not involve a postal ballot. The word “election,” in this context, is defined narrowly to mean either an election of assembly members or an election held in a council constituency, thereby excluding other types of elections from the application of these particular provisions.

The Election Commission retains the power to direct that a postal ballot be used as the method of voting in certain circumstances. By publishing a notification in the Official Gazette – the government’s official journal – at any time before the final deadline for candidates to withdraw their nominations, the Commission can specify that a postal ballot must be employed either for an entire election that is being contested by assembly members or for all or part of a council constituency. This authority allows the Commission to adapt the voting process to logistical challenges, security concerns, or public health considerations that might make in‑person voting impractical.

When an election by assembly members proceeds to the polling stage, the returning officer is obligated to inform each registered elector of the essential details of the poll as soon as practicable after the deadline for candidate withdrawals has passed. The notice must convey the date, the time, and the location where the polling will take place, thereby ensuring that voters have sufficient information to exercise their franchise. This requirement reinforces the principle of fair notice and helps to prevent confusion or disenfranchisement.

Finally, the rules that govern the conduct of the poll for elections by assembly members and for council constituencies are largely drawn from the existing set of regulations that cover ordinary ballot‑paper voting, specifically rules 28 through 35 and rules 36 through 48. These rules apply automatically to any election by assembly members for which the Election Commission has not issued a specific direction under the clause that permits the use of postal ballots, and they also apply to any council‑constituency election unless a postal‑ballot direction covers the whole constituency. However, the application of those rules is subject to certain modifications that are designed to accommodate the particular features of elections involving assembly members or council constituencies. These modifications ensure that while the fundamental principles of the electoral process remain consistent, the procedures can be tailored to the specific context of each type of election, thereby preserving the integrity of the vote while allowing for flexibility in administration.

The provision identified as clause (a) of sub‑rule (l) of rule thirty‑one is expressly excluded when the election in question is one that is conducted by members of a legislative assembly. In other words, the special condition that would normally be applied to the situation described in clause (a) of sub‑rule (l) does not operate in the context of an assembly‑member election; the ordinary rules for that type of election continue to apply without the modification that clause (a) would otherwise impose.

In place of the earlier set of rules numbered thirty‑seven through forty, a new series of regulations is introduced, beginning with rule thirty‑seven‑A which governs the method of voting. This rule states that each individual who is entitled to vote, known as an elector, is permitted to cast only a single vote in any given election regardless of how many seats are to be filled in that election. The intention behind this limitation is to preserve the principle of one‑person‑one‑vote, ensuring that each elector’s influence on the outcome is equal and that no elector can exercise a greater voting power simply because more than one position is being contested.

A further provision clarifies that the rules set out in sub‑rules one, two and four of rule thirty‑seven, which originally dealt with electors in parliamentary and state‑assembly constituencies, are to be applied in exactly the same way to electors who belong to the special graduate constituencies and teachers’ constituencies. Graduate constituencies consist of voters who have attained a university degree, while teachers’ constituencies comprise individuals employed in the teaching profession; both categories have their own separate electoral rolls and are represented in certain legislative bodies. By extending the same procedural requirements to these groups, the regulation ensures uniformity and fairness across all types of constituencies.

The second part of rule thirty‑seven‑A explains the mechanics of how an elector is to indicate his or her choice on the ballot paper. When an elector wishes to vote for a particular candidate as his or her first preference, the elector must write the numeral one—1—in the designated space opposite the name of that candidate. This numeral serves as a clear, unambiguous signal that the candidate is the elector’s top choice. In addition to this first‑preference mark, the elector may also express secondary preferences by writing additional numerals in the spaces next to the names of other candidates. The elector can place the numeral two—2—next to the candidate who is the second choice, and may continue this sequence by writing the numerals three—3—four—4—and so on, in the order of decreasing preference. By doing so, the elector provides a ranked list of choices that can be used in a preferential voting system, where later preferences may be taken into account if the first‑preference candidate does not achieve the required number of votes to be elected.

An explanatory note attached to this sub‑rule clarifies that the numerals used for marking preferences may be written either in the internationally recognised Arabic numeral form, in Roman numeral form, or in any of the numeral systems employed by the various Indian languages, provided that the elector does not write out the numbers in words. This flexibility accommodates the multilingual nature of the Indian electorate, allowing voters to use the numeral style most familiar to them while preserving a standardized visual format that can be easily read and counted by election officials.

Rule thirty‑eight‑A deals with the issuance of ballot papers to electors and sets out a series of safeguards designed to prevent fraud and to ensure that each ballot can be traced to the correct voter without compromising the secrecy of the vote. Before a ballot paper is handed over to an elector, the paper and the attached counterfoil—a small detachable portion used for record‑keeping—must each be stamped on the back with a distinguishing mark that is prescribed by the Election Commission of India. This stamp may include a unique identifier, such as a serial number or a specific colour, that helps election officials verify that the ballot paper is authentic and has not been tampered with. In addition, the presiding officer, who is the official in charge of conducting the poll at a particular polling station, must sign his or her full name on the back of each ballot paper. The presiding officer’s signature provides an additional layer of accountability, confirming that the ballot was issued under official supervision.

When the polling officer actually hands the ballot paper to the elector, a series of procedural steps must be followed. First, the officer records the elector’s entry number from the electoral roll—a comprehensive list of all registered voters—on the counterfoil. This entry number, also known as the roll number, uniquely identifies the elector within the constituency. Second, the elector is required to provide a signature or, if the elector is unable to write, a thumb impression on the same counterfoil. This act of signing or leaving a thumb print serves as the elector’s personal acknowledgment that he or she has received the ballot paper, creating a direct link between the ballot and the individual voter while still preserving the confidentiality of the vote itself.

The regulation also mandates that the polling officer mark the elector’s name in the marked copy of the electoral roll, which is the working version of the roll used on the day of the election. By placing a mark—often a cross or a tick—next to the elector’s name, the officer indicates that a ballot paper has been issued to that particular voter. In addition to this mark, the officer must record the serial number of the specific ballot paper that was handed out, but this requirement varies depending on the type of election. For elections to fill seats in the Council of States, which is the upper house of the national Parliament also known as the Rajya Sabha, the serial number of the ballot paper must be entered in the marked copy of the electoral roll. This enables a precise audit trail for each ballot in a national election. Conversely, for elections to fill seats in a State Legislative Council, which is the upper house of a state legislature, the serial number of the ballot paper is not recorded in the electoral roll, reflecting a different procedural approach for state‑level elections.

A further safeguard is stipulated to ensure that a ballot paper cannot be taken away from the polling station without the elector’s prior acknowledgement. No ballot paper may be delivered to an elector unless the elector has already affixed his or her signature or thumb impression on the counterfoil attached to that ballot paper. This requirement prevents the possibility that a ballot could be issued to an individual who then refuses to acknowledge receipt, thereby reducing the risk of unaccounted‑for ballots that could potentially be misused or lost.

Taken together, these provisions replace the earlier rules thirty‑seven through forty and establish a comprehensive framework for conducting elections that involve multiple seats, diverse constituencies, and a preferential voting system. By mandating a single vote per elector, allowing ranked preferences, standardising the numeral system used on ballots, and instituting strict procedures for the issuance and tracking of ballot papers, the regulations aim to uphold the integrity, transparency, and fairness of the electoral process. The involvement of the Election Commission, the presiding officers, and the polling officers at each step reflects a layered system of checks and balances designed to minimise electoral fraud, ensure accurate vote counting, and preserve the democratic principle that every eligible citizen’s voice is counted equally and securely.

In the conduct of elections, the law sets out a series of detailed procedural safeguards that are intended to preserve the integrity of the voting process while also protecting the secrecy of the individual’s choice. One of the provisions that illustrates the balance between verification and privacy concerns is found in sub‑rule (3) of rule 2. This clause makes clear that, despite any contrary direction that might be contained in sub‑rule (2) of the same rule, it is not required for any presiding officer, polling officer, or any other election official to obtain a thumb impression on the counterfoil as a form of authentication. A thumb impression, which is a fingerprint taken on the back side of a voting slip, is sometimes used as a means of confirming the identity of the person who has been handed a ballot. However, the present regulation expressly relieves officers from that duty, thereby simplifying the process of handing out ballot papers and avoiding the need for the additional step of fingerprinting, which could be time‑consuming and could raise concerns about the handling of biometric data.

Further, sub‑rule (4) of the same provision addresses the handling of serial numbers on ballot papers. It states that, except where rule 39AA applies, no individual present in the polling station may record the serial numbers of the ballot papers that are issued to particular electors. Serial numbers are unique identifiers printed on each ballot paper, and they could theoretically be used to trace a specific paper back to a specific voter. By prohibiting the notation of these numbers in the polling place, the regulation seeks to prevent any possibility of linking a ballot to the person who cast it, thereby reinforcing the principle of a secret ballot. The phrase “subject to rule 39AA” signals that there is a narrowly tailored exception that will be described later, allowing a limited form of verification under strict conditions.

The next clause, sub‑rule (5), deals with the physical concealment of the serial number on the ballot paper before it reaches the voter. It mandates that, before any ballot paper is handed to an elector who is participating in an election to fill a seat or seats in a State Legislative Council—an upper house of the state legislature elected by the members of the legislative assembly—or before a ballot is given to a voter in a local authority constituency, the unique serial number must be hidden in a manner prescribed by the Election Commission. The Election Commission, which is the independent body charged with administering elections, may specify a particular technique—such as covering the number with an opaque strip, using a special printing method, or employing a folding method—to ensure that the number cannot be seen by the voter. This concealment is intended to guarantee that the voter cannot inadvertently reveal the identity of the ballot, which would undermine the secrecy of the vote.

Rule 39A, titled “Maintenance of secrecy of voting by electors within polling station and voting procedure,” sets out a comprehensive set of steps that every elector must follow once a ballot paper has been issued. The rule begins by affirming that any elector who receives a ballot paper under rule 38A or any other provision must keep the act of voting secret while inside the polling station, and to achieve that, the elector must observe the detailed voting procedure that follows. The first part of this procedure requires the elector, upon receiving the ballot, to proceed immediately to one of the voting compartments. These compartments are small, private booths designed to shield the voter from view, thereby ensuring that no one can observe how the ballot is marked. Within the compartment, the elector must then record his or her vote using the article supplied for the purpose, which may be a pen, a pencil, or any other writing instrument approved by the election officials. The rule explicitly references sub‑rule (2) of rule 37A, which defines the precise manner in which a vote must be marked on the ballot, such as striking a box, drawing a cross, or filling in a circle, depending on the type of election.

After the vote has been recorded, the elector is required to fold the ballot paper in such a way that the chosen candidate or option is concealed. This folding is a crucial step because it prevents any onlooker from seeing the marked choice when the ballot is later deposited. In some cases, the elector may be asked to show a distinguishing mark on the ballot paper to the presiding officer; this mark, often a stamp or a printed code, serves to confirm that the ballot is genuine and has not been tampered with. Once the ballot has been folded and, if necessary, the distinguishing mark has been displayed, the voter inserts the folded paper into the ballot box—a sealed container that collects all votes cast at the polling station. Finally, the elector must leave the polling station, thereby completing the act of voting.

The rule also imposes a requirement that each elector should vote without undue delay. This clause is intended to keep the line of voters moving efficiently while still allowing sufficient time for each voter to complete the steps carefully. It also states that no two voters may occupy the same voting compartment at the same time, a measure that prevents any possibility of collusion or intimidation within the private space of the booth. If an elector, after being warned by the presiding officer, refuses to comply with the prescribed voting procedure—such as by refusing to fold the ballot, refusing to show the distinguishing mark when required, or refusing to place the ballot into the box—the presiding officer is empowered to retrieve the ballot paper from that voter. This retrieval may happen whether or not the voter has already marked a choice on the ballot. Once the ballot has been taken back, the presiding officer must write on the reverse side of the paper the words “Cancelled: voting procedure violated” and sign his or her name beneath that notation. All such cancelled ballots are then placed in a separate cover that is clearly labeled “Ballot papers: voting procedure violated,” thereby keeping them apart from the regular ballots that will be counted. The rule makes clear that, in addition to any other legal penalty that may be imposed on the elector for failing to follow the voting procedure, any vote that might have been recorded on a cancelled ballot will not be counted, ensuring that only properly cast votes contribute to the final result.

Rule 39AA creates a narrowly defined exception to the absolute secrecy mandated by rule 39A. It specifies that, notwithstanding the general prohibition on revealing how a voter has voted, the presiding officer must, during the interval between the moment when a member of a political party records his vote on the ballot and the moment before he places the ballot into the ballot box, allow an authorized agent of that political party to verify the choice that the elector has made. This provision is intended to give political parties a limited ability to monitor the loyalty of their members in elections where party members are required to vote for a particular candidate, such as in indirect elections to a legislative council where members of the legislative assembly elect council members. However, the rule also imposes a safeguard: if the elector refuses to show the marked ballot paper to the party’s authorized agent, the presiding officer must immediately retrieve the ballot paper, just as he would under sub‑rule 5 of rule 39A. The retrieved ballot is then processed in the same manner as a ballot cancelled for a procedural violation—its reverse side is marked “Cancelled: voting procedure violated,” it is signed, and it is placed in the separate cover for cancelled ballots, thereby ensuring that the refusal to comply results in the vote being discarded.

In order to make the verification process possible, the rule obliges each political party that has a member casting a vote at a polling station to appoint two authorized agents in Form 22A. This form is a standard document used for declaring the names and credentials of persons who are permitted to act on behalf of the party within the polling place. The requirement for two agents is a practical measure to ensure continuous presence; one agent must remain inside the polling station throughout the entire voting period, while the other stands ready to replace him or her whenever the first agent needs to leave for any reason. This rotation guarantees that there is always a party representative available to observe the verification step, thus preventing any lapse in oversight.

Finally, the provision labeled “40A” is referenced but its substantive content is not reproduced in the excerpt. In the broader context of electoral law, sections numbered in the 40 series typically deal with matters such as the handling of ballot boxes, the counting of votes, the resolution of disputes, or the certification of results. Although the specific details of rule 40A are not given here, it is reasonable to infer that it would complement the preceding rules by providing further procedural guidance for the conduct of elections, perhaps addressing the manner in which ballot boxes are sealed, transported, and opened, or establishing the timeline for the declaration of results.

Taken together, these rules illustrate a careful design intended to protect the anonymity of the voter while still accommodating the legitimate interests of political parties in certain types of elections. The prohibition on recording thumb impressions and serial numbers inside the polling station minimizes the risk that a ballot could be linked to a specific voter, thereby upholding the principle of a secret ballot that is fundamental to democratic practice. At the same time, the provision for party‑authorized agents to verify a member’s vote, coupled with the strict penalties for refusing to comply, reflects the balancing act between secrecy and accountability in contexts where party discipline is a crucial element of the electoral system. By mandating a clear sequence of actions—moving to a private voting compartment, marking the ballot according to a prescribed method, folding the ballot to hide the vote, showing any required distinguishing marks, inserting the ballot into the box, and leaving the polling station—the legislation seeks to make the voting process both transparent to officials and secure for voters. The mechanisms for cancelling improperly handled ballots, recording the cancellation, and separating them from valid votes ensure that any breach of procedure is documented and does not affect the final tally, thereby preserving the integrity of the election outcome.

When an individual goes to a polling place and is unable to read the ballot paper or to mark his or her choice on it in the manner prescribed by rule thirty‑seven A because of illiteracy, blindness or any other physical infirmity, the presiding officer at that station has a specific duty to ensure that the elector’s right to vote is protected without compromising the secrecy of the ballot. The presiding officer must first be satisfied, after a reasonable inquiry, that the elector truly cannot read the paper or cannot record a vote because of the stated conditions. Once that satisfaction has been obtained, the officer is required to allow the elector to be accompanied by another person, referred to as a companion, who must be at least eighteen years of age. The companion must be capable of reading the ballot paper and must be able to mark the vote in exactly the way the elector wishes. In practice this means that the companion will read the list of candidates, listen to the elector’s instructions about which numbers to assign to which candidates, and then write those numbers on the ballot paper in the proper boxes. If the elector also needs assistance in folding the ballot paper so that the vote remains concealed, the companion may perform that task as well, after which the ballot is placed into the ballot box in the usual manner. The law imposes two safeguards on the use of companions. First, no single individual may act as a companion for more than one elector at the same polling station on the same day; this restriction prevents any one person from having undue influence over multiple voters. Second, before a person is allowed to act as a companion, that person must make a formal declaration that he or she will keep the content of the vote secret and that he or she has not already served as a companion for any other elector at any polling station on that day. In the special circumstance of an election in which the electors themselves are assembly members, an additional proviso applies: a companion who is also an elector in that same election may not serve as a companion, thereby avoiding any conflict of interest that could arise from a fellow member influencing the vote of another. All instances in which the presiding officer authorizes a companion under this rule must be entered into a statutory register known as Form fourteen A. The form serves as an official record of each case, noting the identity of the elector, the companion, and the circumstances that gave rise to the assistance. Moreover, if the companion wishes to be sure that he or she is applying the rules correctly, the presiding officer is obligated, upon request, to explain in detail the instructions for recording votes. This explanation includes a step‑by‑step description of how to read the ballot, how to assign the numerical preferences, how to fold the paper, and how to insert it into the ballot box, ensuring that the companion can faithfully act on behalf of the elector while preserving the secrecy and integrity of the vote.

After the polling stations have closed, the handling of the ballot boxes must follow a strict protocol designed to protect the ballots from tampering and to provide transparency to any observers. As soon as practicable after the close of the poll, the presiding officer must, in the presence of any polling agents who may be present, either close the slit through which ballot papers are inserted into each ballot box or, in the case of a box that does not have a mechanical closing device, seal the slit manually and then secure the entire box. This sealing operation is intended to show that no further ballots can be added after the official end of voting, thereby safeguarding the final tally. However, the rule recognises that in some circumstances the counting of votes may begin immediately after the poll closes; if the counting is to start without any delay, the presiding officer is not required to seal the slit or secure the box, because the ballots will be under direct supervision as they are transferred for counting.

The regulations concerning the contents of a ballot box also contain a specific amendment to rule forty‑six, sub‑rule one. In that amendment, the previous clauses labelled (b) and (c) are replaced with new provisions. The first of the new clauses, now designated (b), refers to ballot papers that have been signed in full by the presiding officer under sub‑rule one of rule thirty‑eight A but that have not been handed out to voters. These papers are essentially spare or reserve ballots that remain in the custody of the presiding officer and may be used later if required, for example in the event of a damaged ballot. The second new clause, now labelled (c), concerns ballot papers that have been cancelled because a voter violated the prescribed voting procedure as set out in rule thirty‑nine A. Such cancelled ballots are removed from the pool of valid votes and are kept separately to demonstrate that an irregularity occurred, thereby providing a record for any subsequent review or audit.

The section of the electoral code that deals with the counting of votes in elections where assembly members vote, or in council constituencies, begins with a series of precise definitions that are essential for understanding the counting process. The term “continuing candidate” is used to describe any candidate who, at any particular moment during the count, has neither been elected nor excluded from further consideration. In other words, a continuing candidate remains in the race and may still receive transferred votes. The word “count” has a broader meaning that encompasses several distinct stages. First, it includes all the operations involved in tallying the first‑preference votes that each candidate receives directly from voters. Second, it covers the procedures required to transfer the surplus votes of a candidate who has already reached the quota needed for election; this surplus represents the number of votes that exceed the threshold and must be redistributed according to voter preferences. Third, the count also involves the redistribution of all the votes belonging to a candidate who has been excluded, meaning that the candidate has been eliminated because they cannot possibly reach the quota, and their votes must be transferred to the next preferred candidates indicated on each ballot.

A ballot on which no further preference can be recorded for any candidate who is still continuing is called an “exhausted paper.” Such a paper is considered exhausted when the voter has either not indicated any further preferences beyond those already accounted for, or when the ballot contains a situation that renders it unusable for further transfers. The law specifies two particular conditions that cause a ballot to become exhausted. The first condition occurs when the ballot shows the same numerical figure next to the names of two or more candidates, and those candidates are the next in order of preference; this duplication makes it impossible to determine which candidate should receive the vote. The second condition arises when the next preferred candidate is marked with a number that does not follow consecutively after the previous number, or when more than one number is placed beside the same candidate, thereby breaking the required sequential order of preferences.

The concept of “first preference” is straightforward: it is the numeral one placed opposite the name of the candidate that the voter ranks as their top choice. Similarly, a “second preference” is denoted by the numeral two placed opposite the name of the candidate the voter ranks second, and a “third preference” by the numeral three, and so on for lower preferences. The term “original vote,” when applied to a particular candidate, refers to a vote that originates from a ballot on which that candidate is marked with the first‑preference numeral one. This is the initial allocation of the vote before any transfers occur. The word “surplus” describes the amount by which a candidate’s total vote value—both the original votes and any votes transferred to them from other candidates—exceeds the quota required for election. For example, if the quota is one hundred and a candidate ends up with one hundred and twenty votes, the surplus is twenty votes, which must then be redistributed.

A “transferred vote” in relation to a candidate is any portion of a ballot’s value that is credited to that candidate as a result of the voter’s subsequent preferences. When a ballot lists a second, third, or lower preference for a candidate, and that ballot is transferred during the counting process, the value of that transferred portion is added to the candidate’s total. Finally, an “unexhausted paper” is a ballot on which there remains at least one further preference for a candidate who is still continuing in the count. Such a paper is still active and can be used to allocate additional votes as the counting proceeds, ensuring that every possible voter intention is taken into account until no further transfers are possible.

The law sets out that the detailed procedures contained in rules numbered fifty‑one through fifty‑four are to be applied in exactly the same way when the counting of votes is carried out by members of an assembly or within a council constituency as they are when the counting is performed in a parliamentary constituency or in any other assembly constituency. In other words, the same standards of scrutiny, the same methods for allocating values to votes and the same calculations for determining the number of votes required for a candidate to be elected must be observed regardless of whether the election is for a national parliament, a state assembly, a local council or a body of elected assembly members. This provision ensures uniformity across the different levels of government, preventing any disparity that might arise from using divergent counting rules in different types of elections.

When the time arrives to open the ballot boxes and to examine the packets that contain postal ballot papers, the officer who has been appointed to supervise the election – the returning officer – is required to follow a precise sequence of actions. First, if any postal ballots have been received in sealed envelopes, the returning officer must deal with those envelopes in accordance with the specific sub‑rules that are set out in rule fifty‑four‑A, sub‑rules two through seven. Those sub‑rules describe how the envelopes are to be opened, how the papers are to be checked for authenticity, and how any irregularities are to be recorded. After the envelopes have been processed, the returning officer proceeds to open the physical ballot boxes that were used at the polling stations. From each box the officer removes every ballot paper that has been placed inside, counts the total number of papers that have been taken out, and records that number in a written statement that becomes part of the official election record. The next step is a detailed scrutiny, which means that each ballot paper taken from the boxes, as well as each postal ballot that has been extracted from its envelope, is examined individually to determine whether it complies with the legal requirements for a valid vote. During this scrutiny the returning officer separates those papers that satisfy all the conditions from those that are defective. For each paper that is deemed defective, the officer marks the word “Rejected” on the ballot and also writes a brief note indicating the specific reason for the rejection, such as an incorrect marking or an identifying mark that could reveal the voter’s identity. This systematic approach guarantees that every ballot is treated fairly and that any reasons for invalidity are transparently documented.

The law further defines the circumstances under which a ballot paper must be considered invalid. Subject to the provisions of rule thirty‑eight‑A, which may be applied to elections in council constituencies and also to votes cast by assembly members who are not using postal ballots, a ballot is invalid if the voter fails to place the numeral one in the box that indicates their first preference, or if the numeral one is placed next to more than one candidate’s name, thereby creating ambiguity about which candidate is intended to receive the first preference. A ballot is also invalid if the numeral one appears alongside another numeral next to the same candidate, suggesting that the voter tried to give that candidate more than one preference, which the voting system does not permit. Moreover, any ballot that contains a personal mark, a signature, a distinctive doodle or any other writing that could enable election officials to identify the voter is rejected, because the secrecy of the ballot is a fundamental principle of democratic elections. In addition, the ballot must be marked with the prescribed numeral using the specific article – that is, the official voting token – that has been supplied for the purpose; any other kind of marking, such as using a pen to draw a number, is not acceptable. However, this particular requirement does not apply to postal ballots, which are handled under a slightly different set of rules. The legislation also provides a safeguard: if the returning officer is convinced that a defect in a ballot arose through the error or omission of a presiding officer at a polling station or of a polling officer, the ballot must not be rejected solely on the basis of that defect. This clause protects voters from being disenfranchised because of mistakes made by election officials. An explanatory note clarifies that the numeral “1” that indicates the first preference may be written in any of the accepted numeral systems, including the international Arabic numerals, the Roman numerals, or the numeral forms used in any of the many Indian languages, but the numeral must not be expressed in words such as “one” or “uno”. This ensures that the marking is clear, unambiguous, and uniformly understood by the counting staff.

Once the returning officer has finished rejecting all ballots that are found to be invalid, the remaining valid ballots must be organized for further counting. The officer is required to group the valid ballots into separate parcels, each parcel corresponding to the first‑preference candidate indicated on the ballots it contains. In other words, all ballots that have the numeral one next to Candidate A’s name are placed together in one parcel, all those that have the numeral one next to Candidate B’s name in another parcel, and so on for each candidate who received any first‑preference votes. After the parcels have been assembled, the returning officer must count the number of ballot papers in each parcel and record those numbers, as well as the total number of valid ballots across all parcels. The count for each parcel is then translated into a “value” that is credited to the corresponding candidate; the value of a parcel is simply the number of ballots it contains, reflecting the total weight of the first‑preference support for that candidate. This process of parceling creates a clear and orderly basis for the subsequent stages of the count, especially in elections that use a preferential voting system where votes may be transferred from one candidate to another.

When the election being conducted is for a single seat – for example, a mayoral election or a constituency that returns only one representative – the counting rules simplify in a specific way. Every valid ballot paper is treated as having a value of one point at each stage of the count, meaning that each ballot contributes equally to a candidate’s total. To determine the number of points a candidate must achieve in order to be elected – the “quota” – the returning officer follows a three‑step calculation. First, the officer adds together all of the values that have been credited to the candidates under the parceling rule; this total is essentially the total number of valid votes cast in the election. Second, the officer divides that total by two, which yields half of the total vote count. Third, the officer discards any fractional remainder that may result from the division and then adds one to the whole number obtained. The resulting figure is the quota, the minimum number of points a candidate must reach to win the seat. If, after the first count or after any later count, the total points credited to any candidate meet or exceed the quota, or if only one candidate remains in the running, that candidate is declared elected without further need for redistribution. This rule ensures that the winner has secured a clear majority of the votes, even in a single‑seat contest where the preferential system may otherwise involve multiple rounds of counting.

If, however, after a count no candidate has reached the quota and more than one candidate remains, the returning officer must continue the process by eliminating the candidate who, at that stage, has accumulated the smallest total value of votes. The ballots that were originally placed in that candidate’s parcel – as well as any sub‑parcels that may have been created in previous rounds – are then examined. For each of those ballots, the returning officer looks at the next available preference recorded on the paper – that is, the second choice if the first choice has been eliminated, or the third choice if both the first and second choices have already been eliminated, and so on. The ballots are then regrouped into new sub‑parcels based on those next preferences, each new sub‑parcel corresponding to a continuing candidate who is still in the contest. The officer counts the number of ballots in each new sub‑parcel and adds that count to the total value already credited to the candidate associated with that sub‑parcel, effectively transferring the vote weight from the eliminated candidate to the remaining ones according to the voters’ expressed preferences. Any ballots that have no further preferences – because the voter marked only the eliminated candidate or because all the marked preferences have already been exhausted – are placed in a separate sub‑parcel labeled as “exhausted”. These exhausted ballots no longer affect the outcome, as they cannot be transferred to any remaining candidate. After this redistribution of votes, the returning officer checks again to see whether any of the continuing candidates has now reached the quota. If a candidate meets the quota as a result of the transferred votes, that candidate is declared elected, and the counting process stops. If not, the elimination and redistribution steps are repeated until a candidate satisfies the quota or only one candidate remains, at which point the remaining candidate is declared the winner. This systematic method of eliminating the lowest‑scoring candidate and reallocating their votes according to subsequent preferences ensures that the final result reflects the collective preferences of the electorate as fully as possible, even in a single‑seat election that employs a preferential voting system.

When a candidate must be excluded under the provision identified as clause (a) of sub‑rule (3), a situation may arise in which two or more candidates are tied because they have been credited with exactly the same numerical value and they occupy the lowest positions on the poll. In such an event the electoral officer, known as the returning officer, must apply a further tie‑breaking criterion. The rule directs that the candidate among those tied who received the smallest number of original votes—that is, the votes that were cast directly for that candidate on the first count and not obtained through any later transfers—shall be the one who is removed from the contest. If, however, the count of original votes is also identical for the tied candidates, the returning officer is then empowered to resolve the deadlock by drawing lots, which is a random method such as pulling a name out of a hat or using a similar impartial device, to decide which of the equally placed candidates will be excluded.

In elections where more than one seat is to be filled, a special procedure is required to determine the number of votes that a candidate must obtain in order to be declared elected. This number is known as the quota. The quota is calculated by first treating every ballot paper that is deemed valid as having a value of one hundred. This uniform valuation simplifies the arithmetic that follows. The total value that has been credited to all the candidates under the specific provision labeled clause (c) of rule 74 is then added together. That sum represents the aggregate of all the vote values that have been allocated to the field of candidates at that stage of the count. The next step is to divide this total by a divisor that is equal to the number of vacancies, or seats to be filled, plus one. By adding one to the number of seats, the divisor ensures that the quota will be slightly higher than a simple proportional share of the votes. After performing the division, the result is truncated by discarding any fractional remainder; only the whole number part is retained. Finally, one is added to this truncated quotient, and the resulting integer is the quota. Any candidate whose total credited value reaches or exceeds this quota is thereby entitled to be returned, meaning they are elected to one of the vacant seats.

The returning officer must follow a set of general instructions when applying the detailed rules that govern the counting process, specifically rules numbered from 78 through 82. The officer is required to ignore any fractional parts that arise during calculations; this means that only whole numbers are considered when determining whether a candidate has reached the quota or when transferring vote values. In addition, any preferences—that is, the subsequent choices indicated on a ballot paper—recorded for candidates who have already been either elected or excluded must be disregarded for the purposes of the current count. This prevents the re‑introduction of vote values that have already been fully accounted for in earlier stages of the count.

A candidate is declared elected at the moment when, after any stage of counting or after the transfer of any parcel or sub‑parcel of votes that originated from an excluded candidate, the total value of ballot papers credited to that candidate meets or exceeds the quota that was previously calculated. This declaration can occur at the end of a count, which is a discrete round of tallying, or after a specific batch of transferred votes has been added to a candidate’s total. The moment the quota is satisfied, the candidate’s election is finalised.

If, after a count, a candidate’s total credited value is greater than the quota, the excess amount—known as the surplus—must be transferred to other candidates who remain in the running. This transfer is carried out in accordance with the detailed provisions that follow, and it respects the order of the elector’s preferences as recorded on each ballot paper. The surplus is the portion of the candidate’s vote value that lies beyond what is necessary to secure election, and it is intended to help other candidates achieve the quota.

When more than one candidate possesses a surplus at the same point in the counting process, the rule stipulates that the candidate with the largest surplus should be dealt with first. The other surpluses are then handled in descending order of magnitude. This sequencing is subject to an additional safeguard: all surpluses that arise on the first count must be resolved before any surpluses that arise on the second count are considered, and this pattern continues for subsequent counts. This ensures an orderly and predictable progression of transfers.

In the event that two or more candidates have surpluses of exactly the same size, the returning officer must look to the original votes each of those candidates received at the outset of the election. The candidate who originally attracted the greater number of first‑preference votes is given priority, and his or her surplus is transferred first. Should the original vote totals also be identical, the returning officer again resorts to a random method, such as drawing lots, to decide which candidate’s surplus will be dealt with before the other’s.

The rule provides a detailed method for handling a surplus that originates solely from original votes—that is, votes that were cast directly for the candidate and have not been transferred from any other source. In this situation the returning officer examines every ballot paper that forms part of the candidate’s parcel, which is the collection of papers credited to that candidate. The officer separates the unexhausted papers—those that still contain a subsequent preference that can be transferred—from the exhausted papers, which have no further preferences recorded and therefore cannot be transferred further. The unexhausted papers are then grouped into sub‑parcels according to the next preference indicated on each paper. An additional sub‑parcel is created to contain all of the exhausted papers.

Once the sub‑parcels have been formed, the returning officer determines the value of each sub‑parcel as well as the total value of all unexhausted papers combined. If the total value of the unexhausted papers is less than or exactly equal to the amount of the surplus, the officer transfers every unexhausted paper to the next indicated candidates at the same value at which those papers were originally received by the candidate whose surplus is being dealt with. In other words, no reduction in the value of each paper is necessary because the surplus can be fully satisfied by the unexhausted papers.

If, on the contrary, the total value of the unexhausted papers exceeds the surplus, the officer must allocate the surplus proportionately among the unexhausted papers. To achieve this, the surplus amount is divided by the total number of unexhausted papers, yielding a fractional value that represents the portion of each paper that will be transferred. Each unexhausted paper is then transferred to the next indicated candidate at this calculated fractional value, ensuring that the total transferred value exactly matches the surplus.

When a surplus includes both original votes and votes that have been transferred to the candidate from other candidates, the procedure is slightly altered. The returning officer revisits the most recent batch of transferred papers—the sub‑parcel that was last transferred to the candidate. Those papers are again divided into sub‑parcels based on the next preferences recorded, and the same method described for original‑vote surpluses is applied to these newly formed sub‑parcels. This ensures consistency in how surplus values are handled regardless of their source.

Each group of papers that is transferred to a continuing candidate is added to that candidate’s existing collection of papers as a distinct sub‑parcel. In this way the record of where each batch of transferred votes originated is preserved, which can be important for audit and verification purposes. Any papers that belong to a candidate who has already been elected but have not been transferred under the surplus rules are set aside as “finally dealt with,” meaning they will not be considered again in the counting process.

After all surpluses have been transferred according to the steps just described, it may still happen that the number of candidates who have reached the quota is fewer than the number of seats that need to be filled. In such a circumstance the returning officer must eliminate, or exclude, the candidate who is lowest on the poll—that is, the candidate who currently has the smallest total value of credited votes. The unexhausted papers belonging to that excluded candidate are then redistributed to the remaining candidates who are still in contention, following the next preferences indicated on each paper. Any papers that are exhausted—that is, those that contain no further preferences—are set aside as finally dealt with, just as in the surplus‑transfer stage. This exclusion and redistribution process continues, with the returning officer repeatedly removing the lowest‑ranked candidate and reallocating his or her unexhausted votes, until the required number of candidates have reached the quota and are declared elected.

In the conduct of an election that employs a system of proportional representation by means of the single‑transferable vote, the handling of ballot papers after a candidate has been eliminated from the running follows a precise sequence of steps designed to preserve the integrity of the vote and to ensure that each voter’s preferences are respected as fully as possible. The first stage of this sequence concerns the papers that contain the original, unaltered votes cast for the candidate who has just been excluded. According to the rule designated as paragraph (2), each of these papers is transferred to the next preferred candidate indicated on the ballot, and the transfer value assigned to every such paper is set at one hundred per cent. In other words, the full weight of the original vote is retained when it is moved to the subsequent preference because the voter’s first choice has been removed from the contest.

Having dealt with the original votes, the procedure then turns to the papers that have previously been transferred to the excluded candidate as a result of earlier stages of the count. Paragraph (3) specifies that these transferred papers are to be moved on again, but they must be transferred in the exact order in which they originally arrived at the excluded candidate, and each must be transferred at the same reduced value that was applied when they first reached that candidate. For example, if a ballot had earlier been transferred to the excluded candidate at a value of seventy‑five percent because it originated as a surplus from another elected candidate, it will continue to be transferred at that same seventy‑five percent when it is passed on now. This requirement preserves the proportionality of the vote and prevents any inadvertent inflation or deflation of the ballot’s influence.

Each of the movements of ballots described in the preceding paragraphs is treated as a distinct transfer for the purposes of the count, yet it does not constitute a separate count in its own right. This principle is articulated in paragraph (4), which clarifies that while the act of moving a group of papers from one candidate to another is recorded as an individual transfer event, it does not trigger a new round of tallying the entire election. The count proceeds continuously, aggregating the effects of each transfer within the same overall counting cycle.

A further safeguard is built into the system to avoid over‑filling a seat once a candidate has reached the necessary threshold of support. Paragraph (5) states that if, as a consequence of the incoming transferred papers, a candidate’s total vote value meets or exceeds the predetermined quota – the number of votes required for election – the counting process that is currently underway is allowed to finish, but no additional papers are to be transferred to that candidate thereafter. In practice, this means that once a candidate has secured enough support to be declared elected, any further surplus votes that might otherwise have been passed on to them are instead left where they are, preventing the candidate from accumulating more than the quota.

The whole exclusion and transfer mechanism is repeated iteratively for each successive candidate who is eliminated, moving from the lowest‑scoring candidate upward until a vacancy is filled either by a candidate achieving the quota or by another provision set out later in the rules. Paragraph (6) captures this iterative nature, explaining that the process described in the earlier paragraphs is to be applied repeatedly, one after another, for each candidate who is the lowest on the poll at the time of their exclusion. This continues until the seat in question is finally occupied, either because a candidate reaches the quota through the accumulation of transferred votes or because the special circumstances described in subsequent paragraphs apply.

Occasionally, the count reaches a point where more than one candidate shares the same lowest total of votes at the moment an exclusion is required. In such a tie‑situations, paragraph (7) provides a hierarchy of tie‑breaking criteria. First, the officer conducting the count must look back at the original, unadjusted vote totals recorded for each tied candidate at the start of the election. The candidate who originally received the fewest first‑preference votes is to be excluded. If, however, the original vote totals are also identical, the next step is to examine the sequence of counts to identify the earliest count at which the tied candidates held unequal values; the candidate whose value was smaller at that particular count is then the one to be excluded. This method ensures that the decision is based on the historical progression of the count rather than on an arbitrary choice.

If, after applying the two levels of comparison outlined in the previous rule, the candidates remain indistinguishable—that is, they have the same original vote totals and have remained equal at every stage of the count—then the responsibility for breaking the deadlock passes to the returning officer, the official charged with overseeing the election in that constituency. Paragraph (8) dictates that, under these circumstances, the returning officer must decide the matter by lot, meaning by a random method such as drawing a name from a container or flipping a coin. This random selection is the final, legally sanctioned way to determine which of the equally placed candidates will be excluded from the race.

The rules governing the final filling of any remaining seats are set out in the provision labelled as clause 81. The first sub‑paragraph of this clause explains that when, after a particular count, the number of candidates who have not yet been eliminated is exactly equal to the number of vacant seats that still need to be filled, those remaining candidates are automatically declared elected. No further transfers or calculations are required because the number of eligible candidates matches the number of positions open. The second sub‑paragraph deals with a scenario where only a single vacancy remains and one candidate’s total vote value—taking into account any transferred votes and any surplus that has not yet been reallocated—exceeds the combined total of all other continuing candidates together with any surplus that could still be transferred. In that situation, the candidate with the overwhelming majority is declared elected outright, as it is mathematically impossible for any other candidate to overtake them. The third sub‑paragraph addresses the rare case in which, with only one seat left, exactly two candidates remain, both possessing precisely the same total vote value, and there is no surplus left that could be transferred to alter the balance. In that deadlock, the returning officer again resorts to a lot, selecting one of the two candidates at random to be excluded; the other candidate, now the sole remaining contender, is then declared elected.

The regulations also provide mechanisms for recounts to ensure accuracy and confidence in the final result. Clause 82 sets out the rights of candidates, and where a candidate is unavailable, the rights of his election agent or counting agent, to request a re‑examination of the ballot papers at any stage of the counting process. This request may be made either before the counting begins, during the counting, or after any particular transfer of votes—whether the transfer involves surplus votes from an elected candidate or the redistribution of votes from an excluded candidate—has taken place. When such a request is made, the returning officer is obliged to immediately carry out the re‑examination and to recount the specified set of papers, provided those papers have not already been set aside as definitively dealt with in a previous transfer. The second sub‑paragraph of clause 82 gives the returning officer discretionary power to initiate a recount on his own initiative if he doubts the correctness of a previous count. He may choose to recount the votes once or even more than once, but the rule also clarifies that the officer is not compelled to repeat a recount of the same set of votes more than once unless he deems it necessary.

To aid understanding of how these intricate procedures function in practice, clause 83 refers readers to an illustrative example that is included in the Schedule attached to the rules. This illustration demonstrates, step by step, the application of the provisions from clauses 76 through 81, showing how votes are transferred, how candidates are excluded, how quotas are met, and how the final election of candidates is determined under the prescribed methodology.

Finally, the formal declaration of the election result and the preparation of the official return are covered in clause 84. Once all counting activities, including any necessary recounts and transfers, have been completed, the returning officer must, in accordance with the stipulations of sub‑rule (3) of clause 81, proceed to finalize the result. This involves three distinct actions. First, the officer must complete the official result form required by section 66 of the governing electoral legislation, choosing either Form 23 or Form 23A depending on whether the election was for a single‑member or multi‑member constituency, and then dispatch signed copies of that form to the appropriate authority, the national Election Commission, and the chief electoral officer of the relevant jurisdiction. Second, the officer must prepare a formal return of the election in the form of Form 23B, which serves as the official record of the outcome, and after reporting the result as required by section 67, must send signed copies of that return to both the Election Commission and the chief electoral officer. Third, the officer must allow any candidate, or that candidate’s designated election agent or counting agent, to obtain a copy of the return or to extract the relevant information from it, thereby ensuring transparency and giving each participant the opportunity to review the official documentation. Following the completion of these tasks, the returning officer must then separate the ballot papers that have been deemed valid from those that have been rejected. The valid papers are to be placed together in a single packet, while the rejected papers are to be grouped in a separate packet, thereby concluding the physical handling of the ballots and preserving the evidential trail for any future scrutiny or audit that might be required.

The procedure for sealing the election documents is set out in a series of detailed steps designed to guarantee the integrity and authenticity of the materials that form the basis of the final vote count. First, each packet that contains the individual voting records, the declarations made by electors, and the attestations of their signatures must be sealed. The seal is to bear the official stamp of the returning officer, who is the public official appointed to oversee the conduct of the election in a particular constituency, as well as the seals of any candidates, their election agents, or counting agents who wish to affix their own seals to the same packets. By affixing these seals, the parties involved certify that the contents of the packets have not been altered after the sealing, and they provide an additional layer of accountability because the seal of each participant can be examined later to confirm that it was applied by an authorized person. After the sealing is complete, the next requirement is to record on the outside of each sealed packet a clear description of what is inside, together with the precise date on which the election took place. This description typically includes a concise statement such as “ballot papers for polling station X” or “declarations and signatures of electors for constituency Y,” and the date is written in a standard format to avoid any confusion. The purpose of this labeling is to make it easy for any subsequent audit or judicial review to identify the contents without opening the packet, thereby preserving the sealed condition while still providing necessary information for reference.

A special provision applies when the counting process concerns an election to fill a seat, or several seats, in the Council of States, which is the upper house of the federal legislature. In such cases, before the returning officer applies the seals described above, the law requires that the authorised agent of each political party be given an opportunity to verify how members of that party who are also electors have cast their votes. This verification step ensures that the party can confirm that its members have voted in accordance with any internal party directives, and it also provides an additional safeguard that the ballots have been correctly accounted for before the sealing process makes them immutable. The authorised agent, who is typically a senior official designated by the party to represent its interests in the counting centre, is allowed to inspect the relevant voting records under the supervision of the returning officer. Only after this verification is completed does the returning officer proceed with affixing the seals, thereby completing the sealing and labeling process for the Council of States elections.

Once a candidate has been declared elected, the returning officer must promptly issue a formal document that certifies the election of that candidate. This document, known as a certificate of election, follows a prescribed format identified as Form 24. The certificate serves as an official acknowledgment that the candidate has satisfied all the legal requirements to be declared the winner of the contest. After preparing the certificate, the returning officer must obtain a signed acknowledgment from the candidate confirming that he or she has received the certificate. This acknowledgment is a written statement, signed by the candidate, that attests to the receipt of the document. The signed acknowledgment is then dispatched by registered post, which provides a traceable and secure method of delivery, to the appropriate senior official of the legislative body to which the candidate has been elected. If the election was for a seat in the Council of States, the acknowledgment is sent to the Secretary of the Council of States; if the election was for a seat in a state’s Legislative Council, the acknowledgment is sent to the Secretary of the Legislative Council. This chain of communication ensures that the official records of both the returning officer’s office and the legislative body are synchronized, and it provides an auditable trail that can be referred to in any future dispute about the validity of the election or the issuance of the certificate.

The law also contains a separate part that deals with the financing of political parties, the equitable allocation of broadcast time on electronic media, and the material that must be supplied to parties that have been formally recognised by the election authorities. Within this part, several definitions are set out to give precise meaning to key terms that appear throughout the provisions. The term “cable television network” and the related term “cable operator” are defined in the same manner as they are described in a specific clause of the Explanation to section 39A of the election statutes, which deals with the regulation of media coverage during elections. “Electronic media” is defined in a similar way, referring to the same clause of the Explanation to section 39A, and it generally includes all forms of electronic communication that can be used to broadcast election-related content, such as television, radio, and digital platforms. The expression “political party” carries the meaning given to it in subsection (1) of section 2 of the same statutes, which outlines the criteria that an organization must meet to be considered a political party for the purposes of the election law. Finally, the term “recognised political party” is defined by reference to the Election Symbols (Reservation and Allotment) Order of 1968, a statutory instrument that lists those parties which have been granted official status and are therefore entitled to certain benefits, such as the allocation of a unique electoral symbol and access to public funding.

In order to monitor the flow of money into the political process, the law requires that every recognised political party submit an annual financial report. This report must be prepared in a specific format known as Form 24A and must be filed by the party’s treasurer, or by another individual who has been expressly authorised by the party to act on its behalf. The report is to be submitted to the Election Commission before the deadline for filing the party’s income tax return for that financial year, as stipulated in section 139 of the Income-tax Act of 1961. By synchronising the filing of the contributions report with the tax filing schedule, the legislation ensures that the financial disclosures are made in a timely manner and that they can be cross‑checked against the tax records of the party, thereby reducing the risk of undisclosed or illicit contributions.

The allocation of broadcast time on cable television and other electronic media is governed by a set of rules intended to guarantee that all recognised political parties receive a fair share of exposure during the election period. The Election Commission is charged with classifying the various cable television networks and electronic media outlets into two distinct categories. The first category comprises those networks and outlets that are owned, controlled, or substantially financed by funds provided by the Central Government of India. This includes public service broadcasters and any private channels that receive a significant portion of their operating budget from government grants. The second category includes all other networks and outlets that are not owned, controlled, or substantially funded by the central government, meaning that they operate independently of direct government financing.

For the networks and outlets that fall within the first, government‑funded category, the Election Commission must, in consultation with the relevant Ministry of the Government of India that oversees the sector concerned, determine the total amount of broadcast time that is available for election‑related programming. Once this total time is established, the Commission then distributes the time among the recognised political parties in proportion to each party’s past electoral performance. This proportional allocation is intended to reflect the level of public support that each party previously enjoyed, and it ensures that parties with a larger share of the vote receive a correspondingly larger amount of airtime. The purpose of this allocation is to allow each party to display or propagate election material, such as campaign advertisements, policy statements, or speeches, and to address the public on matters directly related to the election, as stipulated in subsection (1) of section 39A of the election statutes.

The concept of “past performance of a recognised political party” is quantified in a specific manner. For elections that determine the composition of the House of the People, which is the lower house of the national parliament, the past performance is measured by calculating the percentage of the total votes that were cast for that party in the most recent general election. This percentage is obtained by dividing the number of votes received by the party by the total number of votes cast across the entire country in that election, and then expressing the result as a percentage. For elections that determine the composition of a state’s Legislative Assembly, except for the State of Jammu and Kashmir, the same method is applied: the percentage is derived from the number of votes the party received in the last general election for that state's assembly, divided by the total number of votes cast in that election, and expressed as a percentage. By using these percentages, the Election Commission can compute an equitable share of broadcast time for each party, thereby ensuring that the distribution of media resources reflects the democratic weight that each party carried in the most recent electoral contest. This system is designed to promote a level playing field, prevent the monopolisation of media exposure by any single party, and support an informed electorate by providing access to a diverse range of political viewpoints during the critical period leading up to the election.

The provision identified as clause 85D deals with the responsibility of the Central Government to supply material that is essential for the conduct of a general election, whether that election is intended to create the composition of the House of the People, commonly known as the Lok Sabha, at the national level, or to form the Legislative Assembly of any State within the Union of India. The legal text requires that, at the appropriate moment when a general election is scheduled, the Central Government must provide to the Election Commission of India a specific quantity of copies of the electoral roll. The electoral roll is the official list of all persons who are entitled to vote, and it is prepared and finally published in accordance with the Representation of the People Act of 1950, which is designated as Act number 43 of the year 1950. The number of copies to be supplied is not a fixed figure in the statute; instead, it is left to the discretion of the Election Commission, which may determine, based on its assessment of the needs of the electoral process, how many copies are necessary to ensure that the election proceeds smoothly and that all participants have adequate access to the information contained in the roll. Once the Commission has made this determination, it may require that the copies be made available free of cost to the candidates representing recognised political parties. Recognised political parties are those that have achieved a certain status under the law, usually because they have demonstrated a minimum level of electoral performance in previous elections. The distribution of these copies must be carried out by officers who are appointed or specified by the Election Commission. These officers are tasked with the practical duty of delivering the electoral rolls to the eligible candidates, and they are required to act in strict compliance with any general instructions or special directions that the Election Commission may issue for this purpose. In other words, the officers must follow the procedural guidelines, time frames, and handling requirements that the Commission sets out, thereby ensuring that the process of providing the electoral roll is transparent, uniform, and free from any undue influence or irregularities.

The next set of rules falls under Part Eight of the larger regulatory framework and concerns the accounting of election expenses, a critical aspect of maintaining the integrity of the electoral process. Section 86 outlines the specific details that must be recorded in the account of election expenses kept by each candidate or by the candidate’s appointed election agent, as required by Section 77 of the same Act. This account is essentially a detailed ledger that tracks every financial outlay made in connection with the campaign. For each expenditure, the account must include the exact date on which the expense was incurred or authorised, which establishes a chronological record of spending. It must also describe the nature of the expenditure, providing a clear categorisation such as travelling costs, postage fees, printing charges, or any other relevant type of expense. The amount of money involved must be recorded in two parts: the portion that has already been paid and the portion that remains outstanding or unpaid at the time the entry is made. The date on which any payment is actually made must also be entered, creating a link between the authorization of an expense and the fulfilment of the financial obligation. Additionally, the name and address of the payee—the person or entity who receives the money—must be recorded, thereby identifying the recipient of each disbursement. In order to maintain a systematic filing system, each voucher, which is the documentary evidence of payment, must be assigned a serial number, and this number must be entered in the account under the relevant entry. Similarly, if there are any bills that correspond to amounts that have not yet been paid, those bills must also be assigned serial numbers, and the serial numbers must be entered in the appropriate place in the account. Finally, the account must also note the name and address of the individual or organisation to whom any outstanding amount is payable, thereby ensuring that every debt is traceable and that the identity of the creditor is known.

The rules further stipulate that a voucher—essentially a receipt or a similar piece of documentary proof—must be obtained for every single item of expenditure. However, there is a practical exception for certain kinds of expenses where obtaining a voucher is not feasible. For example, expenses such as postage, which often involves the use of standard postal services where a detailed receipt may not be issued, or travel by rail, where the passenger may simply present a ticket that does not constitute a conventional voucher, are considered situations where the law recognises that the procurement of a voucher may be impracticable. In those cases, the candidate or the election agent is not required to produce a voucher, but the expense must still be recorded in the account with as much detail as possible.

All vouchers that are obtained must be physically lodged together with the account of election expenses. They must be arranged in chronological order according to the date on which each payment was made, and each voucher must be numbered in a consecutive series by the candidate or the election agent. The serial numbers assigned to the vouchers must then be cross‑referenced in the account itself, appearing next to the corresponding expenditure entry under the specific item that records the voucher number. This practice creates an audit trail that can be followed by any authorised reviewer, allowing for straightforward verification that the amounts recorded in the account are supported by actual documentary evidence.

There is an additional provision that relaxes the requirement to provide the name and address of the payee for those items of expenditure for which a voucher could not be obtained because of the practical limitations described earlier. In other words, if a candidate is unable to secure a voucher for a particular expense—perhaps because the expense is for a small parcel of postage or for a railway ticket—the candidate is not obligated to provide the detailed payee information for that specific entry, thereby acknowledging the administrative burden that would otherwise be imposed.

Section 87 addresses the procedural duties of the district election officer, a functionary appointed at the level of the electoral district, with respect to the inspection of the accounts of election expenses. Once a candidate has lodged his or her account of election expenses in accordance with the deadline stipulated in Section 78, the district election officer is required, within a period of two days, to place a public notice on the official notice board that is maintained by the officer’s office. The notice must contain three essential pieces of information. First, it must state the date on which the account was lodged, thereby establishing a public record of when the candidate complied with the filing requirement. Second, the notice must include the full name of the candidate whose account has been filed, so that any interested party can identify the relevant filing. Third, the notice must specify the exact time and place where members of the public may go to inspect the account, ensuring that the process remains open and transparent and that any member of the public, or any interested stakeholder, can examine the financial disclosures made by the candidate.

Section 88 provides the public with a right of access to the accounts of election expenses. Any person who wishes to examine an account may do so by paying a nominal fee of one rupee. Upon payment of this fee, the person is entitled to inspect the entire account, which includes all entries and supporting vouchers. Moreover, the law allows that, upon payment of a fee that may be set by the Election Commission for this specific purpose, a person may obtain an attested copy of the account, or of any portion thereof. An attested copy is a certified reproduction that bears an official seal or signature, confirming that it is a true and accurate copy of the original document. This provision ensures that the public, researchers, journalists, or any interested party have the ability not only to view the original records but also to retain a verified replica for further analysis, reporting, or archival purposes.

Section 89 sets out the reporting obligations of the district election officer concerning the lodging of accounts of election expenses and the subsequent decision-making responsibilities of the Election Commission. The district election officer must, as soon as practicable after the deadline for filing accounts has passed, compile a report and forward it to the Election Commission. This report must list each candidate who contested the election, indicate whether that candidate has lodged the required account of election expenses, and if so, provide the exact date on which the account was lodged. The report must also include the officer’s opinion on whether the account was lodged within the time frame and in the manner prescribed by the Act and the accompanying rules. If, in the officer’s judgment, a candidate’s account has not been lodged in accordance with the legal requirements, the officer is required to attach to the report the copy of the account that the candidate did submit, together with all vouchers that were lodged alongside it. This ensures that the Election Commission receives a complete picture of the candidate’s compliance or non‑compliance.

After the district election officer has prepared and submitted this report, the officer must immediately make a public copy of it available by affixing it to the same notice board where the earlier notice of account lodgement was posted. This public posting ensures that the community is aware of the status of each candidate’s compliance with the expense‑reporting obligations, thereby fostering transparency and accountability.

Finally, upon receipt of the report from the district election officer, the Election Commission must promptly review the information and determine whether any candidate has failed to lodge the account of election expenses within the prescribed time limit and in the manner required by law. The Commission’s decision may involve further action, such as issuing directions for compliance, imposing penalties, or taking other remedial measures as authorized under the law. This final step completes the procedural loop, ensuring that the financial aspects of electoral campaigning are monitored, recorded, and, if necessary, enforced, thereby upholding the principles of fairness, transparency, and accountability that are essential to the democratic process.

When the Election Commission, which is the constitutional body entrusted with the administration of elections in the country, determines that a candidate who has contested an election has not lodged, within the period prescribed by the Representation of the People Act and the accompanying set of procedural rules, a detailed account of the expenses incurred during the campaign, the Commission is required to issue a formal written notice to that candidate. This notice is not merely a reminder; it is a summons that calls upon the candidate to explain, in a document known as a “show‑cause” notice, why he or she should not be subjected to the penalty of disqualification prescribed in section ten‑A of the Act for failing to comply with the expense‑reporting requirement. The purpose of the notice is to give the candidate an opportunity to be heard before any adverse action is taken, thereby respecting the principles of natural justice that underlie the legal system.

Once the candidate receives the show‑cause notice, the law provides a window of twenty days during which the candidate may prepare and submit a written representation to the Election Commission. This representation is essentially a defence, in which the candidate may set out any reasons, explanations or mitigating circumstances that might justify the delay or omission in filing the expense account. At the same time as the representation is sent to the Commission, the candidate must also forward a copy of that same document to the district election officer, who is the official responsible for overseeing the conduct of elections within the candidate’s electoral district. In addition, if the candidate has not already furnished a complete statement of the money spent on his or her campaign, that account must be attached to the copy sent to the district election officer. The inclusion of the expense account ensures that the district election officer can verify the figures presented in the candidate’s defence and assess whether the alleged failure was indeed material.

The district election officer, upon receipt of the candidate’s representation and any accompanying expense account, is required to act promptly. Within five days of receiving these documents, the officer must forward them to the Election Commission, thereby ensuring that the Commission has the full record of the candidate’s explanations and the relevant financial data. The officer may also add his or her own observations or comments on the matter, which may include remarks on the credibility of the candidate’s statements, any procedural irregularities observed, or any other information that might assist the Commission in reaching a decision. This step creates a chain of communication that links the local election administration with the central authority, allowing the Commission to make an informed judgment.

Having received both the candidate’s representation and the district officer’s comments, the Election Commission will then undertake a careful consideration of the entire material before it. The Commission may also conduct an inquiry of its own choosing, which could involve calling witnesses, examining additional documents, or seeking clarification on any point that remains unclear. After this thorough review, if the Commission concludes that the candidate has not provided a satisfactory or legitimate reason for failing to submit the required expense account, it is empowered under section ten‑A to pronounce a disqualification. The disqualification takes effect for a period of three years measured from the date on which the order is issued. To give public notice of this serious sanction, the Commission must cause the order to be published in the Official Gazette, the government’s official journal of record, thereby ensuring that the information is available to the public and to any other authority that might need to enforce the disqualification.

The statutes also set out a ceiling on the amount of money that any candidate may spend during an election. Section ninety of the Act states that the total expenditure, which must be recorded under section seventy‑seven and which includes any costs that are either incurred directly by the candidate or authorized on his or her behalf, must not exceed a prescribed limit. These limits are different for parliamentary constituencies, which are the geographic areas that elect members to the national legislature, and for assembly constituencies, which elect members to a state legislature. The specific monetary limits are enumerated in a table that lists each state and union territory in the first column, the maximum permissible expense for a single parliamentary constituency in the second column, and the maximum permissible expense for a single assembly constituency in the third column. By establishing these caps, the law seeks to promote a level playing field among candidates, prevent excessive spending that could influence the fairness of the election, and encourage transparency in campaign finance.

Another provision deals with the situation where a single individual is elected to more than one seat, either in one of the two Houses of Parliament—the Lok Sabha or the Rajya Sabha—or in the two Houses of a state legislature, commonly known as the Legislative Assembly and the Legislative Council. Section ninety‑one outlines the procedure and timeline for such a person to resign from all but one of the seats, because the Constitution allows a person to hold only one elected office at a time. The law provides that the elected individual must tender his resignation within fourteen days of the date of his election as specified in section sixty‑seven‑A, which is the provision that governs the election of members to the two Houses of Parliament. If the individual’s elections to the different seats occurred on different dates, the fourteen‑day period is calculated from the latest of those dates. This rule ensures that the vacancy in the seat(s) the person relinquishes can be filled without undue delay, thereby preserving the effective representation of the electorate.

The resignation must be addressed to a specific authority. The primary recipient is the Speaker of the House, or the Chairman if the resignation concerns the Rajya Sabha, because these officers are the presiding officers of the respective chambers and have the statutory power to accept resignations. If the office of the Speaker or Chairman is vacant at the time, or if it is considered to be in abeyance—meaning that the office is temporarily unable to function—the resignation must be sent to the Deputy Speaker or Deputy Chairman, who act as the next‑in‑command. Should both the principal and deputy presiding officers be unavailable, the resignation is to be addressed to the Election Commission itself. This hierarchical arrangement ensures that there is always an appropriate official to receive and process the resignation, thereby preventing any procedural deadlock. When a resignation is sent to the Election Commission, the Commission, upon receipt, is obligated to forward a copy of the resignation without unnecessary delay to the Secretary of the House concerned, who is the senior administrative officer responsible for the day‑to‑day operations of the legislature. This transmission allows the legislative secretariat to take the necessary steps to record the vacancy and to initiate any subsequent by‑election process.

The safeguarding of election materials is another critical aspect of the law. Section ninety‑two mandates that all ballot boxes, the sealed containers that hold the paper ballots used by voters, must be kept under the custody of the chief electoral officer, who is the senior election official in each state or union territory. The chief officer may issue specific directions on how the boxes should be stored, for example in a secure vault, under police guard, or in another protected location, in order to prevent tampering or loss. In an amendment, the law also requires that electronic voting machines, if they are used in a particular election, be placed under the custody of the district election officer, who is responsible for the conduct of the election in a specific district. This ensures that the machines are secured at a level that is close to the polling stations while still being subject to oversight.

The district election officer, in addition to safeguarding the voting machines, must also maintain a safe repository for a wide array of election‑related documents. These include packets of ballot papers that have not been used, each still attached to a counterfoil—a paper strip that records the serial number of the ballot paper for verification purposes. The officer must also keep the packets of ballot papers that have already been used, whether they were valid votes, tendered votes that were subsequently rejected, or any other category. The counterfoils of the used ballot papers, which serve as a record of the total number of ballots printed and issued, must likewise be stored securely. Printed paper slips that are sealed in accordance with rule fifty‑seven‑C, which deals with the preservation of certain procedural documents, are also part of the custody responsibilities. Furthermore, the officer must retain the packets that contain the marked copies of the electoral roll—the official list of all eligible voters in the constituency—or, where the electoral roll is maintained in a different format, the list required under subsection one or two of section one‑fifty‑two. The law also requires the preservation of registers of voters in Form‑seventeen‑A, which are the official registers used at polling stations to record the names of those who have actually cast their votes. Declarations made by electors, together with the attestations of their signatures, must be kept, as these serve as evidence of the identity and eligibility of the voters. Finally, any other papers that relate to the conduct of the election, such as correspondence, notices, and procedural checklists, are to be retained in safe custody. An important proviso clarifies that when an election is held in a constituency—whether it be an assembly constituency, a parliamentary constituency, or a council constituency—that spans more than one administrative district, the custody of all these documents is to be assigned to one of the district election officers who has jurisdiction over the constituency, as directed by the Election Commission. This centralization of custody helps avoid confusion, ensures consistency in the handling of the documents, and facilitates any future audit or verification that may be required.

Taken together, these provisions illustrate the comprehensive framework that governs the conduct of elections, the responsibilities of candidates, the mechanisms for accountability, and the safeguards designed to protect the integrity of the electoral process. By mandating timely filing of expense accounts, setting clear limits on campaign spending, establishing procedures for the resignation of multiple seats, and detailing the custody of ballot boxes, voting machines, and related paperwork, the legislation seeks to promote transparency, fairness, and public confidence in the democratic institutions of the nation.

In the event that an election is conducted by members of an assembly, the official documents that are generated for that election must be placed under the strict supervision of the returning officer, who is the individual legally designated to receive and manage the results of the poll. This requirement is intended to preserve the integrity of the election process by ensuring that the paperwork is kept in a secure location where it can be accounted for at every stage, thereby preventing any unauthorized access or tampering that might undermine public confidence in the outcome. The returning officer’s custody of the papers serves as a safeguard, providing a clear chain of custody that can be audited if any dispute arises about the conduct of the election.

The rules governing the production and inspection of election papers are laid out in considerable detail to balance the need for transparency with the necessity of protecting the secrecy of the ballot and the confidentiality of certain electoral records. While the documents remain in the care of the district election officer, who is the official responsible for overseeing elections within a particular district, or, where appropriate, the returning officer, several categories of papers must remain sealed and untouched except under the authority of a competent court. Among these are the packets that contain unused ballot papers, each of which still has its attached counterfoil – the portion of the paper that records the serial number and other identifying information used to verify authenticity. Likewise, the packets that hold the ballot papers that have already been used in the voting process – whether those ballots were validly cast, tendered for consideration, or ultimately rejected as invalid – must remain sealed. The counterfoils that correspond to those used ballots are also to be kept in separate sealed packets, as they serve as a crucial audit trail linking each ballot to its official registration number. In addition, any printed paper slips that have been sealed in accordance with the specific provisions of rule 57C, which prescribes a particular method of sealing to ensure tamper‑evidence, are likewise protected from inspection. The marked copy of the electoral roll, or the list that is maintained under the relevant subsections of section 152, which records who is entitled to vote in the election, must also remain unopened, as must the registers of voters compiled on form 17‑A, a standardized document that records each voter’s details for official purposes. Finally, the packets that contain the declarations made by electors – statements in which voters affirm their identity and eligibility – together with the attestations of their signatures, are also to be kept sealed. The overarching principle behind these restrictions is that no person or authority, other than a court that has issued a proper order, may open or examine the contents of these documents. This ensures that the privacy of each voter’s choice is maintained and that the official record of the election cannot be altered or examined in a manner that could compromise its reliability.

A related provision addresses the sealed control units that are created under rule 57C and placed under the custody of the district election officer. These control units, which may contain electronic components or other materials integral to the election process, are likewise subject to the same restriction: they may not be opened or inspected by anyone unless a competent court specifically orders such an action. This clause reinforces the legal protection afforded to sensitive election materials, recognizing that the mere act of opening a sealed unit could potentially reveal information that might affect the fairness of the election or expose the materials to damage or misuse.

Despite the stringent protection afforded to the categories described above, the legislation also provides for a degree of openness in relation to other election documents. Subject to any conditions that the Election Commission may impose, and upon payment of a fee that the Commission is authorized to set, all other papers that relate to the conduct of the election become available for public inspection. This openness allows interested parties, such as political candidates, journalists, or ordinary citizens, to examine the procedural aspects of the election, thereby promoting transparency and accountability. Moreover, if an individual makes a formal request, copies of these publicly inspectable documents must be furnished, ensuring that the information is not merely visible but also reproducible for further analysis or record‑keeping. In particular, copies of the official returns that are transmitted by the returning officer under the requirements of rule 64, or alternatively under the provisions of sub‑rule (1)(b) of rule 84, must be made available. These returns, which constitute the formal tally of votes and the declaration of the election outcome, can be obtained from the returning officer, the district election officer, the chief electoral officer, or directly from the Election Commission itself. The law stipulates a modest fee of two rupees for each copy, a symbolic amount intended to cover administrative costs while ensuring that financial barriers do not prevent access to essential electoral information.

When the election has concluded and the necessary verifications have been completed, the various papers must be disposed of in a manner that respects both legal requirements and practical considerations. The disposal process is governed by a hierarchy of directives that may be issued either by the Election Commission, which is the supreme authority overseeing elections, or by a competent court or tribunal that has been called upon to resolve a specific dispute. Unused ballot papers – those that were printed for the election but never actually employed by voters – must be retained in their sealed packets for a period of six months. After this retention period, the Election Commission may issue instructions on how these papers should be destroyed, typically by means that ensure they cannot be reconstructed or used fraudulently, such as shredding or incineration. In the case of electronic voting machines that are kept by the district election officer under the provisions of sub‑rule (1A) of rule 92, these devices must be retained in an intact condition for a duration that the Election Commission determines appropriate. The machines may not be redeployed in any subsequent election without obtaining explicit prior approval from the Commission, a safeguard designed to prevent the reuse of equipment that might have been compromised or that may no longer meet the standards required for a secure vote.

All other packets that fall under sub‑rule (1) of rule 93 – that is, the collections of used ballots, counterfoils, marked rolls, voter registers, and electors’ declarations – are required to be kept for a longer period, specifically one year, before they can be destroyed. However, there is a further restriction concerning the packets that contain the counterfoils of the used ballot papers, as well as any printed paper slips that may have been generated under rule 57C. These particular items cannot be destroyed unless the Election Commission first grants permission, recognizing their potential value as evidence in any future audit or legal challenge. Finally, any remaining documents that pertain to the election but do not fall within the specific categories outlined above are to be retained for a period that the Election Commission deems suitable. This flexible retention period allows the Commission to adapt its record‑keeping practices to the needs of future investigations, policy reviews, or historical archiving. Collectively, these detailed provisions reflect a careful balancing act: they protect the confidentiality and integrity of the voting process, ensure transparency and public access to non‑sensitive information, and establish clear, time‑bound procedures for the secure disposal of electoral materials.

The law that governs the conduct of elections includes a provision that requires any person who wishes to contest an election petition to attach a sworn statement, known as an affidavit, to the petition. This affidavit must be executed, that is, signed and sworn, before an authorized official who has the power to administer oaths. The officials who are permitted to receive such an oath are a magistrate of the first class, a notary public, or a commissioner of oaths. A magistrate of the first class is a judicial officer who presides over the most serious criminal and civil matters in a district, and whose authority includes the power to take sworn statements. A notary public is a public officer appointed by the state to witness the signing of documents and to certify that the signatures are genuine, while a commissioner of oaths is a person specially designated to administer oaths for the purpose of authenticating documents. The affidavit that is attached to the election petition must be prepared in the specific format that is identified as Form 25 in the relevant rules. Form 25 contains the standardized layout and required particulars that ensure the affidavit is complete, legible, and legally valid. By insisting on this uniform format, the law seeks to avoid ambiguity, to facilitate the efficient handling of petitions by the courts, and to guarantee that the factual assertions made by the petitioner are presented in a clear and consistent manner.

In a separate provision, the Election Commission – the constitutional authority that is charged with the overall supervision of the electoral process, including the preparation of electoral rolls, the conduct of voting, and the declaration of results – is given a broad power to issue instructions that it deems necessary for the proper functioning of the electronic voting devices that have been introduced in many jurisdictions. This authority is not unlimited; it is subject to the other detailed provisions that are laid down in the same set of rules, which may prescribe the limits of the Commission’s discretion, the procedures for consultation, or the circumstances under which a direction may be issued. Nonetheless, the clause recognises that the Commission must be able to respond flexibly to technical, logistical, or procedural issues that arise during the use of voting machines, such as the need for calibration, the handling of software updates, the provision of backup power supplies, or the resolution of any malfunction that could affect the integrity of the vote. By giving the Commission this power, the legislature intends to ensure that the election machinery operates smoothly, that any irregularities are promptly corrected, and that the confidence of the electorate in the electronic voting process is maintained.

The rules also set out a detailed requirement concerning the maintenance of up‑to‑date lists of elected representatives who are called upon to elect members of other legislative bodies. In the case of a state election in which the members of a state’s Legislative Assembly – the lower house of the state legislature, whose members are popularly elected by the citizens of the state – are asked to vote for a seat or seats in the upper house of the national Parliament, known as the Council of States, or for a seat or seats in the Legislative Council of the state – the upper house of the state legislature – the official who is responsible for conducting that particular election, called the returning officer, must keep a current register of every member of the Legislative Assembly. This register must include the full name of each member and a correct residential address. The purpose of requiring the addresses to be “corrected up‑to‑date” is to prevent errors in communication, to ensure that each legislator receives the necessary ballot papers or notices, and to avoid the possibility of fraud that could arise from outdated contact information. The format of the register – that is, the way the information is arranged on paper or in an electronic database – must follow the specifications that the Election Commission may issue from time to time. The Commission therefore has the authority to prescribe a standardized template that will make the list easy to verify, to copy, and to use in the conduct of the indirect election.

An explanatory note clarifies how the term “members of the Legislative Assembly of a State” is to be interpreted when it appears in the context of an election for the Council of States. In that specific situation, the reference is to be read as meaning the elected members of the state’s Legislative Assembly, not to any other persons who might be associated with the Assembly, such as appointed officials, staff, or former members. This clarification is important because the Constitution and the electoral statutes differentiate between elected representatives, who have a constitutional right to vote in the indirect election, and other individuals who may be involved in the legislative process but do not possess that voting right.

The same duty of maintaining a current list is imposed on a returning officer who is conducting an election that involves an electoral college drawn from a Union territory, which is a region that is administered directly by the central government rather than having a full state status. When the members of the Union territory’s electoral college are called upon to elect a member of the Council of States, the returning officer must compile a list of those electoral college members, again with their correct, up‑to‑date addresses, and must present that list in the format prescribed by the Election Commission. The term “electoral college” here denotes a body of individuals who have been selected, often by virtue of holding certain offices or positions, to act as electors on behalf of the Union territory’s population in the indirect election to the upper house. The requirement for an accurate address list serves the same purposes as in the state assembly case: ensuring that all electors are properly notified, that ballots are delivered without delay, and that the election process is transparent and free from irregularities.

When an election is contested, a crucial question that often arises is how many votes a candidate must obtain in order to either secure election or to forfeit the security deposit that candidates are required to pay when they file their nomination papers. The security deposit is intended to deter frivolous candidacies; if a candidate fails to achieve a certain threshold of support, the deposit is forfeited as a penalty. The rules provide a precise definition for calculating the number of votes that are deemed to have been obtained by a candidate who is not ultimately elected, for the purpose of applying the proviso – that is, the additional clause – to subsection 4 of section 158, which deals with the return of the deposit. The definition distinguishes between two categories of candidates. The first category includes “continuing candidates,” meaning those who remain on the ballot through the entire counting process and whose votes are tallied in the final count after all preferences have been distributed and all rounds of counting are completed. For a continuing candidate, the number of votes that is considered to have been obtained is simply the total that appears in the final count. The second category comprises “candidates excluded from the poll,” which refers to individuals who, during the counting process, are eliminated because they have the lowest number of votes at a particular stage and are therefore removed from further consideration. For such excluded candidates, the number of votes deemed to have been obtained is the total that they held at the end of the count that immediately preceded the round in which they were excluded. This distinction matters because the vote totals for excluded candidates can change from one round to the next as preferences are transferred; the rule captures the point at which the candidate’s vote total is frozen for the purpose of determining deposit forfeiture.

In addition, the rule refers to a “quota” that is defined elsewhere in the set of regulations – specifically in rule 75 or rule 76 – as the benchmark number of votes that a candidate must secure in order to be elected. For the particular purpose of deciding whether the deposit is returned or forfeited, the quota is also treated as the number of votes that would be sufficient to secure the return of a candidate’s deposit. In other words, if a candidate’s vote total, as determined by the appropriate counting stage, meets or exceeds the quota, the candidate’s deposit is returned; if it falls short, the deposit is forfeited. This linkage ensures that the same quantitative standard that determines electoral success also governs the financial penalty for lack of sufficient support, thereby providing a consistent and transparent metric for both outcomes.

The rules also set out a detailed procedure for the service – that is, the formal delivery – of an order of requisition that is issued under section 160. Section 160 empowers an authority, typically a government department responsible for the maintenance of public order or the execution of a specific statutory function, to requisition premises, vehicles, or other property for official use. The order of requisition must be communicated to the person or entity that is being asked to surrender the property. The method of service depends on whether the recipient is a corporate body (such as a company, partnership, or firm) or an individual person.

If the recipient is a corporation, partnership, or firm, the order must be served in the same manner that a court summons is served under the procedural rules governing civil litigation, specifically rule 2 of Order XXIX or rule 3 of Order XXX of the First Schedule to the Code of Civil Procedure, 1908. The Code of Civil Procedure is the principal legislation that sets out the procedures for civil courts in the country, and the First Schedule contains detailed provisions on how various legal documents must be delivered to ensure that the recipient receives proper notice. By linking the service of a requisition order to these established procedures, the law ensures that the requisition is served in a manner that is both legally recognized and enforceable, thereby protecting the rights of the corporate entity while also giving the requisitioning authority a clear procedural path.

When the person to whom the requisition order is addressed is an individual – that is, a natural person rather than a corporate entity – the rules provide three alternative ways to effect service. The first method is personal delivery, whereby the order is physically handed to the individual or otherwise placed in the individual’s immediate possession. The second method allows the order to be sent by registered post, which provides a documented trail and requires a signature upon delivery, thereby creating proof that the order reached the intended recipient. The third method is a fallback for situations where the individual cannot be located despite reasonable efforts. In such cases, the order may be left with any adult member of the individual’s family, or it may be affixed to a conspicuous part of a premises where the individual is known to have last lived, conducted business, or worked for gain. A conspicuous part means a location that is clearly visible to anyone who enters the premises, such as a front door, a window, or a prominent wall. This approach is intended to ensure that the individual is given constructive notice – that is, the law treats the individual as having been informed of the order even though direct personal delivery was not possible – while also preserving the due‑process rights of the individual by using reasonable and documented means of service.

Finally, the rules specify a strict time limit within which an aggranted party may seek a review of a compensation award that has been determined under specific provisions of the law. Section 168 deals with the assessment of compensation that may be payable in certain circumstances – for example, compensation for damage to property, loss of life, or other statutory liabilities. Subsection 1 of section 168 applies to persons who are directly affected by the amount of compensation awarded, while subsection 2 applies to the owners of a vehicle, vessel, or animal who are dissatisfied with the compensation that has been fixed for the loss or damage to their property. If either of these parties believes that the amount determined is incorrect, unfair, or otherwise unsatisfactory, the law allows them to apply for a reference to arbitration – a process in which an independent arbitrator, rather than a court, will review the matter and make a binding decision.

The period allowed for making such an application is fourteen days. This fourteen‑day clock starts to run from the date on which the compensation amount is formally determined and communicated. If the determination is made in the presence of the interested party or the owner, the period begins on that very date. However, if the determination is made in the absence of the interested party – for example, if a decision is rendered by an authority without the party being present – then the period starts from the date on which the party receives the official notice informing them of the decision. The requirement of a fourteen‑day window is intended to balance two competing interests: on the one hand, it provides a relatively swift avenue for aggrieved parties to challenge a decision before they are forced to accept it, thereby protecting their right to a fair hearing; on the other hand, it imposes a clear deadline to prevent the indefinite postponement of the enforcement of compensation awards, which could otherwise lead to delays in the administration of justice and the final settlement of claims. By setting out this precise timeline, the rule ensures that both the authorities responsible for determining compensation and the parties seeking redress operate within a predictable and orderly framework.
