The practice of watching over elections, commonly known as election observation, has taken many different forms across the world’s democratic systems. In some countries, the task of monitoring the conduct of polls is shared between domestic participants, such as local volunteers, civil‑society groups, and officials appointed by the nation’s election authority, and external bodies that travel from abroad to assess the fairness of the process. International organizations such as the United Nations, the Commonwealth of Nations, and a variety of other multinational election‑watch groups frequently send teams of experts to observe elections in nations that request or welcome their presence. Their reports can lend credibility to the outcome, highlight irregularities, and provide recommendations for improvement. In contrast, the Indian experience has historically been rooted almost entirely in domestic mechanisms. From the earliest days after independence, the responsibility for safeguarding the integrity of the vote has rested with Indian officials and institutions, without a regular practice of inviting foreign observers to intervene in the conduct of elections.

The notion that Indian election observers would be drawn from within the country’s own civil service evolved gradually over several decades. In the first few elections after 1950, complaints about procedural failures or violations were relatively scarce, and the administrative apparatus for dealing with them was rudimentary. When a grievance did arise, the response was typically limited to the dispatch of a few senior staff from the headquarters of the Election Commission of India, the constitutional body charged with overseeing all aspects of the electoral process. These early officials were sent to the specific constituency where a problem had been reported, but there was no systematic practice of moving observers from one state to another. The idea of assigning an observer who was permanently based in a different region of the country was virtually unheard of.

As India’s electorate expanded and the political landscape became more complex, the number of complaints and the variety of issues reported during elections grew substantially. The simple model of sending a handful of headquarters staff proved inadequate, and the Commission recognized the need for a more robust, flexible system of observation. One of the first steps in this direction was the practice of sending senior officers who already belonged to the same state as the constituency under scrutiny. This approach allowed the observer to be familiar with local conditions while still bringing an elevated level of authority and experience to the task. Nonetheless, the true turning point came in the early 1990s, when the Election Commission began to regularly appoint senior civil servants from across the nation, irrespective of the state in which they ordinarily served, to act as observers in constituencies that were far from their home jurisdictions. This shift was motivated by a desire to ensure impartiality, to prevent the appearance of local bias, and to bring a uniform standard of oversight to all parts of the country.

Over the ensuing two decades, the practice of deputing election observers from one state to another became a permanent feature of India’s electoral management. The term “deputation” in this context refers to the temporary assignment of a civil servant to a role outside his or her regular posting, with the expectation that the officer will return to the original post after the assignment is completed. By the turn of the twenty‑first century, the pool of officials most frequently called upon for such duties comprised members of the All India Services and various Central Services. The All India Services are a group of elite administrative cadres that include the Indian Administrative Service (IAS), the Indian Police Service (IPS), and the Indian Forest Service (IFS). Officers in these services are recruited through a highly competitive national examination and are posted throughout the country, often moving from one state to another during their careers, which makes them well suited to the role of neutral observer. In addition to the IAS and IPS, the Election Commission also draws on officers from specialized central agencies such as the Indian Revenue Service (IRS), which handles the nation’s tax administration; the Central Board of Direct Taxes (CBDT), which is the authority for direct tax matters; the Central Board of Excise and Customs (CBEC), which oversees indirect taxes and customs duties; and the Indian Defence Estate Service (IDES), which manages properties owned by the armed forces. These officers bring with them a wealth of experience in handling complex administrative tasks, maintaining discipline, and enforcing regulations, all of which are essential qualities for ensuring that elections proceed without undue interference or fraud.

The legal foundation for appointing election observers in India rests on a combination of constitutional authority and statutory provision. The Constitution of India grants the Election Commission broad “plenary” powers, meaning that the Commission may take any action it deems necessary to conduct free and fair elections, provided that such action does not conflict with other constitutional provisions. Complementing this constitutional mandate, the Parliament enacted the Representation of the People Act in 1951, a comprehensive statute that governs the conduct of elections, the qualifications of voters, the registration of political parties, and the resolution of electoral disputes. While the original version of the Act contained a detailed framework for many aspects of the electoral process, it did not initially include an explicit provision for the appointment of observers with the authority to intervene directly in the counting of votes.

In August of 1996, the Act was amended to insert a new clause, designated Section 20B, which formally empowered the Election Commission to nominate observers and gave those observers specific powers to safeguard the integrity of the vote count. The amendment reflected a growing recognition that the mere presence of observers was not sufficient; they needed the legal authority to act decisively if they detected serious irregularities. Section 20B thus created a statutory mechanism through which the Commission could assign an officer of the government to monitor the conduct of elections in a particular constituency or a group of constituencies, and to carry out any additional responsibilities that the Commission might delegate.

The text of Section 20B is fairly straightforward but carries significant implications. The first subsection authorizes the Election Commission to nominate an observer who must be a government officer. This requirement ensures that the observer possesses the requisite administrative standing, access to official records, and the ability to issue directives that will be respected by other election officials. The observer’s primary function, as stated, is to “watch the conduct of election or elections” in the designated area and to perform any other tasks the Commission entrusts to him or her. This broad language gives the Commission flexibility to tailor the observer’s duties to the particular challenges of a given election, whether those challenges involve logistical issues, security concerns, or allegations of malpractice.

The second subsection delineates the critical powers that an observer may exercise. Most notably, the observer can order the returning officer—a senior election official responsible for administering the poll in a constituency—to halt the counting of votes at any point before the official announcement of results. The returning officer’s duties include the collation of ballot papers, the verification of their authenticity, and the final declaration of the winner. By granting observers the authority to pause this process, Section 20B provides a check against situations where the counting might be compromised. The law specifies several scenarios that would justify such an intervention. For example, if the observer believes that “booth capturing” has occurred on a large scale, the counting can be stopped. Booth capturing is a form of electoral fraud in which a party or its agents forcibly take control of a polling station, often by intimidation or violence, and then cast votes on behalf of voters who are not present or who are coerced. The observer may also intervene if ballot papers have been unlawfully removed from the custody of the returning officer, if they have been lost, destroyed—whether accidentally or deliberately—or if they have been tampered with to the extent that the true result cannot be ascertained. These provisions give the observer a powerful tool to protect the sanctity of the vote when the physical integrity of the ballots is threatened.

When an observer exercises the power to halt counting or to refuse the declaration of results, the law requires immediate reporting of the matter to the Election Commission. This reporting obligation ensures that the central authority is promptly informed of any serious disruption and can take coordinated action. Upon receiving such a report, the Commission must examine all relevant facts and circumstances and then issue appropriate directions. The legal framework provides several possible courses of action, referenced as Sections 58A, 64A, and 66 of the Representation of the People Act. While the details of these sections are beyond the scope of this narrative, they generally empower the Commission to order a fresh count, to direct a re‑poll in the affected area, or to take disciplinary measures against officials who failed to prevent or who participated in irregularities.

The explanatory note attached to Section 20B clarifies that the term “Observer” is inclusive of certain senior officials within the Election Commission itself, such as a Regional Commissioner, who may be assigned the duty of watching the conduct of elections in a constituency or group of constituencies. This means that the observer’s role is not limited to officers drawn from the broader civil service; it also encompasses senior election‑administration officials who have specialized expertise in electoral law and procedure.

The duties of an observer, therefore, rest upon a combination of seniority, experience, and legal authority. Observers are typically drawn from the higher echelons of the administrative and police services, where they have spent many years managing complex governmental functions, supervising large teams of staff, and making decisions under pressure. Their extensive background equips them to identify subtle forms of misconduct, to evaluate the adequacy of security arrangements at polling stations, and to assess whether the logistical aspects of the election—such as the distribution of electronic voting machines or the maintenance of voter registers—are being handled correctly. Because they are senior officers, they are also able to command respect from local officials, including the returning officer, the district election officer, and police personnel stationed at the polling sites.

In addition to their oversight of the actual voting process, observers play a crucial role in ensuring the efficient and effective management of the election at the field level. Field management encompasses a wide range of activities, such as the deployment of polling personnel, the verification of voter identity, the handling of ballot papers or electronic voting devices, and the coordination of security forces to prevent violence or intimidation. An experienced observer can spot potential bottlenecks—such as insufficient voting booths in a densely populated area—or detect irregularities in the way electoral rolls have been prepared. By intervening early, the observer can advise the local election staff on corrective measures, thereby reducing the likelihood of disputes after the polls close.

The evolution of election observation in India reflects a broader trend toward institutionalizing safeguards against electoral fraud and enhancing public confidence in democratic outcomes. While the early years of the Republic relied on a relatively modest, ad‑hoc system of complaint handling, the institutional memory accumulated over successive elections demonstrated that a more systematic, legally empowered observer cadre was essential. The statutory empowerment provided by Section 20B of the Representation of the People Act, coupled with the administrative capacity of the All India Services and central revenue services, has created a framework that can respond swiftly to threats against the electoral process. This framework not only allows for immediate intervention when irregularities are detected, but also ensures that the Commission retains ultimate control over the final outcome through its plenary powers under the Constitution.

In practical terms, when an observer arrives in a constituency, he or she typically begins by reviewing the deployment plan for polling personnel, checking the security arrangements, and confirming that ballot papers or electronic voting machines have been stored securely. During the polling day, the observer may make random visits to polling stations to observe the conduct of voting, the handling of voter identification, and the behavior of election staff. If the observer notices any deviation from the prescribed procedures—such as unauthorized individuals attempting to influence voters, irregularities in the sealing of ballot boxes, or signs that electronic voting machines have been tampered with—the observer can immediately instruct the returning officer to suspend the activity in question. Should the observer determine that a more serious breach has occurred, such as widespread booth capturing or the loss of a substantial number of ballot papers, the observer has the authority under Section 20B to order a halt to the counting process. The observer must then communicate the reasons for the halt to the Election Commission, providing a factual account and any evidence collected. The Commission, after evaluating the situation, may decide to order a fresh count, a re‑poll in the affected areas, or other remedial measures as prescribed by the relevant sections of the Representation of the People Act.

The presence of senior observers also serves a deterrent function. Knowing that a highly experienced officer with the power to stop counting is monitoring the process can dissuade candidates, party workers, or local officials from attempting to manipulate the vote. This deterrence is especially important in regions where political competition is intense and where the temptation to engage in coercive tactics may be high. Moreover, the observers’ reports become part of the official record of the election, providing transparency and accountability. After the election, these reports can be examined by courts, parliamentary committees, or civil‑society watchdogs that are tasked with assessing the fairness of the process.

In summary, election observation in India has transformed from a modest, locally confined activity to a sophisticated, legally sanctioned system that leverages the expertise of senior civil servants drawn from across the nation’s administrative and revenue services. The statutory basis for this transformation lies in Section 20B of the Representation of the People Act, 1951, which grants the Election Commission the authority to nominate observers and equips those observers with the power to intervene decisively when the integrity of the vote counting process is jeopardized. By deploying experienced officers with a deep understanding of administrative procedures and by providing them with clear legal powers, the Election Commission seeks to ensure that each election is conducted in a free, fair, and transparent manner, thereby reinforcing the confidence of the electorate and the legitimacy of the democratic system as a whole.

The role of an election observer, as defined by the statutory framework governing the conduct of elections in the country, is fundamentally that of a vigilant sentinel who monitors the electoral process from the ground level and relays unfiltered, real‑time information to the Election Commission. In practical terms, the observer must act as the eyes and ears of the Commission throughout the entire period that an election is being conducted. This metaphorical description emphasizes that the observer’s primary function is to watch, listen, and record what is happening in polling stations, counting centres, and other venues where election‑related activities occur. The observer is not intended to serve as a spokesperson or public relations conduit for the Commission; rather, the observer is to provide direct, factual inputs that originate from the field and that serve as a bridge between the electorate, the candidates, the political parties, and the machinery that administers the election. The purpose of this arrangement is to guarantee that every provision of the relevant statutes, including the Acts of Parliament that govern elections, the detailed rules that have been promulgated under those Acts, the procedural guidelines that dictate how voting and counting must be carried out, the specific instructions issued by the Commission for each phase of the electoral cycle, and any supplementary guidelines that have been prepared for particular circumstances, are observed with strict impartiality by all participants. In other words, the observer’s mandate is to verify that the legal and procedural architecture designed to safeguard the integrity of the vote is being honoured by election officials, party agents, and voters alike.

Any observations, comments, or reports that the observer prepares are classified as confidential material. The confidentiality clause is absolute: the information may be used solely by the Election Commission itself and may not be disclosed to any other agency, including the press or any private media organization. Consequently, observers are expressly prohibited from fielding inquiries from journalists, whether those inquiries arise during the election period or after the polls have been concluded. This restriction is intended to prevent the premature release of partial or unverified information that could influence public perception or affect the conduct of the election. The observer’s duty to keep observations confidential does not, however, preclude a limited and constructive dialogue with senior election officials who are responsible for the operational aspects of the election. For example, the observer may discuss specific findings with the Chief Electoral Officer (CEO), the Returning Officer (RO), or the District Electoral Officer (DEO) in order to facilitate mid‑course corrections that improve the administration of the poll. Such discussions are allowed because they serve the ultimate goal of ensuring that the election proceeds smoothly and fairly, but the formal written report that the observer submits to the Commission must not be duplicated or distributed to any other individual, including the aforementioned senior officials. The observer must retain a single, original copy of the formal report for the Commission’s use and must not mark or circulate copies for any other purpose.

It is essential to understand that the observer’s deployment is not designed as a fault‑finding mission. The overarching objective is to assist the field administration in achieving a free and fair poll, meaning an election in which voters can cast their ballots without intimidation, fraud, or procedural irregularities, and in which the counting of votes is conducted transparently and accurately. The presence of observers is meant to reinforce confidence in the electoral system, to provide a safety net against potential violations, and to offer practical recommendations that can be acted upon promptly.

The process by which an officer is appointed as an observer begins with an official communication from the Election Commission of India (ECI). This communication is transmitted through the Chief Electoral Officer of the relevant state or union territory and, where applicable, through a designated nodal officer who serves as the liaison between the state administration and the central Commission. The Chief Electoral Officer and the nodal officer are responsible for coordinating a range of logistical and administrative matters, including the preparation and provision of a list of officers who are eligible for appointment as observers. The briefing meeting that follows the appointment is a mandatory session in which the observers receive detailed instructions about their duties, the legal framework they must operate within, and the protocols for reporting. No officer may be granted an exemption from attending this briefing; the rule is absolute because the briefing contains essential guidance that ensures uniformity of action across all observers. If an observer needs to be replaced for any reason, such a replacement can only take place with the explicit permission of the Election Commission. Requests for replacement are not entertained unless there are serious, demonstrable reasons that justify the change, thereby preserving the integrity of the observer cadre.

Observers are also subject to strict regulations regarding travel and absence from their designated headquarters. Whenever an observer wishes to leave the headquarters for personal reasons, or for official duties that are unrelated to the observer’s core responsibilities, prior permission must be obtained from the Commission. The permission process is formal: the observer must submit a written request to the Observer Planning Section, which is the unit within the Commission that manages observer deployments and schedules. The request must be approved in writing before the observer may consider the leave as authorized. This requirement applies not only to observers who are currently deployed in the field but also to those who are in a reserve or undeployed status, such as reserve General officers, Police officers, and Counting observers who are on standby pending assignment. Until a written approval is received, any absence is deemed unauthorized and may be treated as a breach of duty.

Requests for exemption from observer duties—whether on account of health concerns, family emergencies, or other personal circumstances—must be routed through the sponsoring authority that originally appointed the observer. The sponsoring authority might be a government department, a police department, or another agency that seconded the officer to the Commission. An exemption request is not considered valid unless the observer receives a formal written communication from the Commission confirming that the exemption has been granted. This dual‑layered approval system ensures that the Commission retains ultimate control over the composition of the observer team and can maintain the necessary staffing levels to monitor the election effectively.

In addition to domestic travel restrictions, observers are barred from undertaking any foreign trips during the interval that begins with the briefing session and ends with the final completion of the electoral process. This prohibition is intended to prevent any potential conflict of interest, distraction, or loss of focus that could arise from overseas travel. No observer may submit a request for a foreign trip to the Commission during this period. However, there is an exception for cases where an officer has been selected, after the briefing, to attend a foreign training programme that has been officially sponsored by the Department of Personnel and Training (DOPT). In such exceptional circumstances, the DOPT may recommend that the Commission release the officer from observer duties, provided that the Commission can substitute the officer with another individual of equivalent rank or a higher rank to ensure that the observer team remains fully staffed. All such substitution requests are to be processed by the nodal officers who act as the point of contact between the DOPT, the sponsoring agency, and the Commission.

When an observer is already deployed in the field, any request for temporary absence or leave must follow a specific chain of command. The observer must submit the request to the Zonal Deputy Election Commissioner (DEC) or the Secretary responsible for that zone. The request is then examined by the Zonal Division, which is the administrative unit that oversees electoral operations within that geographical zone. The Zonal DEC, who is the competent authority for that zone, makes the final decision on whether to grant the leave. As with all other types of permission, the observer must receive a written communication from the Commission confirming the approval; only then is the leave considered legitimate. The correspondence related to such requests must be addressed specifically to the Deputy Election Commissioner who is in charge of the state in which the observer is operating, using the official name and title to avoid ambiguity.

To facilitate efficient communication and to provide a central repository for all relevant information, the Election Commission has established a dedicated web portal for observers. This portal can be accessed through the Commission’s official website, typically by navigating through the top‑left corner menu, selecting “ECI Officials,” then “Quick Links for ECI Officials,” and finally “Observers’ Portal.” Upon registration, each observer receives a user identifier that is identical to the unique ECI code assigned to them for official purposes. For security reasons, observers are required to change the default password the first time they log in, after which they can access the portal’s features. The portal serves as the primary conduit for transmitting messages, instructions, and updates from the Commission to the observers. All the latest procedural directives, circulars, and guidance notes issued by the Commission are posted on the portal, and a message board is available where the Commission can post targeted communications specifically for observers. Because the portal is the most reliable and up‑to‑date source of information, observers are instructed to check it regularly throughout the election cycle to ensure that they are aware of any new developments, changes in procedure, or urgent notifications that may affect their duties. By relying on this digital platform, the Commission can maintain a continuous flow of information, reduce the risk of misinformation, and ensure that every observer operates with the most current guidance at all times.

Section 20B of the Representation of the People Act, 1951, which is the principal legislation governing the conduct of elections in India, confers upon certain individuals known as Observers a limited but significant set of statutory powers. These powers are expressly designed to enable the Observers to intervene in the electoral process at critical junctures in order to safeguard the integrity of the vote. In particular, the law authorises an Observer, acting on the basis of a direction issued by the Election Commission, to instruct the Returning Officer – the official responsible for the overall administration of a particular constituency’s election – either to halt the counting of ballots or to refrain from making a declaration of the result when the circumstances described in Section 20B arise. The statutory authority thus granted to the Observer is complemented by a broader set of functional responsibilities that are essential to the smooth and transparent conduct of elections. These responsibilities may be grouped into several major categories, each of which demands a careful and systematic approach.

One of the primary duties of an Observer is to monitor the procedures that the Returning Officer follows when scrutinising nomination papers and when candidates withdraw their candidature. The scrutiny stage is the point at which the Returning Officer examines each candidate’s application to confirm that all legal requirements have been satisfied, and the withdrawal stage allows candidates who no longer wish to contest to formally step back. The Observer must watch these processes closely, document any irregularities that are observed, and report them promptly to the Election Commission so that corrective action can be taken without delay. In addition to direct observation, the Observer is expected to review the video recordings of the nomination process. Modern elections in India are required to be captured on video as part of a wider effort to increase transparency, and these recordings provide a permanent record that can be examined for any procedural lapses or violations. When complaints are lodged concerning the nomination phase – for instance, allegations that a candidate was denied an opportunity to file a nomination or that procedural rules were ignored – the Observer must investigate these allegations thoroughly, using the video evidence as a primary source. The Observer also has the responsibility to address any lingering grievances that political parties or individual candidates may have regarding the allocation of election symbols. Symbols, such as a hand, a lotus, or a bicycle, are crucial for voter recognition, especially among illiterate voters, and any perceived unfairness in their assignment can become a source of dispute.

Another critical area of oversight concerns the enforcement of the Model Code of Conduct, commonly abbreviated as MCC. The Model Code of Conduct is a set of guidelines issued by the Election Commission that applies to political parties, candidates, and their agents from the date the election schedule is announced until the final result is declared. Its purpose is to create a level playing field and to prevent the use of government resources, coercion, or other unfair advantages during the campaign period. The Observer is tasked with monitoring the implementation of the MCC by reviewing video footage of public meetings, rallies, and other campaign events. If the video evidence indicates that a party or candidate has violated the code – for example, by making promises of monetary gifts, distributing liquor, or using official government machinery for campaign purposes – the Observer must flag the violation. In certain circumstances, the Observer may also attend major rallies in person to obtain first‑hand observations that cannot be captured on video, thereby ensuring a comprehensive assessment of compliance. Related duties include overseeing the enforcement of the Defacement of Property Act, which prohibits the vandalism of public or private property for political purposes, and providing training to Micro‑Observers, who are volunteers or officials assigned to monitor specific polling stations and other localized activities.

While the principal responsibility for examining the financial statements of candidates rests with a specialized group known as Expenditure Observers, the General Observer may also be called upon to review candidate expenditures under exceptional circumstances. Such exigencies arise when the Election Commission issues a direct instruction, perhaps because of an emerging suspicion of overspending or irregularities in the reported accounts. In such cases, the General Observer must examine the relevant financial documents, compare the declared expenses with the permissible limits established by law, and report any discrepancies to the Commission for further scrutiny.

The logistical aspects of conducting a postal ballot for service voters – that is, members of the armed forces, the police, and other uniformed services who are often stationed far from their home constituencies – also fall under the Observer’s remit. The Observer must verify that the postal ballot papers have been correctly dispatched to the intended recipients, that appropriate facilitation counters have been set up at the polling stations for election officials, police personnel, and security staff, and that these arrangements conform to the most recent guidelines issued by the Election Commission. A detailed report on the status of these activities must be prepared and submitted to the Commission, documenting any shortcomings or delays that could affect the timely receipt and return of postal ballots.

Technical oversight of the randomisation process is another area where the Observer’s expertise is required. Randomisation refers to the method by which election personnel – such as presiding officers, polling officers, and other staff – are allocated to particular polling stations in a manner that prevents any systematic bias or manipulation. This process is supported by specialised software that generates randomised assignments. The Observer must examine the functioning of this software, verify that the algorithm has been applied correctly, and obtain a report from the District Election Officer – the official who oversees election administration at the district level – concerning the first level of randomisation. The aim is to ensure that the deployment of staff is both impartial and transparent.

Finally, the Observer plays a direct role in supervising the counting of votes and the declaration of results. During the counting phase, which typically proceeds in a series of rounds, the Observer is required to sign the round‑wise counting sheets. This signature serves as an attestation that the Observer is satisfied with the conduct of the count and that no irregularities have been observed. Should the Observer notice any deviation from the prescribed procedures – for instance, tampering with ballot papers, the presence of unauthorized individuals, or any other form of misconduct – the Observer has the authority to instruct the Returning Officer to halt the counting process or to suspend the announcement of the result. The Observer must then promptly bring the matter to the attention of the Election Commission, which will issue further directions on how to resolve the issue.

Beyond these specific executive functions, the Observer is expected to maintain a comprehensive overview of all stages of election management, effectively acting as a sentinel who watches over the entire electoral ecosystem. This broader mandate encompasses a wide spectrum of activities, beginning with the nomination stage and extending through to the final declaration of the winner. In practice, General Observers are instructed to arrive in the constituency at least one day before the final day for filing nominations. Although they do not physically witness the nomination process as it unfolds, they must obtain the video recordings of the nomination activities from the Returning Officer during this initial visit. By reviewing these recordings, the Observer can assess whether the Returning Officer has adhered to the Election Commission’s instructions regarding the number of persons permitted to be present in the nomination area, the proper handling of nomination forms, and other procedural requirements. Any significant breach, such as the presence of an excessive crowd that could influence the applicant, must be reported with reference to the specific Commission directives that have been violated.

The scrutiny of nomination papers is a quasi‑judicial function, meaning that it resembles a legal proceeding in which the Returning Officer must apply the law impartially, without any external pressure. The Observer’s role in this context is to ensure that the Returning Officer is fully aware of the latest procedural instructions, that they possess the most recent list of candidates who have been disqualified under the law, and that they have access to the current catalogue of election symbols. While the Observer should not intervene in the actual decision‑making process, they are expected to monitor the proceedings closely and to report any glaring errors – such as the wrongful acceptance of an ineligible candidate or the erroneous rejection of a valid nomination – to the Election Commission. In the event that a legal provision requires further examination, the Observer may remind the Returning Officer that the scrutiny proceedings can be adjourned to allow the necessary time for a proper legal review. This ensures that the due process is observed and that any decision can be defended on a sound legal basis.

The allocation of election symbols to political parties and individual candidates is another technically intricate task that demands careful oversight. Certain symbols are reserved for parties that meet specific criteria, such as those recognized as national or state parties, while other symbols are considered free and may be assigned to candidates on a first‑come, first‑served basis. The Observer must verify that the Returning Officer is cognizant of these provisions, that they are working from the most up‑to‑date list of political parties and their corresponding symbols, and that any symbol concession orders previously issued by the Election Commission are being honoured. After symbols have been allotted, the Returning Officer prepares Form 7A, which is the official list of all contesting candidates in the constituency along with their assigned symbols. The Observer should ensure that this form is accurately completed and reflects the final decisions made during the symbol allotment process.

The management of the electoral roll – the official register of all eligible voters in a constituency – also falls within the Observer’s supervisory remit. Near the close of the nomination period, new voter identification cards, known as EPICs (Electors Photo Identity Cards), are printed and must be distributed to the voters. The Observer must check that these cards are handed directly to the electors and are not retained by any intermediary, which could lead to delays or misallocation. Although the electoral roll cannot be altered to delete voters once it has been frozen for the election, the Observer must still track individuals who have been reported as absent, deceased, or duplicated in the system. A separate, polling‑station‑wise list of such voters should be compiled so that election officials can flag any discrepancies on the day of voting. The final supplement to the electoral roll – a document that incorporates all last‑minute changes such as the addition of new voters and the correction of errors – is prepared after the withdrawal deadline has passed. This preparation involves manually marking the “mother roll,” which is the master list of voters, and the previous supplement. The Observer must closely oversee this activity to ensure that the process is carried out meticulously and that any mistakes are identified and corrected before the final roll is used on polling day. Moreover, the copies of the electoral roll that are provided to each candidate for their own verification must be identical to the master roll that will be used at the polling stations. Any divergence could give rise to disputes about the legitimacy of the voter list and must therefore be prevented.

In summary, the role of an Election Observer, as defined by Section 20B of the Representation of the People Act, 1951, encompasses a wide array of duties that blend statutory authority with practical oversight functions. From the moment candidates file their nominations, through the scrutiny and symbol allocation stages, to the management of the electoral roll, the enforcement of the Model Code of Conduct, the supervision of the randomisation of election staff, and finally the observation of vote counting, the Observer acts as both a guardian of legal compliance and a conduit for information to the Election Commission. By diligently performing these tasks, the Observer contributes to the credibility and fairness of the electoral process, helping to ensure that the will of the electorate is expressed through a transparent, orderly, and legally sound election.

The observer assigned to the election must have at hand the most recent set of instructions that have been issued by the Election Commission, which were dated the eleventh day of December in the year two thousand and thirteen. These instructions constitute the authoritative guidance on how the observer should conduct his or her duties in relation to the various phases of the electoral process. In the first phase, known as the campaign period, the observer’s responsibilities are primarily supervisory and advisory rather than executive. During this time the observer, who is designated as a General Observer, is expected to keep a vigilant watch over the implementation of the Model Code of Conduct, often abbreviated as MCC. The Model Code of Conduct is a set of rules and guidelines that political parties, candidates and their supporters must follow once the election schedule has been announced, with the purpose of ensuring that the electoral contest remains fair, free from intimidation, and free from the use of state resources for partisan advantage. The observer must watch for any breaches of these rules, such as the use of government machinery for campaigning, the distribution of illicit financial incentives, or any other activity that could be classified as an electoral offense.

When the observer identifies a lapse or a possible violation of the Model Code of Conduct, the appropriate procedure is to bring this matter to the immediate attention of the District Election Officer, who is the senior official responsible for the conduct of elections in a given district, as well as to the Chief Electoral Officer, who oversees the entire state’s electoral machinery. The observer must also be prepared to make a formal report to the Election Commission itself if the circumstances warrant it. It is important to note that, while the observer must alert the appropriate officials, he or she must refrain from taking any direct executive action, such as ordering the removal of a candidate or shutting down a political rally, because such powers are reserved for the elected and appointed officials of the election administration.

A further element of the observer’s role during the campaign period involves direct interaction with the candidates. The observer is required to convene meetings with all the contesting candidates in order to explain in plain language the provisions contained in the Model Code of Conduct and to convey any specific instructions that have been issued by the Election Commission under the authority of that code. These meetings serve the dual purpose of ensuring that candidates are fully aware of their legal obligations and of providing an opportunity for the observer to answer any questions the candidates might have about the interpretation or application of those rules.

In addition to formal meetings, the observer must also be present at a variety of public political events. These may include large political rallies, speeches by senior party leaders, and the visits of high‑profile campaigners who are often referred to as star campaigners. By observing such events, the observer can assess whether the conduct of the participants adheres to the standards set out in the Model Code of Conduct, such as restrictions on the use of public spaces, limits on the distribution of campaign material, and compliance with rules on electioneering within a specified “silence period” before the polling day.

The observer must also keep a close watch on the work that is being carried out by the teams that have been specifically constituted to enforce the Model Code of Conduct. These enforcement teams are typically composed of election officials, police personnel, and sometimes independent monitors, and they are tasked with intervening when a violation occurs. The observer’s monitoring role involves confirming that these teams are active, that they have the necessary resources, and that they are following the procedural guidelines set out by the Commission.

An especially delicate area of observation concerns the phenomenon of dummy candidates, surrogate advertisements, and paid news. Dummy candidates are individuals who are entered into the election roster not with the genuine intention of winning, but rather to create confusion among voters or to split the vote share of a particular opponent. Surrogate advertisements refer to promotional material that appears to be independent or non‑partisan but is in fact produced and funded by a political party or candidate. Paid news, a term that denotes the practice of publishing or broadcasting content that is presented as editorial or newsworthy, while actually being paid for by a candidate or party, is another violation that the observer must be on the lookout for. Detecting these practices requires a careful review of candidate filings, media content, and the financial disclosures that parties are required to make.

When video surveillance has been employed to monitor the activities of particular candidates—an approach that the Election Commission may authorize in order to prevent illicit activities—the observer must also review the recordings. This video trailing, as it is commonly called, involves the systematic recording of a candidate’s public appearances, rallies, and other campaign-related events. The observer’s task is to examine the footage for any infractions, such as the use of prohibited symbols, the distribution of unapproved literature, or any form of intimidation directed at voters.

Turning now to the preparations that must be undertaken before the actual polling takes place, the observer’s duties extend into what is known as the pre‑poll election management phase. One of the first tasks is to oversee the preparation and dispatch of postal ballot papers. These ballot papers are used by service voters, such as members of the armed forces or government employees who are posted away from their home constituencies. The observer must ensure that the postal ballot papers are prepared immediately after the official list of contesting candidates, referred to as Form 7‑A, has been finalized. Timely preparation is essential because any delay could disenfranchise service voters, who rely on these special ballots to exercise their franchise.

The composition of the election staff is another critical element that requires careful oversight. The initial randomization, or allocation, of staff to various duties is carried out before any observers arrive on the scene. This first randomization is intended to prevent any pre‑existing bias or undue influence. However, subsequent randomizations—specifically the second and third allocations—must be conducted in the presence of the observers. By observing these later randomizations, the observer can verify that the process remains transparent and free from manipulation.

Similarly, the electronic voting machines, commonly known as EVMs, and the accompanying Voter‑Verifiable Paper Audit Trail devices, abbreviated as VVPATs, undergo a two‑stage randomization process. The first stage of this randomization is performed prior to the arrival of the observer, while the second stage must be executed while the observer is present. This dual‑stage approach ensures that the machines are assigned to polling stations in a manner that cannot be anticipated by any party, thereby reducing the risk of tampering or the placement of compromised equipment.

Training of election personnel is a cornerstone of the entire electoral process, and the observer must pay special attention to this area. The observer is tasked with confirming that proper training programs have been organized for all election staff, with particular emphasis on the most recent instructions issued by the Election Commission concerning the operation of EVMs and VVPATs. Such training typically covers technical aspects, such as how to set up the machines, how to conduct a mock poll to verify functionality, and how to handle any malfunctions that might arise on polling day. It also includes procedural elements, such as the correct method of sealing and storing the machines, as well as the protocol for handling any complaints that may be lodged by candidates or voters.

The handling of reserve EVMs and VVPATs involves a series of logistical steps that are described using a specific notation: P‑2 and P‑3 refer to the dispatch of reserve machines to a designated cluster point, while P+1 denotes the arrival of these machines at the polling parties. The observer must confirm that a government building has been earmarked at the cluster point to serve as a secure location for the storage of these reserve devices. In addition, the observer must verify that the locations of the intermediate strong rooms, which are fortified storage facilities used to safeguard the machines before they are sent to polling stations, have been communicated in writing to all contesting candidates. The same written communication must also convey the specific date and time when these strong rooms will be opened for inspection, thereby providing an additional layer of transparency.

Security of the strong rooms and the machines they contain is of paramount importance. The observer must ensure that robust security arrangements are in place, which typically involve a contingent of armed police personnel who guard the strong rooms at all times. This armed presence is intended to deter any attempts to tamper with the machines or to unlawfully access the stored voting materials. Moreover, the observer must confirm that a system for videographic documentation has been established within the strong rooms. This video record serves as an immutable log of every entry and exit, thereby providing an additional safeguard against any illicit activity.

The observer must also verify that the instructions issued by the Election Commission concerning the issuance of postal ballot papers to the polling staff are being followed. This includes confirming that the appropriate number of postal ballots are made available to each polling station and that the mechanisms for casting votes via postal ballot are functional and secure.

A further component of the pre‑poll responsibilities involves visiting each polling station to ensure that a thorough verification of fitness has been carried out by the election officials. The observer must examine whether the list of polling stations has been formally approved by the Election Commission and whether the minimum basic facilities—such as adequate lighting, ventilation, and accessibility for persons with disabilities—have been provided. This verification step helps to guarantee that voters will be able to cast their ballots in a comfortable and dignified environment.

The observer must also become familiar with the process of vulnerability mapping, a systematic exercise undertaken by the District Election Officer and the Senior Police Officer. This exercise involves identifying polling booths and clusters that are considered “critical” because they are vulnerable to disturbances, violence, or other forms of disruption. The observer is required to review the resulting list of critical polling stations and critical clusters, and to ensure that appropriate measures—such as increased security presence or alternative voting arrangements—have been planned for those locations.

In collaboration with the District Election Officer and the Senior Police Officer, the observer must discuss and give approval to the District Security Plan. This plan outlines the broader law‑and‑order strategy for the district during the election period. The observer must review the availability and deployment of specialized police forces, such as the Central Armed Police Forces (CPF), the State Armed Forces (SAF), and the regular district police. The observer also evaluates the preventive measures that have been put in place by the law‑and‑order machinery, such as the positioning of rapid‑response teams and the establishment of communication links among different security agencies.

A comprehensive communication plan must also be reviewed by the observer. This plan details how information about the election will be disseminated to the public, how candidates and parties will be notified of procedural changes, and how emergency messages will be transmitted in case of unforeseen events. The observer must confirm that dry runs—practice exercises that test the communication system—have been successfully conducted.

The observer must also assess the arrangements for the control room, which serves as the central hub for monitoring the election’s progress in real time. This includes checking that a robust complaint monitoring system is in place, whereby any grievances raised by voters, candidates, or election staff can be logged, tracked, and addressed promptly.

A further pre‑poll task involves the training and placement of micro observers. These are individuals who are deployed at a granular level—often at each polling station—to provide detailed, on‑the‑ground reporting of any irregularities. The observer must ensure that these micro observers receive appropriate training, that they are equipped with the necessary tools, and that their deployment schedule aligns with the overall election timetable.

Logistical arrangements for the dispatch of election materials must also be verified. The observer must confirm that the dispatch of items such as ballot boxes, voting machines, and ancillary equipment is scheduled to occur on the day preceding the poll, unless an exception is granted. Any deviation from this schedule must have prior approval from the Election Commission, ensuring that the timing of material movement does not become a point of manipulation.

The observer must also scrutinize the counting arrangements that will be employed after the polls close. This includes reviewing the layout of counting centres, the procedures for opening sealed ballot boxes, the handling of the VVPAT slips, and the mechanisms for ensuring that the count proceeds in a transparent and verifiable manner.

When the actual day of polling arrives, the observer’s focus shifts to poll‑day management. The observer must monitor the placement of sector officers—senior election officials responsible for overseeing a group of polling stations—and the deployment of micro observers at each station. The presence of these officers is essential for ensuring that the polling process adheres to the prescribed guidelines.

The observer must also supervise the conduct of mock polls, which are trial runs carried out prior to the actual voting to verify that the electronic voting machines and the VVPAT devices are functioning correctly. The observer must review the mock poll certificates, which are formal documents signed by the Presiding Officer that certify the successful completion of the mock poll in the prescribed format. In cases where a polling station has either no polling agents or only a single agent, the observer must examine the implications of this shortfall and ensure that appropriate remedial steps are taken.

During the polling process, the observer is required to keep a continuous record of the pace of voting, noting the percentage of voters who have cast their ballots at regular intervals. This monitoring helps to detect any unusual delays, bottlenecks, or patterns that might indicate attempts at manipulation or procedural failures. The observer must also stay alert for any special events that occur on poll day, such as demonstrations, unexpected weather conditions, or technical glitches, and must document these occurrences meticulously.

If any delays or temporary suspensions of voting arise—perhaps due to a malfunctioning voting machine, a security incident, or an unexpected power outage—the observer must record the circumstances, assess the impact on the overall voting schedule, and report the situation promptly to the Chief Electoral Officer and, if necessary, to the Election Commission. The observer must also ensure that the receipt of polling staff and the collection of polling materials are carried out in an orderly fashion. Each receiving team should be equipped with a detailed checklist that enumerates every item to be received, such as ballot papers, voting machines, security seals, and auxiliary supplies. The observer must verify that no non‑statutory documents—those that are not required by law—are inadvertently locked together with the electronic voting machines or the VVPAT devices inside the strong room, as such mixing could compromise the integrity of the sealed equipment.

In situations where special events have been reported at particular polling stations—such as incidents of violence, equipment failure, or unusually high voter turnout—a special counter must be set up to process the receipt of polling parties from those stations. The observer must ensure that proper documentation is completed for each case, and that a statement or report from the Presiding Officer is obtained when required, thereby creating a transparent audit trail.

The observer must also confirm that after the conclusion of voting, the reserve electronic voting machines and VVPAT devices, as well as any non‑functional machines that were used in mock polls and subsequently replaced, are promptly collected from the sector officers in accordance with the Election Commission’s instructions. This step is critical to prevent any unauthorized use of the equipment after the poll has ended.

Following the completion of the poll, the post‑poll phase begins, and the observer’s duties continue. The observer must participate in the verification of the counting process, ensuring that the results are tabulated in accordance with the legal framework and that any discrepancies are investigated thoroughly. The observer also plays a role in the final reporting to the Election Commission, summarizing observations, highlighting any irregularities that were detected, and offering recommendations for future improvements to the electoral process. By adhering to these comprehensive responsibilities across the campaign, pre‑poll, poll‑day, and post‑poll phases, the observer contributes to the overarching goal of conducting free, fair, and credible elections, thereby upholding the democratic principles that are the foundation of the nation’s governance.

The examination of all documents that pertain to the conduct of a poll constitutes a fundamental analytical instrument for assessing whether an election has been carried out in accordance with the established legal and procedural standards. By scrutinizing such paperwork, election officials and independent observers are able to determine whether any irregularities have occurred and, if necessary, to decide whether a fresh round of voting, commonly referred to as a repoll, must be ordered. The process of scrutiny is not applied indiscriminately to every polling station; rather, it is reserved for those locations that satisfy the specific criteria set forth in the instructions issued by the Election Commission. These criteria typically involve indicators such as unusually high numbers of rejected ballots, complaints lodged by candidates or parties, or any other anomalies that might suggest a breach of the proper conduct of the election. The scrutiny must be conducted in the presence of an appointed observer, and it takes place on the day following the actual polling, thereby ensuring that the observer can verify the integrity of the documentation while the memories of the event are still fresh.

Following the completion of this document review, a formal report is prepared and submitted to the Election Commission. This report details the overall conduct of the poll, highlights any deviations from the prescribed procedures, and makes a recommendation regarding the necessity of a repoll if any serious violations are identified. The report serves as an official record that the Commission may use to take corrective action, and it also provides transparency to the public and the political parties involved.

When the voting process has concluded and the ballots have been cast, the next critical phase is the counting of votes. The responsibility for arranging the counting operation rests with the District Election Officer, who is often abbreviated as DEO. The DEO must ensure that the venue, the equipment, the security arrangements, and the staff are all prepared to handle the large volume of votes in a manner that is both efficient and free from manipulation. One of the safeguards built into this system is the randomization of the counting staff. This means that the individuals who will actually count the votes are selected by a random process, and this selection is performed while an observer watches, thereby reducing the risk of collusion or bias in the assignment of personnel.

In addition to the randomization of the staff, there is a specific protocol concerning the order in which different types of ballots are counted. Postal ballots, which are votes cast by individuals who are unable to be physically present at a polling station, are required to be counted before the electronic voting machines, or EVMs, are opened. However, the counting of the electronic votes is not allowed to be delayed until the postal ballot count is fully completed; instead, both processes are to proceed in parallel, ensuring that the overall timetable for vote tabulation remains within the limits prescribed by the electoral schedule.

The observer assigned to the counting operation has several essential duties. First, the observer must verify that the tallies produced by the primary counting staff match exactly the figures generated by the additional counting staff, who are drawn from the central government establishment. This cross‑checking helps to confirm that the numbers have been accurately recorded and that no unauthorized alterations have been made. Second, the observer must oversee a random test of two electronic voting machines during each round of counting. In practice, this involves selecting two machines at random, opening them under supervision, and verifying that the votes stored inside correspond to the printed results. If any discrepancy or technical fault is discovered during this test, the observer must ensure that corrective measures are taken in accordance with the directions issued by the Election Commission of India, often abbreviated as ECI.

During the counting process, the results for each round are announced as soon as they have been finalized. This practice of round‑wise announcement provides timely information to candidates, political parties, and the public, and it also helps to maintain confidence in the transparency of the election. Once the counting is complete and the observer is satisfied that all procedures have been followed correctly, the observer certifies that the counting process has been properly concluded. This certification is a prerequisite for the Returning Officer, who is the official responsible for officially declaring the election results, to make the final announcement. In cases where the observer identifies the need for a recount—perhaps because of a contested result or an identified irregularity—a separate report must be filed with the Election Commission detailing the circumstances that prompted the recount.

The reporting obligations of observers are extensive and are set out in a series of annexes that accompany the election guidelines. All observers, whether they are general observers, police observers, or counting observers, are required to submit both an arrival report and a departure report to the Election Commission. The arrival report must be filed immediately after the observer reaches the constituency or district where they will be stationed, while the departure report is to be submitted just before they leave that area. The format of these reports is standardized and can be found in the second annex to the guidelines.

In addition to these basic arrival and departure documents, the Commission expects a set of mandatory reports from general observers. These reports are intended to capture the observer’s observations at critical stages of the election cycle. The first of these is a report that must be filed immediately after a candidate withdraws their candidature, as such withdrawals can affect the dynamics of the contest. The second mandatory report is due two days before the polling day; it provides a snapshot of the preparations and any concerns that may have arisen in the lead‑up to voting. The third report must be submitted right after the polling has ended, documenting any incidents that occurred during the actual act of casting votes. The fourth report follows the scrutiny of Form 17A, which is the official document that records the number of votes cast at each polling station; this report is filed on the day after the poll. The fifth and final mandatory report for general and counting observers is submitted after the counting of votes has been completed, summarizing the entire process and noting any issues that arose. Each of these reports follows a prescribed format that is attached as annexes to the election manual.

Police observers also have a parallel set of reporting requirements, though the timing of their submissions differs slightly to reflect their specific duties in maintaining law and order. Like their general counterparts, police observers file an arrival report as soon as they enter the assigned constituency or district. Their first substantive report is due on the second day after they have arrived, providing an early assessment of the security situation. A second police report is required on the fifth day of their presence, offering a more detailed evaluation of any law‑enforcement activities that have taken place. The third police report is timed for two days before the polling day, focusing on the preparedness of the police force, the deployment of personnel, and any potential security threats. The fourth police report is filed immediately after the polling has concluded, noting any disturbances, incidents of violence, or other law‑enforcement matters that occurred during voting. Police observers also submit a departure report just before they leave the constituency, as well as a self‑certification document that records the dates of arrival and departure, confirming that they have complied with the required schedule.

All of these reporting mechanisms are designed to create a comprehensive, transparent record of the election process from start to finish. By requiring observers from multiple agencies—general, police, and counting—to document their observations and submit them in a timely and standardized manner, the Election Commission ensures that any irregularities can be identified quickly, that appropriate remedial actions can be taken, and that the public can have confidence in the legitimacy of the electoral outcome. The meticulous nature of the reporting, the presence of observers during critical procedural steps such as scrutiny, counting, and random testing of electronic voting machines, and the requirement that all findings be communicated directly to the Election Commission together form a robust system of checks and balances that underpins the democratic process.

The Commission, which in the context of Indian elections is the Election Commission of India, may sometimes need to receive information from its field observers on a matter that is so urgent that waiting for a written report would be impractical. In such cases the rules expressly permit oral communication, for example by telephone, or by any other rapid and reliable means of transmission, during the course of a field visit. The purpose of allowing this flexibility is to ensure that any emerging problem, such as a breach of the model code of conduct or an unexpected logistical difficulty, can be addressed without delay. Nevertheless, the guidelines stress that telephone conversations should, as a matter of good practice, be conducted preferentially with the Secretary who is responsible for the relevant area, because the Secretary is the senior official who can take immediate action or relay the information to the appropriate department. After the verbal exchange, the observer is required to send a written confirmation of the discussion. This written message may be in the form of a short letter, an official email, or a fax, and it serves two purposes: it creates a permanent record of what was communicated and it allows the Commission to verify that the information was accurately transmitted and understood.

Observations made by the election observers are treated as confidential material, which means that the content of their reports is not to be disclosed publicly or shared with parties that are not directly involved in the election administration. To preserve this confidentiality, the observers are instructed to submit their reports exclusively to the Zonal Secretary or the Deputy Election Commissioner who is concerned, and to do so through the official channels of the Election Commission of India. The Zonal Secretary and the Deputy Election Commissioner are senior officials within the Commission who have the authority to handle sensitive information, and their offices are equipped to store such reports securely. By limiting the flow of the reports to these designated officials, the system safeguards the integrity of the information and prevents any inadvertent leakage that could influence the electoral process.

When it comes to the physical arrival of observers in the constituencies, the regulations provide a detailed timetable and a set of procedural steps to ensure that the observers are able to commence their duties without unnecessary delay. General observers and police observers, who are appointed to monitor the overall conduct of the election as well as the law‑and‑order situation respectively, are required to be present in the constituency at least one day before the final day for filing nominations. This timing allows them to become familiar with the local environment, verify that all preparatory work has been completed, and address any last‑minute issues that may arise before candidates submit their nomination papers. Upon reaching the assigned Assembly Constituency (for general observers) or Parliamentary Constituency (for police observers), the observers must confirm that their local telephone numbers and other contact details have been publicly advertised in coordination with the District Election Officer (DEO) or the Returning Officer (RO) responsible for that constituency. Publicizing the contact details enables political parties, candidates, and members of the public to reach the observers quickly if they have concerns or require assistance.

The District Election Officer, who is the chief election official at the district level, may designate an English‑proficient officer to act as a liaison for the observer. This liaison officer meets the observer at the point of entry into the state—typically at the airport, railway station, or a designated checkpoint—and then escorts the observer to the accommodation that has been arranged for the duration of the assignment. The DEO is also responsible for making all necessary logistical arrangements in accordance with the specific instruction issued by the Commission, identified as instruction number 464/OBS/2023/OPS dated 14 August 2023. These arrangements include providing suitable lodging, a reliable vehicle for official travel, and communication facilities such as internet access, fax machines, and basic office stationery. The aim is to ensure that the observer can perform his or her duties without being hindered by lack of basic amenities.

In addition to these logistical provisions, the DEO must supply the observer with a set of essential documents at the time of arrival. This package includes the items listed in the checklist known as Annexure‑I, which details the information and particulars that an observer needs to carry out an effective monitoring exercise. The documents also comprise the District Election Plan, which outlines the schedule of electoral activities in the district, and a map of the area that highlights the locations of polling stations, counting centres, and other key venues. By providing these materials promptly, the DEO enables the observer to quickly orient himself or herself to the geography and operational timeline of the constituency.

The interaction between the observer and the various district‑level election officials is structured to promote comprehensive coordination. The DEO is tasked with convening a formal meeting as early as possible after the observer’s arrival, and this meeting should bring together a full complement of officials: the Returning Officers and Assistant Returning Officers who are in charge of the constituency, the Superintendent of Police who oversees security, other election officials, and the nodal officers responsible for specific functions such as the media cell, enforcement of the model code of conduct, and monitoring of election expenditure. The presence of all these officers in a single forum allows the observer to be briefed on the particular issues that require special attention and to assess the overall state of preparedness of the district administration. During this meeting, the observer should actively seek clarification on several critical aspects. These include verification that the required officers for both returning and assistant returning duties have been posted, that the venues intended for the receipt, storage, and counting of ballot boxes have been identified and inspected, that the final list of polling stations has been approved, that the mechanisms for enforcing the model code of conduct and for monitoring campaign expenditure are operational, that a primary list of polling personnel has been prepared, and that arrangements for receiving information from the public and political parties—such as control rooms and coordination points between the police and the election officials—are in place. The observer is expected to review the checklist once more after the meeting and, should any deficiency be noted, to raise the issue with the Election Commission so that corrective measures can be taken before the election proceeds.

One of the most sensitive stages of the electoral process is the scrutiny of nomination papers, during which the Returning Officer examines each candidate’s submission to ensure that it complies with legal requirements. Observers are required to attend this scrutiny closely, not to intervene directly but to monitor that the procedures are carried out transparently and in accordance with the law. If confusion or irregularities arise, the observer’s duty is to report the matter to the Commission rather than to issue instructions to the Returning Officer, thereby preserving the hierarchical chain of command. Prior to the scrutiny, observers must confirm with the Returning Officer that the latest instructions and orders issued by the Commission, which are relevant to the statutory duties of the Returning Officer, have been received. These instructions encompass several important documents: the most recent list of political parties and the symbols assigned to them, the Commission’s guidelines on the criminalization of politics and the accompanying affidavit forms that candidates must attach to their nomination papers (as specified in various Commission instructions and orders dated from 2003 to 2016 and amended from time to time), the current list of individuals who are disqualified from contesting elections, the latest versions of Forms A and B that political parties use to nominate their official candidates for symbol allocation, and an authenticated copy of the electoral roll for the constituency. The observer’s role is to study these documents carefully before interacting with the Returning Officer, thereby ensuring that the officer is fully aware of the current regulatory framework and its implications.

Since the election authorities have mandated that the nomination and scrutiny processes be conducted through the electronic system known as ENCORE, observers must also verify that every nomination has been entered into the system accurately, that the digitization is complete, and that the electronic scrutiny reflects the same standards as a manual review. This digital verification adds an additional layer of accountability and reduces the risk of clerical errors.

The allocation of election symbols to candidates and parties, a step that follows the scrutiny of nominations, is likewise subject to careful observation. Although the original excerpt does not elaborate on this point, the general principle is that observers must ensure that the symbol allotment is carried out in strict accordance with the Commission’s guidelines, that the list of symbols is up‑to‑date, and that any disputes are resolved promptly and transparently. By attending to each of these stages—communication with the Commission, confidentiality of reports, timely arrival and accommodation of observers, coordinated meetings with district officials, meticulous monitoring of nomination scrutiny, and careful oversight of symbol allotment—the election observation framework strives to uphold the integrity, fairness, and smooth conduct of the electoral process throughout the nation.

Immediately after the process of withdrawal of nominations has been brought to a conclusion, the Returning Officers—who are the officials charged with administering the election in each constituency—must commence the next critical phase, which is the allotment of electoral symbols. The allotment of symbols is a fundamental step in the conduct of elections because each candidate must be identified on the ballot paper by a unique emblem that voters can recognize, especially in areas with high rates of illiteracy. The Returning Officer therefore reviews the list of candidates who remain in the contest after withdrawals and proceeds to assign to each of them the appropriate symbol according to the rules laid down by the Election Commission. This task is not merely a mechanical exercise; it requires careful verification that the symbols being allocated are available, have not already been assigned to another candidate in the same constituency, and comply with the broader framework of symbol allocation for political parties across the State or Union Territory.

To ensure transparency and fairness during this crucial activity, a team of election Observers is positioned at the Returning Officer’s office. These Observers, who may be officials of the Election Commission, representatives of civil society, or other appointed monitors, are tasked with overseeing the entire allotment process. Their presence serves to deter any possibility of irregularities, to provide an independent check on the correctness of the assignments, and to certify that the procedures follow the statutory guidelines. By being present, the Observers can immediately flag any discrepancy, such as the inadvertent duplication of a symbol or the assignment of a symbol that a party is not entitled to use, and can demand corrective action before the process is finalized.

In order to carry out the symbol allocation accurately, the Returning Officer must have at hand the most up‑to‑date compilation of all political parties that are recognized within the jurisdiction, together with the catalogue of election symbols that have been officially granted to each party. This list includes not only the symbols of parties that have been recognized by the Election Commission in the particular State or Union Territory where the election is being held, but also those symbols that have been allotted to parties that enjoy recognition in other States. For parties that are not yet recognized in the State but hold recognition elsewhere, the Commission may issue special concession orders that permit those parties to use their nationally or regionally recognized symbols within the local contest. Additionally, the Returning Officer must be aware of any orders that have been issued concerning the allocation of a “Common Symbol” to registered parties that have not yet achieved the status of a recognized political party. The Common Symbol is a generic emblem made available by the Commission to allow unrecognized parties to contest elections without having to secure a unique emblem of their own. All such concession orders, whether they relate to cross‑state recognition or the use of the Common Symbol, must be consulted and incorporated into the allotment process to avoid any legal challenge later on.

Once every candidate has been assigned a symbol, the Returning Officer prepares a formal document that lists all contesting candidates together with the symbols that have been allotted to them. This document is known as Form 7A, a special statutory form that must be completed in a prescribed format and then published for public scrutiny. Particular attention must be paid to the sequence in which the names of the candidates appear on this list. The Election Commission has issued detailed instructions on the order of entries—often based on alphabetical order, constituency code, or the order of nomination filing—so that the list is uniform across the entire State and can be easily cross‑referenced with other official records. Any deviation from the prescribed ordering could lead to confusion among voters, election officials, and the parties themselves, and could also be construed as a procedural irregularity. Consequently, the Returning Officer must rigorously verify that the list complies with the Commission’s guidance before signing it.

The completed Form 7A is a document of great importance because it constitutes the official record of who is standing for election and under which symbol, information that will be used by voters, political parties, and the media. Because of its significance, it is essential that copies of the form reach the Chief Electoral Officer of the State or Union Territory as quickly as possible. The Returning Officer therefore arranges for the original, signed copy of Form 7A to be dispatched by a special messenger—an authorized courier who is entrusted with the safe and speedy delivery of sensitive electoral documents. Upon receipt, the Chief Electoral Officer consolidates all such forms received from the various constituencies within the State, ensuring that each one is correctly filed and that the aggregate data are accurate. After consolidation, the Chief Electoral Officer again uses a special messenger, or alternatively a secure “camp bag” system, to forward the complete set of documents to the Election Commission at the national level. This final transmission ensures that the Commission has the full, verified record of symbol allotments for every candidate, which is indispensable for the preparation of the official ballot papers and for the overall integrity of the electoral process.

In the event that a parliamentary election is scheduled, the Election Commission undertakes the responsibility of publishing a single, unified list that contains the names of every individual who has been accepted as a contestant for the contest. This document, commonly referred to as the Consolidated List of Contesting Candidates, is made available in both English and Hindi, the two official languages that the Commission uses for nationwide communication. The purpose of issuing the list in these two languages is to ensure that all political parties, candidates, and members of the public across the diverse linguistic landscape of the country can understand and verify the information without any barrier created by language. It is not unusual, however, for some nominations to be originally filed in a regional language that is specific to a particular state or union territory. For instance, a candidate in Tamil Nadu may submit his nomination papers in Tamil, while a candidate in West Bengal may do so in Bengali. The use of local languages reflects the federal structure of the nation and the constitutional guarantee that citizens may communicate with the authorities in their mother tongue.

Nevertheless, the official procedure requires the Returning Officer— the election official appointed for each parliamentary constituency, whose duties include overseeing the entire electoral process from nomination to the final count— to prepare two separate copies of the official nomination summary, known as Form 7A. One copy must be prepared in English and the other in Hindi. These forms are essentially a certified record of all candidates who have successfully cleared the scrutiny stage, and they serve as a reference for both the Commission and the political parties. Once the two language versions of Form 7A have been prepared, the Returning Officer must transmit them to the Election Commission through the same channels that are described earlier in the procedural manual, typically involving secure electronic transmission followed by physical dispatch for archival purposes. In practice, this step may sometimes encounter logistical difficulties, especially in remote or linguistically diverse areas where the Returning Officer may not have sufficient resources or staff proficient in Hindi. In such cases, the Commission has provided a fallback mechanism: the Chief Electoral Officer (CEO), who is the senior election official at the state level, is authorized to step in and produce the Hindi version of the form. The CEO has the authority and the necessary linguistic expertise to ensure that the Hindi translation complies with the official standards. Regardless of whether the Returning Officer or the CEO prepares the Hindi copy, the English version of Form 7A and the version prepared in the local language— the language in which the original nominations were filed— must always be forwarded by the Returning Officer. This dual‑language requirement is designed to maintain transparency and to allow all interested parties to cross‑check the information in a language they are comfortable with.

When the election under consideration is for a State Legislative Assembly rather than the national Parliament, the protocol changes slightly to reflect the federal arrangement of powers. In this situation, the Chief Electoral Officer of the particular state assumes the responsibility of publishing the Consolidated List of Contesting Candidates. The list is prepared in the official language of that state, which could be Hindi, Bengali, Tamil, Malayalam, Gujarati, or any other language that has been accorded official status by the state legislature. This approach respects the principle of linguistic autonomy within the Indian union and ensures that the electorate at the state level receives the information in the language most commonly used in that region.

Turning to the matter of electoral rolls, the Commission has received a number of serious complaints during previous electoral cycles. These complaints allege that the lists of eligible voters that were placed at the polling booths— the locations where voters cast their ballots— differed in content from the copies of the rolls that had been provided to the political parties and to the candidates for their own scrutiny. The electoral roll, essentially a register of all citizens who are entitled to vote, is a foundational element of the electoral process. Any discrepancy between the roll used at the polling station and the roll supplied to the candidates could give rise to allegations of manipulation, disenfranchisement, or unfair advantage, thereby undermining public confidence in the fairness of the election.

Having examined these grievances with appropriate concern, the Commission has issued a set of detailed instructions intended to eliminate any such divergence. First, the roll that will be placed at each polling booth must be a certified true copy of the roll that has already been handed over to the candidates and political parties. The certification process is governed by the Conduct of Elections Rules of 1961, a comprehensive statutory framework that defines the responsibilities of election officials. Under these rules, a designated officer— typically the Returning Officer— and a subordinate officer, both of whom are specifically tasked with ensuring the authenticity of the document, must sign every page of the roll that will be used at the polling station. This dual‑signature requirement creates an auditable trail, confirming that the document has not been altered after it was originally issued to the contestants.

In addition to the signatures of the officials, a duplicate copy of the same roll must be supplied to an independent observer. Observers are appointed by the Commission to monitor the conduct of the election and to provide an impartial check on the process. The observer’s role, in this context, is to compare the roll that is physically present at the polling booth with the roll that had been previously distributed to the candidates. By physically verifying that the two copies are identical, the observer can confirm that voters will be judged against the same criteria regardless of whether they are being examined by the Returning Officer or by the political party representatives. The observer must conduct this verification on the actual day of polling and at each station that they visit. Any discrepancy discovered in this comparison must be immediately reported to the Returning Officer, and the observer must discuss the issue thoroughly with the officer to ensure that the problem is resolved before any voting takes place. The Commission expects that this dialogue will eliminate any opportunity for a complaint on this particular point. Moreover, the Commission has instructed that these procedural details be explained to the candidates and political parties during a pre‑polling briefing, so that all stakeholders are aware of the safeguards that have been put in place.

The responsibilities of the observers extend beyond the verification of the electoral rolls. They are also tasked with monitoring an extensive action plan that deals with “residual electors.” Residual electors are individuals whose entries in the electoral database are incomplete or problematic. These may include electors who have applied for an Electoral Photo Identity Card (EPIC) but have not yet received it, persons who are absent from the polling station because they have moved away but still have a familial link recorded in the system, electors whose registration has expired, and duplicate entries where the same person appears more than once. The Commission has mandated that a polling‑station‑wise list of such problematic entries be prepared. If any individual whose name appears on this special list shows up at the polling station on election day, election officials are required to conduct strict identity verification. This typically involves checking the voter’s EPIC, comparing photographs, and confirming other biographical details, thereby reducing the risk of fraudulent voting.

Another crucial component of the electoral process involves a meeting that must be convened by the Returning Officer, and which must be attended by an observer as well as all the candidates and their party representatives. This meeting is scheduled for either the last day on which a candidate may withdraw his candidature, or for the following day, and it serves as an orientation session that covers a wide range of procedural and regulatory topics. The participants are briefed on the essential provisions of the Model Code of Conduct, a set of rules that comes into force from the date the election schedule is announced and that restricts the behavior of political parties and candidates in order to ensure a level playing field. They are also given detailed instructions on how to report election‑related expenditures. This includes the specific format that must be used for expense reporting, the rate list that defines permissible expenditure limits for various categories, and the frequency with which such reports must be submitted. The time and place for submitting these financial disclosures are also clarified, ensuring that candidates understand their obligations under the law.

The meeting also covers the process for obtaining permissions for activities that involve the public domain, such as the use of vehicles for campaign rallies, the organization of processions, and the holding of public meetings. Candidates are instructed on the procedural steps they must follow to secure the necessary approvals, the documentation required, and the timelines within which applications must be filed. Furthermore, the candidates receive a briefing on the schedule for the preparation of Electronic Voting Machines (EVMs) and the Voter‑Verifiable Paper Audit Trail (VVPAT) devices that accompany them. The EVM is the electronic device that records each vote, while the VVPAT is a paper slip that provides a physical verification of each vote cast, thereby enhancing transparency. Candidates are told about the date and time when these machines will be set up at the polling stations and are given an overview of the role they may be asked to play, such as witnessing the sealing of the machines. They are also educated on the technical functioning of the VVPAT system, including how the paper slip is generated and later counted during the audit process.

In addition to procedural matters, the observer explains the channels of communication that will be available for candidates and political parties to interact with election officials. Specific details are provided regarding the times when the observer can be contacted, the phone numbers and email addresses that should be used, and the designated locations where candidates may meet the observer for any queries or concerns. The meeting also addresses the appointment of various agents who will assist in the conduct of the election at the polling stations. These include the polling agent, who represents a candidate at the polling place to ensure that the voting process is conducted fairly; the counting agent, who observes the tallying of votes after the polls close; and the election agent, who oversees the overall election strategy for a candidate. The rights and duties of each of these agents are explained in detail so that candidates know exactly what authority they have and what responsibilities they must fulfill.

An important concept that the observer introduces during the briefing is the “worry list.” This is a mechanism that allows a candidate to formally record any concerns or apprehensions about potential unfair practices, procedural lapses, or irregularities that may affect the integrity of the election. The candidate can note the specific polling stations where such concerns exist, describe the nature of the issue, and submit this information to the observer. The worry list thus becomes a formal record that can be used to trigger further investigation or corrective action by election officials. The observer also informs candidates about any recent directives issued by the Election Commission that modify existing practices or introduce new requirements, ensuring that all participants are aware of the latest regulatory environment.

The briefing concludes with a comprehensive explanation of several mobile applications that have been introduced by the Commission to facilitate compliance and improve transparency. The c‑VIGIL app is highlighted as a tool through which any violation of the Model Code of Conduct can be reported instantly, allowing the Commission to respond promptly. The EMS app is described as a system used for the randomization and allocation of EVM machines to polling stations, a process designed to prevent tampering. The Suvidha app, which means “convenience” in Hindi, is presented as a platform through which candidates can apply for various election‑related permissions, such as those for rallies, use of public spaces, or deployment of campaign vehicles. A dedicated version of the Suvidha app, known as the Suvidha Candidate app, enables candidates to monitor the status of their applications in real time, thereby reducing uncertainty and improving efficiency.

Following the finalization of the list of contestants by the Returning Officer, the observer is required to undertake field visits throughout the constituency. The purpose of these visits is to acquire a nuanced understanding of the social, economic, and political characteristics of the area. Observers are expected to travel to as many polling stations as possible, with particular emphasis on three categories of stations. First, they must visit every newly created polling station, which may have been added due to demographic changes, redistricting, or an increase in the number of eligible voters. Second, they must identify and inspect polling stations that are considered sensitive, perhaps because they are located in regions with a history of communal tension, high rates of electoral violence, or intense competition among political parties. Third, they must reach out to distant or remote polling stations that are situated far from the constituency’s administrative center, as these locations often face logistical challenges such as inadequate infrastructure, limited transportation, and difficulties in the timely delivery of election materials. By physically inspecting these varied sites, the observer gains first‑hand knowledge of the ground realities, which can inform subsequent decisions about resource allocation, security arrangements, and voter assistance measures. This comprehensive on‑the‑ground assessment helps ensure that the election is conducted in a manner that is fair, efficient, and responsive to the unique conditions of each constituency.

The purpose of a systematic review of the overall preparedness of the law‑and‑order machinery in a constituency or a district is to guarantee that the security framework is sufficiently robust to manage the heightened demands that arise during the election period. When an election observer arrives in a given area, the first responsibility is to arrange a detailed discussion with the senior officials of the police department, the administrative officers, and any representatives of central security forces who may be deployed. This meeting must be scheduled at a mutually convenient time and must take place no later than four days after the observer’s arrival, thereby allowing enough time for the exchange of information before the polling day approaches. During this conversation the observer should systematically examine a series of critical topics. First, the adequacy of police personnel must be assessed, which involves counting the number of officers currently on duty, checking the ratio of officers to the estimated number of voters, and determining whether the existing force can be expected to respond promptly to any disturbances. Second, the observer must consider the requirement for the deployment of central forces—such as the Central Reserve Police Force, the Indo‑Tibetan Border Police, or any other paramilitary unit that the central government may assign to augment local police capabilities. The need for such forces is usually dictated by the size of the electorate, the geography of the area, and any known security sensitivities.

The third point of discussion concerns preventive actions that have already been taken. These preventive measures may include the issuance of preventive detentions, where individuals suspected of planning violent acts are held in custody before the election; the execution of bond requirements, whereby candidates or agents are required to submit a financial guarantee that may be forfeited if they violate electoral rules; and the deposition of arms, meaning the collection and secure storage of firearms that might otherwise be used to intimidate voters. The observer should verify that each of these steps has been carried out in accordance with the law and that the records are complete and transparent. Fourth, the observer must review the results of vulnerability mapping, a process in which analysts identify “critical clusters” – groups of polling stations that are particularly susceptible to disruption because of their location, the density of voters, or past incidents of unrest – and then pinpoint the exact polling stations that fall within those high‑risk zones. Vulnerability mapping is essentially a risk‑assessment exercise that relies on historical data, intelligence reports, and geographic information system (GIS) technology to produce a visual representation of potential trouble spots.

Fifth, the discussion must turn to the sector plans for policing on the day of the poll. A sector plan is a detailed operational blueprint that divides the district into manageable zones, assigns specific police units to each zone, and outlines the patrol routes, checkpoint locations, and response times that are expected. The observer should scrutinize these plans to confirm that they reflect the findings of the vulnerability mapping and that they allocate sufficient personnel and resources to each sector. Sixth, the response strategy for the polling day must be evaluated, with particular attention paid to the transportation logistics for electronic voting machines (EVMs) and the accompanying Voter‑Verifiable Paper Audit Trail devices (VVPATs). These devices are the electronic hardware that records each vote and produces a paper receipt for verification; they must be moved securely from storage facilities to the polling stations, and the observer should ensure that the chain of custody is documented, that armored vehicles or escorted convoys are used where needed, and that contingency plans exist for any mechanical failure or security breach. Finally, the observer must discuss the sensitivity of inter‑district, national, and international boundaries, because districts that border other states or foreign nations often face additional security challenges such as cross‑border smuggling of arms, infiltration by non‑resident voters, or disputes over jurisdiction that could be exploited to create unrest.

In addition to the conversational review, the observer is required to confirm that the vulnerability index mapping for the entire district has already been completed. This mapping is an analytical product that assigns a numerical score to each geographic unit based on criteria such as previous incidents of electoral violence, the presence of organized crime, the concentration of political supporters, and the accessibility of the area. The observer must verify not only that the mapping exists, but also that it has been used to identify the critical clusters and the specific polling stations that fall within those clusters. Once the critical sites have been identified, the sector plan for police patrolling should be examined to see whether it has been calibrated to the needs revealed by the vulnerability index, and whether there is a clear indication of any additional forces—either from the state police, the central paramilitary units, or auxiliary volunteer groups—that will be required to maintain order.

Another important but often overlooked component of election security concerns the control of alcohol distribution. The observer should therefore monitor the distilleries that operate within the district to assess the stock levels of liquor. The rationale behind this monitoring is that a sudden, unexplained surge in the outflow of alcohol during the election period can be an indicator of attempts to use liquor as a means of influencing voters, either by providing free drinks to sway opinions or by creating a climate of intoxication that reduces the likelihood of orderly voting. To evaluate whether any abnormal increase is occurring, the observer should compare the current outflow figures with the average outflow recorded over the preceding six months, thereby establishing a baseline against which deviations can be measured. Any significant excess should be flagged for further investigation by the appropriate enforcement agencies.

The observation of the implementation of the Model Code of Conduct (MCC) forms a separate but equally vital part of the observer’s duties. The Model Code of Conduct is a set of guidelines issued by the Election Commission of India that governs the behavior of political parties, candidates, and public officials during the election season, with the aim of ensuring a level playing field and preventing the misuse of state machinery. It is the observer’s most important and crucial task to ensure that the MCC is applied in a non‑partisan manner and that its provisions are enforced effectively across the entire district.

Among the principal areas that demand strict vigilance are the use of vehicles for campaigning without the required permission, a violation that can give a candidate an unfair advantage by allowing them to reach more voters quickly and with greater visibility. The observer must also be alert to the deployment of muscle power, meaning the use of hired thugs or party workers to either mobilize supporters or to intimidate opponents and prevent them from casting their votes. Another major concern is the flow of liquor, money, and other gifts that may be distributed to influence voter behavior. These inducements can include cash handouts, free meals, or any material benefit offered in exchange for a vote. Additionally, the observer must watch for divisive tactics that manifest as inflammatory or condemnable speeches, which may incite communal tension, religious discord, or caste‑based violence. Finally, the observer must be prepared to address any defacement of property—such as the vandalism of public buildings, political party offices, or private homes—by applying the appropriate legal provisions of the state in which the election is being held. If the state lacks a specific statute dealing with such vandalism, the observer should follow the latest instructions issued by the Election Commission of India, which typically prescribe a uniform approach for handling property damage during elections.

To guarantee that enforcement of the Model Code of Conduct is carried out efficiently, the observer should verify that enforcement squads have been formed with clearly defined territorial jurisdictions. This means that each squad should be assigned a specific geographic area for which it holds exclusive responsibility, thereby preventing overlap and ensuring accountability. The composition of these squads should include both civilian staff and police personnel, reflecting a blend of administrative oversight and law‑enforcement capability. The observer must also ensure that detailed instructions concerning the roles, responsibilities, and operating procedures of these squads have been uploaded to the official website of the Election Commission after the announcement of the election. These instructions typically cover the procedures for logging complaints, the escalation matrix for serious violations, and the guidelines for interacting with political parties.

All enforcement teams are required to make use of the c‑VIGIL investigation application, a digital platform designed to record, track, and resolve violations of the Model Code of Conduct. The observer must confirm that each team has the c‑VIGIL app installed on their devices, that it is functioning correctly, and that every reported violation entered into the system is being addressed in a timely manner by the responsible enforcement officers. In parallel, the observer himself or herself must install the c‑VIGIL observer application, which provides a personal dashboard for monitoring all cases that arise within the observer’s jurisdiction. By continuously checking this dashboard, the observer can verify that each complaint progresses through the stages of verification, action, and closure, thereby ensuring that no infraction is ignored or left unresolved. In this way, the observer’s role extends beyond mere observation to active participation in a transparent, technology‑enabled enforcement process that upholds the integrity of the electoral exercise.

The observer who is assigned the task of monitoring compliance with the Model Code of Conduct (MCC) must adopt a methodical and systematic approach that begins with the receipt of a complaint. When a grievance is lodged, it is ordinarily forwarded by the District Election Officer (DEO) or the Returning Officer (RO) to an inquiry officer whose responsibility is to examine the facts of the allegation. The observer’s role is to follow the progress of that inquiry from its inception through to its conclusion, and to verify that the corrective measures that are prescribed in the MCC are actually put into effect. In practical terms, this means that the observer must keep a close watch on how the inquiry officer gathers evidence, interviews witnesses, and documents the findings, and then confirm that any remedial action—such as removal of illegal campaign material, imposition of fines, or other sanctions—is carried out in a timely manner. The observer must also be prepared to counsel the RO or the DEO on the specific provisions of the MCC that have been breached and to suggest the most appropriate administrative or legal response. If, despite these advisories, the authorities responsible for enforcing the code fail to act, the observer is obligated to report the failure without delay to the Election Commission of India (ECI), the apex body that supervises the conduct of elections across the nation. In order to make the reporting process both transparent and verifiable, the observer is encouraged to employ videographic documentation; video recordings provide an objective record of the incident, the conduct of the officials, and the subsequent actions taken, thereby serving as a powerful tool for accountability.

It is essential to understand that observers function as the eyes and ears of the ECI rather than as operational executives on the ground. Their primary duty is to observe, record, and communicate, not to become directly involved in the enforcement actions themselves. This distinction is critical because the MCC must be applied uniformly across all constituencies, yet in practice there have been numerous instances where similar violations have been dealt with in disparate ways. To illustrate the variety of scenarios an observer may encounter, several case studies are presented as teaching aids. These examples are intended to sensitize observers to the nuanced judgments they must make when faced with real‑world situations that test the boundaries of the code and the observer’s own impartiality.

In the first scenario, an observer notices a motor vehicle bearing a political party’s flag and a large contingent of campaign workers equipped with a microphone, all of which are operating without the requisite permit that the MCC mandates for such public rallies. The observer, acting together with his Police Support Officer (PSO), detains the vehicle and summons the local police, issuing a written directive that specifies the exact action the police must take. In another possible course of action, the observer contacts the concerned Superintendent of Police (SP) by telephone to report the violation, follows up with a formal written letter, and requests an Acknowledgment of Transfer of Responsibility (ATR) from both the SP and the RO, thereby creating a paper trail that documents the request and the expected response. Should the police or the RO fail to act on the observer’s written request, the observer must promptly inform the ECI of this lapse, ensuring that the higher authority is aware of the failure to enforce the code. A third, more aggressive, approach would be for the observer to issue a written instruction demanding the immediate arrest of the individuals in the vehicle. While this last method might appear decisive, the ECI prefers the second method because it respects the procedural hierarchy and creates a clear record of the request without overstepping the observer’s role. Moreover, to produce a ripple effect that deters future violations, the observer should ensure that the incident and the subsequent actions taken are reported in the media through the appropriate channels of the RO, thereby creating public awareness and reinforcing the message that the MCC will be enforced. It is important to note that the observer is not expected to engage directly with the press or to provide personal briefings; the responsibility for media interaction rests with the election officials.

In a second illustrative case, a political party submits a complaint alleging that a rival party is storing illegal firearms at an undisclosed location. The complaining party does not reveal the exact site but asks the election machinery to conduct a raid at a later‑specified address. The observer must navigate the tension between responding to a serious security allegation and preserving the confidentiality of the information. One possible, but inappropriate, reaction would be for the observer to simply comply with the demand and order the SP to dispatch a police team without further scrutiny. A more suitable response, endorsed by the ECI, involves the observer taking the complaint and the location details under strict confidence, instructing the SP to act on the information, and simultaneously sending a videography team with the police to document the operation. This approach ensures that the observer does not become a partisan actor by physically accompanying one side or the other, a situation that could be perceived as bias. The observer’s independent judgment and the maintenance of confidentiality are crucial because any hint of partisanship could undermine the credibility of the election process and the observer’s own standing. The observer should also arrange for the video footage to be stored securely and shared with the ECI as part of the post‑operation report, thereby providing an impartial record of the raid’s legality and the conduct of the parties involved.

The third scenario deals with the conduct of a designated “star campaigner,” an individual who travels across constituencies funded centrally by the political party’s national committee. Suppose this campaigner delivers a speech that contains inflammatory remarks capable of offending a particular segment of the population, and the speech is widely disseminated through national and local media outlets. In one possible reaction, the observer records the speech, transcribes the offending passages, and forwards the material to the ECI while simultaneously prompting the election machinery to initiate appropriate remedial action that reflects the seriousness of the breach. The response must be proportionate; for instance, if the speech incites communal tension, the observer may recommend that the campaigner be barred from further public appearances until a thorough inquiry is completed. An alternative, less effective approach would be for the observer to ignore the incident at the national level and allow only a local-level response, relying on regional media coverage alone. A third, even more detrimental, course of action would be to take no steps at all, thereby allowing the inflammatory content to circulate unchecked. The ECI clearly prefers the first approach because the national visibility of the speech magnifies its potential to disrupt the electoral environment, and failure to inform the central authority could exacerbate communal discord and erode public confidence in the integrity of the election process.

In a fourth illustrative case, the observer witnesses the defacement of public property through the placement of unauthorized political posters, an act that violates the MCC’s provisions concerning the preservation of public spaces and the prohibition of using public property for campaign purposes. The observer might be tempted to take matters into his own hands by stepping out of his vehicle and physically removing the posters. However, the recommended course of action, as advocated by the ECI, is for the observer to promptly notify the RO, requesting that the enforcement squad with jurisdiction over the area be dispatched to remove the posters. While notifying the RO, the observer should also document the violation through video recording, thereby creating an indisputable visual record of the illegal activity. After the enforcement squad has acted, the observer should follow up within a day or two to verify that the posters have indeed been removed and that the associated costs have been properly accounted for in the official expenditure records. This follow‑up ensures that the corrective action is not only performed but also reflected in the financial statements, which is essential for maintaining transparency and preventing the misuse of public funds. The ECI’s policy underscores the importance of effective observation rather than direct intervention; the observer’s function is to facilitate the enforcement actions of the designated authorities rather than to become a de facto enforcer.

Beyond the specific case studies, the ECI’s overarching philosophy is to ensure that any lapses in adherence to the MCC are addressed promptly and proportionately, and that information about the corrective measures taken is disseminated through an appropriate chain of communication. This chain includes local officials, district administrators, state election officers, and ultimately the national election machinery. By circulating details of the action taken, the ECI aims to create a deterrent effect, signaling to all political actors that violations will not be tolerated and that enforcement will be swift and visible. The transparency of the process also helps to build public trust, as voters can see that the electoral authority is actively monitoring and correcting infractions.

Finally, it is essential to recognize that every act of campaigning—whether it involves a rally, a public speech, the distribution of pamphlets, or the display of banners—has an associated financial implication. The MCC requires that all election‑related expenditures be accounted for within prescribed limits, and observers must be mindful of the fact that unregulated or undisclosed spending can distort the fairness of the competition. Consequently, observers should remain vigilant not only for violations of behavioral norms but also for any signs of illicit or excessive campaign spending, ensuring that the financial dimension of the election is kept within the bounds set by law. In summary, the observer’s duties encompass the careful receipt and tracking of complaints, the monitoring of investigative procedures, the recommendation of appropriate remedial actions, the documentation of incidents through video, the maintenance of impartiality, and the communication of outcomes to the Election Commission of India. By adhering to these principles, observers help to safeguard the democratic process, uphold the rule of law, and ensure that the Model Code of Conduct functions as an effective instrument for maintaining the integrity and fairness of elections throughout the country.

The Election Commission entrusts a group of specially designated officials, known as Expenditure Observers, with the responsibility of matching every permission that has been granted for campaign activities to the corresponding financial statements that have been filed by the candidates and their political parties. In practice this means that whenever a political organization seeks approval to hold a rally, to set up a booth, or to use any public resource in the course of its electioneering, the observer must locate the entry in the expense report that records the money spent for that permission and verify that the amount reported is consistent with the actual cost incurred. This systematic cross‑checking is intended to ensure that all outlays are transparent and that no hidden or unauthorized spending escapes scrutiny. If, after this comprehensive review, the observer discovers that a particular expenditure has not been disclosed in the submitted statements, the observer is required to apply the standard rates that are in force in the relevant district. These standard rates are predetermined cost benchmarks that reflect typical market prices for goods and services within that area, and they serve as a default valuation for any unreported spending, thereby preventing candidates from concealing the true scale of their financial outlays.

To carry out this monitoring function, the Commission creates a separate cadre of Expenditure Observers, each of whom is assigned to a specific constituency or group of constituencies. The observers operate under a detailed set of instructions that have been compiled into a dedicated manual, sometimes referred to as a guideline, which has been prepared by the Election Commission of India. This manual explains the procedural steps that observers must follow, the documentation they must collect, the analytical techniques they should employ, and the reporting formats they must use. Before beginning their field work, each observer is expected to study the manual thoroughly so that they are fully acquainted with the principles of expenditure monitoring, the legal thresholds for permissible spending, and the mechanisms for flagging violations. The manual also outlines the timelines for filing expense reports, the sanctions that may be imposed for non‑compliance, and the channels through which observers can seek clarification from the Commission’s central office.

Historical experience has shown that the influence of money in elections often begins even before the actual campaigning starts, notably at the stage when political parties distribute tickets to prospective candidates. The allocation of tickets can be used as a lever to reward loyalty or to secure the cooperation of individuals who are willing to channel resources to the party’s cause. Once the tickets have been handed out, the flow of money takes on a variety of forms that are enumerated in the Commission’s guidance, although the list is not exhaustive and observers are cautioned that other, less obvious methods may also be employed. One common practice involves appointing agents at individual polling booths whose explicit task is to purchase “floating” votes, meaning votes that can be swayed by monetary inducement rather than by party ideology. Another method consists of making large donations to clubs, community associations, or other organized groups with the intention of influencing the members of those groups to vote in a particular way. In a similar vein, parties may dispense generous sums to low‑level party workers, who in turn distribute portions of those funds directly to voters, thereby creating a chain of cash transfers that is difficult to trace.

Rallies and public meetings often feature hired crowds that are paid to attend and to cheer for the candidate, creating the illusion of widespread popular support. The organization of such events may involve renting people to fill the venue, a practice that inflates the apparent size of the audience and can be used to justify larger expenditures on venue hire, security, and logistics. Beyond formal rallies, candidates frequently appear at social occasions that are unrelated to politics, such as mass weddings, community feasts, religious ceremonies, night vigils, and inauguration events. At these gatherings, gifts may be presented on behalf of the candidate, serving both as a token of generosity and as a subtle means of building goodwill among the attendees. Acceptance of honors, felicitation awards, or invitations from educational institutions, charitable organizations, or cultural bodies also provides candidates with opportunities to project an image of civic involvement while subtly promoting their electoral ambitions.

In some constituencies, the distribution of free alcoholic beverages or the issuance of liquor passes to voters is employed as a direct inducement, exploiting the cultural significance of such items in certain communities. The use of “dummy” candidates—individuals who formally file nomination papers but have no intention of winning—can be a tactical move to make use of the quota of electioneering vehicles that are allotted to each candidate, thereby allowing the primary party to field a larger number of campaign vehicles than would otherwise be permissible. Advertising strategies also feature sophisticated ploys, such as surrogate advertisements in print media or electronic broadcasts, where the promotion of a candidate’s image is carried out by individuals or organizations that are not officially linked to the campaign. This approach enables the candidate to benefit from the publicity without having to record the associated costs in the official expenditure statement. Bringing popular cinema stars, well‑known musicians, or celebrated sportspeople to campaign events is another avenue through which parties seek to attract crowds and generate enthusiasm, often paying substantial sums to the celebrities while attempting to disguise the expense as part of a broader entertainment budget.

Finally, the provision of material items to voters—ranging from caps and vests to umbrellas, bicycles, and other useful goods—is a widely reported practice. By distributing such items, a candidate creates a tangible sense of personal benefit for the electorate, which can translate into votes. Each of these methods, whether overt or covert, contributes to the overall financial picture of an election campaign, and the observers are charged with the task of identifying, recording, and evaluating them in accordance with the guidelines set forth by the Commission. Their vigilant monitoring is essential to preserving the integrity of the electoral process, ensuring that the democratic principle of fair competition is not undermined by the unchecked deployment of financial resources.

The responsibility of the Observers, who are appointed by the Election Commission to oversee the conduct of elections, is fundamentally to guarantee that every directive issued by the Commission is applied with exacting precision and that no deviation, whether intentional or accidental, occurs in the way those directives are implemented. In order to fulfil this duty, it is essential that each Observer first become thoroughly acquainted with the body of instructions that are currently in force. These instructions are not hidden or dispersed in obscure files; rather, they are made publicly accessible through two principal channels. The first channel is the Observer Portal, an online platform specifically designed to provide Observers with the resources they need to carry out their monitoring functions. The second channel is the official website of the Election Commission of India, which hosts the same set of instructions and is regularly updated to reflect any amendments, new guidelines, or clarifications issued by the Commission. By consulting these sources, Observers can ensure that their understanding of the procedural requirements is both current and comprehensive, thereby enabling them to detect any irregularities that might arise during the electoral process.

The following discussion sets out, in an expanded and detailed form, the essential points and highlights of the various instructions that pertain specifically to the matter of election expenditure. These points are presented not merely as a checklist, but as a narrative that explains the legal foundations, the practical implications, and the procedural mechanisms that together create a robust framework for the transparent and accountable financing of electoral campaigns.

Section seventy‑seven of the Representation of the People Act, 1951 is the statutory provision that imposes the primary obligation on every candidate who wishes to contest an election. Under this section, a candidate—whether he chooses to manage his finances personally or delegates that task to an election agent—must maintain a distinct and accurate accounting record of every expense that is incurred in connection with the election. The period covered by this accounting requirement begins on the day the candidate files his nomination papers and ends on the day the election result is officially declared, with both the nomination date and the declaration date included within the accounting window. The purpose of this requirement is to create a complete financial trail that can be examined by the authorities and, if necessary, by the public, to verify that the candidate has complied with the legal limits on spending and has not engaged in prohibited financial practices such as the use of undisclosed funds or the receipt of illicit contributions.

Section seventy‑eight of the same Act builds on the accounting obligation by prescribing a deadline for the submission of the final expense account. Within thirty days after the election of the returned candidate—meaning the candidate who has been declared the winner—the contesting candidate must lodge, with the District Election Officer (the official responsible for overseeing the conduct of elections within a specific district), a full account of all election‑related expenses. This submitted account must be a true and faithful copy of the accounting record that the candidate or his election agent kept pursuant to section seventy‑seven. The thirty‑day window provides sufficient time for the candidate to reconcile receipts, verify the completeness of the record, and ensure that the account accurately reflects all inflows and outflows. Failure to meet this deadline or to submit a true copy of the account is treated as a serious breach of the law, as it hampers the Commission’s ability to monitor compliance with spending limits and to detect possible violations.

To aid the Commission and the Observers in monitoring election spending, the law further requires each candidate to open a dedicated bank account that is used exclusively for the purpose of handling election‑related funds. This bank account must be established at least one day before the candidate files his nomination papers, thereby ensuring that any monetary transaction that occurs after the nomination can be clearly traced to this specific account. All money that is intended for campaign activities—whether it originates from the candidate’s personal resources, from contributions made by supporters, or from any other source—must be deposited into this account. By centralising all campaign‑related funds in a single, identifiable account, the system reduces the likelihood that money will be concealed, mixed with personal finances, or moved through informal channels that are difficult to audit.

The requirement to maintain an account of election expenses applies even in circumstances where a candidate does not mount a serious campaign. For example, a candidate may withdraw his candidacy at a late stage, may decide not to contest the election actively, or may incur only a minimal outlay limited to the mandatory security deposit that must be paid to the Returning Officer. In such cases, the law still obliges the candidate to file an expense account, albeit one that reflects the limited financial activity. This provision prevents candidates from exploiting the system by appearing to spend nothing while still benefitting from the privileges of candidacy, and it ensures that the official record accurately captures every financial transaction associated with the electoral process.

The consequences for non‑compliance with the expense‑accounting requirements are severe. If a candidate fails to lodge the prescribed account within the statutory period, the candidate becomes liable to disqualification under section ten‑A of the Representation of the People Act, 1951. Disqualification under this provision bars the candidate from contesting any election for a period of three years. This three‑year ban serves both as a punitive measure and as a deterrent, signaling to all participants that adherence to financial transparency rules is a non‑negotiable condition of participation in the democratic process.

In order to standardise the manner in which candidates document and present their financial information, the Election Commission has issued a specific format for the register that each candidate must maintain. This register functions as a ledger, where each entry records a particular expense, the date on which it was incurred, the nature of the expense, and the amount spent. The register must be supported by documentary evidence such as vouchers, bills, receipts, and invoices. These supporting documents must be arranged in strict chronological order, which facilitates easy verification and prevents the possibility of retroactive insertion or deletion of entries. The Commission’s prescribed format ensures uniformity across all candidates, thereby simplifying the task of the Returning Officer and the designated observers who must review these records.

Alongside the register, the Commission also mandates the preparation of an “Abstract of Election Expenses.” This abstract is a summary sheet that consolidates the detailed entries from the register into aggregated totals for each category of expenditure, such as advertising, travel, staff wages, and office expenses. By completing this abstract, the candidate provides a concise overview that can be quickly assessed for compliance with the overall spending ceiling that applies to the particular election. The abstract serves as a bridge between the granular data in the register and the high‑level compliance checks performed by the election authorities.

In addition to the register and the abstract, candidates are required to submit an affidavit—a sworn statement—accompanying the “Register of day‑to‑day expenses” and the “Abstract of Expenses.” The affidavit is a legal document in which the candidate declares, under oath, that the information presented is true, complete, and accurate to the best of his knowledge. By signing the affidavit, the candidate affirms his personal responsibility for the veracity of the financial disclosures and acknowledges that any falsehood or omission may attract criminal liability.

The documents that the Commission prescribes—namely the register, the abstract, and the affidavit—must be made available to candidates in the official languages of the state or region where the election is being held. This means that the forms can be printed in Hindi, English, or any other locally approved language that is used for printing the electoral rolls. Providing the documents in the appropriate language ensures that all candidates, regardless of their linguistic background, can understand the requirements and comply with them correctly.

Every supporting voucher that records a day‑to‑day expense, such as a receipt for the purchase of campaign literature or a bill for a venue rental, must bear the full signature of either the contesting candidate or his election agent. The requirement for a full signature, as opposed to a partial or abbreviated mark, serves as a verification mechanism that ties each transaction directly to an authorised individual. This prevents the submission of forged or unauthenticated documents and creates a clear chain of accountability.

The register, together with the abstract and the accompanying affidavit, must be made accessible for inspection by the Returning Officer or a designated officer on three separate occasions prior to the polling day. The law stipulates that there must be a minimum interval of four days between each inspection, thereby providing sufficient time for the candidate to address any observations or queries raised during the earlier inspections. The first of these inspections may take place on or after the third day following the last date for withdrawal of nominations. This timing ensures that the candidate’s financial records are examined after the field of contesting candidates has been finalised, but well before the commencement of intensive campaigning, allowing any irregularities to be corrected in a timely manner.

When a candidate contests elections in more than one constituency—a situation that occasionally occurs in certain types of elections—he is required to maintain a distinct set of accounts for each constituency. Each set of accounts must be prepared in accordance with the same rules, and the candidate must lodge a separate expense register, abstract, and affidavit for each constituency. This segregation prevents the commingling of funds across constituencies and enables the Election Commission to monitor spending limits on a constituency‑by‑constituency basis.

Once the candidate has submitted the required financial records, the Returning Officer or the Designated Officer assigned to scrutinise the accounts must create two photocopies of the relevant pages of the register. One of these copies is to be displayed publicly on the notice board maintained by the Returning Officer, thereby providing transparency and allowing any interested party—such as political parties, media, or members of the public—to view the expense details. The second copy is retained in a separate file that is kept as a formal record for each constituency. This file serves as an official archive that can be consulted in the event of a dispute, an audit, or a legal challenge. At the conclusion of the entire electoral process, the Returning Officer must forward these records to the District Election Officer as part of the final submission of election‑related documents.

Finally, the law provides a mechanism to address the situation in which a candidate refuses or neglects to produce the register of daily election expenses when duly summoned by the Designated Officer or observer. If the candidate does not comply with the notice to produce the register, the District Election Officer is empowered to lodge a formal complaint under section one hundred seventy‑one‑one of the Indian Penal Code. Section 171‑1 deals with the offence of “dishonestly making a false statement, or fabricating false evidence,” and its invocation underscores the seriousness with which the legal system treats the concealment or falsification of election‑related financial information. The complaint may lead to criminal prosecution, further reinforcing the principle that transparency in election financing is a cornerstone of a fair democratic process.

In sum, the network of duties imposed on Observers, the detailed accounting obligations placed on candidates, and the stringent enforcement mechanisms established by the Election Commission together constitute a comprehensive system designed to safeguard the integrity of electoral financing. By familiarising themselves with the instructions, by monitoring compliance at each stage—from the opening of the dedicated bank account to the final submission of audited registers—Observers play a pivotal role in ensuring that the democratic process remains free from the distortions that can arise from undisclosed or excessive campaign spending. The elaborate procedural safeguards, ranging from mandatory signatures on vouchers to multiple scheduled inspections, create multiple layers of oversight that make it increasingly difficult for any irregularities to go undetected. Consequently, the framework not only promotes transparency and accountability but also reinforces public confidence in the legitimacy of the electoral outcomes.

Any individual who wishes to obtain a copy of the day‑to‑day accounts that are kept by the Returning Officer in connection with a particular election may do so, but such a request is subject to the payment of the normal copying charges that are ordinarily levied for the reproduction of official documents. The requirement that the applicant pay these standard fees ensures that the administrative costs of providing the copies are recovered and that the process does not become a burden on the election machinery. In practice, the applicant must submit a written request to the Returning Officer, specifying the particular set of accounts desired, and the officer will then prepare the copies and charge the applicant according to the prevailing schedule of fees for paper, printing, and any other material costs.

A candidate who is contesting an election is required to keep a detailed register of all expenses incurred in the conduct of the campaign. This register is not limited to the expenditures that arise after the candidate’s nomination has been formally accepted; rather, it must also include any costs that were incurred before the nomination date if those costs relate to the preparation of campaign material, the creation of propaganda, or any other activity that is subsequently used during the post‑nomination phase of the election. For example, if a candidate commissions the design and printing of pamphlets, posters, or digital content prior to the filing of the nomination paper, those outlays must be recorded in the register because the material will be deployed after the nomination is officially lodged. The purpose of this comprehensive accounting requirement is to give the Election Commission a full picture of the financial resources that support the candidate’s electioneering, thereby preventing the concealment of early‑stage spending that could otherwise give an unfair advantage.

The travel expenses that are borne by senior leaders of a political party when they move from place to place to promote the party’s programme are, under the rules, not to be treated as part of the election‑related expenditure that is attributable to any particular candidate or to the candidate’s election agent. This exemption applies provided that the party communicates, within seven days of the official notification of the election, the names of those leaders whose travel costs are being claimed. The communication must be sent both to the Election Commission of India and to the Chief Electoral Officer (CEO) of the State in which the election is being held. By furnishing the names within the stipulated period, the party establishes a clear record that the travel was undertaken in the capacity of a party functionary rather than as a direct campaign activity for a specific candidate, and the expenses can therefore be excluded from the candidate’s financial statements.

If, however, the political party fails to provide the required list of leaders within the seven‑day window, the law treats the situation differently. In such a case the travel costs incurred by all party leaders, regardless of whether they were intended for general party promotion or for a particular candidate’s campaign, are automatically deemed to be part of the election expenses of the candidate concerned. Consequently, those costs must be recorded in the candidate’s account of election expenditure and will be taken into account when assessing whether the candidate has exceeded the permissible limit of campaign spending. The rule is intended to encourage parties to be prompt and transparent in reporting the identities of the individuals whose travel is being funded, thereby preventing inadvertent or deliberate inclusion of unreported expenses in a candidate’s account.

The Supreme Court of India, in the landmark judgment of Kanwar Lai Gupta versus Amarnath Chawla (reported in the All India Reporter, 1975, Supreme Court, page 308), clarified the distinction between expenditure that is genuinely incurred by a political party for its own general propaganda and expenditure that can be directly linked to the election of a particular candidate. The Court held that any spending by the party that can be identified with the election of the specific candidate, even if it is not formally recorded as the candidate’s own outlay, must be treated as if it had been authorized by the candidate and therefore added to the candidate’s total election expenditure. The judgment further emphasized that a candidate does not operate in isolation from the party; if the party wishes to protect the candidate from the risk of disqualification for exceeding the spending ceiling, it must exercise effective control over any direct or indirect spending that promotes the candidate’s electoral prospects. This principle of implied authorization means that the candidate is responsible for party‑sponsored activities that are clearly intended to benefit his or her campaign, and the election authorities will aggregate such costs into the candidate’s financial statement for purposes of compliance.

The costs associated with the provision of bullet‑proof vehicles and other specially equipped automobiles that are used by ministers at the Union and State levels, as well as by senior leaders of political parties, are also to be borne by the candidates whose campaigns benefit from those vehicles. In other words, if a candidate’s election campaign makes use of a bullet‑proof car for security or for transporting the candidate and his entourage, the expense of acquiring, maintaining, or operating that vehicle is to be recorded as part of the candidate’s election expenses. The same principle applies to any other cars that are employed by political leaders in the context of a campaign, ensuring that the financial burden of security arrangements does not escape the limits imposed on election spending.

All motorised transport that is used by a candidate for the purpose of conducting the election campaign—whether it is a two‑wheeler, a motor‑bike, a scooter, a moped, or any other type of vehicle—must be lodged with the District Election Officer (DEO). Lodging in this context means that the candidate is required to formally register the vehicle with the election administration, providing details such as the make, model, registration number, and the intended period of use. This registration allows the DEO to keep a record of the vehicle’s deployment, to verify that it is being used in accordance with election law, and to ensure that any associated fuel, maintenance, or depreciation costs are properly accounted for in the candidate’s financial statements.

Whenever a political party or an individual candidate wishes to employ an aircraft or a helicopter as part of an election campaign—perhaps to travel quickly between distant constituencies, to transport campaign staff, or to carry out aerial publicity—prior notice must be given to the Chief Electoral Officer of the relevant state. The notice must contain specific pieces of information that enable the election authorities to monitor the use of such expensive and high‑profile transport. These details include the number of aircraft or helicopters that will be used, the names of the companies from which they are hired, the total hire charges that have been paid or are payable, a description of the geographic areas that will be covered by the flights, the total number of sorties or individual flight missions that will be undertaken, and a passenger manifest listing the names of all individuals who will be on board each flight. By collecting this data, the CEO can assess whether the aerial movements constitute a legitimate campaign activity, can verify that the associated costs are recorded in the appropriate candidate’s accounts, and can guard against the misuse of such resources for purposes unrelated to the election.

Advertising expenditures that are incurred by a political party in connection with the election of a particular candidate, or in connection with a group of candidates running under the same party banner, are treated as expenses that have been authorized by the candidates themselves. Consequently, the costs of newspaper advertisements, television spots, radio announcements, online banner ads, and any other media placements that promote the candidacy of a specific individual or a slate of candidates must be entered into the election expense accounts of those candidates. If the party makes a single payment that benefits a group of candidates—such as a joint television campaign that mentions several party members—then the total amount spent is to be divided equally among all the candidates who are covered by the advertisement. This method of apportionment ensures that each candidate bears a proportionate share of the collective promotional effort and that the overall spending limit is respected on an individual basis.

When the government, through its various security agencies, constructs barricades, rostrums, or other temporary structures for the purpose of safeguarding a political rally, these costs are considered part of the election expenditure of the candidate whose constituency hosts the event. If the rally is addressed by a party leader and several candidates from the same party are present, the cost of the security‑related construction is to be divided equally among all those candidates. The District Election Officer (DEO) of the district where the rally takes place is responsible for obtaining a final invoice or cost statement from the relevant government agency within three days of the event. The DEO then communicates each candidate’s share of the expenditure to the candidates themselves, and also informs the Returning Officer or District Election Officer of any other constituencies where additional candidates from the same party may have been present. This rapid reporting mechanism ensures that the financial impact of security arrangements is promptly reflected in the candidates’ accounts.

If the expenses for such security structures are financed directly from the funds of the rally’s organizer—meaning that the party or its campaign committee pays out of its own coffers rather than relying on a government allocation—then the organizer’s outlay is likewise reflected in the election accounts of the candidate or group of candidates who were present at the rally. In effect, any money that the party spends to create the physical environment for a campaign event becomes part of the financial picture that the Election Commission reviews in order to enforce the statutory limits on election spending.

Finally, the Election Commission has issued a specific clarification regarding the small booths, kiosks, or stands that candidates set up outside polling stations on the day of voting. These structures, although they may appear modest, are now expressly treated as part of the individual candidate’s campaign rather than as a component of general party propaganda. As a result, all costs incurred in erecting, furnishing, staffing, or maintaining these candidate‑specific booths must be recorded as expenses authorized by the candidate or his or her election agent. Such costs are therefore included in the candidate’s total election expenditure and are not eligible for any exemption that might apply to broader party‑wide promotional activities. This rule ensures that even the seemingly minor expenditures associated with the final day of the election are captured in the overall accounting, thereby maintaining the integrity and transparency of the electoral finance system.

The election administration of each district will be strengthened by the creation of a dedicated Expenditure Monitoring Control Room that includes a continuously operating call centre. From the moment the election is officially announced, a telephone service will be available around the clock, seven days a week, to receive reports of any corrupt or illegal activity connected with the conduct of the poll. The call centre will be equipped with a toll‑free telephone number that is easy for members of the public to dial without incurring any cost. In order to handle a large volume of calls and to prevent a single line from becoming a bottleneck, the service will be provided with three or four “hunting” lines – a technical arrangement in which several telephone circuits are linked together so that an incoming call is automatically directed to the first free line. The number will be widely advertised through posters, flyers, radio announcements and electronic media, ensuring that any voter, election official, or observer who becomes aware of bribery, intimidation, vote‑buying, or any other form of malpractice can promptly report it. A senior officer, appointed specifically to manage the control room and call centre, will assume overall responsibility for the operation. This officer’s duties will include receiving each complaint, documenting the details accurately, and forwarding the information without delay to the appropriate district or sub‑district official who has the authority to investigate and take corrective action. To guarantee that the service does not lapse at any time, the call centre will be staffed with a sufficient number of trained operators who will be scheduled to man the telephone lines at all hours, including night shifts and holidays. The operators will be instructed on how to record the identity of the caller, the nature of the complaint, the location and time of the alleged wrongdoing, and any supporting evidence that may be offered. Their records will be entered into a centralized database that can be accessed by the election officials responsible for follow‑up, thereby creating an audit trail that can be reviewed by higher authorities if necessary.

In parallel with the call centre, each district will establish a specialized media cell that operates under the direction of the District Election Officer, commonly abbreviated as DEO. The media cell will be headed by a Public Relations Officer, a professional whose primary role is to manage communication between the election machinery and the press, as well as to ensure that accurate and timely information reaches the electorate. One of the chief functions of this cell will be the systematic collection of newspaper clippings, broadcast transcripts, and advertisements that pertain to the election. This collection will cover all printed media, including national dailies, regional newspapers, and vernacular language publications that are read by local communities. Whenever a clipping appears in a language that is not understood by the central monitoring team, the media cell will provide a translation so that the content can be evaluated by the observers who are monitoring compliance with the Model Code of Conduct, commonly referred to as MCC. The process of gathering these materials will be carried out on a daily basis, ensuring that the observers have up‑to‑date information about public discourse, campaign messaging, and any potential violations. The liaison officer, who is stationed in the field and acts as the point of contact between the media cell and the observers, may be tasked with retrieving and forwarding these clippings. To compensate for the additional travel and effort involved, an allowance will be provided to the liaison officer in accordance with the prevailing rules for field assignments.

Another important responsibility of the district media cell will be the preparation and dissemination of daily briefing notes that summarize the steps taken by election officials during the day to address any breaches of the Model Code of Conduct. These notes will be written in a neutral tone and will not contain any direct quotations from the observers, thereby preserving the independence of the monitoring function while still informing the press of the actions being taken. By issuing these briefings, the media cell helps to maintain transparency and to demonstrate that any allegations of misconduct are being taken seriously and investigated promptly. In addition, the media cell will be tasked with ensuring that all public meetings held by candidates, political parties, or their supporters are recorded on video. The videography teams will capture the speeches, crowd interactions, and any incidents that may arise, after which the recordings will be transmitted to the observers for review. This visual evidence assists the observers in identifying any violations of the Model Code of Conduct, such as the use of inflammatory language, distribution of prohibited goods, or attempts to influence voters through intimidation. The media cell therefore serves as both a conduit for information flow to the public and a support mechanism for the observers who are tasked with safeguarding the integrity of the electoral process.

The preparation of polling personnel and their subsequent training constitute a critical component of a transparent and credible election. To that end, the Election Commission has devised a three‑stage randomisation procedure that governs how polling staff are selected, assigned to specific constituencies, and finally placed at individual polling stations. In the first stage, the Commission draws a random sample from a comprehensive database that contains the names of every government employee within the district who is eligible for election duties. This database includes teachers, clerks, police personnel, and other civil servants whose regular duties can be temporarily reassigned to election work. The random selection is intended to eliminate any possibility of bias or manipulation in the appointment of polling staff, thereby reinforcing public confidence in the neutrality of the election workforce. Although the randomisation is completed before the arrival of the observers, the observers are required to examine the resulting list to verify that the process was indeed random and that no irregularities are evident.

The second stage of randomisation concerns the formation of polling teams and the allocation of each team to a particular Assembly Constituency, which is the electoral district for state‑level legislative elections. The composition of each team—typically consisting of a presiding officer, a first officer, and a second officer—will be determined through a second random draw, ensuring that the teams are not pre‑arranged in a manner that could favor any political party. The third and final stage occurs just before the polling personnel are dispatched to the field, usually on the day preceding the election. At this point, each polling team is assigned to a specific polling station, which is the physical location—such as a school or community hall—where voters will cast their ballots. For the election to the Lok Sabha, which is the lower house of the national Parliament, the Commission has issued a directive that polling staff should, as far as practicable, be randomised within the boundaries of the Parliamentary Constituency to which they belong. This arrangement enables the staff members to cast their own votes using the Electronic Direct Count (EDC) system at their designated polling stations, thereby preserving their right to participate in the election while also ensuring that they are not placed in a location where they might be subject to undue influence.

The effectiveness of the entire polling operation hinges on the quality and comprehensiveness of the training that the polling staff receive. Observers must closely monitor the training sessions to confirm that all personnel who have been appointed for poll duty have indeed undergone the required instruction. The trainers themselves must possess an in‑depth understanding of the electoral procedures, the legal framework, and the technical aspects of the voting equipment. It is considered a best practice to appoint Sector Officers—senior officials who oversee specific functional areas of the election machinery—as trainers, because they bring both experience and authority to the instructional process. Training should be conducted in relatively small groups rather than large assemblies, a method that encourages interaction, allows questions to be addressed promptly, and ensures that each participant can focus on the material without being lost in a crowd. The curriculum must cover every facet of poll management, ranging from the operation of Electronic Voting Machines (EVMs) and the associated Voter‑Verifiable Paper Audit Trail (VVPAT) devices, to the handling of non‑electronic tasks such as the completion of forms, issuance of certificates, and maintenance of security protocols at the polling station. Particular emphasis should be placed on the most recent instructions issued by the Election Commission, because these updates often contain critical procedural changes or clarifications that the polling staff would not otherwise be aware of. By ensuring that training is thorough, up‑to‑date, and accessible to all polling personnel, the observers help to create a polling environment that is both efficient and resistant to fraud.

A central element of the modern Indian electoral system is the use of Electronic Voting Machines, commonly abbreviated as EVMs, which have largely replaced the traditional paper ballot system. In addition to the electronic ballot unit (the part of the machine that records the vote), each EVM is equipped with a Control Unit that is operated by the polling staff and a Voter‑Verifiable Paper Audit Trail (VVPAT) device that produces a printed slip for each vote cast, allowing voters to confirm that their selection has been recorded correctly. Observers assigned to a district are required to verify that a sufficient stock of EVMs and VVPAT units, which have been certified as fit for use (indicated by the “FLC‑OK” marking), is present and accounted for. This inventory check helps to prevent shortages or tampering that could compromise the voting process.

The Election Commission also issues detailed guidelines to Chief Electoral Officers and District Election Officers concerning the training and awareness programmes that must be conducted for the use of EVMs. These guidelines are primarily digital, reflecting the Commission’s emphasis on electronic dissemination of information. Observers must ascertain whether the prescribed training programmes have actually been implemented, and whether the participants—including presiding officers, polling officers, and especially the third polling officer who is in charge of the Control Unit on the day of the poll—have attained a functional understanding of how to operate the machines. The third polling officer’s role is particularly critical because the Control Unit governs the overall functioning of the EVM, including the activation of the ballot unit, the verification of results, and the secure storage of the machines after voting concludes. If this officer lacks proper training, the integrity of the entire electronic voting process could be jeopardised.

Finally, observers should familiarize themselves with the latest set of instructions that the Election Commission releases concerning the operation, handling, and security of EVMs and VVPATs. These instructions may include technical specifications, procedural checklists for the pre‑poll, poll, and post‑poll phases, and emergency protocols to be followed in the event of a machine malfunction. By staying well‑versed in these directives, observers are better equipped to identify any deviations from standard operating procedures and to report them promptly, thereby contributing to a transparent, accountable, and trustworthy election.

The voter‑verifiable paper audit trail, commonly abbreviated as VVPAT, was first introduced into the Indian electoral system in the year two thousand thirteen with the express purpose of enhancing the transparency and credibility of the voting process. In practical terms, the VVPAT is an auxiliary device that is physically attached to each electronic voting machine, or EVM, and its function is to produce a tangible, printed record of the choice made by the elector. When a voter presses the button corresponding to his or her preferred candidate on the ballot unit of the EVM, the VVPAT automatically generates a small slip of paper on which the name of the candidate, the party symbol, and a unique serial number are printed. This printed slip is then displayed through a transparent viewing window for a brief interval of seven seconds, allowing the voter to see that the electronic system has correctly captured his or her selection. After this short viewing period the slip is automatically cut and deposited into a sealed compartment that is built into the VVPAT unit. The sealed compartment is designed to be tamper‑evident, and the collection of paper slips stored within it serves as a permanent audit trail that can be consulted after the polling has concluded. In the event of a dispute or a routine verification, election officials may retrieve these printed records and cross‑check them against the electronic tallies recorded by the EVMs, following the specific procedures laid down by the Election Commission of India, often referred to by its abbreviation ECI. The presence of the printed slip therefore adds an additional layer of confirmation for the voter, complementing the visual cue of a glowing light‑emitting diode (LED) that illuminates near the candidate’s button on the ballot unit, as well as the audible beep that the electronic machine emits each time a vote is cast.

To guard against any perception or reality of manipulation, the Election Commission has instituted a systematic randomisation process for the allocation of both electronic voting machines and their attached VVPAT devices. This randomisation is carried out in two distinct stages. The first stage occurs before any election observers are allowed onto the premises; at this point, each EVM‑VVPAT pair is assigned to a particular parliamentary or assembly constituency in a manner that is designed to be unpredictable and free from external influence. The second stage takes place later, in the full presence of the contesting candidates, their authorized representatives, and any accredited observers, and involves the further allocation of each machine to a specific polling station within the constituency. By splitting the allocation into two phases and by ensuring that the final stage is conducted under the watchful eyes of all interested parties, the commission seeks to eliminate any suspicion that a particular machine might have been pre‑programmed or otherwise tampered with to favour a specific candidate or party.

The commissioning of the electronic voting machines and their accompanying VVPAT units is another critical step that is performed under strict supervision. The Returning Officer, who is the senior election official responsible for the conduct of the poll in a given constituency, oversees the commissioning in the presence of the contesting candidates or their duly authorised representatives. During this ceremony the machines are powered up, a series of functional tests are carried out, and a set of sample VVPAT slips is printed to demonstrate that the audit trail mechanism is operational. The Election Commission’s instructions, which are issued as detailed procedural manuals, must be followed to the letter. An important security measure during commissioning is that any VVPAT slips generated for testing purposes are destroyed immediately after the test is completed; the standard practice is to shred these slips on a daily basis so that no extraneous paper records remain that could be later used to infer the configuration of the machines.

A further element of the pre‑poll preparation involves the Symbol Loading Unit, abbreviated as SLU, which is the device used to load the party symbols, candidate names, and serial numbers onto the electronic voting machines. Throughout the commissioning period, which typically spans several days in each assembly constituency or segment, all SLUs together with their ancillary accessories are placed under the custody of the Returning Officer or the Assistant Returning Officer, depending on the local organisational structure. The SLUs are stored in a secure location within the commissioning premises to prevent unauthorised access. Once the symbol‑loading process for a constituency has been completed, the SLUs that were used are again stored according to the protocols prescribed by the Election Commission. These protocols dictate the physical security arrangements, the chain‑of‑custody documentation, and the eventual disposal or reuse of the units. As with the commissioning of the machines themselves, the instructions issued by the Election Commission must be adhered to precisely, ensuring that no irregularities arise from the handling of the symbol‑loading equipment.

Preparing for the actual date of polling involves a host of preventive measures aimed at curbing electoral malpractice. The most serious threats to a free and fair election include the illegal capture of polling booths, the rigging of vote counts, and the creation of an atmosphere of fear or panic among the electorate that might deter people from exercising their franchise. Such illicit tactics differ from one state to another and even from one constituency to another, reflecting local political cultures and the tactics of unscrupulous actors. Observers appointed by the Election Commission are expected to become familiar with the specific tricks of the trade that are prevalent in the areas where they will be deployed. They must identify neighborhoods, polling stations, or community hotspots that have historically been prone to intimidation, violence, or other forms of electoral disruption. In order to maximise the effectiveness of their oversight, observers are required to coordinate amongst themselves and to develop a confidential plan that delineates the particular locations they intend to monitor on the day of the poll. This plan is to be kept strictly secret and must not be disclosed to anyone, including the District Election Officers, the Returning Officers, the escort officers, liaison officers, or the police staff on site. The secrecy of the plan helps to prevent any advance warning that could be used by potential wrongdoers to evade observation or to stage disruptive activities elsewhere.

Another important aspect of election logistics concerns the dispatch centres, which serve as the hubs from which polling parties—groups of election officials, security personnel, and support staff—are sent out to their assigned polling stations. Observers are tasked with visiting these dispatch centres prior to the commencement of voting in order to assess the manner in which the dispatch operations are being carried out. Their brief reports focus especially on whether the formation of polling parties follows a randomised pattern, as mandated by the Election Commission, thereby preventing any systematic bias or collusion in the assignment of staff to particular polling stations. By verifying that the randomisation of polling parties is faithfully implemented, observers help to safeguard the neutrality of the election workforce.

Micro observers form a distinct class of election monitors who are drawn from the ranks of central government employees and personnel of public sector undertakings. Their primary role is to observe the conduct of the poll on the actual voting day, and they are assigned to individual polling stations where they become part of the polling team. Unlike senior observers, micro observers are positioned at the grassroots level, seated within the polling station itself, and are required to be present before the commencement of the mock poll, which is the trial run conducted to ensure that all equipment is functioning correctly. After the poll concludes, each micro observer must complete a standardized report, known as the “Report of Micro Observers,” detailing any irregularities, procedural lapses, or incidents of concern that they witnessed. The training of these micro observers takes place two days before the poll, under the guidance of senior observers and with the assistance of the Returning Officer. This training equips them with the knowledge of polling procedures, the identification of potential malpractices, and the correct method of completing their observation report. Because the number of critical polling stations is limited, micro observers are deployed strategically rather than being spread thinly across every location. In practice, they are often positioned at a central hub on the day of the election and then dispatched to specific stations as the need arises—such as when a polling station lacks a regular polling agent, when a location is deemed particularly sensitive, or when remote geography makes it difficult for senior observers to reach. In some instances, micro observers may travel together with the polling parties themselves, accompanying them to remote or difficult‑to‑access polling stations to ensure that the election staff are supervised throughout the entire process. By concentrating their limited resources on the most vulnerable or strategically important polling stations, the election administration seeks to achieve a high level of oversight without overburdening the system with unnecessary personnel.

30. SPECIAL OBSERVERS

When circumstances that are deemed extraordinary arise during the conduct of an election, the Election Commission of India (ECI) has the authority to dispatch a cadre of individuals known as Special Observers. These observers are selected for their expertise, impartiality, or particular knowledge of the political environment in a given area, and they are assigned a precise agenda that is drawn up by the ECI. Although they are given a specific set of tasks, Special Observers are considered to be on an equal footing with the ordinary observers who are posted in the various polling constituencies. In practice this means that they share the same rights of access to polling stations, the same ability to move about the constituency, and the same protection under the law as any other observer. Their chain of command, however, is distinct: they report directly to the central authority of the Election Commission rather than to any district‑level officer, and they do not exercise any supervisory power over the regular observers. To fulfil their mandate effectively, Special Observers must acquire a comprehensive picture of the on‑the‑ground situation. For this reason they are required to engage in discussions with, and to obtain information from, the other observers who are operating in the same district or constituency. By sharing observations, exchanging notes on voter behaviour, and learning about any irregularities that have been reported, the Special Observers can form a more accurate assessment of the electoral climate and can then relay their findings directly to the Commission for timely intervention.

31. POLL DAY ACTIVITIES

One of the most critical duties that observers—whether ordinary or special—must perform on the day of the poll is to supervise the actual voting process itself. On the designated polling date, every observer who is present in a constituency is expected to visit as many polling booths as physically possible during the hours that the polls are open. Because the number of booths can be large and the time limited, observers coordinate among themselves to decide which specific polling stations each of them will attend, thereby ensuring that there is no unnecessary duplication of effort and that coverage is maximised. In order to make the most efficient use of the limited time, observers often carry packed meals with them so that they do not have to return to a base for food, and they commence their field visits well before the official start of voting. The visible presence of these officials in the field serves as a powerful deterrent against any attempts at electoral malpractice, such as ballot stuffing, intimidation of voters, or tampering with voting machines, because potential wrongdoers know that their actions are being watched by impartial representatives of the Commission. While moving from booth to booth, observers maintain a constant line of communication with the District Administration, using any of the available means—telephone calls, wireless devices, very high frequency (VHF) radio sets, or other approved channels—to report observations, request assistance, or seek clarification on procedural matters.

In addition to the supervision of the actual voting, the Commission has instituted a requirement that a mock poll be conducted before the real poll begins. The purpose of this rehearsal is to demonstrate that the Electronic Voting Machines (EVMs) and the Voter‑Verifiable Paper Audit Trail devices (VVPATs) are functioning correctly, and to give the polling agents an opportunity to become familiar with the operation of the equipment. The Presiding Officer at each polling station is required to sign a certificate, formatted in the manner prescribed by the Commission, confirming that the mock poll was carried out and that the machines performed satisfactorily. Observers are tasked with verifying that this mock‑poll procedure has indeed taken place and that the appropriate certificate has been issued. Equally important is the requirement that any electronic result generated during the mock poll be erased from the machine’s control unit and that any paper slips produced by the VVPAT be removed, so that no residual data from the rehearsal can contaminate the actual election results.

During the course of the day, observers must remain vigilant for any unusual patterns of activity—or the lack thereof—around polling stations, because such anomalies can be early indicators of attempts to subvert the integrity of the election. For example, a conspicuous absence of women in the voting queue may suggest that intimidation or other forms of coercion are at work. To recognize such signs, observers are advised to study the historical record of electoral malpractices in the region, to understand which kinds of irregularities have been observed in past elections, and to use that knowledge to spot tell‑tale signs of misconduct. Throughout the polling hours they maintain continuous contact with the Returning Officer (RO), the Assistant Returning Officer (ARO), sector magistrates, and any other officials on duty. This communication channel serves two purposes: it allows observers to receive timely feedback about incidents that have been reported by other officials, and it enables them to convey specific observations that may require corrective action by the authorities, such as the deployment of additional security personnel or the investigation of a suspected case of voter intimidation.

Observers are also permitted to enter the interior of polling stations to monitor the progress of the vote count and to verify that the prescribed procedures are being followed by the polling officials. This includes checking that the polling agents representing each contesting candidate are present, that the Presiding Officer’s diary is being updated accurately with entries such as the number of voters who have cast their ballots, and that the Register of Voters (Form 17A) matches the tally displayed on the EVM. When an observer completes an inspection of a booth, he or she signs the official visit sheet, records the exact time of the visit, and notes any observations that may be relevant for later reporting. Special care is required at stations where only a single candidate’s agent is present, because the lack of multiple agents can increase the risk of procedural violations.

Beyond the act of voting, observers have a responsibility to oversee the secure handling of the voting equipment after the polls close. This involves taking stock of the collected EVMs and VVPATs, ensuring that they are packaged correctly, and verifying that the transport convoys that move the machines from the polling stations to the designated collection points do so under appropriate security arrangements. Once a convoy has departed from a polling station, it is not to make any unscheduled stops; its sole destination is the strong‑room where the machines are to be stored pending the counting process. The integrity of the chain of custody is reinforced by the requirement that the Presiding Officer complete Form 17C, a detailed record of the equipment and documents received, in a completely accurate manner. This form, together with the Presiding Officer’s diary, must be deposited at the collection centre and stored in the strong‑room, forming part of the official evidence trail for the election.

32. RECEPTION OF POLLING PARTIES

On the day following the polling, particular attention must be given to certain polling stations that may have experienced disruptions or raised concerns. Three categories of stations are singled out for special scrutiny: those where the polling process was temporarily halted because of a malfunction of an EVM or any other technical issue; those where serious complaints have been lodged by voters, agents, or officials; and those where the certificate confirming the conduct of the mock poll has not been received. The Returning Officer, who is the chief election official for the constituency, is required to compile a detailed report that lists each of these affected stations by name and by the official station number. This report must be transmitted to the District Election Officer (DEO) and to the relevant observer no later than four o’clock in the afternoon on the same day. In order to preserve the integrity of the election materials associated with these problem stations, the EVMs, VVPATs, and all accompanying documents must be received not through the ordinary public counters but via a specially designated counter set up for this purpose. This procedural safeguard helps to prevent tampering and ensures that the documents are handled by personnel who are aware of the sensitivity of the case. Finally, the Presiding Officer’s diaries for these stations must be examined meticulously, because they contain the chronological record of events, observations, and actions taken on the day of the poll, and any discrepancies in those diaries could be indicative of procedural breaches that need to be investigated further.

At the reception centre, which is the designated place where election equipment and related documentation are initially received and temporarily stored, it is essential that a copy of Form 17‑C be kept in close physical proximity to the corresponding electronic voting machine (EVM). Form 17‑C is the official record that identifies the specific EVM assigned to a particular polling station; it contains details such as the serial number of the machine, the polling station code, and the date of the election. To protect this critical document from loss, damage, or unauthorised access, the copy of Form 17‑C must be placed inside the strong‑room that houses the EVM. A strong‑room is a reinforced, lockable enclosure that meets security specifications laid down by the Election Commission, designed to prevent tampering or theft of election material. While the Form 17‑C copy may remain together with the EVM, no other paperwork—such as diaries of the presiding officer, voter registers, or observation reports—should be stored inside the same strong‑room. All ancillary documents must be kept in a different, also secure, strong‑room that is physically separate from the one containing the EVMs. This separation facilitates quick and convenient access to the documents when they are required for verification, while still maintaining the integrity of the voting machines themselves.

The scrutiny of the Register of Voters and other election‑related documents is a systematic exercise carried out after the conclusion of polling at selected polling stations. The Register of Voters, commonly referred to as Form 17A, is a ledger that lists every eligible voter in the constituency, along with identifying particulars such as name, age, and address. In addition to Form 17A, other critical records include the diaries maintained by the presiding officers (the officials responsible for overseeing the conduct of the poll at each station), the reports generated by micro‑observers (individuals tasked with monitoring the polling process on a very granular level), the visit sheets that document the movements of election officials, and the reports submitted by Zonal Magistrates, who are judicial officers assigned to supervise the legal aspects of the election in a particular zone. The Election Commission has instituted a policy aimed at deterring electoral malpractices by mandating that these documents be examined in detail after the poll has ended. The selection of polling stations for scrutiny is not random; instead, it follows a detailed set of criteria formulated by the Commission, which may consider factors such as the history of complaints, the demographic profile of the electorate, or any irregularities reported on the day of voting. Once a polling station has been earmarked for scrutiny, the examination of the documents is scheduled to begin at eleven o’clock in the morning on the day immediately following the poll. This timing ensures that the documents are still fresh, that any observations made by poll officials are still vivid in memory, and that any discrepancies can be identified while the evidence is still intact.

The observer assigned to a polling station must adhere to a travel plan that guarantees a minimum stay of thirty‑six hours after the poll has been completed. This requirement is stipulated by the Commission to ensure that the observer remains on site long enough to oversee the entire post‑poll process, which includes the sealing of the EVMs, the transportation of the machines to the counting centre, and the handling of any immediate post‑poll complaints. By remaining in the constituency for at least a day and a half after the poll, the observer can verify that all procedures are being followed correctly and can respond promptly to any emerging issues.

When it comes to the possibility of a re‑poll, the observer’s report becomes the most decisive piece of evidence that the Election Commission relies upon to determine whether a fresh poll is warranted. A re‑poll is a repeat of the voting process at a particular polling station, ordered when the original poll is judged to have been compromised by incidents such as violence, technical failure of the voting machines, or any other irregularity that could have affected the free and fair expression of the voters’ will. Because the decision to order a re‑poll can have significant political and logistical consequences, observers are required to be especially vigilant and alert to any incident or activity—whether observed directly or reported by reliable third parties—that might have vitiated, that is, nullified or weakened, the integrity of the original poll. In such circumstances, the observer must draft a specific, detailed report for the Commission, describing the nature of the incident, the time and location at which it occurred, the parties involved, and any evidence that supports the claim of a compromised poll.

Occasionally, information that could affect the assessment of a polling station’s conduct reaches the Commission from sources other than the observer on the ground. In such cases, the Secretary or Deputy Election Commissioner may contact the observer by telephone and request clarification, additional verification, or an on‑the‑spot enquiry. The observer is expected to respond promptly, making use of whatever resources are available within the limited time frame, and to provide a reasoned answer that the Commission can rely upon. After taking into account the observer’s report together with any other inputs—such as the findings of micro‑observers, police reports, and media coverage—the Commission decides whether a re‑poll is necessary, and if so, which polling stations will be subject to it. The ultimate goal is to ensure that the re‑poll is conducted in a manner that is free from the same problems that led to its ordering.

The conduct of the re‑poll itself must be free from any influence that could vitiate, or invalidate, the outcome. To achieve this, the observer is tasked with providing a comprehensive, specific report on how the re‑poll was carried out. Normally, the re‑poll is scheduled for the second day after the original polling date, unless the Commission specifies a different timeline because of special circumstances, such as logistical constraints or security concerns. In the event that a re‑poll is ordered, several procedural safeguards must be observed. First, the electronic voting machines and the associated Voter‑Verified Paper Audit Trail (VVPAT) devices that will be used in the re‑poll must be drawn from a reserve stock that has not been previously used. This reserve list is maintained precisely to ensure that fresh, uncompromised equipment is available for any contingency. Once the appropriate machines have been selected, the Election Commission must inform all candidates and their agents of the re‑poll, providing them with the necessary details so that they can make appropriate arrangements for their agents and supporters.

Each of the machines designated for the re‑poll must bear an address tag that clearly indicates that they are intended for a re‑poll, specifying both the date of the re‑poll and the polling station number. This tagging helps to avoid any confusion during transportation, storage, and eventual counting. In addition to the address tag, a pre‑printed sticker that reads “Re‑poll EVM/VVPAT” must be affixed to each machine and to its carrying case. This visual cue serves as an additional layer of identification, reinforcing the distinction between machines used in the original poll and those earmarked for the re‑poll.

After the re‑poll has been completed, the strong‑room where the machines are stored must be reopened in the presence of the candidates, their agents, and the observer. This reopening provides an opportunity for all interested parties to witness the placement of the re‑poll machines alongside the machines that were used in the original poll. By storing the two sets of machines together, the Commission ensures that they will be counted together in a single, transparent counting process, thereby preventing any accidental or deliberate separation of the votes. To further eliminate any possibility of confusion at the counting stage, a bold‑print sticker that says “TO BE COUNTED” must be applied to each re‑poll machine at the moment it is placed in the strong‑room. Conversely, a bold‑print sticker that reads “NOT TO BE COUNTED” must be affixed to the machines that were used in the original poll, indicating that those machines have already been accounted for and should not be recounted. The stickers are manufactured to precise specifications: each measures fifteen centimetres by ten centimetres, and the colour of the sticker varies depending on the type of election—pink for Assembly Constituency (AC) elections and white for Parliamentary Constituency (PC) elections. This colour‑coding assists election officials in quickly identifying the nature of the election at a glance.

In addition to the physical stickers, the unique identification numbers of the re‑poll machines must be entered into the Election Management System (EMS). The EMS is a digital platform that tracks the allocation, movement, and status of every piece of election equipment. By updating the system with the identifiers of the re‑poll machines, the Commission creates an electronic audit trail that can be cross‑checked with the physical stickers, thereby providing a double layer of verification.

At the conclusion of the polling day, each observer is required to submit a report using the prescribed format. This end‑of‑poll report must detail the role played by the polling agents, who are the representatives of the political parties or candidates stationed at the polling place to monitor the conduct of the poll. The report must also enumerate the number and nature of any complaints received at each polling station, categorising them by type—such as allegations of booth‑capturing, voter intimidation, or malfunctioning equipment—and indicating whether they were resolved on the spot or referred to higher authorities. In addition to these routine items, the observer must prepare a comprehensive narrative that highlights any significant factors that might warrant the ordering of a re‑poll. This narrative may include observations of procedural breaches, evidence of coercion, or technical failures that could have affected the outcome. The comprehensive report serves as an essential input for the Commission’s decision‑making process, ensuring that any recommendation for a re‑poll is grounded in concrete, documented evidence.

The counting of votes is another critical phase that is governed by a set of detailed procedures established by the Election Commission. To begin with, the Commission has issued a standard format that must be used for the approval of counting centres, which are the venues where the votes from the various polling stations are tallied. The Returning Officer, who is the chief election official for a constituency, is tasked with personally inspecting each counting centre to verify that it meets the specifications laid down by the Commission. These specifications cover aspects such as the availability of secure storage space for the EVMs, the presence of reliable power supplies, adequate lighting, and the provision of a controlled environment that prevents any unauthorised access. After conducting the inspection, the Returning Officer submits a proposal to the Commission, recommending the counting centre for approval.

During the early phase of this inspection—specifically before the scrutiny of nomination papers, which are the documents submitted by candidates to confirm their eligibility—observers stationed in the constituency must verify that the data required by the Commission, as set out in the format for counting‑centre approval, has already been transmitted to the Commission. This verification ensures that the process is proceeding in a timely manner and that the Commission has the necessary information to grant approval without undue delay. Moreover, the observers, working collaboratively, conduct a preliminary assessment of each counting centre’s facilities. They check that the physical infrastructure aligns with the Commission’s standards, that there is sufficient space for the secure handling of the EVMs and VVPATs, and that the environment is conducive to an accurate and transparent counting process. By performing this joint inspection, the observers help to safeguard the integrity of the counting phase, reducing the risk of irregularities that could arise from inadequate facilities or procedural lapses.

In summary, the series of procedures outlined above—from the careful storage of critical documents in dedicated strong‑rooms, through the systematic scrutiny of voter registers and other paperwork, to the meticulous handling of re‑polls and the thorough inspection of counting centres—are all designed to uphold the fundamental principles of free, fair, and transparent elections. Each step incorporates multiple layers of verification, both physical and electronic, and involves the coordinated efforts of election officials, observers, candidates, and agents. By adhering to these detailed guidelines, the Election Commission seeks to minimise the possibility of electoral malpractices, ensure that any incidents are promptly identified and addressed, and ultimately preserve the confidence of the electorate in the democratic process.

The guidance that is set out in the official handbook prepared for the Returning Officer must be read together with the most recent set of instructions that have been issued by the Election Commission of India. It is essential that every election observer, whether appointed by a political party, an independent body, or the Commission itself, studies these newer instructions with great care. Once the observer has fully understood the content of both the handbook and the Commission’s latest directions, the observer is then expected to enter into a constructive dialogue with the Returning Officer who is in charge of a particular counting centre. The purpose of this dialogue is to identify any gaps in the existing arrangements and to work collaboratively with the Returning Officer to implement any further improvements that may be required. These improvements must bring the facilities and procedures at the counting centre up to the level of quality and compliance that has been prescribed by the Commission, ensuring that the counting process is both reliable and trustworthy.

One of the most significant aspects of the Commission’s recent instructions concerns the physical infrastructure that must be provided for observers and members of the media who are present in the counting centres. The Commission now makes it mandatory for the Returning Officer to allocate a dedicated space for each observer. This space may take the form of a separate room that is completely independent of the main counting hall, or it may be a smaller, partitioned cubicle that offers sufficient privacy for the observer to perform his or her duties without disturbance. In addition to the physical space, the observer’s workstation must be equipped with a standard domestic telephone line, often referred to as an STD (Subscriber Trunk Dialing) telephone, which enables the observer to make long‑distance calls within India. A fax machine must also be installed in the same area so that the observer can send and receive documents in the traditional facsimile format, a requirement that remains relevant in many official communications despite the increasing use of electronic mail.

Observers also have a technical responsibility to verify that the electronic systems used for transmitting the final election results are fully operational. The Returning Officer, together with the District Election Officer and the technical staff who assist them, must test the ENCORE software well before the counting day. ENCORE is a specialized computer application that has been developed to allow the rapid and secure transmission of the final, certified result from each counting centre to the central server of the Election Commission of India. The observer is required to confirm that the software has been installed correctly, that all necessary hardware components such as computers and network connections are functioning, and that the password supplied to the observer is working as intended. Once the observer is satisfied that the system is ready, the observer will use the designated password to initiate the transmission of the data to the Commission’s central database. Because the data that is sent through ENCORE is automatically uploaded to the public website of the Commission, any error in the transmitted figures would instantly become visible to the entire nation. Consequently, the Commission insists on conducting a “dummy run” – a full rehearsal of the data entry and transmission process – on a date that has been specifically allocated for this purpose. This rehearsal allows any technical glitches or procedural misunderstandings to be identified and corrected before the actual counting takes place, thereby safeguarding the integrity of the official result.

The legal framework that governs the role of observers during the counting of votes is set out in the Representation of the People Act, 1951, particularly in Section 20B of that statute. This provision confers a specific and powerful authority on observers: they are empowered to halt the counting process if they encounter circumstances that fall within the scope of the section, and they may also direct the Returning Officer or the Assistant Returning Officer not to proclaim the result until the identified issue is resolved. In essence, the law gives observers a supervisory function that is more than merely advisory; it allows them to intervene directly and to demand an immediate response from the election officials. The law also requires observers to communicate any such intervention without delay to the Election Commission, ensuring that the highest authority is kept informed of any irregularities that could affect the outcome of the election. The Commission therefore expects that observers will take an active, vigilant role in overseeing the counting, and that they will act as a crucial line of defence against any procedural lapses or attempts at manipulation.

The selection of the staff who actually carry out the counting of votes must be carried out in a random and transparent manner on the morning of the counting day, before the counting begins in earnest. Randomisation of staff serves to prevent any pre‑arranged collusion between counting personnel and any interested party. The Commission places a strong emphasis on the need for the entire counting arrangement to be orderly, well‑structured, and free from any appearance of bias. It is not enough for the counting to be merely efficient; the process must also be demonstrably transparent and accurate, so that all participants, including the candidates and their agents, can have confidence in the result. To this end, the Commission’s existing instructions explicitly provide that the counting agents—those representatives appointed by each candidate—must be given a clear and unobstructed view of the counting activity at every counting table. This visual access is intended to eliminate any doubt about the fairness of the count, as the agents can witness each ballot being counted and tallied in real time.

The Commission has also issued a comprehensive set of orders that lay down the detailed specifications for the selection of counting centres and the management of the counting process within those centres. These specifications cover a wide range of logistical considerations, such as the size of the venue, the availability of power supply and backup generators, security arrangements, seating capacity for the staff and observers, and the layout of tables and booths. By adhering to these detailed orders, the Returning Officer ensures that the counting centre meets the standards required for a smooth and credible counting operation.

In order to guarantee the accuracy of the final result, the Returning Officer must prepare a round‑wise statement that details how many votes each candidate received at each counting table. This round‑wise statement is essentially a breakdown of the tally for every stage of the counting process. Both the Returning Officer and the observer are required to personally verify that the number of votes recorded for each candidate at each table matches the figures that appear in Part II of Form 17C, which is the official “Result of Counting” form that is filled out for each table. After confirming that the numbers are identical, the Returning Officer and the observer each place their initials beneath the total for each table, providing a clear, handwritten indication of their personal verification. The observer must also retain a copy of the detailed table‑wise, polling‑station‑wise, round‑wise breakdown of the votes in his or her own folder, creating a separate record that can be consulted if any dispute arises later.

As an additional safeguard, observers are required to perform a random cross‑check of the votes recorded on electronic voting machines (EVMs). Specifically, the observer must select two EVMs at random from those that have been counted in a given round. With the assistance of additional counting staff—personnel who have been allocated to help the observer with this verification—the observer re‑counts the votes on those two machines. The purpose of this exercise is to confirm that the original count for each candidate on those machines is correct. Only after the observer is satisfied that the re‑counted figures agree with the original count does the observer countersign the relevant column of the prescribed proforma, which is the official form used to record the votes from each EVM. The additional counting staff are responsible for preparing the result of the two randomly selected EVMs in the format required by the proforma, thereby providing the observer with the necessary data for the verification.

At the conclusion of each round of counting, the tallying process must be carried out in a systematic and transparent manner. The Assistant Returning Officer in charge of the counting hall announces the progress of that round both verbally and visually. Visually, the results are written on a blackboard or whiteboard that is positioned so that it can be seen clearly from any point in the hall. The announcement is then repeated over the public address system, ensuring that all persons present—candidates’ agents, observers, media representatives, and members of the public—receive the same information at the same time. In larger counting centres that contain multiple halls, the public announcements may be coordinated centrally, with a single voice broadcasting the updates to all halls simultaneously, thereby maintaining uniformity of information across the entire venue.

When the final results have been compiled, the winning candidate identified, and all the requisite paperwork signed by the Returning Officer, the observers must verify that the detailed result is transmitted immediately to the Election Commission’s official website. For this transmission, the software known as Genesys is used. Genesys is a secure, web‑based application that allows the electronic uploading of the final result sheet, ensuring that the data reaches the Commission’s central server in a timely and tamper‑proof manner. The observer’s role in this stage is to confirm that the transmission has been completed successfully and that the data displayed on the website accurately reflects the signed result.

Finally, after the result has been declared, the Returning Officer is required to send to the Chief Electoral Officer (CEO) the completed copies of three critical forms. The first is the Final Result Sheet in Form 20, which provides a comprehensive summary of the votes each candidate received across all constituencies. The second is the Declaration of Result in Form 21C, a formal statement that declares the winner of the election and is signed by the Returning Officer. The third is the Return of Election in Form 21E, which is the official document that records all the procedural details of the election, including the conduct of the poll, the counting, and any irregularities that may have been observed. The observer must ensure that each of these documents is correctly filled out, that any necessary corrections have been made, and that the duly signed copies are forwarded to the CEO without delay. By overseeing this final administrative step, the observer helps to close the electoral process in a manner that is fully compliant with the legal and procedural requirements set out by the Commission.

It is important to understand that the name of a candidate, as it appears on the official Nomination Form, is treated as the definitive identifier for that individual throughout the entire electoral process. In other words, once a candidate has entered their name on the Nomination Form, that exact spelling and format become the reference point for all subsequent documents that mention the candidate. This rule is designed to eliminate any ambiguity that might arise from variations in spelling or the use of different versions of a name. Consequently, the roster of individuals who are contesting the election, which is recorded in Form 7A, must contain the candidate’s name in precisely the same way it was entered on the Nomination Form. The spelling, spacing, diacritical marks and any other orthographic features must be reproduced without alteration, unless the Returning Officer (often abbreviated as RO) grants a specific exemption under Rule 8 of the Conduct of Elections Rules, 1961. That rule permits a deviation only after a careful review and for reasons that are justified and documented.

When the counting of votes is completed and a candidate is declared the winner from a particular constituency, the victorious candidate’s name is then entered in the official declaration of the result, which is captured in Form 21C. This form is a critical legal record that confirms who has been returned by the electorate. Equally important is the fact that the same name must reappear in the return of the election, which is documented in Form 21E, as well as in the certificate of election, Form 22. These three forms—Form 21C, Form 21E, and Form 22—constitute the core set of documents that certify the outcome of the election, and any discrepancy among them can lead to legal challenges, administrative confusion, or even the invalidation of the result. Therefore, it is absolutely imperative that the name appearing on Form 21C, the return documented on Form 21E, and the certificate issued under Form 22 are identical to the name that was originally listed on the list of contesting candidates in Form 7A. The principle of consistency must also extend to the ballot paper itself, which bears the names of the candidates that voters will select. In practice, this means that the name on the ballot, the name on Form 7A, and the names on Forms 21C, 21E, and 22 must all match exactly, without any deviation in spelling, punctuation, or order.

To ensure that this strict uniformity is observed, election observers play a pivotal role. Observers are tasked with impressing upon the Returning Officers the critical nature of this naming consistency and with verifying that full compliance is achieved at every stage of the process. In addition to their supervisory function, observers are also expected to ensure that both Returning Officers and Assistant Returning Officers (often abbreviated as AROs) receive appropriate training in the use of the ENCORE portal, which is the electronic system used for generating the round declaration form, known as RDF, as well as Forms 21C and 21E. Mastery of the ENCORE portal is essential because it automates the production of these documents and reduces the likelihood of human error, thereby supporting the overarching goal of maintaining identical naming across all electoral records.

The duties of an observer are guided by a set of clear do’s and don’ts, which are designed to promote professionalism, impartiality, and thoroughness. First and foremost, an observer must attend the briefing sessions that are organized by the Election Commission, as well as the debriefing sessions that follow each phase of the election. These sessions provide vital information about the schedule, the specific responsibilities that observers will assume, and any updates to procedures or regulations. In preparation for field work, an observer should prepare a detailed tour program well in advance. This program outlines the specific constituencies that will be visited, the dates and times of each visit, and the intended duration of each stay. Once the program has been drafted, it must be communicated to the Chief Electoral Officer, the District Election Officer, and the Returning Officer responsible for each constituency that will be covered. This advance notice helps the electoral officials coordinate logistics, allocate resources, and ensure that the observer’s presence is integrated smoothly into the overall election management plan.

Observers must pay close attention to the quantitative and qualitative parameters that the Commission sets for each tour, such as the total number of visits, the length of each visit, and the overall period during which the visits are to be conducted. These parameters are not arbitrary; they are calibrated to balance thorough oversight with the practical constraints of time and manpower. Accordingly, an observer must adhere strictly to the prescribed schedule and must not unilaterally extend a visit beyond the authorized duration without prior approval. Transparency in the observer’s activities is further reinforced by the requirement that the observer’s tour programme be made publicly available within the constituencies that are assigned. Publicizing the programme helps to build trust among the electorate, political parties, and other stakeholders, and it serves as a deterrent against any attempts to manipulate the observation process.

In identifying which polling stations or areas deserve particular scrutiny, observers should apply a systematic approach based on vulnerability mapping and historical data. Areas that have been flagged as vulnerable—perhaps because of previous incidents of electoral fraud, intimidation, or logistical difficulties—must be visited with heightened vigilance. Similarly, polling stations that have a record of past irregularities, such as ballot stuffing, booth capturing, or the use of prohibited materials, warrant close monitoring. Newly created polling stations, which may lack established procedures or experienced staff, also require careful observation to ensure that they are set up correctly and operate smoothly on election day. Finally, any polling station that, during the course of the election, appears to be becoming a flashpoint for tension—perhaps due to local disputes, communal sensitivities, or the presence of high‑profile candidates—must be added to the observer’s list of priority sites.

Beyond the focus on specific locations, observers are also responsible for ensuring that the physical inventory of election materials is sufficient and accurately recorded. This includes items such as ballot papers, indelible ink, voting booths, and security seals. A thorough verification of the stock levels helps to prevent shortages that could disrupt voting or create opportunities for malpractice. In parallel, observers must strictly adhere to the technical guidelines laid out in the Electronic Voting Machine (EVM) – Voter‑Verified Paper Audit Trail (VVPAT) Manual, 2018, Edition 3, as well as the accompanying Status Paper on EVM Edition 3. These documents provide detailed instructions on the operation, handling, and maintenance of the electronic voting devices, which have become a central feature of the modern electoral system. To fulfill this requirement, observers should familiarize themselves with the functionality of EVMs and VVPATs through hands‑on training sessions and rehearsal drills. By doing so, they will be equipped to identify any irregularities in the deployment or operation of the machines, such as tampering, power failures, or procedural breaches.

Public confidence in the electronic voting process is another area where observers have a critical role. They must verify that sufficient public awareness campaigns have been conducted so that both the media and the general electorate understand how the machines work, why they are secure, and how the paper audit trail provides an additional layer of verification. This outreach helps to dispel myths and reduce the likelihood of misinformation spreading during the election. In addition to technical oversight, observers are tasked with conducting an independent assessment of the overall law‑and‑order situation in each constituency. This assessment involves gauging the presence and effectiveness of security personnel, monitoring any reports of violence or intimidation, and evaluating whether the environment is conducive to a free and fair vote.

The observers’ role also encompasses providing guidance, advice, and facilitation to the District Electoral Officer (DEO) and the Returning Officer in the execution of poll arrangements. Their support is aimed at ensuring that the election is conducted in a manner that is transparent, peaceful, participatory, and consistent with the legal framework governing elections. Part of this support includes conducting random checks of as many polling stations as possible. By visiting polling stations in an unpredictable manner, observers can deter potential misconduct and verify that each station adheres to the prescribed standards. They also have the responsibility to monitor compliance with the Model Code of Conduct, which includes prohibitions on the transfer of election materials, the use of government resources for campaigning, and other forms of undue influence.

In terms of security, observers must keep track of the deployment of central forces—such as paramilitary units or central police contingents—to ensure that they are positioned in a way that maximizes their impact in maintaining order, especially in areas identified as sensitive. After completing each field visit, an observer is required to submit a comprehensive report to the Election Commission within twenty‑four hours of returning to the headquarters. This report should capture observations, incidents, and any recommendations for corrective action. In addition to the full report, observers may be asked to send spot reports in real time when urgent or emergent issues arise. Any development that demands immediate remedial action—such as evidence of ballot tampering, violent clashes, or administrative failures—must be brought to the immediate attention of the Returning Officer and the Commission, without waiting for the scheduled reporting cycle.

The submission of reports can be done through the designated Observer Portal, an electronic platform that ensures secure and timely transmission of information. Alternatively, where electronic submission is not feasible, observers may place their reports in a sealed envelope addressed to the Secretary responsible for the particular state or union territory under observation. Participation in meetings convened by District Election Officers or Returning Officers—often involving political party representatives—is also part of the observer’s remit, as it provides a forum for discussing logistical issues, addressing grievances, and fostering cooperation among stakeholders.

Financial transparency forms another pillar of the observer’s duties. Observers should conduct an independent assessment of the election expenditure incurred by candidates, political parties, or any other individuals involved in the electoral process. This involves reviewing campaign finance disclosures, examining posters, pamphlets, and other promotional material, and engaging directly with local residents to gauge whether any illegal or excessive spending has occurred. The observers are also expected to inspect the Register of Election Expenditure that is prescribed by the Commission, ensuring that all reported figures align with the legal limits and that any discrepancies are noted.

Before departing from the headquarters to commence field duties, observers must obtain prior permission from the Commission, a procedural safeguard that ensures proper coordination and accountability. While on assignment, observers must maintain the highest standards of conduct, recognizing that they are themselves under observation by the Election Commission. They must exhibit professionalism, ethical behavior, and personal integrity that befit senior officers tasked with safeguarding the democratic process. Their conduct must be guided by the principles of honesty, diligence, and responsibility, and they must avoid any appearance of bias, partisanship, or personal gain.

Cultural sensitivity is also essential; observers should respect local customs, traditions, and social norms, refraining from expressing personal political opinions or preferences that could be interpreted as influencing the electorate. By upholding the democratic values enshrined in the Constitution—such as free expression, equal suffrage, and the rule of law—observers help to reinforce public confidence in the electoral system. Moreover, they are encouraged to lead modest lives during the observation period, avoiding any displays of extravagance or lavishness that might create a perception of privilege or disconnect from the ordinary citizenry. In sum, the role of the election observer is multifaceted and demanding, requiring meticulous attention to procedural detail, a deep understanding of electoral law, technical competence with voting equipment, and an unwavering commitment to the principles of democratic governance.

The observers must display the highest level of due diligence and professional acumen in conveying their opinions and reports to the commission about any election-related incident.

38. REVIEW POINTS FOR CENTRAL OBSERVERS

1. Polling Station:

The election authorities have taken a series of comprehensive steps to adapt the conduct of the electoral process to the extraordinary circumstances created by the COVID‑19 pandemic. First, a postal ballot facility has been put in place for those voters who have been specifically identified as beneficiaries under the latest instructions issued by the Election Commission. These instructions outline the criteria for eligibility, the documentation required, and the procedural safeguards designed to ensure that the postal voting mechanism operates securely and efficiently, thereby allowing eligible citizens to cast their votes without having to attend a polling station in person. The identification of beneficiaries has been carried out by district officials in close coordination with the commission, and the necessary forms and envelopes have been distributed in accordance with health guidelines, including the use of personal protective equipment and social distancing measures during the handling and dispatch of ballot papers.

In parallel with the postal voting arrangements, the preparation of the electoral roll has been intensified to provide each voter with a Voter Information Slip. This slip contains essential details such as the name of the polling station, the date and time of voting, and other logistical information that helps voters locate their designated polling place. Although the slip does not display the voter's photograph, it incorporates a Quick Response (QR) code that can be scanned by election officials to verify the authenticity of the voter’s identity and to monitor the distribution of the slips in real time. The QR code system is linked to a central monitoring platform that records when and where each slip is issued, enabling the authorities to track coverage and to quickly address any discrepancies. In addition, a comprehensive voter guide is being distributed alongside the slips. The guide explains the voting procedure, the use of electronic voting machines (EVMs) and Voter Verified Paper Audit Trails (VVPATs), and provides information on the rights and responsibilities of voters, thereby enhancing voter awareness and confidence in the electoral process.

The Model Code of Conduct, a set of rules that govern the behavior of political parties and candidates during the election period, is being enforced with heightened rigor. Specialized enforcement mechanisms have been instituted, including the deployment of flying squads, excise teams, and border check posts that operate under the direct supervision of the commission’s officers. These squads are tasked with monitoring compliance with the code, preventing the use of prohibited inducements, and ensuring that electoral activities do not violate the stipulated norms. Moreover, a detailed set of Standard Operating Procedures (SOPs) has been prepared for the critical final seventy‑two‑hour window that leads up to the counting of votes. These SOPs delineate the responsibilities of each official, the sequence of actions to be taken, and the safeguards to be observed, thereby ensuring that the counting process proceeds in a transparent, orderly, and secure manner.

A systematic vulnerability mapping exercise has been undertaken to identify the most at‑risk communities and geographic pockets within each district. District Magistrates, Sub‑Divisional Officers, and Returning Officers have collectively compiled lists of vulnerable hamlets and small settlements, and have conducted field visits to assess the specific challenges present in those areas. Particular attention has been paid to the regulation of liquor production units and outlets, recognizing that the availability of alcohol can exacerbate public disorder during elections. Authorities have therefore instituted a monitoring regime that includes regular inspections, licensing verification, and, where necessary, the temporary suspension of operations. Detailed records of actions taken against non‑compliant establishments have been maintained, illustrating the proactive stance of the administration in mitigating potential sources of unrest.

The transportation and communication plans that support the election have been scrutinized for both quality and fidelity. Transportation plans outline the routes and schedules for moving election personnel, security forces, and the electronic voting equipment to and from polling stations, ensuring that all movements are documented and that contingencies are in place for unexpected disruptions. The communication plan addresses the need to maintain reliable contact with polling stations located in shadow or hard‑to‑reach areas, employing a mix of radio, satellite links, and mobile networks to guarantee that real‑time information about voter turnout, logistical issues, or security concerns can be transmitted without delay. These arrangements are designed to prevent isolation of remote polling stations and to ensure that they receive the same level of support as urban centers.

A cadre of micro‑observers has been trained and deployed to provide an additional layer of oversight at the grassroots level. These observers receive specialized instruction on how to document any irregularities, how to use simple reporting tools, and how to coordinate with higher‑level officials. Their presence is intended to increase the transparency of the process by creating a reliable, on‑the‑ground record of electoral activities. In addition, all polling staff have undergone intensive training that places special emphasis on the technical aspects of the electoral process, particularly the operation of electronic voting machines and the VVPAT system. The training curriculum covers the mechanical and software components of the machines, the procedures for securing them before and after voting, and the protocols for troubleshooting any technical glitches that may arise. By ensuring that every member of the polling team is well‑versed in these critical technologies, the authorities aim to reduce the likelihood of malfunctions and to preserve the integrity of the vote.

Security forces, including Central Police Forces (CPFs) and State Armed Police (SAPs), have been deployed in accordance with the detailed guidelines issued by the Election Commission. The deployment plans have been calibrated to reflect the specific risk profile of each polling station, with vulnerable and critical locations receiving heightened security measures. In vulnerable pockets and hamlets, CPFs have been tasked with conducting route‑marches and area domination exercises, whereby they move through the neighborhoods at predetermined times to deter any potential disturbances. Records of the dates and times of these visits are maintained to provide a transparent audit trail. Moreover, a daily action plan has been prepared in consultation with the District Election Officer (DEO) and the Returning Officer (RO), outlining the tasks to be undertaken by security personnel each day. The effectiveness of CPF deployment has been periodically reviewed, and any shortcomings identified—such as gaps in coverage or delayed response times—have been addressed through corrective actions and reallocation of resources.

A robust complaint redressal mechanism has been established to handle grievances, suggestions, and feedback from voters, candidates, and political parties. This mechanism operates through both a dedicated website and a network of call centers. The toll‑free number 1950 has been widely publicized through media campaigns, posters, and community outreach to ensure that citizens are aware of how to raise concerns. District contact centers staffed by a sufficient number of trained agents are responsible for logging each complaint into the National Grievances Services Portal (NGSP), a centralized system that tracks the status of each case from submission to resolution. The mandate is that all complaints be investigated and resolved within twenty‑four hours, a target that has been reinforced through performance monitoring dashboards and periodic audits of the complaint handling process.

Information technology applications play a pivotal role in managing the myriad aspects of election administration. The c‑VIGIL system, a digital platform designed for monitoring and investigating violations of the Model Code of Conduct, is supported by a district control room that receives and processes new cases. Flying squads and static surveillance teams are registered within the c‑VIGIL framework, allowing investigators to assign cases, receive field reports via a mobile application, and monitor the progress of investigations in real time. The system specifies a prescribed timeline for case disposal, and the observers themselves are equipped with a c‑VIGIL observer app that enables them to review open cases, verify compliance, and provide on‑site feedback. Parallel to c‑VIGIL, the Voter Helpline application has been promoted widely; it offers functionalities such as searching for one’s polling station, submitting forms, lodging complaints, and accessing candidate affidavits and election results. The Saksham ECI app has been specifically tailored for voters with disabilities (PWD), allowing them to request special accommodations such as marking themselves as PWD in the voter database and requesting wheelchair assistance. These requests can be downloaded and processed at the district election officer level, ensuring that the necessary arrangements are made well before polling day.

The ENCORE application serves as an integrated digital platform for the nomination process. It provides an information and communication technology (ICT) helpdesk that assists candidates in digitizing their nomination papers through the Suvidha portal. The goal is to achieve complete digitization of all nominations, with the Returning Officers examining each application solely within the ENCORE system and marking them as accepted, rejected, withdrawn, or contested. The Suvidha Permission app centralizes the handling of all election‑related permissions, ensuring that approvals are issued within the stipulated timelines and that there is a single point of reference for permission status. Candidates are also informed about the Suvidha Candidate app, which allows them to track the progress of their permission and nomination status, thereby reducing uncertainty and streamlining communication between candidates and election officials.

On the day of the election, a comprehensive monitoring arrangement is put in place to oversee all activities at polling stations and counting centers. Specific protocols have been drawn up for the EVM (Electronic Voting Machine) strong rooms, the secure locations where the machines and the accompanying VVPAT devices are stored before, during, and after voting. Video recording equipment has been installed in these strong rooms to capture every entry and exit, providing an immutable record that can be reviewed in the event of any dispute. Temporary shelters have been arranged for representatives of political parties who need to be present near the strong room to observe the handling of the equipment while maintaining a safe distance. Each official who handles the EVMs and VVPATs is supplied with disposable gloves and hand sanitizers to minimize the risk of virus transmission, and the lighting in the strong rooms has been calibrated to ensure clear visibility for both the officials and the recording devices. Detailed security plans have been formulated that assign specific forces to guard the strong rooms, the counting centers, and any other critical locations, thereby creating multiple layers of protection against tampering or unauthorized access. After the conclusion of polling, a thorough sanitization process is carried out in the strong rooms, using approved disinfectants to cleanse all surfaces, equipment, and fixtures, ensuring that the environment is safe for the personnel who will handle the machines during the counting phase.

Collectively, these measures reflect a concerted effort by the election administration to safeguard the democratic process while responding to the challenges posed by a public health crisis. By integrating health precautions, technological tools, rigorous security protocols, and transparent grievance mechanisms, the authorities aim to deliver an election that is not only free and fair but also resilient to the unprecedented circumstances of the present moment.

The arrangements concerning electronic voting machines (EVMs) and the accompanying Voter‑Verifiable Paper Audit Trail devices (VVPATs) must be examined in great detail to ensure that every procedural safeguard has been observed. First, it is essential to verify whether a mock poll was carried out at the stage of candidate setting, and whether this trial involved a statistically representative sample amounting to five percent of all EVMs and VVPATs that had been selected at random. Such a mock poll serves the purpose of exposing any latent technical glitches, training the staff in a controlled environment, and confirming that the machines record votes accurately before the actual election begins. In addition, the competency of the polling officials who will be handling these electronic devices must be scrutinised. Specifically, one must determine whether every official received adequate training on the operation, troubleshooting, and secure handling of both EVMs and VVPATs, and whether this training was organised in batches that did not exceed fifty participants per session, a size deemed optimal for effective learning and interaction. It is also necessary to establish the total number of such training batches that were convened, as this figure indicates the scale of the capacity‑building effort undertaken by the election authority.

Beyond the initial classroom‑type instruction, the election commission also mandates that hands‑on training and doubt‑clearing sessions be provided to the members of each polling party during the dispersal phase, that is, when the polling personnel are distributed to their respective polling stations. The purpose of these sessions is to give each officer a practical opportunity to familiarise themselves with the physical devices, to ask specific questions about the workflow, and to resolve any uncertainties that might otherwise impair the smooth conduct of voting. An audit must therefore confirm that such practical engagements have indeed been scheduled and executed, and that the necessary resources—including functional machines and technical experts—were made available at the time of dispersal.

Another critical point of verification concerns the functional status of the machines at the moment of candidate setting. It is necessary to record how many EVMs and VVPATs were found to be non‑functional during this phase, because any malfunctioning unit could jeopardise the integrity of the electoral process if not replaced promptly. Moreover, the election management system (EMS) must contain an entry for each defective unit, documenting the nature of the fault, the serial number, and the corrective action taken, thereby creating an audit trail that can be examined later.

The commission’s instructions also require that the vehicles used by the polling parties, including those of sector officers, be fitted with Global Positioning System (GPS) tracking devices. This technological measure enables real‑time monitoring of the movement of reserve EVMs and VVPATs, ensuring that they are transported securely and that any deviation from the prescribed route can be detected immediately. The assessment must therefore determine whether this directive has been implemented not only in letter, by installing the hardware, but also in spirit, by actively monitoring the telemetry data and responding to any anomalies.

In accordance with the Election Commission of India’s (ECI) guidelines, intermediate strong rooms—temporary secure storage facilities used during the transit of machines—must be established where required. These rooms are to be equipped with reinforced doors, fire‑resistant safes, and surveillance equipment, and must conform to the specifications laid down by the commission. Verification must confirm the existence of such arrangements, their compliance with the prescribed standards, and their readiness for use.

Finally, the round‑the‑clock surveillance of EVM control rooms demands the deployment of an adequate number of staff members. Continuous monitoring is essential to detect any unauthorized access, tampering, or technical failures as they occur, and to respond without delay. The audit must therefore establish whether sufficient personnel have been assigned to this task, and whether shift patterns and backup arrangements are in place to guarantee uninterrupted oversight throughout the election period.

Turning to the mechanisms for monitoring polling turnout, the commission has introduced an information and communication technology (ICT) application that requires timely data entry by the Returning Officer (RO) and the Assistant Returning Officer (ARO). Every two hours, the officials are expected to submit a report that captures the number of voters who have cast their ballots up to that point. It must be verified that individual user accounts for the RO and the ARO have been created within the application, and that these accounts are actively being used to record the interim figures. Moreover, the system must capture disaggregated data, distinguishing between male, female, and other categories of voters, as well as providing estimates of the overall turnout at the beginning of the poll, the final turnout at the close of voting, and the precision of the entered numbers. The assessment should confirm that the officials understand the importance of entering accurate gender‑based data and that they are following the prescribed protocol for updating the platform at the required intervals.

On the day designated for counting the votes, a series of logistical and security arrangements must be put in place to safeguard the integrity of the process. The receipt and handling of postal ballots—votes cast by voters who are unable to attend a polling station in person—require a systematic management plan that ensures these ballots are collected, verified for authenticity, and safely delivered to the counting centre. Adequate security provisions must be arranged for the strong room where the ballots and the electronic devices are stored, adhering strictly to the commission’s instructions concerning physical barriers, access control, and surveillance. Closed‑circuit television (CCTV) cameras and videography equipment must be installed in accordance with the existing guidelines, providing continuous visual records of all activities within the counting venue.

To enhance transparency, micro‑observers—independent personnel tasked with monitoring the counting process at each individual counting table—must be deployed. Their presence helps to deter any attempt at manipulation and provides an additional layer of verification. The counting halls themselves must be arranged as per the commission’s specifications, which include appropriate seating, lighting, ventilation, and segregation of staff to prevent crowding. Proper barricading, both inside and outside the counting centres, is essential to control the flow of people and to protect the premises from external interference. The routes used to transport EVMs from the polling stations to the counting centres must be thoroughly sanitized and guarded, with security personnel deployed along the entire path to prevent theft or tampering.

In addition, the verification of the VVPAT paper slips—printed records that correspond to each electronic vote—must be carried out following the specific procedures outlined in the commission’s letters numbered 51/8/VVPAT/2017‑EMS dated 13 October 2017 and 5 December 2017. These letters detail the steps for matching the paper audit trail with the electronic tally, the criteria for selecting a sample of slips for verification, and the documentation required for any discrepancies. It is crucial to confirm that the counting centre has the necessary infrastructure to perform this verification, including adequate lighting, secure tables, and trained staff.

Technical infrastructure is also a key component of a secure counting operation. The venue must be equipped with a leased line internet connection capable of delivering a minimum speed of eight megabits per second, and a backup leased line of the same capacity must be on standby to ensure uninterrupted connectivity. Computer systems, printers, and cybersecurity measures—including firewalls, antivirus software, and secure login protocols—must be in place to protect the data transmitted through the ENCORE portal, the online platform used for election management. An uninterrupted power supply (UPS) must be installed to bridge any short outages, and a generator capable of providing at least eight hours of backup power must be available to sustain the entire counting process in case of a grid failure.

Furthermore, the Returning Officers and Assistant Returning Officers need to be proficient in generating the round declaration forms, specifically RDF form 21C and 21E, through the ENCORE portal. These forms constitute the official certification of the results for each constituency and must be completed accurately and submitted within the prescribed timeframe. Training sessions should have been conducted to ensure that the officers can navigate the portal, input the required data, and upload the documents without error.

The unprecedented circumstances created by the COVID‑19 pandemic imposed additional health and safety requirements on all election‑related activities. The Ministry of Health and Family Welfare (MoHFW), in conjunction with the Election Commission, issued comprehensive guidelines on social distancing, the use of personal protective equipment, sanitisation of surfaces, and the management of crowds at polling stations, counting centres, and throughout the electoral supply chain. It is essential to verify that these norms were observed during every stage of the electoral process, from the handling of EVMs to the conduct of counting, and that any deviations were promptly rectified.

Preparedness of the Chief Electoral Officer (CEO) team must also be assessed in relation to several specialised functions. The team is responsible for overseeing the pre‑certification of political advertisements, a process that involves reviewing broadcast and printed content for compliance with election laws before it is aired or published. This includes the scrutiny of paid news, wherein media outlets receive monetary compensation for favourable coverage, a practice that is prohibited. The CEO’s office must maintain detailed reports on each advertisement examined, document the findings, and take the follow‑up actions stipulated by the commission, such as issuing notices or demanding corrections. In parallel, a rapid response mechanism must be in place to counter the spread of fake news and misinformation, which can undermine public confidence in the electoral system. This mechanism typically involves monitoring social media platforms, issuing clarifications, and coordinating with law‑enforcement agencies to remove or flag false content. The CEO team should also engage in proactive dissemination of accurate information, aiming to steer the public narrative towards factual reporting and to highlight success stories that showcase the efficient functioning of the electoral machinery. Collaboration with local media houses and the media division of the Election Commission is essential in this regard, as it helps to amplify positive messages and to build public trust.

Any other significant issues that arise during the conduct of the election must be recorded and addressed promptly. This catch‑all clause ensures that unforeseen challenges, such as logistical bottlenecks, technical failures, or security incidents, are not ignored but are instead logged, investigated, and resolved in accordance with the established protocols.

Finally, the observer mobile application, a digital tool designed specifically for the diverse categories of election observers—including general observers, expenditure observers, police observers, and awareness observers—plays a pivotal role in streamlining the monitoring process from the moment observers are allocated to a constituency. The application is compatible with both Android and iOS operating systems and can be downloaded from the official Google Play Store and Apple App Store. Once installed, observers must log in using a unique observer code and password, which grants them access to a personalized dashboard. Through this interface, observers receive notifications about their deployment, can submit mandatory arrival reports, and manage their profiles throughout the election cycle. The app also provides a wealth of operational details, such as the status of their deployment, a digital version of their observer identification card, and a list of flying‑squad teams assigned to the same assembly constituency. By selecting a team identifier, observers can initiate a direct phone call to the squad leader, facilitating rapid communication. All required report formats—ranging from daily activity logs to incident reports—are available within the application, enabling observers to submit information directly without resorting to paper forms. A noticeboard feature displays real‑time updates on polling and counting activities, while a live‑case tracker presents a summary of citizen complaints, the history of each case, and the actions taken by the flying‑squad teams. Observers can also view field‑unit reports and the returning officer’s reports associated with each case, ensuring a comprehensive view of the situation on the ground. In urgent circumstances, observers have the ability to flag a case as a cVIGIL incident, prompting immediate attention from higher authorities. This integrated digital ecosystem not only enhances the efficiency and accountability of election monitoring but also fosters greater transparency by making critical information readily accessible to all stakeholders involved in safeguarding the democratic process.

The Election Commission has increasingly turned to information technology as a means of strengthening the democratic process, with the specific aim of encouraging greater participation from ordinary citizens and ensuring that the conduct of elections is as open and transparent as possible. In recent years a suite of digital tools has been introduced, each designed to address a particular aspect of election management and to provide real‑time data that can be acted upon by the authorities responsible for enforcing the Model Code of Conduct. One of the most prominent of these tools is the cVIGIL application, a mobile‑based platform that enables members of the public to document and report alleged violations of the Model Code of Conduct or of election‑related expenditure rules. By allowing a citizen to capture a photograph, audio recording or video clip with a smartphone, the system creates a time‑stamped piece of evidentiary material that can be used by the Commission’s enforcement teams as a reliable record of the alleged infraction.

The cVIGIL application is built upon Geographic Information System (GIS) technology, which is a method of mapping and analyzing data that is tied to specific locations on the earth’s surface. In practice this means that when a user activates the camera function within the cVIGIL app, the software automatically records the exact latitude and longitude of the device at the moment the image or video is taken. This “auto‑location” feature supplies investigators with a fairly accurate pinpoint of where the incident occurred, thereby reducing the time required for field officers to locate the site. The precision of the geotagging is valuable because it allows the so‑called flying squads—mobile teams of officials tasked with rapid response—to navigate directly to the spot of the alleged violation and to initiate any necessary action without the delays that would otherwise be caused by vague or incomplete information.

In order to make the reporting process as inclusive as possible, the cVIGIL system has been divided into several interrelated mobile applications, each serving a distinct user group. The first component, known as the c‑VIGIL Citizen Mobile App, is intended for use by any individual who resides within the boundaries of an electoral constituency. After downloading the app and signing in, a citizen can lodge a complaint by attaching a visual or audio record of the suspected breach. The interface also offers an option to submit the report anonymously, meaning that the complainant’s personal details are not revealed to the authorities; this feature is designed to protect the identity of whistle‑blowers who might fear retaliation. The automatic geotagging that occurs when the device’s camera is engaged ensures that the location data are captured without any additional steps on the part of the user, thereby simplifying the reporting process and improving the reliability of the information that is forwarded to the enforcement teams.

A second, complementary tool is the Investigator App, which is installed on the smartphones and tablets used by field units such as the flying squads and the static surveillance teams that monitor election activities from fixed locations. When a complaint is received through the citizen app, the investigator’s device notifies the relevant team, and the software imposes a strict time limit for reaching the incident site. Specifically, the field unit is required to arrive at the location within fifteen minutes of receiving the alert; only after physically inspecting the scene are they permitted to upload a follow‑up report that confirms the findings and records any further evidence. This built‑in deadline is intended to compel rapid action and to prevent the loss of crucial evidence that might otherwise dissipate over time. In addition to responding to citizen‑initiated complaints, the Investigator App enables the field officers to file suo‑motu cases—reports that are generated by the authorities themselves, without any external prompting—whenever they observe a breach of the Model Code of Conduct during their routine patrols. The ability to initiate suo‑motu reports through the same digital platform has added a valuable layer of proactive enforcement to the overall system.

The third component of the cVIGIL suite is the Monitor App, which provides senior officials, including Chief Electoral Officers and other officials of the Election Commission of India, with a comprehensive overview of every case that has been logged within their jurisdiction. Through a mobile interface, these officials can view live updates on the status of each complaint, track the progress of investigations, and identify which reports have yet to receive a response. The real‑time monitoring capability allows the leadership to highlight or prioritize particular cases that may demand urgent attention, thereby exerting pressure on the field teams to adhere to the stipulated timelines. By making the flow of information transparent not only to the public but also to the highest levels of election administration, the Monitor App serves to reinforce accountability throughout the enforcement chain.

Overall, the deployment of the cVIGIL ecosystem represents a concerted effort by the Election Commission to harness modern information technology for the purpose of safeguarding the integrity of the electoral process. By leveraging the ubiquity of smartphones, the precision of GIS‑based geotagging, and the immediacy of mobile data transmission, the system empowers ordinary citizens to become active participants in the monitoring of election conduct. At the same time, it equips field investigators with the tools needed to respond swiftly and document their actions in a verifiable manner. The hierarchical structure of the three apps—citizen reporting, investigator response, and senior‑level monitoring—creates a closed feedback loop that promotes both speed and transparency. In practice, the Commission has committed to providing complainants with a status report within one hundred minutes of filing a complaint, a promise that underscores the organization’s dedication to timely redressal. The cumulative effect of these technological interventions is expected to increase public confidence in the fairness of elections, reduce the incidence of violations, and ultimately contribute to a more robust and participatory democratic system.

The cVIGIL Decider application together with the Returning Officer (RO) Dashboard constitutes a specialised digital environment that is made available to the District Electoral Officer, who is commonly abbreviated as DEO, and to the Returning Officers who are responsible for overseeing the conduct of elections within their respective constituencies. The purpose of this suite of tools is to provide a systematic mechanism through which the officials can review and adjudicate the cases that have been generated by the investigative staff employed by the Election Commission. When an investigator discovers a possible violation of the Model Code of Conduct, or any other statutory breach related to the electoral process, a complaint is entered into the system and subsequently forwarded to the Decider module. Within the Decider interface the officer can view all of the complaints that have been recorded under the MCC, which stands for Model Code of Conduct, and can monitor the status of each complaint as it progresses in real time. This live‑view capability ensures that the official is constantly apprised of the latest developments without the need for separate manual updates. Once the field unit submits a report that documents the findings of the investigation, the Decider or the Returning Officer is empowered to take one of several predefined actions. The options include dropping the case entirely if it is found to be without merit, deciding the case by rendering a final determination that may involve imposing a penalty or taking corrective steps, or escalating the matter to a higher authority if the situation warrants further scrutiny. All of these functions are delivered through mobile applications that have been published on the two dominant public distribution platforms for smartphones, namely the Google Play Store for Android devices and the Apple App Store for iOS devices, thereby ensuring broad accessibility for election officials regardless of the type of handset they use.

The Suvidha portal represents another cornerstone of the Election Commission’s digital transformation strategy, offering a range of services to candidates, political parties, and their representatives. The portal is accessible through a public web address and is designed to simplify the procedural aspects of filing nominations, seeking permissions for election‑related activities, and tracking the status of those requests. In the first instance, the portal hosts an online nomination facility that allows a prospective candidate to create a personal account on the system, complete the electronic nomination form, and attach the required affidavit. The affidavit is a statutory declaration that outlines the candidate’s personal, financial, and criminal background, and is a mandatory component of the nomination process. After the online form has been completed, the candidate is required to deposit the prescribed security amount, which serves as a financial guarantee that the nomination is genuine. The system also provides the candidate with the ability to view the availability of time slots for submitting the physical documents to the Returning Officer, thereby enabling the candidate to plan the in‑person visit efficiently. Once the electronic submission is finalized, the candidate must print a hard copy of the completed form, have it notarized in accordance with legal requirements, and physically hand over the documents along with any supporting paperwork to the Returning Officer. Although the digital pathway streamlines many of the administrative steps, it does not replace the traditional offline filing method; the offline submission continues to be valid and is retained as a parallel option for those who prefer or require it.

Beyond the nomination process, the Suvidha portal includes a dedicated permissions module. This module is intended for candidates, political parties, and any authorized representatives who need to obtain official clearance for a variety of election‑related activities such as public meetings, political rallies, the use of loudspeakers, the establishment of temporary campaign offices, and other similar functions. Applicants can submit their requests through the same online interface, and the system automatically generates a unique tracking identifier that allows the applicant to monitor the progress of the request. The portal also provides real‑time status updates, so that candidates are informed promptly when a permission is granted, rejected, or requires additional information. The underlying workflow is supported by a separate mobile application known as the Nodal Mobile App, which is installed on the devices of officials from a range of government departments that have jurisdiction over specific aspects of the permission process. These departments include, for example, the fire services, revenue authorities, the public works department, and the police. Each of these nodal officers is registered within the ENCORE system, a comprehensive electronic framework that the Election Commission uses to coordinate inter‑departmental approvals. When a permission request is lodged by a candidate, the relevant nodal officers receive an instant push notification on their mobile devices. They can then review the particulars of the request, examine any attached documentation, and either accept or reject the application. In cases where an objection is raised, the officer is required to record the reasons for the objection within the app, thereby creating an audit trail that can be reviewed by higher authorities if necessary. The mobile platform enables these officials to take immediate action, which helps to reduce delays that might otherwise occur in a paper‑based system.

Candidates who have submitted their nomination or permission requests—whether through the web portal or through physical delivery—can also make use of a Candidate Mobile App that mirrors the functionality of the Suvidha portal. Once a submission is entered into the ENCORE system, the app allows the candidate to receive periodic updates on the status of their nomination, the progress of any pending permissions, and any feedback that may be required from their side. The app synchronizes with the central database and displays a concise summary of each pending item, thereby giving candidates a clear picture of where they stand in the overall electoral preparation timeline. Like the other applications described, this Candidate Mobile App is made available for download from both the Google Play Store and the Apple App Store, ensuring that users with Android or iOS devices can benefit from its features.

In addition to the dynamic tracking of nominations and permissions, the Election Commission provides a public-facing Candidate Affidavit Portal, which is a separate website that aggregates the complete list of individuals contesting an election. The portal displays each candidate’s profile, the current status of their nomination (whether it has been accepted, is pending, or has been rejected), and the full text of their affidavit, which includes disclosures about assets, liabilities, educational qualifications, and any criminal antecedents. By making this information openly accessible, the portal promotes transparency and enables voters to make informed decisions based on the factual background of each candidate. The portal can be reached through a dedicated URL and is designed to be searchable, allowing citizens to locate a candidate by name, constituency, or party affiliation.

The Know Your Candidate (KYC) application is another digital tool developed by the Election Commission to empower the electorate with easy access to information regarding the criminal records of candidates. The application, which is available for both Android and iOS operating systems, presents a user‑friendly interface where voters can browse through a curated list of candidates. For each candidate, the app indicates whether the individual has any pending criminal cases, the nature of those cases, and the status of any convictions. This feature is especially useful for citizens who wish to avoid supporting candidates with serious legal issues, thereby encouraging a more accountable and law‑abiding political culture. The KYC app is distributed through the same mainstream app stores—Google Play and Apple App Store—so that it can be installed on a wide range of smartphones.

The Electronically Transmitted Postal Ballot System, commonly abbreviated as ETPBS, is a specialized mechanism designed to facilitate voting for service voters, who are typically members of the armed forces, government employees posted away from their home constituencies, or other eligible individuals who cannot physically attend a polling station. Under this system, the Election Commission generates a blank postal ballot electronically and transmits it to the service voter through secure electronic channels. Once the voter receives the blank ballot, they fill it in according to the prescribed instructions and then return the completed ballot to the designated election office using speed post, a rapid postal service that ensures timely delivery. This approach combines the security of electronic transmission for the distribution of the ballot with the reliability of a physical courier for the return of the vote, thereby preserving the integrity of the electoral process while accommodating the logistical constraints faced by service voters.

Finally, the Saksham ECI app, also referred to as the PwD (Person with Disabilities) app, is an inclusive digital solution that addresses the unique needs of voters who have visual, auditory, or mobility impairments. The application allows an elector with a disability to submit a variety of requests to the Election Commission through their smartphone. These requests include marking the voter’s record as belonging to a person with disabilities, applying for a new voter registration, requesting a migration of the voter’s electoral roll entry to a different constituency, seeking correction of details on the Electoral Photo Identity Card (EPIC), and requesting the provision of a wheelchair to aid in accessing polling stations. The app leverages the accessibility features already built into modern mobile operating systems, such as screen readers for blind users, voice commands for those with limited hand function, and visual contrast settings for individuals with low vision. By integrating these features, the Saksham app ensures that voters with disabilities can independently manage their electoral interactions without requiring extensive assistance. As with the other applications described, the Saksham app can be downloaded from both the Google Play Store for Android devices and the Apple App Store for iOS devices, thereby extending its reach across the diverse smartphone user base in the country.

The Voter Turnout App is a digital tool that has been introduced to make it possible for election officials, members of the press, and the general public to monitor the progress of voting in real time. When a Returning Officer – the official who is in charge of overseeing the conduct of elections in a particular Assembly Constituency or Parliamentary Constituency – enters provisional turnout figures into the system, those numbers are instantly reflected on the application. This capability allows anyone with access to a smartphone or tablet to see, at any moment during the election, how many voters have cast their ballots in each constituency, providing an up‑to‑date snapshot of voter participation. In addition to serving the administrative function of recording and publishing the data, the application has been designed for use by media organisations that wish to capture live estimates of turnout for broadcast or online reporting. Because the app updates continuously throughout all phases of the election – including the initial voting day, any subsequent phases, and the final counting stage – it offers a comprehensive view of electoral engagement from start to finish. The application can be downloaded free of charge from the Google Play store, which is the official marketplace for Android devices, making it widely accessible to anyone who owns a compatible handset.

The ENCORE Counting platform, accessed through the web address https://encore.eci.gov.in/, represents a fully integrated, end‑to‑end solution that is used by Returning Officers to record the votes that have been polled at each polling station, to compile the data on a round‑by‑round basis, and to generate the various statutory reports that are required by law. The term “end‑to‑end” indicates that the entire counting workflow – from the moment a ballot is marked by a voter to the final tabulation of results that are submitted to the Election Commission – is carried out within a single, secure computer system. By digitising the process, the application eliminates the need for manual data entry on paper, reduces the risk of transcription errors, and speeds up the production of official documents such as constituency‑wise vote totals, percentage calculations, and other legally mandated statements that must be filed after the count is completed. The system also supports the creation of round‑wise reports, which break down the tally after each counting session, thereby providing transparency and enabling observers to verify that the numbers are consistent throughout the counting period.

The publication of results on the Election Commission’s official Results website, found at http://results.eci.gov.in/, and the accompanying visual display known as Results Trends TV, are central to the Commission’s strategy of providing a single, authoritative source of electoral data. The timely release of round‑wise information – meaning the results after each batch of votes has been counted – is critical because it helps to prevent the spread of misinformation and ensures that all stakeholders, from political parties to ordinary citizens, receive the same accurate figures at the same moment. Once the Returning Officers have entered the counting data into the ENCORE system, those figures are automatically reflected on the public website under the heading “Trends and Results.” The website presents the numbers using infographics, which are graphical representations such as charts, maps, and animated diagrams that make complex data easier to understand at a glance. In many public places, including outside the counting hall where the tally takes place, large display screens are set up to show an auto‑scrolling panel that continuously updates with the latest results. This visual presentation not only informs the public but also serves as a deterrent against speculation, because anyone can verify the numbers directly from the official source.

The EVM Management System, abbreviated as EMS, is a specialized software solution created to keep track of the inventory of Electronic Voting Machines (EVMs). An EVM is a compact electronic device that records each vote as a digital signal, replacing the traditional paper ballot. Managing thousands of such machines across a vast country requires a robust system that can monitor the location, status, and condition of each unit. One of the most important features of the EMS is its support for the randomization protocol that is applied before the machines are sent to polling stations. Randomization means that each EVM is assigned to a polling station through a process that is designed to be unpredictable, thereby preventing any possibility of tampering or bias. This assignment is carried out in the presence of representatives of political parties, candidates, and other authorised observers, ensuring that the selection is transparent and witnessed by all interested parties. By documenting every step of the allocation, the EMS contributes to a fair and transparent voting process, reinforcing public confidence that the machines have not been pre‑programmed or otherwise manipulated.

The Voter Service Portal, which can be accessed at https://www.voters.eci.gov.in/ and was previously available through the domains https://nvsp.in and https://voterportal.eci.gov.in, serves as an online gateway for citizens to obtain a wide range of electoral services without having to visit a physical office. Through this portal a user may view the electoral roll – the official list of all eligible voters in a particular area – and verify whether his or her name appears correctly. The portal also allows individuals to apply for a new voter identification card, commonly known as a voter ID, as well as to submit online requests for corrections if there are errors in the name, address, or other details on an existing card. In addition, the system provides information about the specific polling booth where a voter is assigned to cast his or her ballot, and it displays the boundaries of the Assembly Constituency (the state‑level electoral district) and the Parliamentary Constituency (the national‑level electoral district) that the voter belongs to. For those who need assistance, the portal lists contact information for the Booth Level Officer – the official responsible for a single polling station – and the Electoral Registration Officer, who oversees the entire registration process in a district. By consolidating these functions in a single, user‑friendly website, the Election Commission has streamlined the interaction between citizens and the electoral machinery.

The Voter Helpline App, abbreviated as VHA, extends many of the same capabilities offered by the Voter Service Portal to mobile users, allowing citizens to manage their electoral affairs directly from a smartphone or tablet. The application is available on both the Google Play store for Android devices and the Apple App Store for iOS devices, ensuring that it reaches the broadest possible audience. Through the VHA, a citizen can initiate a request for a new voter identification card, submit an online application for correcting details on an existing card, and obtain information about the polling booth assigned to them. The app also provides the names and contact numbers of the Booth Level Officer and the Electoral Registration Officer, facilitating direct communication when assistance is required. By offering these services in a mobile format, the VHA makes it possible for voters to resolve issues and obtain essential information at any time and from any location, thereby reducing the need for in‑person visits to election offices.

The Observer Portal is a web‑based system that has been created to coordinate the activities of the various categories of election observers who are appointed by the Election Commission to monitor the conduct of the poll. These observers include general observers, who are typically members of civil society; police observers, who are senior police officials tasked with overseeing law and order; and expenditure observers, who examine the spending of political parties and candidates to ensure compliance with legal limits. All the latest instructions, guidelines, and circulars issued by the Commission are posted on the portal, giving observers immediate access to the most current procedural information. The portal also allows each observer to verify and, if necessary, update personal details such as bank account information – which is needed for the swift disbursement of any allowances or reimbursements – and a local mobile telephone number, which the Commission uses to send time‑critical SMS alerts. Observers can log in to view their deployment schedule, see which constituency they have been assigned to, and submit their statutory reports directly through the online interface. The system also features a notice board where the Commission can post announcements, and it provides functionality for viewing, uploading, and printing official documents such as the Form 7A, which contains details about a candidate’s election expenses. A further feature is the ability to download and print an Observer Identification Card, which serves as official proof of the observer’s authority while in the field. The portal is presently undergoing a revamp and redevelopment to improve its usability, security, and integration with other election‑related platforms.

Complementing the web‑based observer system, the Observer Mobile App delivers the same suite of functionalities to the handheld devices carried by general, police, and expenditure observers. When an observer receives a deployment order, the complete schedule – including dates, locations, and specific tasks – is automatically pushed to the app, enabling the observer to have a clear, portable itinerary. The mobile app also provides real‑time access to all notices issued by the Election Commission, as well as detailed constituency information such as the list of polling stations and the names of local officials. One of the most significant innovations of the app is its integration with the cVIGIL system, a digital platform used to record and track complaints of electoral malpractices. Through the observer app, users can view all active cVIGIL cases within their jurisdiction, read the descriptions, and add their own observations or remarks. These observations help the Returning Officer – the official responsible for overseeing the count in that constituency – to assess each complaint and decide on any necessary corrective action. In addition, the app displays the locations of flying squads – specialised rapid‑response police teams that are deployed to address emergencies or security incidents during the election. Observers can locate these squads on an interactive map, view details of the work being performed, and initiate a phone call directly from the app, eliminating the need for separate phone numbers or separate coordination channels.

The BLO App, formerly known as the GARUDA App, is a purpose‑built mobile application for Booth Level Officers (BLOs), who are the election officials tasked with maintaining the accuracy of the electoral roll at the level of an individual polling station. By moving their duties onto a digital platform, the app enables BLOs to perform a range of tasks more efficiently and with greater transparency. One of the core functions is a checklist that guides the officer through the verification of forms, ensuring that each required document – such as the voter’s declaration, address proof, and age proof – is examined and recorded correctly. The app also facilitates the collection of data regarding the Assured Minimum Facility (AMF) and the Extended Minimum Facility (EMF), which are standards set by the Commission to guarantee that each polling station has the essential equipment, such as voting booths, signage, and accessibility features, needed for a smooth voting process. By entering this information digitally, the system can quickly flag any deficiencies for remedial action. Another important feature is the ability to capture the geographic coordinates – the latitude and longitude – of each polling station using the built‑in GPS of the officer’s smartphone. These coordinates are then linked to mapping services such as Google Maps, allowing voters to locate their assigned polling station easily on a digital map, which is especially helpful for first‑time voters or those who have moved recently. The BLO App also supports the uploading of photographs of the polling station, providing visual evidence of the layout and the condition of the premises. In addition to recording data, the app allows the officer to submit forms on behalf of electors, for example when a voter requests a correction to their entry in the electoral roll. Finally, the app includes a module for house‑to‑house verification, enabling the BLO to systematically visit residences within the polling area, confirm the eligibility of residents, and update the roll in real time. By integrating all of these functionalities into a single, secure mobile platform, the BLO App streamlines the administrative workload of Booth Level Officers, reduces the likelihood of errors, and contributes to a more accurate and trustworthy electoral roll.

The ERONET, which stands for Electoral Roll Officer Network, is a comprehensive software application that has been made available across the entire territory of India for the purpose of enrolling new voters, correcting existing entries, and facilitating the migration of electors from one electoral roll to another. The system is not only accessible to the general public—every citizen of India can log on to the designated online portals and submit the required information—but it also serves as the back‑end processing engine for a wide hierarchy of officials within the Election Commission of India. These officials include the Chief Electoral Officers of each state and union territory, the District Election Officers who oversee electoral activities at the district level, the Electoral Roll Officers who are responsible for maintaining the accuracy of the voter lists, and the Booth Level Officers who manage the operations at individual polling stations. All twenty‑six states and union territories share a common technical infrastructure that is centrally managed at the national level, which ensures uniformity in data handling and processing across the country. At the heart of this infrastructure lies the Unified National Photo Electoral Roll, abbreviated UNPER, a single, consolidated database that contains photographic and demographic information for approximately ninety‑five crore registered electors, making it one of the largest civil‑status registries in the world. The design of ERONET follows a standardized approach to form handling, database schema, and the layout of printed electoral rolls, thereby eliminating the inconsistencies that previously arose from having multiple, region‑specific systems. By automating the entire lifecycle of electoral roll management—from the initial registration of a voter, through field verification where election officials confirm the authenticity of the applicant’s details, to the decision‑support tools that help electoral registration officers evaluate and approve applications—the system also provides a suite of value‑added services that are integrated into a single, cohesive platform. Citizens who wish to interact with this system may do so through the Voters’ Service Portal, which is reachable at the web address voter dot eci dot gov dot in, or by using the Voter Helpline mobile application that is available for smartphones. When a user fills out any of the various online forms—such as those for new enrollment, name correction, address change, or constituency migration—the data are transmitted directly to ERONET, where the application is logged, processed, and tracked. The applicant receives periodic alerts and notifications by short message service (SMS) or email each time a significant step is completed, ensuring that the voter remains informed throughout the procedure. One of the primary functionalities of ERONET is online form processing; after a citizen submits a form on the designated portal, the system automatically generates an acknowledgment message that is sent to the applicant, confirming receipt. The form is then routed to the Electoral Registration Officer (ERO) or the Assistant Electoral Registration Officer (AERO) for review and further action. The system also contributes to the overall health of the electoral roll, a concept that refers to the accuracy, completeness, and up‑to‑date status of the voter list, which is mandated by the Representation of the People Act of 1950. This Act is the principal piece of legislation governing elections in India and outlines the procedures for preparing, revising, and publishing the electoral rolls prior to each election. By maintaining a high standard of data integrity, ERONET helps ensure that the roll is free from duplications, obsolete entries, and other anomalies that could compromise the fairness of the electoral process. Another important feature is the linkage of each voter’s entry to family information, which enables the system to allocate polling booths in a manner that allows members of a single family to vote at the same location, thereby reducing inconvenience and minimizing the potential for confusion on election day. When an Electoral Registration Officer is assigning parts and sections of a constituency, they can view the family details associated with each applicant, allowing them to make informed decisions about booth allocation. The platform also supports EPIC portability; EPIC stands for Electoral Photo Identity Card, which is the unique identification document issued to every voter. When an elector wishes to move from one constituency to another—perhaps because of a change of residence—the system facilitates the migration while retaining the same EPIC number, eliminating the need to obtain a new card. The migration process is streamlined through seamless communication between the EROs of the originating and destination constituencies, and built‑in de‑duplication mechanisms ensure that the same individual is not inadvertently entered twice in the national roll. In addition to handling online submissions, ERONET accommodates offline form processing. In situations where a voter submits a paper form, a data entry operator can input the information into the system, after which the Assistant Electoral Registration Officer allocates the appropriate workflow tasks for the operator and generates a QR code that encapsulates the form data, facilitating tracking and verification. Over time, the system has been enhanced with a range of alert and monitoring capabilities that are available to officials at every level—from the local Electoral Registration Officer up to the Chief Electoral Officer of a state and the Election Commission of India itself. These capabilities include the ability to trigger “suo moto” actions, meaning that an officer can initiate corrective measures without waiting for an external prompt, especially when logical errors or data entry inconsistencies are detected. The digitization of offline forms, the integration of objective field verification processes, and the generation of unique EPIC numbers are all synchronized with the national electronic roll, ensuring that every state works from the same up‑to‑date dataset. The system also produces a variety of reports, notifications, and alerts that can be viewed on monitoring dashboards, and it supports the printing of the electoral roll for all states, the printing of photo voter slips, and the production of EPIC cards, thereby covering the full spectrum of electoral administration tasks within a single, unified environment.

The National Grievances Services Portal is another major initiative introduced by the Election Commission of India to provide a single, integrated interface for addressing complaints, suggestions, and feedback from a wide array of stakeholders. This portal, often abbreviated as NGSP, functions as a comprehensive grievance redress mechanism that caters not only to ordinary citizens and registered electors but also to political parties, election candidates, members of the media, and election officials operating at the national, state, and district levels. By consolidating all these interactions into one platform, the portal serves as a common point of contact for the entire electoral ecosystem. The design of the portal follows the principle of a seamless, integrated help desk that also incorporates the functionalities of a call centre and a single‑window service centre. In practical terms, this means that any individual or organization can lodge an IFSC, which stands for Information, Feedback, Suggestion, and Complaint, through multiple channels. An online mode is available via the portal itself or through a contact centre that handles telephone inquiries, while offline modes include traditional methods such as fax, mailed letters, or in‑person meetings. Once an IFSC is submitted, the system automatically creates a single interface through which all subsequent handling of the grievance takes place. Election officials—including Electoral Officers at various levels, District Election Officers, Chief Electoral Officers, and officials of the Election Commission—are all part of the same workflow, ensuring that each issue is assigned directly to the appropriate user as soon as it is registered. This automated assignment reduces delays, improves accountability, and allows for real‑time monitoring of the status of each complaint. By providing a unified platform for grievance management, the National Grievances Services Portal enhances transparency, encourages citizen participation, and strengthens the overall integrity of the electoral process in India.
